Commentary:
Q1(i) was well answered with most candidates supplying a wide range of needs that can be met by CI products. However, some candidates discussed CI products in general, including details of the various benefits that are provided by CI products. This did not score any additional marks as the question did not ask for details of the benefits provided by CI products.
Q1(ii) was also generally well answered with candidates again discussing a wide variety of concerns the regulator might have had and providing commentary on the figures in the table and in particular the trends over time. The better candidates provided possible reasons for the various trends seen and why these might, or might not, be causing the regulator concerns.
Some candidates missed that the figures showing the maximum benefit paid out were adjusted for inflation, each being in 2024. The increase in maximum CI benefits paid out over the period was a real increase of (64,000/50,000)^(1/19)-1 = c1.3% p.a. This is equivalent to a nominal rate of increase of around 5.3% p.a. which is higher than annualised price inflation which has been around 4% p.a.. CI premiums had been increasing roughly in line with price inflation, and therefore had been increasing at a slightly lower rate than CI benefits. So it is likely that policyholders diagnosed with a critical illness are likely to be finding that the purchasing power of the benefit they receive has been increasing in real terms. Overall, this is likely to be meeting policyholder’s needs.
Q1(iii) was less well answered than Q1(ii) with candidates not always providing a wide enough range of factors to score highly. Whilst candidates often mentioned product design, product literature, obtaining feedback and monitoring, fewer discussed reviewing customer needs, persistency and claims experience, administration and industry standards in any detail.
Q1(iv) was also less well answered. Many candidates produced a list of key indicators that should be monitored but few made the other points in the solution about what to do with the information collected, how frequently the statistics should be compiled, who should monitor the information etc.

Q2(iii). Unfortunately, there were two typos in the introduction to this question. The references to Company XYZ should have been to Company ABC. The introduction should have read:
’The finance director of Company ABC has proposed that alternative growth strategies are considered. Two strategies put forward are:
 Company ABC to start selling individual income protection policies  Company ABC to start selling additional health and care insurance lines within Country A.’ This caused several candidates some confusion. Some candidates implicitly assumed the question should have referred to Company ABC; other candidates wrote clearly that they were not sure which company the question was referring to and stated which company they assumed it referred to. Other candidates assumed the question was about strategies involving Company XYZ rather than Company ABC.
The marking scheme sets out the solutions for the question as it should have read, i.e. referring to Company ABC. Candidates who answered the question assuming the reference was to Company XYZ were awarded marks for points that were appropriate under this assumption.
For those candidates who understood that the question should have been about Company ABC, the first alternative strategy of selling of individual income protection policies was generally well answered with candidates providing a good range of points. The second strategy was less well answered For those who assumed that the question was about Company XYZ the overall average mark for this question were a little lower than for those who assumed the question referred to Company ABC.
All the scripts which were not reviewed under the script review process, where a candidate had answered this question assuming it referred to Company XYZ were identified. The answers provided to this question were then additionally reviewed by a member of the examining team to assess whether this would have affected the Pass/Fail grade awarded to those scripts. In all cases, the final Pass/Fail grade would have remained the same.

Commentary:
Q3(i). Most students scored well on this question, providing a good range of reasons the employer may want to close the in-house medical scheme.
Q3(ii) As expected, candidates generally also scored highly on this question. Stakeholders less frequently mentioned by candidates were the medical schemes regulator, providers such as actuarial consultants or scheme auditors, staff directly employed in the management of the health scheme and shareholders, if there were any.
Q3(iii) This question was reasonably answered. However, relatively few candidates discussed in any detail the potential impact on the take home pay of employees, that the insurer would likely have a different outlook than the employer; for example, employees may lose any influence over benefit design, or that there were potential issues over underwriting and the risk to those with pre-existing health conditions having to pay high premiums or deductibles or not being able to get cover at all.
Q3(iv) was less well answered. Many candidates concentrated on the communication aspects of the new deal, for example, the company explaining the new regime to employees. Fewer candidates discussed increasing pay to help offset any additional medical costs, subsidising the premiums payable or negotiating with the insurer to accept current employees and family members without any underwriting.
Q3(v) Candidates found this to be difficult; candidates generally did not score well for this question. Few took the approach laid out in the marking scheme of investigating the different funding approaches the company could have taken and giving the corresponding recommendations. However, many candidates scored marks by suggesting that the employer might reducing the scheme benefits or look for alternatives with other insurance companies.
