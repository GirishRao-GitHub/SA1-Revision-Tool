Commentary:
Part (i) of this question required candidates to consider how higher than expected inflation over the next 5 years could impact a health and care insurance company’s business in general, other than claim rates and claim amounts. This was generally well answered with candidates providing answers that covered a wide range of considerations. The better candidates discussed that products that are renewed annually would be repriced and would be less exposed to inflation risk.
Most candidates included discussion of areas such as investment, competition, persistency, expenses, new business and reinsurance. However, only the better candidates discussed operational and counterparty risk. Few candidates discussed the possible actions of management, the effects of state benefits payable or the effects of options and guarantees.
Some candidates did discuss claim rates and amounts in their answer. As these areas were specifically excluded in the question candidates gained no credit for this.
Part (ii) was generally well answered, with relevant comments being made, particularly on the possible effects on claim amounts. The question states that the insurer writes both individual and group business; however, not all candidates discussed the effects on group business claim rates and amounts.
On income protection the better candidates discussed the potential effects on recovery rates and time spent off work and possible selective lapses. Few candidates discussed that the effect on claim rates would depend on the mix of claim definitions, own occupation, any occupation, or activities of daily living, in the business written by the insurance company.
For critical illness few candidates discussed that the possible effects may depend on the structure of the health system. If all health costs are covered, claim rates may not be impacted as the cost of living will not impact the ability to seek medical care and so the diagnosis rates. If the health costs are met out of pocket, there may be a delay in seeking medical care. This may not affect all claim causes equally, heart attack or stroke may be seen more immediately (so less impact to the claim rate) than say cancer diagnosis. A delay in seeking medical attention may cause a higher rate of claims qualifying as critical severity. A delay by the healthcare system to action a diagnosis, may increase the rate of claims as the condition continues to deteriorate to higher (critical) severity.
For PMI, for individual cover few candidates mentioned that the excess limit may be breached for an increasing number of events due to higher medical inflation which will increase the claim rate seen by the insurer or that, as PMI premiums are generally short term, the premiums will be reset based on higher claim amount which may make it unaffordable for those most likely to claim (lower socio-economic groups), and this could reduce the claim rate in the future. Also, for individual business, retention of unhealthy lives may reduce as customers will have less funds available to purchase insurance which could leave Company Y with a healthier pool of policyholders meaning its claim rates could be lower.
For hospital cash plans (HCP) points generally missed include that HCP are intended to keep customer premiums low so there may be a no-claims discount or excess limit which will deter an increase in claims rates or that it is less likely to have higher rates of claim in the short term as hospital cash plans are usually for larger surgeries or longer hospital stays which will be linked to the health of the individual (and not inflation).
Part (iii) was also reasonably well answered with candidates providing comments on a wide range of possible actions. As for part (i) few candidates discussed options and guarantees.
Candidates did not always provide reasons for their suggested actions; for example, they may have said the company should write more group business but did not state why (that group business premiums are reviewed annually and hence the insurer is less exposed to inflation risk). Some candidates only provided generic actions and did not discuss possible actions for each product line.
Part (iv) was not well answered with most candidates only providing one or two possible investigations.
On part (v) whilst most candidates listed several ways expenses loading may be made, only the better candidates discussed that a fixed amount per policy could be specific to product (IP and CI) but would not be specific to the higher socio-economic grouping target or that expense loading by premium might unfairly allocate expenses to those in lower socio- economic groups who have lower sum insureds but higher premiums due to rating at the time of underwriting and that expense loading by sum insured would best meet the required objective as it is most related to higher socio-economic group for CI and IP.
Part (vi) was not well answered. There are many other adjustments that might be made; for example, changes in business mix, moving to products less sensitive to inflation, higher margins required for products susceptible to higher inflation risk.  The resulting premiums for different product liens would need to be considered in relation to the insurer’s competitive environment, attitude to risk etc. If profitability levels for certain products would no longer be achievable the insurer might sell the products as a loss leader or stop selling them or substitute them with more profitable products.
