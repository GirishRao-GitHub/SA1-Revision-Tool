Commentary:
Parts (i) and (ii) of this question required candidates to consider the reasons for underwriting income protection products and the suitability of the approach used to meeting these reasons together with the advantages and disadvantages to customers of the approach used.
In general, both of these question parts were well answered, with candidates discussing the main information sought for underwriting purposes. For income protection this includes information on health and lifestyle to ascertain the likelihood of the potential policyholder claiming and how long a claim might last.
The insurer would wish to reduce anti-selection but also be able to decline as few a number of potential policyholders as possible. The insurer would also wish to consider the time and expense required for the underwriting approach against the resulting claims experience of policyholders.
For part (ii) whilst most candidates provided several advantages, fewer candidates discussed disadvantages such as the process taking a long time for some customers, but which could still end with the customer being declined cover or having to pay a very high premium for cover.
The remainder of the question parts required candidates to set out various considerations that the insurer would need to make if it were to change to using an automated underwriting process using machine learning and artificial learning algorithms.
Part (iii) was well answered with candidates providing a wide range of additional information that the insurer should obtain on the proposed underwriting system.
Part (iv) was less well answered with candidates generally not providing a sufficient number of different distinct ideas to score well. Many candidates did not show a good understanding of the potentially wide-ranging impact on XYZ business from implementing AutoUW’s underwriting system, and therefore did not come up with enough distinct ideas to score well.
For example few candidates discussed that the insurer's existing claims data would potentially not be representative of future claims experience, the potential changes in future business mix, the potential effects on reserves and capital or that the insurer would be completely dependent on the underwriting process, which could cause problems if AutoUW ceased to trade or the underwriting system did not work as expected. Some candidates made general comments but did not relate them to the context of the question; for instance, mentioning reinsurance but not discussing the need for the insurer to discuss the implications of the proposed new underwriting approach with their reinsurers in terms of any existing treaty or their reinsurer’s ability to provide advice on implementation of the new approach.
Given the large number of marks available for this question part, it would have been helpful for candidates to have spent an appropriate amount of time planning their answers to produce a good range of ideas, and then developing those ideas in their answer.
In parts (v) and (vi) candidates often found it hard to provide sufficient concerns a regulator might have beyond the potential for discrimination, such as the risk the insurer does not understand the underwriting system and the potential impact on profitability and solvency or the risk of over reliance on third party technology and hence did not score well.

Commentary:
This question considered a country which currently had both private and public health care systems, but which was proposing to set up a social insurance system to replace them.
Part (i) was generally not well answered with candidates providing too few distinct points to score highly. Those candidates who considered the implications of the change from both public and private perspectives tended to score high marks (particularly when considering medical practitioners and the public). The better candidates linked points to the context provided in the question. A few candidates appeared to think that the new regime would include private insurance cover even though the question states that 'Private medical insurance would no longer cover any benefits provided for under the social insurance scheme'.
It is vital that candidates fully read the question before starting to write their answers in the exam.
Whilst part (a) was reasonably answered, only the better candidates tended to discuss issues such as the government needing to consider the legislation required, how the scheme would be administered, how long it would take to set up the new scheme and whether any transitional arrangements would be required.
For part (b) the main issue would be that the insurance industry could no longer offer many of the PMI products that it currently sells so would need to consider whether it could provide complementary products or move into other product areas such as critical illness or helping administer the scheme.
For part (c) the better candidates realised there would be different issues for medical practitioners who worked in the private sector and those who worked in the public sector. In general this part was not well answered.
Similarly, in part (d) few candidates considered the issues for those citizens who had private medical insurance and those who used public healthcare services.
Part (ii) was a standard bookwork question on different ways of funding healthcare provided by the state and was well answered.
Part (iii) was also generally well answered although not all candidates discussed that payroll deductions may not work well for self-employed people or those who run small businesses.

Commentary:
Questions such as part (i) require candidates to discuss a wide range of areas to be considered by the insurer, tailored to the scenario described, to score well. Here relevant areas included the benefits to be provided by the products, the expenses likely to be incurred, underwriting, issues arising to the distribution of the product, capital requirements and potential regulatory issues. For example, not all candidates made the connection that as there was no premium there was a need to consider how the launch and administration expenses would be met. Similarly, there would likely not be any underwriting which could lead to selection issues.
The question stated that the product would be valid for a 6-month term, would be available to customers who spent a minimum amount of money and would be available for a limited promotional period.  The better candidates identified these details from the question, and made specific points relating to each of these. For example, the minimum spend should be set at a level appropriate for the insurer's target market, but this would also affect the number of people signing up, large numbers of people signing up might lead to capital strain, even with a limited promotional period.
Part (ii) and (ii) were not well answered. Candidates should be able to provide several relevant points by considering that the regulator would wish to ensure the financial soundness of the insurer and to promote consumer protection.  A major issue for financial soundness would be whether the insurer could meet the liabilities given that the product has no premiums. Under consumer protection there would likely be concerns as to whether the customers understood the product, whether other policyholders would effectively be meeting the cost of the product, that the insurer may be getting information on the spending patterns of customers and that the insurer may provide poor service as the product is effectively free for those taking it up.
Part (iv) was not well answered.  Most candidates suggested that an advertising campaign would cover a wider range of people and have a more tailored message. However, only the better candidates suggested that the cost would be more certain but that it would be difficult to measure how successful the campaign was.
There was a general deterioration in the average mark’s that candidates scored over the last few question parts which may point to some candidates experiencing time pressure in this exam.
