 ii)  Characteristics of different factors that  have an impact on lapse assumptions: 
x Age
 ŌĆō Younger policyholders tend to have a high er lapse rate than older policyholders, 
except at ages just prior to retirement. 
x Frequency of premium reviews (long-term policies)  ŌĆō A policyholder with monthly premium 
payments has twelve times as many opportun ities for lapsation as one paying annually. 
However when premiums are collected automati cally through ECS facility and cancellation 
requires a positive action by the insured, persistency usually significantly increases, 
therefore worst persistency is seen in quarterl y and half yearly mode compared to annual 
and monthly model policies. 
x Duration  ŌĆō Most blocks of policies will have laps e rates that decrease by duration. Lapse 
rates will usually become level after a period of time. They will often be expressed as a 
constant level after a specific duration. 
x After - sales service  ŌĆō Policyholders buying health is a sophisticated customer and therefore 
after sales service, regular renewal notice are very important factors affecting product 
lapses. 
x Distribution methods including distribution channels  ŌĆō Banc-assurance as a distribution 
channel has much better customer loyalty, there is  less miss-selling in the interest to protect 
image of the bank and therefore better persis tency is experienced compared to agency 
model. 
x Past claims experience (regular claimants are less likely to lapse)  ŌĆō those policyholders who 
are claiming regularly, they see a cash benefit in continuing policy and much lesser 
likelihood of their lapsing the policy. 
x The economic situation  ŌĆō when the economic cycle is down it will have a co-relation with 
increased lapsation. 
x Competitiveness  ŌĆō Premium levels can have a significant impact on lapsation. If 
policyholders can find equivalent coverage el sewhere for less money, they are much more 
likely to lapse a current policy than otherwise. Premium increases can have a drastic effect 
on lapsation. Some insurers have experience d situations in which the entire expected 
aggregate increase in premium was offset by increased lapsation. 
x Occupation  ŌĆō The occupation of the policyholder can have a significant impact on 
persistency. While this is usually considered in analysis of disability income covered, it is 
usually not considered for medical coverage.

i. Following are the major heads of Assets of a S tandalone Health Insurance Company in India:  
1. Investment:  This consists of GOI bonds, corporat e bonds, infrastructure bonds, housing 
bonds, mutual funds, money market instruments,  equity shares, etc. As far as value is 
concerned this is generally the most important asset category in the balance sheet. The currency of the assets is generally INR and tenure can be long term, medium term, short 
term, liquid, etc. depending on the CompanyŌĆÖs investment policy for various assets classes.  
2. Fixed Assets:
 This consists of land & building, o ffice furniture, equipment, vehicles, 
software, etc. As the name suggests this is lo ng term in nature and annual depreciation is 
charged on its value. 
3. Cash & Bank Balance:  This is the most liquid asset and consists of cash, bank balance, short 
term deposits, etc. 
4. Miscellaneous Assets:  Other assets are pre-paid expenses, deposits, advances, reinsurance 
recoverable, etc. These generally are of comparati vely small in value and may be of short to 
medium term. 
 Following are the major heads of Liabilities of a Standalone Health Insurance Company in India: 
1. Unearned Premium Reserve (UPR):  This would be a major chun k of outsider liability of a 
health insurance company. Since,  tenure of majority of the heal th policies is one year the 
nature of this reserve is short term only. 
2. Claims Reserve:  This consists of outstanding claim reserve and reserve for claims incurred 
but not reported (IBNR). Since, health insurance is a short tailed line of business, nature of 
this is short-term. If the company is writing ov erseas health insurance too, then actual claim 
amount for outstanding claims may be in fo reign currency and converted in INR for the 
purpose of showing in balance sheet.

 3. Other Reserves:  There can be Catastrophic (CAT) Reserve or Premium Deficiency Reserve

iii. It is not absolutely necessary to strictly match all assets and liabilities by duration. Neither

have adequate measures to minimise adverse im pact of mismatching if any. Mismatching may

iv. a) Exactly matched by duration:  If the portfolio is exactly matched then there is no risk of 
liquidating investment at inopportune time (because of movement in interest rate or equity 
value). All the liabilities due may be met by the proceeds of the assets and there may not be any 
gap. But on the other hand, the assets will be of shorter duration as we have seen in point (i) 
liabilities of health insurers are of short term  in nature. This means lower yield in normal 
circumstances. 
 
      b)   Not matched by duration:  If the portfolio is not exactly matched, in the absence of free assets or 
   net positive future cash flows, the company may be compelled to sell its long term investment to  
  meet its liabilities. If for example interest rate  is higher at that time, the realisation from the 
   sales proceeds of long term bonds, etc. will be  lower resulting in loss on sale of investment.  
  However, the return in terms of normal yield wi ll be higher if money is invested in longer term 
  assets. 
Essentially company needs to maintain a trade- off between risk and reward in managing its 
assets and liabilities.   (4 Marks)
