The Ind AS 117 rule that changes everything: The gross LIC and the RCH must be measured independently. You cannot set one assumption for the gross and a different one for the net. If you update the recovery rate assumption on the gross LIC, the same update must flow through to the RCH — the reinsurer shares in the same underlying risk. The only legitimate difference between the two is the credit risk adjustment on the RCH.
The gross LIC is the present value of all future monthly benefits expected to be paid to Priya — using current best-estimate assumptions, discounted at the rate locked in at initial recognition.

The risk adjustment on the LIC: Just like the LRC, the gross LIC carries its own RA — the compensation for bearing the uncertainty that recovery takes longer or shorter than expected. Under Ind AS 117, the RA on the LIC is recognised separately and releases to the insurance service result as time passes and duration uncertainty resolves.

The RCH is the mirror of the gross LIC — but it is an asset, and it must carry one additional adjustment that the gross never needs: the expected credit loss from the possibility that the reinsurer defaults.

At an A rating, the credit adjustment is small but not zero. The ECL must be recognised even if the insurer has never had a recovery failure from this reinsurer — Ind AS 117 requires a forward-looking, probability-weighted credit assessment.
What cannot be different between gross and RCH: Recovery rate assumption, mortality assumption, benefit amount, and discount rate must all be identical. The RCH is derived from the gross — not independently estimated. Auditors will specifically test that no assumptions have been quietly adjusted to inflate the RCH relative to the gross cession percentage.
