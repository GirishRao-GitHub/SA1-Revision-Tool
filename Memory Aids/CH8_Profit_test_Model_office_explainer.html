These cashflows depend on assumptions: claim rates, lapses, inflation, investment return. The uncertainty in these assumptions is exactly what the margin is meant to cover.
Single policy profit test — step 2

Discount to a single NPV

Collapse the cashflow stream into one number using the Risk Discount Rate (RDR)

No margin case
RDR = 8% (base required return, stripped of any safety margin)
NPV = +£42
Just barely profitable
With margin
RDR = 11% (base + 3% margin for risk)
NPV = −£18
Requires higher premium to be viable
The margin in the RDR is the lever. A higher RDR means you need a higher premium to achieve a positive NPV — that extra premium is the buffer against bad experience.
So the question becomes: how big should that margin be? That's what the sensitivity analysis answers.
Single policy profit test — step 3

Test with adverse experience

Three ways to stress-test the assumptions and find the right margin

Deterministic sensitivity Manually shift each assumption to its plausible worst case (e.g. claims 20% higher, lapses double). Re-run the NPV. See how much the RDR needs to rise to stay profitable.
Simple, transparent, easy to explain to management.
Stochastic model Assign probability distributions to each assumption. Run thousands of simulated futures. The spread of NPV outcomes directly tells you the variance — and hence the required margin.
More rigorous, but distributions are hard to define for lapses/social trends.
Analytical methods Use maths (e.g. variance formulas) to propagate uncertainty from assumptions through to NPV, without simulation. Limited to variables with tractable distributions.
Efficient but only works for certain variable types.
Output of this step: A chosen RDR margin (e.g. +3%) that makes the product financially resilient to the range of plausible bad outcomes — for one policy.

Question 1: Future solvency
Does the new product cause the company's free assets to fall dangerously low if claims experience is bad? Could it trigger a regulatory solvency breach?
Question 2: Embedded value
Does the product create or destroy shareholder value? A profitable single policy might still damage embedded value if it increases aggregate risk significantly.

The model office might reveal that a particular assumption — say, lapse rates — matters far more to company-level solvency than the single policy test suggested. This can feed back and change the margins the actuary chooses.
This is the feedback loop: model office insights → revised view of financial significance → revised margins.
Putting it together

How the two tools interact

They answer different questions and inform each other

Single policy profit test
→ What RDR margin makes one policy viable under stress?
↓ feeds margin into pricing
Model office
→ Does that margin protect the whole company under stress?
↓ may suggest larger margins are needed
Final pricing basis
→ Margins that balance risk, competition & return on capital
Exam tip: The model office doesn't replace the single policy test — it adds a layer. The single policy test sets the per-policy margin. The model office validates (or challenges) that choice at the company level. Both are needed.
