Monitoring Stage of the Actuarial Control Cycle
SP1 Ch.29 · SA1 Ch.19–20 CONSOLIDATED STUDY MATRIX — OVERVIEW
① The Three Pillars of Monitoring
① Why Monitor?
  • Update assumptions for future experience
  • Monitor adverse trends in experience
  • Compare actual vs expected → corrective action
  • Provide management information for decisions
  • Inform pricing & reserving adequacy
  • Check underwriting effectiveness & anti-selection
  • Assess reinsurance need / profit share entitlement
  • Identify profitable products, markets, channels
  • Feed back into the actuarial control cycle
② What is Monitored?
  • New business volumes & mix
  • Mortality & morbidity (incidence & termination)
  • Claims amounts & cost trends
  • Persistency / lapse rates
  • Expenses (initial, renewal, termination, claims, investment)
  • Investment returns
  • Renewal rates · Option take-up rates
  • Customer satisfaction · Reinsurance performance
  • Solvency levels · Competition & regulation
③ How? Data Requirements
  • Reasonable volume — stable & consistent
  • Homogeneous risk groups (credibility vs granularity)
  • Same source, grouping criteria, reliability across groups
  • Period: long enough for volume, short enough for relevance
  • Expenses: ~12 months · Morbidity: 3–5 years
  • Own portfolio + market + reinsurer data
  • Two strands: Direct A vs E · Analysis of Surplus/Profit
  • Frequency depends on class (e.g. LTC annual; new product expenses quarterly)

③ Analysis of Surplus / Profit — The Two Bases
Analysis of Surplus Arising (Long-Term Insurance)
Surplus arising = (A₁ − L₁) − (A₀ − L₀) · Reconciliation of start-year to end-year surplus, broken into contributory factors
Statutory Basis Reserves include prudential margins
Liabilities defined as: Supervisory reserves with prudential margins baked in. Surplus = Assets − Prudential Reserves.
Sources of Surplus
Opening AdjustmentsModel/data corrections; methodology changes (e.g. ESG, proxy model updates)
Return on Opening SurplusExpected investment return on the opening surplus (at discount rate)
Economic VariancesDifferences in actual vs expected investment returns, inflation, FX. Usually the dominant source. Includes mismatch profit/loss.
Non-Economic (Insurance) VariancesMorbidity, mortality, persistency, expense differences vs valuation assumptions
Assumption ChangesUpdates to non-economic or economic valuation assumptions
New Business ImpactPremiums less initial outgo + investment return to valuation date. On prudent basis → often negative (NB strain)
Other VariancesCapital injections, tax changes
Unexplained MovementsResidual — management should set acceptable tolerance level
Key Characteristic
Prudential margins release to surplus over time. An insurer expects to see positive surplus items on a prudent basis. Alarm if any item is negative.
Process (Roll-Forward)
1Re-run start-year valuation model
2Apply opening adjustments (corrections, methodology changes)
3Run with new non-economic assumptions → isolate assumption change impact
4Roll forward using actual investment returns + new economic scenarios → economic variance
5Adjust for known non-economic A vs E differences (expenses, morbidity etc) → insurance variance
6Add new business written in period
7Adjust for capital injections, tax changes
8Compare final rolled-forward B/S to actual year-end B/S → unexplained
Order matters — no uniquely correct method, but must be consistent year-to-year for trend comparisons.
Solvency II Basis BEL + Risk Margin + SCR
Liabilities defined as: Technical Provisions = BEL + Risk Margin. Surplus = Assets − TP (or Assets − TP − SCR if capital included).
Additional Sources vs Statutory
Opening AdjustmentsAs statutory — corrections, methodology changes
Return on Opening SurplusAs statutory — expected return on opening surplus
Economic Variances (incl. MA/VA)Dominant source. Includes mismatch profit. Under matching adjustment: surplus reflects difference between actual spread/defaults and fundamental spread assumption. Under volatility adjustment: spread on actual holdings vs EIOPA representative portfolio.
Non-Economic VariancesAs statutory — morbidity, mortality, persistency, expenses
Assumption ChangesAs statutory — but economic assumption changes include risk discount rate changes
Risk Margin ChangesChanges in allowances for diversification within RM calculations; reinsurance strategy changes can have significant impact → identified separately
SCR ChangesChanges in size of SCR components; shift from standard formula to internal model; inclusion of new risk factors
New Business / Other / UnexplainedAs statutory
Key Characteristic
No prudential margin release. BEL is best estimate → insurer expects to see both positive and negative items. A negative result is not alarming unless large or persistent. Under Solvency II, profit & loss attribution is a regulatory requirement (internal model approval).
Process (Roll-Forward)
1–8Same roll-forward process as statutory
+Additionally allow for changes in Risk Margin over valuation period (simplified approach likely — complexity of RM calculation)
+Additionally allow for changes in SCR if surplus defined as assets minus TP minus SCR
+For MA portfolios: identify variance from difference between actual defaults/spreads and fundamental spread assumption
If PVIF is zero or immaterial (common under Solvency II for EU insurers), analysis of change in EV = analysis of change in free surplus. No additional value from EV analysis beyond AoS.

④ Analysis of Profit — Short-Term Insurance (e.g. PMI)
Short-Term Profit Analysis
No long-term reserving basis involved. Profitability assessed by yearly cohort with quarterly analyses to establish up-to-date positions. Three key indices monitored — quarterly results feed into pricing for new business.
Rate of Incidence
= No. of separate claims ÷ Exposed to risk
Measures frequency of claims. Preferred over loss ratio for trend detection.
Average Cost Per Claim
= Total incurred claims cost ÷ No. of separate claims
Measures severity. Combined with incidence gives full picture of claims experience.
Loss Ratio
= Total incurred claims cost ÷ Earned premium
Quick & simple but hides frequency vs severity trends. Use as cross-check only.
Incurred Claims must include:
  • Claims paid to date (OS)
  • Claims reported, payment outstanding
  • Claims incurred but not yet reported (IBNR)
PMI Specific Points:
  • Duration-based cohorts — selection effect in early years (lower loss ratios)
  • NCD system: must assess cohort experience quickly to adjust promotion/demotion rules
  • Group PMI: straightforward — claims vs premiums + expenses per scheme
  • Consistent claim definition essential for cross-insurer comparisons
⑤ Analysis of Change in Embedded Value (Long-Term only)
Analysis of Change in EV  ·  EV = Net Assets + PVIF
Net Assets = Free Surplus + Required Capital (less cost of holding)
EV does not include value of future new business. Any new business written in the period → change in EV. Analysis typically over one year. Components of change in EV:
① Return on Net AssetsActual investment return on opening net assets. Split: expected return + excess over expected. For McEV: expected = risk-free rate.
② Expected Return on In-Force'Unwind' of discount rate on opening PVIF = PVIF(t)×r − S(t). Transfer from PVIF to net assets as expected profits emerge. Zero net impact on total EV.
③ Experience VariancesDifferences between actual & expected experience (economic + non-economic). Economic: in net assets. Non-economic: in PVIF (e.g. higher CI lapses → fewer future profits → lower PVIF).
④ Operating Assumption ChangesChanges to projection/experience basis (non-economic) AND supervisory valuation basis (economic, incl. risk discount rate). Projection basis changes mainly affect PVIF; supervisory basis changes affect both net assets and PVIF.
⑤ New Business ContributionPV of future profits from new business written. Prudent basis: ↓ net assets (NB strain) but ↑ PVIF. Best estimate basis: ↑ net assets, neutral PVIF. Overall EV increases if business is profitable.
⑥ Other ItemsModel changes, capital injections, tax changes, exceptional expenses — as for analysis of surplus.
Solvency II impact on EV analysis: Under Solvency II, PVIF often zero/immaterial (BEL basis → no explicit prudential margins to release). In this case, AoE in EV = analysis of change in free surplus. Required capital component changes due to: actual investment return on RC, differences in actual vs expected release of RC, frictional lock-in cost differences, new business increasing RC, methodology/calibration changes. Many EU health & care companies no longer carry out formal EV analysis under Solvency II.
Uses of EV Analysis: Validate calculations, assumptions & data Reconcile successive years' values Management information Executive remuneration data Publication in accounts (value of new business) Identify profitable lines · Return on capital Identify drivers of profit at customer level

⑧ Customer Value
What it measures Value placed on an existing or potential new customer throughout their lifetime. Monitored via persistency rates and/or EV-based measures including expected profits from future cross-selling and fulfilling changing needs.
How it's analysed EV analysis techniques identify drivers of profit/loss at customer level. Simulation/scenario approaches used where traditional financial models are insufficient. Analysis of impact of business decisions (marketing, distribution, UW approach, product design, pricing) on customer value.
Special cases Legacy portfolios & closed funds: customer value analysis to maximise profit from the existing block — e.g. outsourcing admin/claims management to reduce costs, while monitoring impact on lapses. Modelling requirements are complex with limited data on customer behaviour.

Economic Conditions & Claims (§4.1)
Economic conditions can significantly distort health & care claims experience — must be identified and separated from genuine long-term trends.
Economic Factor Impact on Claims
Recession / High unemployment↑ IP claims (stress-related illness; reluctance to return to work if job at risk). ↑ PMI usage before potential job loss.
Economic upturn↓ IP claims (more willing to return to work). ↑ new business.
Inflation (medical cost)↑ PMI claim amounts even if frequency unchanged. Trend more important than day-to-day volatility.
Interest rates↓ rates → ↑ reserve values → impacts surplus analysis (economic variance).
Measuring impact: Compare affected period vs unaffected benchmark period for same company (or unaffected company in same market). Adjust for inflation and other unrelated factors.
Anti-selection, Non-disclosure & Early Claims (§3)
Anti-selection: Potential policyholders use private health information to take cover on favourable terms — those most likely to claim are most likely to insure.
Non-disclosure: Applicant asked for information but fails to disclose it. Distinct from anti-selection (which may be passive).
⏱ Durational effect: Impact concentrated in Year 1 and to a lesser extent Year 2. After that, adversely selected policyholders will have already claimed. Experience "reverts to normal" from Year 3+ — other factors dominate.
Actuary's response: investigate early claims by cause · review proposal questions · review UW sum insured triggers · review supplementary questionnaire · review sales methods & target market. Incorporate in pricing & reserving via durational select tables.
The Insurance Cycle (§4.3)
PHASE 1 High Profits — Rates high; insurers profitable; new entrants attracted; existing insurers try to expand.
PHASE 2 Greater Competition — More insurers → premiums soften; commissions raised; advertising increased. Margins trimmed. Often coincides with economic upturn optimism.
PHASE 3 Losses — Reduced profits or losses. Cycle depressed. Insurers exit or reduce involvement.
PHASE 4 Upswing — Losses reduce appetite; premium rates rise; smaller insurers struggle. Leads back to Phase 1.
More influential in short-term group risks (PMI, group IP) than long-term individual CI/IP (premium locked in at inception). Different classes at different cycle points simultaneously. Inter-company data (e.g. CMI pooled data) helps identify where own experience sits relative to cycle.

Investigates risks covered, broken down by grouping policies of similar nature.

One-Way TableEach rating factor considered individually. Simple but may conceal correlations between factors.

Movement Analysis — Delay Methodology (§7.3)
Movement rates (lapse, NB, cancellation, endorsement) must use movements actually taking place in the period, not movements reported — reporting delays distort early readings.
Lapse rate (short-term):
Lapse Rate = Lapses (period x) ÷ Renewals Invited (period x)

Delay adjustment: Build development table from history showing % of lapses processed by end of each subsequent month (e.g. 37% month 0 → 68% month 1 → 89% month 2 → 97% month 3 → 100% month 4). Divide observed count by % to project ultimate.

Example: April lapses observed = 177 at end of May (month 1). 68% processed. Estimated ultimate = 177 ÷ 0.68 = 260. Lapse rate = 260 ÷ 880 renewals = 30%.
Mid-term cancellation rate = cancellations ÷ mean in-force (m-type rate)
New business rate = new policies ÷ renewals invited (same base as lapse rate — direct comparison)
Portfolio signal: Lapse rate > NB rate → portfolio contracting. Lapse rate < NB rate → portfolio expanding.
