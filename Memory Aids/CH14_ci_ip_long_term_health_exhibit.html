Long-term health insurance: how the liability really works
Critical illness (CI) and income protection (IP) have multi-year coverage, deferred claims, and benefit periods — making their accounting very different from annual health policies.
Choose a product to see its structural features. These drive everything about how the liability is measured.
Critical illness (CI)
Lump-sum on diagnosis of a covered condition. Single payment, then cover ends. High-severity, low-frequency.
Income protection (IP)
Monthly benefit paid throughout a disability. Benefit period can run years — even to retirement. Frequency moderate, duration uncertain.
The liability profile of a long-term health policy is not a straight line. It peaks, then falls — shaped by claim probabilities, discount rates, and benefit obligations.

For long-term products, the CSM is not released evenly. Coverage units — the basis for release — change as the remaining risk profile changes year by year.
Annual health (PAA) — straight line
12-month policy. Each month is identical. CSM release = 1/12 per month.
CI / IP (GMM) — front-weighted
20-year policy. Earlier years carry more risk per unit. CSM release accelerates in early years.
Why does it matter? Under PAA (short-term policies), profit is earned straight-line. Under GMM (long-term CI/IP), profit is earned faster early — because the insurer is bearing more risk in year 1 than year 15. If you benchmark the two without understanding this, CI/IP books look artificially profitable early and thin late.
