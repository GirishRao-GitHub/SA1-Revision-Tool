Why LRC is not the same as expected claims
A self-contained explainer using one policy: Ramesh pays ₹12,000 annual health premium on 1 April 2026. All amounts in ₹.

The insurer's three promises to Ramesh:
1. Pay his claims (up to the sum insured) — expected to cost ₹7,500.
2. Bear the uncertainty that claims could be more than ₹7,500 — this is a real service, and it has a cost.
3. Administer his policy for 12 months — process claims, maintain the panel, send renewal notices.

Why are LRC and expected claims different?
Because the insurer does more than just pay claims. It also runs the claims department, maintains the hospital network, sends letters, employs doctors on its panel — all of this costs money. Under Ind AS 117, those future servicing costs (₹500 here) must be included in the LRC alongside the claims estimate. So: LRC = ₹7,500 claims + ₹500 expenses = ₹8,000.
Part 3 — What each component means in plain words
Each of the four layers has a specific purpose. Understanding the purpose makes the accounting logic clear.
Expected claims — ₹7,500 inside LRC
The actuary's best guess of what Ramesh will actually claim over 12 months. It is the central estimate — a 50th percentile view, with no cushion for bad luck. In a portfolio of 10,000 Rameshes, this is the average expected payout per policy.
Example: if Ramesh has a history of one hospitalisation every two years costing ₹15,000, the annual expected claim is ₹7,500. Simple probability math.
Future expenses — ₹500 inside LRC
These are the directly attributable costs of servicing Ramesh's policy over the next 12 months — the portion of claims-processing salaries, TPA fees, hospital empanelment costs, and renewal administration that can be directly linked to his policy. General overheads (CEO salary, rent, IT) are NOT included here — they go to the P&L as period costs.
Under old IGAAP, these expenses were just charged to P&L when incurred. Under Ind AS 117, they are estimated upfront and included in the fulfilment cash flows, giving a truer picture of what the policy actually costs to deliver.
Risk adjustment (RA) — ₹2,000 separate component
Claims could be higher than ₹7,500. Maybe Ramesh gets cancer. Maybe there's a dengue outbreak. The RA is the extra amount the insurer holds to compensate itself for bearing this uncertainty. It is NOT a claims reserve — it is the price of uncertainty, similar to the "risk loading" in actuarial pricing. Under Ind AS 117, it must be calculated at a specified confidence level (IRDAI has not yet prescribed the method, but the 75th percentile is common internationally).
Why does this matter? Under old IGAAP, the risk loading was hidden inside the unearned premium reserve. Nobody could see it separately. Now it is explicit — investors can see exactly how much cushion the insurer holds, and how conservative or aggressive its risk assumptions are. As months pass without a major claim, the RA releases to profit, rewarding the insurer for bearing risk that did not materialise.
Contractual service margin (CSM) — ₹2,000 profit in a box
After covering fulfilment costs (₹8,000) and the risk charge (₹2,000), the remaining ₹2,000 is the profit margin the insurer locked in when it wrote this policy. Under old IGAAP, an insurer could book a portion of this profit on day one. Ind AS 117 says: no — you must hold it on the balance sheet as a liability (the CSM) and release it to the P&L gradually as you actually provide cover each month.
The discipline this creates: If the policy turns out to be loss-making (claims > premium), there is no CSM — instead, the loss is recognised immediately. Profitable and loss-making policies can no longer offset each other at the portfolio level. Each cohort stands on its own.

Why monthly revenue = ₹1,000 (not ₹625)? Because revenue under Ind AS 117 is not just claims — it is the full service provided. Each month the insurer earns: ₹625 (claims portion) + ₹42 (expense portion) + ₹167 (RA release) + ₹167 (CSM release) = ₹1,000. The insurer provided cover worth ₹1,000 that month — that is the revenue. The claims cost of ₹625 is a separate expense line. The difference (₹375) is the monthly insurance service result — the profit from underwriting that month.
Part 5 — The key equation to remember
Premium (₹12,000)
= Fulfilment cash flows [LRC] (₹8,000) + Risk adjustment [RA] (₹2,000) + Contractual service margin [CSM] (₹2,000)

Fulfilment cash flows [LRC] (₹8,000)
= Expected claims (₹7,500) + Expected future expenses (₹500)

So: Expected claims ≠ LRC
LRC is larger than expected claims because it also includes the cost of servicing the policy. The gap between them (₹500 here) is small for simple annual health policies but can be much larger for complex products with significant ongoing administration.
