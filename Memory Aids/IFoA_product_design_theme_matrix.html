1. Benefit structure & form
  • Lump sum vs regular income — both possible; form must meet customer needs
  • Term of benefit: rest of life vs fixed term (2, 5 years)
  • Indemnity (full care costs) vs fixed/indexed benefit — fixed greatly reduces financial risk
  • Benefit level linked to level of disability (tiered) — reduces cost but adds complexity
  • Benefits paid to policyholder or direct to care provider
  • Index-linked benefits: salary index suits salary-related costs; accommodation costs may differ
  • Integration with state LTC provision to maximise value
  • Fixed lump sum on diagnosis — not indemnity
  • Severity-based partial payments for less serious events
  • Consider whether sum assured kept pace with inflation — real value matters to policyholders
  • Adding conditions should reflect genuine customer need; 100 conditions with 50–60% payout rate signals poor benefit design
  • Benefits regularly increased to keep pace with inflation to preserve buying power
  • RoP: pre-specified event triggers — surrender, lapse, end of term, death, partial claim, conversion, moratorium period
  • Partial payment on lower-severity events (e.g. superficial melanoma at 10% of sum insured)
  • Children's cancer: standalone benefit or accelerated; lump sum covers treatment costs
  • Mental health: lump sum on diagnosis of qualifying severe condition
  • Severity-based tiers across mental health conditions to be defined clearly
  • Benefit as % of pre-disability salary (replacement ratio)
  • Deferred period before benefit starts (1, 3, 6 months)
  • Maximum benefit period and payment cessation conditions
  • Stepped/partial disability benefit — reduces on partial return to work
  • Workers vs managers differentiation (group)
  • Maximum replacement ratio to encourage return to work
  • Fixed cash sum on hospitalisation (per night or per event)
  • Benefit per night of hospital stay links payment more closely to need
  • Low benefit limits essential for micro-level affordability
  • Fixed sum — not indemnity — keeps administration simple
  • Free hospital cash: no premium; benefit funded through distribution budget
  • Outpatient benefit: fixed cash on GP/clinic visit (possible add-on)
  • Choice of lump sum or regular income to meet student living needs
  • Benefits match students' living costs (rent, education fees, loan repayments)
  • Conditions prevalent among university students (e.g. glandular fever, serious illness)
  • Cash benefit usable for any purpose — immediate care, rehabilitation, lifestyle change
  • Student chooses benefit amount balanced against premium affordability
  • IP vs OP vs day care boundaries must be explicit to avoid disputes
  • Reimbursement or cashless; cashless removes upfront payment anxiety
  • Free hospital cash: per-night benefit for each overnight stay in hospital
  • Product must fit with services available in the distribution outlet (e.g. grocery pharmacy)
  • Group PMI: one set of benefits or tiered by employee grade
2. Claim definitions & triggers
  • ADL-based trigger: how many ADLs failed to trigger benefit
  • Cognitive impairment as separate or additional trigger
  • Definitions of each ADL must be unambiguous
  • Care setting definitions: home care vs residential vs nursing care
  • Ongoing benefit contingent on survival and continuance of incapacity
  • CI list must reflect conditions that are severe, frequent and clearly definable
  • Survival period (e.g. 30 days post-diagnosis) — key control mechanism
  • Definitions must be precise to avoid ambiguity at time of claim
  • Severity thresholds must be clear for partial payment tiers
  • Product literature must ensure policyholders understand when claims will/will not pay out
  • Children's cancer: definition of "child" (age limits) — clearly stated
  • RoP trigger events precisely defined (e.g. surrender only allowed after initial period)
  • Mental health: condition must be measurable and objectively defined — avoids ambiguity at claim
  • Some mental health conditions may not be suitable if they cannot be clearly defined
  • Severity levels for mental health conditions to be defined as rigorously as physical CI conditions
  • Definition of incapacity: own occupation vs any occupation — key design choice
  • Linked claims period: when a new claim can begin after first
  • Clear salary definition (basic vs total compensation)
  • Disability definitions must be clear to limit disputes
  • Second medical opinions and periodic review criteria specified at outset
  • Hospital stay definition: minimum nights required (e.g. 3 nights vs 1–2 nights)
  • Hospital definition: which facilities qualify — risk of moral hazard if too broad
  • Outpatient: visit to outpatient facility, clinic or doctor — per-visit trigger
  • Waiting period for new entrants or gap in cover exceeds specified period (90–180 days)
  • Good health statement required on policy restart after lapse
  • Conditions to reflect those prevalent among students — not exclusively cancer/cardiac
  • Trigger must be clear: inability to study/work due to qualifying illness
  • Less serious conditions (e.g. glandular fever) may need different trigger threshold than serious CI
  • Cash plan type: trigger on specific events — simpler for unsophisticated buyers
  • IP vs OP vs day care distinction explicitly defined
  • Pre-authorisation as a claim trigger control
  • Hospital cash: trigger = overnight hospital stay; excluded if day patient
  • Exclusions (e.g. cosmetic treatment) explicitly listed
  • Pre-existing condition definition and exclusion period stated at outset
3. Target market & customer need
  • Ageing population planning for future care needs
  • Those without family support structures
  • Seeking to avoid being a financial burden on family
  • Pre-planners (pre-funded) vs immediate needs (already in or about to enter care)
  • Broader financial planning need: removing uncertainty of care costs
  • Individuals with financial dependants
  • Cancer/cardiac fear — desire for income replacement certainty on diagnosis
  • Those whose existing PMI does not cover income loss
  • Need to be sold to the correct target market for Consumer Responsibility compliance
  • Children's cancer: parents buying to cover children — anti-selection risk prominent
  • RoP: those wanting premium certainty; wealth management and tax planning clients
  • Mental health: individuals who fear debilitating mental conditions; workplaces with high stress
  • Overseas treatment: those needing high-cost treatment abroad and certainty of access
  • Employers with salaried workforce requiring income protection
  • Workers segment (lower income) vs managers (higher income) — different products
  • Self-employed or those without employer sick pay (individual IP)
  • First-time individual IP buyers in developing markets
  • Individuals with high fixed financial commitments (mortgages, loans)
  • Low-income population (circa $2/day for microinsurance)
  • Uninsured patients seeking some financial protection from hospitalisation costs
  • Price-sensitive segment fearing billing uncertainty
  • Grocery chain's existing customer base for free hospital cash
  • Social benefit objective: reaching those excluded from conventional insurance
  • Undergraduate university students with low financial sophistication
  • Need to cover living costs (rent, tuition fees, loan repayments) during illness
  • Students may also have dependants; peace of mind for the main provider
  • Low brand awareness of insurance companies among target group
  • Concentrated market: all students pass through enrolment events
  • Middle-income households: gap between state cover and actual need
  • Large employer groups seeking employee benefit packages
  • Option 3 (government not-for-profit): lower-income individuals priced out of private PMI
  • Grocery chain distribution: customers of major retail chain
  • Community-rated PMI targets wider population including poorer health lives
4. Affordability & premium structure
  • Pre-funded: long-term level premium over working life
  • Immediate needs: higher single or short-term premium
  • Regular premium makes large eventual benefit affordable
  • Surrender value option can improve marketability but raises pricing complexity
  • Limitations (caps/excesses/limits) help control cost to remain affordable and profitable
  • Guaranteed vs reviewable premiums — key design choice with risk/cost trade-offs
  • Premiums have increased broadly in line with price inflation historically
  • Severity-based design allows lower entry-level premium
  • Premium must reflect true risk — not inflated by irrelevant CI condition additions
  • Reviewable premiums for new business give flexibility in high-inflation environments
  • Each RoP option adds significantly to premium — risk of making product unaffordable
  • 70% of SACI sales in one market included RoP — regulator concern about affordability
  • Children's cancer option priced separately as add-on — keeps base premium intact
  • Mental health conditions addition increases premium; impact on competitive position must be assessed
  • Phased introduction — RoP not offered on lower quartile sum insured policies — mitigates affordability risk
  • Payroll-aligned premium collection for group schemes
  • Tiered benefit levels to suit different income bands
  • Employee co-contribution as an option
  • Co-insurance with employer reduces employer cost
  • Reviewable premiums for new IP business reduce exposure to inflation risk
  • Micro: premium must be in range of $2–$3 per annum for family cover — extremely low margin
  • Group structure reduces collection costs and distribution expense loading
  • Free hospital cash: no premium — costs funded through marketing/distribution budget
  • System development costs for free product are significant — require business case
  • Loyalty features (cashback) encourage consistent premium payment and reduce lapse
  • Premium must be affordable for student income levels
  • Student chooses benefit amount balanced with what is affordable
  • Badging with specialist insurer may enable lower expense loading — passing on savings
  • Expense sharing via specialist systems reduces per-policy cost
  • Community rating restricts premium variation — affordability for higher-risk lives
  • Government insurer: low-cost product; minimal underwriting requirements
  • Free hospital cash: no premium at all — designed to drive distribution partnership awareness
  • Paid-for product variant may be offered alongside free product as upsell
  • Co-pays and deductibles used to manage cost without changing premium
5. Anti-selection & underwriting design
  • Long waiting periods given the long-tail nature of LTC claims
  • Full medical underwriting; pre-existing cognitive impairment excluded
  • Early entry incentivised (lower premium, no underwriting issues)
  • Surrender value must allow for anti-selection: surrenders likely from less ill policyholders pre-claim; from shorter life expectancy post-claim
  • Survival period is the primary anti-selection mechanism
  • Full medical underwriting for individual policies
  • Compulsory group participation removes adverse selection
  • Waiting period before CI benefit payable
  • Selective lapsing: healthier policyholders lapse leaving worse risk pool — must be monitored
  • Children's cancer: parents with sick children will actively seek cover — high anti-selection risk
  • Mental health: pre-existing conditions must be excluded; difficult to underwrite compared to physical conditions
  • RoP features change lapse behaviour — could alter adverse selection profile of in-force book
  • Compulsory group removes adverse selection; individual CI requires robust medical UW
  • At-work clause for new group joiners
  • Free cover limit: no underwriting required below threshold
  • Linked claims period prevents claim manipulation (fragmented short claims)
  • Medical evidence and periodic review required
  • Reducing free cover limits on group schemes tightens anti-selection control
  • Waiting period if gap in cover exceeds 90–180 days
  • Good health statement required on policy restart
  • Group structure reduces individual adverse selection
  • Minimum/maximum ages for enrolment
  • Limiting claims per month reduces moral hazard
  • Target population (students) generally healthy — lower anti-selection risk at outset
  • Low financial sophistication may mean students do not actively select against insurer
  • However, sick students may seek cover disproportionately — waiting periods needed
  • Group sale at enrolment (not consumer-initiated) reduces self-selection bias
  • Community rating removes risk-based pricing — creates cross-subsidy between healthy and ill
  • Risk equalisation mechanism between insurers to address inter-company adverse selection
  • Government insurer: minimal underwriting — higher anti-selection risk initially
  • Free hospital cash: open access design; moral hazard present without per-visit limits
  • Initial experience worse due to early takers more likely to perceive benefit (and claim)
6. Data availability for pricing
  • Challenging to set assumptions at outset — no previous experience data
  • May borrow from external sources: other insurers' statutory returns, actuarial consultants
  • Relevance of external data uncertain: different products, target markets, underwriting approaches
  • Actuarial control cycle used to review and update assumptions regularly as experience builds
  • Large volumes at outset can build credible data faster
  • Morbidity data needed: incidence, severity, claim duration for each condition
  • Disease surveillance data and population statistics used where insurer data thin
  • Historic claims data may not be representative of future experience after redesign
  • Need data to assess appropriateness of new conditions to be added to CI list
  • Children's cancer: lack of credible historical data is key challenge
  • Mental health: XYZ Health will initially have no data to price mental health conditions — external sources needed
  • Frequency of mental health claims may allow data to build quickly if coverage significant
  • RoP: selective lapse behaviour data needed; must monitor lapse profile of policies with RoP vs without
  • Employment and occupation data; industry-level morbidity
  • Return-to-work rates; incidence and termination rates by age/gender/occupation
  • No local market data for individual IP in developing markets — first-mover limitation
  • Actuaries may need local expert input on rating factors and cultural effects on claims
  • Microinsurance: surveillance data on disease; population data; data from analogous countries
  • Information on expected claim frequency and duration given at outset — kept under regular review
  • No local experience at outset — significant uncertainty in assumptions
  • Hospital cash: customer service query data and lapse data used post-launch to assess product fitness
  • Company X does not have sufficient in-house expertise to price the product
  • Badging arrangement with specialist insurer gives access to their experience data
  • Local student population health data needed — likely not available from insurer's own book
  • Specialist insurer's data reduces pricing margins — improving affordability
  • Government insurer: no previous experience — must borrow from other PMI insurers' statutory returns
  • Relevance of comparator data limited: different products, markets, underwriting approaches
  • External actuarial consultants consulted for assumption setting
  • Reinsurers can provide data support for novel product types
7. Reinsurance & risk sharing
  • Very long-tail liability: significant reinsurance challenge
  • Limited reinsurance availability for LTC in many markets
  • ALM challenge: long liabilities require carefully matched assets
  • Lower investment returns increase cost of pre-funded benefit — reinsurance cannot fully substitute for this
  • Reinsurance essential for large sum insured per policy
  • Multi-pay product requires reinsurance for multiple future payment obligations
  • Selective lapsing impacts reinsurance premium per policy — reinsurer considers this
  • Reinsurers used for pricing advice where local data thin
  • Children's cancer: reinsurance needed for novel and potentially high-severity risk
  • Reinsurance cost and availability must be confirmed before launching children's cancer option
  • RoP: surrender value basis and reserves affect reinsurance terms
  • Mental health: reinsurers may apply significant margins or decline given data scarcity
  • Reinsurance essential (XL or quota share) for long-tail IP disability
  • Capital strain from new IP business — reinsurance helps manage capital requirement
  • Reinsurers provide expertise for markets with no local IP experience
  • Badging arrangement (for specialist product) is a risk-sharing alternative to traditional reinsurance
  • Microinsurance: badging arrangement with external specialist insurer — an alternative to reinsurance
  • Reinsurance less critical for simple fixed-benefit low-sum-assured products
  • Stop-loss reinsurance may be considered if disease incidence spikes
  • Badging arrangement: Company X fronts; specialist insurer holds risk and prices the product
  • Profit-sharing arrangement between Company X and specialist insurer is possible
  • Risk capital held by specialist insurer — Company X focuses on sales and distribution
  • Government pool or catastrophe reinsurance required for mandatory schemes
  • Community-rated insurer: reinsurance needed to manage concentration risk across similar risk profiles
  • Risk equalisation between insurers acts as a form of intra-market risk sharing
8. Distribution & sales channel
  • IFA / independent financial adviser — complex long-term product requires face-to-face advice
  • Face-to-face channel appropriate given complexity and older target market
  • Bancassurance possible for pre-funded variant
  • IFA channel exposes insurer to churning risk — commission clawback needed
  • Online for simple, lower sum insured CI products
  • IFA / tied agent for complex multi-pay or higher sum insured CI
  • Bancassurance for simple lump-sum products
  • Distribution channel affects business mix — online skews to younger, simpler cover
  • IFA for RoP CI — product complexity requires advice
  • 70% of sales including RoP in one market suggests IFA channel drives RoP take-up
  • Children's cancer: agent with medical knowledge supports explanation at point of sale
  • Mental health CI: IFA or tied agent to explain conditions covered and when claims pay out
  • Corporate HR and employee benefits consultant for group IP
  • Broker for individual IP
  • State may be a large employer — political dynamic in some markets
  • Not typically a retail/online product due to complexity
  • Microinsurance: community-based distribution; group employer collection
  • Hospital cash: grocery chain as primary distributor — cashier/checkout upsell
  • Digital booking for hospital cash claims and confirmation
  • Hospital partnership aids fraud control and claim process simplification
  • National body of student representatives as distributor — face-to-face at enrolment week
  • Sale made during first week of university with parents present — improves lapse experience
  • Representatives must be trained to avoid mis-selling risk
  • Knowledgeable peer distributors overcome low brand awareness in target market
  • Low remuneration level needed to keep expense ratio manageable
  • Corporate HR for group PMI; employer is the scheme sponsor
  • Grocery chain: direct-to-consumer through retail footprint for hospital cash
  • Government insurer: auto-enrolment or payroll deduction
  • Community rating: no selective underwriting so broader channel possible
9. Capital, expenses & financial viability
  • Very long-tail liability — significant capital strain
  • ALM: long liabilities require long-duration matching assets
  • Pre-funded: new business strain from initial expenses; long period to recoup
  • Surrender value basis must not systematically give policyholders more than asset share
  • Expense loading adjusted for anticipated lapse experience
  • Lump sum reserve — longer-tail than PMI but simpler than IP
  • Selective lapsing causes adverse anti-selection increasing per-policy capital requirement
  • Multi-pay CI: need to reserve for multiple future payment obligations
  • New business volumes affect ability to cover fixed costs — economies of scale matter
  • Children's cancer option: specific capital impact must be quantified
  • RoP: surrender value basis adaptation increases reserve; adjust for costs of medical assessment
  • Mental health addition: initial capital loading until data develops
  • RoP on declined claims: reserve must anticipate event frequency and policyholder behaviour
  • Long-tail disability claims — significant long-term reserves required
  • Capital strain from new IP business; ROC assessment required before launch
  • Setting up a dedicated claims management team: cost vs benefit must be assessed
  • Expense of rehabilitation programmes — offset by improved loss ratio over time
  • Free hospital cash: expenses (launch, claims) funded from marketing budget — no premium income
  • Large system development costs to launch digital product
  • Insurer may need to set volume limits to avoid solvency strain from free product
  • Microinsurance: very low margin — requires very large volumes to be financially viable
  • Badging arrangement: Company X incurs no underwriting capital — specialist holds risk
  • Company X's per-policy expense loading reduced by specialist systems and processes
  • Legal and admin costs of badging arrangement must be factored into viability
  • Long-term ROC may not meet shareholder requirements if pricing is in specialist's hands
  • Government insurer: no previous experience for capital setting — challenging from outset
  • Community rating: high-risk lives admitted without loading — capital buffer needed for adverse mix
  • New business strain magnified by potential large scale volumes for government insurer
  • Free hospital cash: capital consideration — limits on volume written to manage solvency
10. Regulatory & TCF compliance
  • Limited regulatory framework for LTC in many markets — product filing uncertain
  • Long-term liability must be recognised in statutory reserves
  • TCF risk if surrender value appears poor value to policyholder
  • Benefit not sufficient to cover actual care costs — mis-selling risk
  • 50–60% claims payout rate is a TCF failure — Consumer Responsibility regulations triggered
  • Product must provide "good consumer outcomes" and "fair value" under new regulations
  • CI definitions must be as clear and fair as possible in product literature
  • Monitoring: claims payout %, margin levels, complaints, service levels — all required
  • Regulator investigated CI market after low payout ratio became systemic
  • Regulator concerned RoP makes SACI unaffordable — phased withdrawal proposed as remedy
  • Adding RoP must be in policyholders' best interest — IFA responsible for assessing suitability
  • Children's cancer: TCF concern if anti-selection causes product to become unviable
  • TCF issue when changing product terms on existing policies (e.g. deductibles for LTC) — may need to apply to new business only
  • Benefit not to exceed salary — over-insurance regulatory restriction
  • Regular monitoring of underwriting policy compliance required
  • Regulator may require justification for any tightening of claim definitions or underwriting
  • Consumer Responsibility principles apply: product must be sold to correct target market
  • Social benefit aim must be balanced with commercial viability
  • Free hospital cash: regulatory concern about financial soundness if claims exceed marketing budget
  • Regulator may question whether free product distorts market or misleads customers
  • Campaign design features needed to reduce risk of mis-selling by untrained retail staff
  • Mis-selling risk: student representatives may not be sufficiently knowledgeable to explain product
  • Insurer must ensure reps are trained and sales processes policed
  • TCF: students (financially unsophisticated) are potentially vulnerable customers
  • Consumer Responsibility: product must meet students' genuine financial needs
  • Community rating mandated by regulator: premiums may only vary by age, location, marital/smoker status
  • Risk equalisation required between insurers under community rating regime
  • Government not-for-profit insurer must comply with solvency framework despite not-for-profit status
  • Portability and guaranteed renewability rules may apply to all PMI products
11. Simplicity & transparency
  • ADL trigger definitions must be unambiguous — no room for subjective interpretation at claim
  • Clear escalation of benefit as care needs increase
  • Transparent care setting definitions (home vs residential vs nursing)
  • Mis-selling risk if benefits not sufficient to cover actual cost of care at time of claim
  • CI definitions must be precise — ambiguity at claim stage is a regulatory and reputational risk
  • Clear product literature: customers must understand when cover pays out — and when it does not
  • Adding many conditions (up to 100) can obscure what is genuinely covered
  • Simple trigger (diagnosis confirmed = payment) is the strongest consumer proposition
  • RoP trigger events must be clearly described — customers must understand each option
  • Mental health conditions: criteria for a qualifying claim must be set out in plain language
  • Children's cancer: age limits and qualifying cancer types must be explicit
  • Complex products (multi-tier, multi-benefit) increase mis-selling and mis-understanding risk
  • Clear incapacity definition eliminates disputes at claim stage
  • Salary-linked benefit formula must be transparent and verifiable
  • Policy wording sufficiently tight to reduce unexpected claims and anti-selection
  • Rehabilitation terms and return-to-work process clearly communicated at outset
  • Core requirement for microinsurance: simple and straightforward for unsophisticated market
  • Terms and conditions set out clearly and in accessible language
  • Fixed cash benefit — no indemnity complexity — clear to policyholder
  • Hospital definition and hospital stay minimum communicated unambiguously
  • Low financial sophistication: product must be among simplest available in market
  • Complexity deters sales and increases mis-selling risk for this target market
  • Student representatives must be able to explain the product themselves — complexity limits this
  • Cash plan type product (fixed trigger, fixed cash) is the simplest design suitable
  • IP vs OP vs day care definitions communicated clearly — reduces disputes
  • Free product scope: what is and is not covered under the free hospital cash must be explicit
  • Community rating: rationale for premium variation must be transparent to consumers
  • Grocery chain staff need simple product narrative to explain at point of sale
12. Flexibility, options & guarantees
  • Pre-funded vs immediate needs: separate product designs for different buyer stages
  • Home care vs facility care benefit options
  • Inflation-linked benefit escalation option
  • Surrender value option (not guaranteed): improves marketability; significant actuarial and TCF complexity
  • Option to receive benefit as lump sum or regular income
  • Severity-based tiers — partial payment for less severe events
  • Guaranteed vs reviewable premiums — trade-off between certainty for policyholders and risk for insurer
  • Standalone or accelerated (linked to life policy)
  • Options and guarantees may not be offered in microinsurance context
  • RoP event types: multiple options — surrender, lapse, end of term, death, partial claim — each priced separately
  • Children's cancer: standalone option or add-on to adult policy
  • Mental health conditions: consider severity tiers within mental health (as per physical CI)
  • Multi-pay option: benefit reinstated after first claim — niche but differentiating design feature
  • Deferred period choice (1, 3 or 6 months)
  • Own occupation vs any occupation definition choice
  • Optional partial disability benefit
  • Tiered benefit levels across salary bands (group)
  • Reducing replacement ratio at different salary levels to control moral hazard
  • Reviewable premium rates and reviewable benefit terms as built-in flexibility
  • Annual product renewal — removes long-term guarantee risk
  • Outpatient benefit as optional add-on to hospital cash base product
  • Minimum hospital stay threshold adjustable based on emerging claims experience
  • Choice of lump sum or income stream — student selects based on personal need
  • Amount of benefit chosen by policyholder — modular design
  • Optional add-on to cover other insurance costs (general insurance, dental)
  • Modular group PMI: add-ons for maternity, dental, wellness
  • Tiered benefit options (basic to comprehensive) for employer group
  • Community rating: reduced flexibility in risk-based pricing is the trade-off for access
  • Free hospital cash leads into paid-for product — flexible upsell pathway
13. Claims management built into design
  • Independent ADL assessment by qualified assessor at claim trigger
  • Regular review of care needs — benefit may change over time
  • Care manager / case manager to control ongoing care costs
  • Surrender value basis in claim phase requires individual medical assessment
  • Single lump sum on event — simplest claims management of all product types
  • Survival period (30 days) is the primary claims control
  • Independent medical verification of diagnosis required
  • Multi-pay: second claim requires new event and defined linked claims period between events
  • RoP on declined claims: process for returning premiums after declined claim carefully specified
  • Children's cancer: high emotional and financial severity — claims process must be sensitive and swift
  • Mental health: independent psychiatric assessment at claim — analogous to ADL assessment for LTC
  • Partial payment tiers reduce incentive to exaggerate severity at claim stage
  • Return-to-work incentive: benefit reduces on partial return — designed in from outset
  • Rehabilitation programme integral to product proposition — early intervention reduces long claims
  • Deferred period absorbs short-term sickness and reduces claim frequency
  • Dedicated claim management team can significantly improve loss ratio (cost-benefit must be assessed)
  • Minimum hospital stay nights — primary claims control mechanism
  • Partnership with hospitals to identify fraudulent claims
  • Limit on number of claims per month — reduces moral hazard
  • Declined claims tracked and reported to management as product fitness metric
  • Rehabilitation services offered to encourage students back from illness
  • Claims underwriting at point of trigger (medical certificate of incapacity)
  • Simple claim trigger minimises disputes for financially unsophisticated policyholders
  • Pre-authorisation before hospital admission for PMI
  • GP referral required before specialist access in managed care model
  • Network vs non-network co-pay differential as claims control
  • TPA oversight and bill audits for group PMI
14. Competitive differentiation
  • Underdeveloped LTC market in many countries — first-mover advantage
  • Monopoly risk: lack of competition reduces innovation and may disadvantage consumers
  • Economies of scale for market leader may enable better pricing and product quality
  • Differentiation through surrender value option — not universally offered
  • Covering 100 conditions is not a genuine differentiator if payout rate remains low
  • Severity-based / multi-pay design is a genuine differentiator vs standard CI
  • Consumer Responsibility regulations force market-wide redesign — early movers gain advantage
  • Online vs face-to-face channel affects competitive positioning
  • Mental health CI coverage: innovation differentiator; makes insurer look forward-thinking
  • Children's cancer: first-mover advantage — but also first-mover risk with no benchmark data
  • RoP design: differentiator in markets where not yet standard; risk of making product expensive
  • Overseas treatment top-up: no direct competitor — USP if properly priced
  • First-mover advantage for individual IP in markets with no existing provision
  • Risk of uncertainty on experience: no market data makes pricing margins critical
  • Dedicated claims management team is a differentiator — better policyholder outcomes
  • Removing initial UW (as a competitor did) can drive market share but increases morbidity risk
  • Transparency and certainty of fixed cash benefit is USP vs uninsured status quo
  • Free hospital cash: novelty and low barrier to entry drives customer acquisition
  • Advertising campaign alternative — reach beyond existing customer base but harder to track conversion
  • Free product creates customer testimonials and trust for paid product upsell
  • Badging with specialist insurer gives credibility despite no internal expertise
  • Student representative channel is novel — not used by mainstream insurers
  • Product uniquely tailored to student conditions — not repurposed from adult CI
  • Cross-sell opportunity: specialist insurer's customer base may be accessible to Company X
  • Community rating widens access — competitive positioning against private risk-rated PMI
  • Product innovation encouraged by permitting overseas insurers into market
  • Government insurer: lowest-cost product in market — competitive by design not by profit
  • Wellness features (wearables, health checks) as differentiator for commercial PMI
15. Monitoring & post-launch review
  • Actuarial control cycle: regularly reassess assumptions; update as experience builds
  • Premium review process triggered by materially adverse experience
  • Monitor lapse rates — selective surrenders distort remaining risk pool
  • Review of ADL assessment process and care setting trends over time
  • Consumer Responsibility: monitor claims payout %, margin levels, complaints, service levels
  • Management information produced frequently (monthly/quarterly)
  • Action log maintained to track and resolve identified issues
  • Annual premium review for reviewable CI business based on experience analysis
  • Board-level review of key statistics — limits set for each metric
  • Monitor RoP take-up rate and profitability — phased withdrawal if unaffordable
  • Track selective lapse behaviour: does RoP change lapse profile adversely?
  • Mental health: monitor claim frequency and build data for future pricing review
  • Children's cancer: close monitoring of claim incidence and severity for pricing update
  • Claims ratio tracked regularly since launch
  • Loss ratio analysis: frequency vs severity vs mix vs underwriting change
  • Regular underwriting policy review — risk of becoming uncompetitive if not updated
  • Return-to-work rates monitored as a KPI of rehabilitation programme effectiveness
  • Claims ratio tracked against expectation since launch
