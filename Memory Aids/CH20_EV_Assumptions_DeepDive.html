How Assumptions
Drive the EV Calculation

A complete walkthrough of every assumption that enters the EV calculation, why each one is set differently from pricing and reserving, how the Risk Discount Rate is built up, and how assumption changes feed into the Analysis of EV Movement — using the SA1ity Check source material throughout.

00

The Big Picture First

Before diving into individual assumptions, you need to fix one idea firmly in your mind. In an EV calculation, assumptions play a fundamentally different role than in pricing or reserving.

Core Principle — from your SA1 material (Ch.12, Section 4.3)

In pricing and reserving, risk is captured by loading the assumptions themselves (e.g. using a higher morbidity rate than best estimate). In an EV calculation, assumptions are set at best estimate, and all the risk is instead captured in a single number: the Risk Discount Rate (RDR). This is the master key to understanding why EV assumptions look the way they do.

The EV Formula — and where assumptions live
EV= ANW+ VIF
ANW — uses the supervisory reserving basis to value current reserves. No projection assumptions needed here.
VIF — uses the projection basis (best estimate assumptions + RDR) to project future shareholder profits. THIS is where all the assumption action is.
From your material — Ch.12 Section 4

"The calculation of EV will actually require two different bases: (1) a reserving basis — the supervisory reserving basis, always used regardless of the purpose of the EV; and (2) a projection basis — the insurer may use a different projection basis depending on the purpose for which the EV is being calculated."

01

The Two Bases Inside Every EV

This is the single most confusing thing about EV assumptions, and once you crack it, everything else falls into place. Every EV calculation uses two completely different sets of assumptions simultaneously.

Basis 1

Supervisory Reserving Basis

Used to calculate the current reserves and the future change in reserves year by year.

  • Always the official IRDAI/regulatory basis
  • Includes prudence margins (MAD)
  • Sets the ANW (assets − reserves − required capital)
  • Also drives how reserves release into profit each future year
  • For Indian insurers: includes ASM ≥ 150% of RSM buffer
⟷
Basis 2

Projection (EV) Basis

Used to project future experience — premiums, claims, expenses, lapses, investment income.

  • Best estimate assumptions — no prudence margins
  • Risk captured via the Risk Discount Rate
  • Used to project cashflows over remaining policy terms
  • May differ between internal management EV and published EV
  • For M&A: seller uses realistic; buyer uses cautious basis
Why does this matter?

The shareholder profit in each year = actual cashflow − increase in supervisory reserves. So you're taking future cashflows projected on best estimate assumptions, and deducting reserve movements calculated on a prudent supervisory basis. When supervisory reserves are more prudent than best estimate, they build up excess margin — and as those margins are released over time, hidden profit emerges. The VIF captures all of this.

Indian context — IRDAI regime

Under the current Indian supervisory regime, reserves include explicit Margin for Adverse Deviation (MAD). As IRDAI transitions towards Risk-Based Capital (RBC), the reserving basis will move closer to best estimate + explicit risk margin. This will change the split between ANW and VIF — the VIF will capture less hidden prudence margin, but the model will become more transparent. The EV concept becomes even more important in an RBC world.

02

Every Assumption — Compared Across Three Bases

For each assumption, the EV projection basis will differ from pricing and reserving. Click each assumption to see exactly how and why — with Indian health insurer context throughout.

M
Morbidity / Claim Incidence Rates
IP incidence · CI rates · LTCI ADL triggers · PMI frequency — the biggest driver of VIF
▶

Pricing Basis

Best estimate + explicit margin. For Indian market where own data is sparse, margin can be substantial. CI: each illness valued separately. IP: incidence by occupation, age, deferred period. Data from reinsurers heavily used.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Prudent / MAD added. For IP: higher inception rates assumed; lower recovery rates. For CI: no reduction for survival probability (conservative). For LTCI: higher ADL deterioration rates. Indian regime: explicit margin specified or actuarial judgment.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate only — no margin. The actuary's single best view of future claim experience. Any risk from uncertainty is captured in the RDR instead. Must be reviewed regularly as experience emerges.

Why it matters for VIF: Morbidity is the single largest driver of claim costs for health products. A 5% higher morbidity rate reduces annual profit significantly, compounded over the remaining term. For a 15-year IP policy, a 10% morbidity worsening can reduce VIF by 15–25% depending on the RDR. In India, own-data scarcity means the gap between best estimate and prudent morbidity can be large — so the VIF contains substantial "hidden" margin from the reserve basis that emerges as profit.
L
Lapse / Discontinuance Rates
Selective withdrawal · early termination · impact on future premium income
▶

Pricing Basis

Best estimate + margin. Direction of margin depends on product: for IP, higher lapse means fewer future premiums AND selective withdrawal of healthy lives, leaving sicker pool → prudent to assume lower lapse.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

For IP: typically lower lapse rates than best estimate (prudent = unhealthy lives stay). For CI: lower lapse = more claims exposure = conservative. Adverse direction varies by product.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate lapse rates from own experience. High lapses reduce VIF by cutting off future premium income. Selective withdrawal risk (healthy lives lapse) is a secondary effect captured in morbidity drift — the RDR should account for this uncertainty.

Selective withdrawal problem: Your material flags: "morbidity & lapse rates — The higher the level of lapses, the greater the opportunity for selective lapsing, where better risks leave, causing the morbidity experience for the business as a whole to deteriorate." In EV, this dynamic interaction is handled through the consistency principle — lapse and morbidity assumptions must be set together, not independently.
E
Expenses & Expense Inflation
Per-policy costs · claims handling · overhead allocation · commission structure
▶

Pricing Basis

Best estimate per-policy expenses amortised over expected policy lifetime. Initial expenses spread over renewal years. Commission — higher initial, lower renewal. Volume assumptions affect fixed-cost allocation.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Net premium valuation: expenses may be excluded entirely (only benefits valued). Gross premium valuation: best estimate expenses with margin. Future expense inflation added for long-term contracts.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate per-policy costs from expense investigation. No margin. Expense inflation must be consistent with the investment return assumption. Volume assumptions affect fixed-overhead per-policy costs — falling volumes hurt VIF.

Key for VIF: Because EV uses best estimate expenses without margins, the VIF is higher than you might expect from the pricing profit test (which includes expense margins). The difference is one source of "embedded" profit. Expense inflation in India (~6–8% p.a.) must be consistent with the investment return assumption — your material states: "Investment return and inflation — the long-term relationship between expense inflation and rates of return on different types of assets must be consistent."
I
Investment Return & Risk Discount Rate
The most technically complex assumption — see dedicated section below
▶

Pricing Basis

Expected return on assets backing the contract. For IP/CI: mostly fixed interest bonds. Investment return is a minor driver of pricing (premium paid and claims are the main cashflows). Must be consistent with inflation assumptions.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Often a locked-in or prescribed rate (passive basis). Under market-consistent approach: risk-free discount rate regardless of actual assets held. For LTCI: very sensitive as cashflows run for decades.

EV Projection Basis

Two distinct uses: (1) Asset projection: expected return on assets backing reserves (used in cashflow projection); (2) RDR: risk-free rate + risk premium (used to discount the profit stream). These are different rates — confusing them is a common error.

The critical distinction: Investment income (earned on assets) flows into the numerator as part of the annual profit calculation. The RDR is used in the denominator to discount that profit stream back to today. For PMI, investment income is minimal (short-tail business, not much float). For LTCI, investment income can be the second largest income item after premiums, and it must be consistent with the inflation assumption on care costs.
Mo
Mortality & Survival Rates
Direction of prudence varies by product — this trips candidates in exams
▶

Pricing Basis

Separate rates for healthy lives and lives in claim. For CI: survival period assumption affects when (and whether) the lump sum is paid. For LTCI: lower mortality = policyholder in care longer = higher total claim cost.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Prudent direction depends entirely on the product: LTCI prudent = lower mortality (claimant lives longer, more care). IP prudent = lower mortality (more recovery possible but also longer claim). CI prudent = higher incidence more relevant than mortality.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate mortality for the specific product mix. Indian population data from IALM (Indian Assured Lives Mortality) tables adjusted for own experience. Particularly important for LTCI where mortality drives the run-off.

Exam trap: Lower mortality is not always prudent for health insurers. It depends on the product: for LTCI (you're paying benefit while the person is alive and in need of care), lower mortality increases liability. For term assurance or CI, lower mortality means fewer deaths, but CI exposure remains. Always think about which direction of experience change increases the insurer's liability.
MI
Medical / Benefit Inflation
India-specific risk: 12–15% p.a. — critical for IP claim amounts and PMI costs
▶

Pricing Basis

Explicit assumption, especially for reviewable PMI. Medical inflation in India historically 12–15% p.a. For guaranteed IP benefits (indexed to earnings): must use earnings inflation consistently. Failure to allow for medical inflation is the most common cause of PMI portfolio deterioration in India.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

For PMI: shorter tail so less sensitive. For long-term IP benefits that are inflation-linked: prudent upward revision to medical inflation assumption. IRDAI requires explicit actuarial certification of reserve adequacy.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate medical inflation. Must be consistent with the investment return assumption (key consistency principle). For IP: affects average claim amount each year. For PMI (not in VIF): affects short-term UPR adequacy only.

Indian context: Medical inflation is the biggest assumption risk for Indian health insurers. If your EV projection basis uses 8% medical inflation but actual experience runs at 14%, the actual profits will be far below VIF projections. This creates large negative "experience variances" in the Analysis of EV Movement. Your material (Pillars & Hooks, P2) flags: "Medical inflation ~12–15% p.a. in India; applying historical trend forward is essential — failure leads to rapid deterioration."
T
Taxation
Three types — often overlooked, always in the examiner's solution
▶

Pricing Basis

Taxes on premiums (GST in India, 18% on health insurance premiums). Taxes on profits (corporate tax). I–E basis (investment income less expenses). Tax timing differences affect profitability.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Tax position affects regulatory surplus. IRDAI may specify how deferred tax assets/liabilities are treated. Premium tax (GST) flows through as part of premium income — must be modelled consistently.

EV Projection Basis

Best estimate current and future tax position. VIF is always expressed net of tax. The IAI 1222 solution explicitly states: "The current and likely future tax position of the company must also be allowed for." Tax changes (e.g. GST rate change) would trigger an assumption change in the EV analysis.

For exams: When asked to describe the EV projection assumptions, always include taxation. Candidates frequently omit it. The after-tax shareholder profit must be projected — pre-tax EV would overstate shareholder value. In India, GST at 18% on health premiums is a material item.
NB
New Business — Not in the VIF
Crucial distinction: EV is in-force only; new business adds to Appraisal Value
▶

Pricing Basis

Critical — new business volume and mix assumptions affect fixed-cost allocation per policy. If fewer policies are sold, fixed costs spread over fewer units = higher unit cost = lower profitability.

Supervisory (IRDAI)

Generally assumes run-off (no new business). Reserve calculations only cover existing policies. New business strain (extra capital needed for new policies) is separate from reserve adequacy.

EV Projection Basis

VIF explicitly ignores new business. Only in-force policies are projected. Volume/mix uncertainty that affected pricing is no longer relevant — actual in-force book is known. This is why your material says: "Part of the uncertainty in pricing (volume/mix) does not exist for EV — actual volumes are known."

Appraisal Value: To include the value of future new business, you move from EV to Appraisal Value = EV + Value of New Business (VNB). VNB is the present value of future new business contributions. This is used in M&A. Your Pillars & Hooks material states: "Appraisal value = EV + multiplier × VNB; used in M&A; multiplier reflects franchise value, growth prospects, and execution risk."

Building the Risk Discount Rate

The RDR is the most technically subtle assumption in the EV calculation. It is not a market-observable rate — it is constructed from first principles using CAPM theory plus product-specific adjustments. From your material (Ch.15, Section 3): "Investors will demand a return equal to the risk-free rate plus a risk premium. Such a rate of return is called the risk discount rate."

Features that increase the product-specific RDR premium (from your SP1 study material):
— Lack of historical data on the insured risk
— High benefit guarantees or policyholder options (GIOs, reviewability)
— Significant overhead cost allocation
— Complex benefit design or definitions (e.g. CI illness definitions)
— New or untested market (new Indian LTCI market)
Features that reduce the product-specific RDR premium:
— Simple benefit/definitions; well-established market; product is short-term (less long-run uncertainty)

The key rule — never forget this

From your material: "The risk discount rate must be higher than the risk-free rate — hence changes in market rates of interest should cause risk discount rates to change." And: "The overall return earned on the whole capital of the company, generated from its entire business activities, needs to meet the required rate of return (cost of capital)." The RDR is not arbitrary — it is derived from the shareholders' opportunity cost of capital.

The reserve interaction — the most confusing part

Notice that the "Increase in reserves" row uses the supervisory reserving basis (prudent assumptions), while all the cashflows above it use the projection basis (best estimate). In Year 1, reserves build rapidly (high initial reserve strain), reducing profit. In later years, as the policy matures, reserves release back — increasing profit. The VIF captures the sum of all these future profits discounted at the RDR. The excess of the supervisory reserve over the best estimate reserve creates an implicit margin — which slowly "unlocks" as profit over the policy term.

07

The Consistency Principle

From your material: "Assumptions must be considered in their totality, not in isolation. Key correlated pairs include..." In an EV calculation, inconsistent assumptions can produce a result that is internally contradictory — and an examiner will spot this instantly.

Morbidity ⟷ Lapse Rates

Higher lapses → selective withdrawal of healthy lives → remaining pool is sicker → morbidity rate rises. If you assume high lapses but low morbidity, your VIF is overstated. They must move together in the same direction.

Investment Return ⟷ Inflation

In India, if you assume 8% investment return, you cannot also assume only 4% medical inflation — real economic relationships imply they move together. Fisher equation: nominal return ≈ real return + inflation. Inconsistency overstates net profit.

Morbidity ⟷ Claims Expenses

Higher claim volume → more claims admin expense. You cannot assume claims spike without also assuming claims handling costs rise proportionally. Per-claim expense assumption and incidence rate must be consistent.

Investment Return ⟷ RDR

The RDR must be higher than the investment return assumed on risk assets. If projected investment return > RDR, you are assuming the business earns more than shareholders require — which may be temporarily true but not sustainable as a long-term assumption.

New Business Volume ⟷ Fixed Expenses

Fixed overheads are allocated per-policy based on assumed volume. If volume falls (e.g. from higher lapses), per-policy fixed cost rises, reducing VIF. This is why EV is sensitive to persistency — not just from lost premiums but from expense spiral.

Morbidity ⟷ Investment Return

Economic downturns affect both. In recessions: unemployment rises → IP claims spike AND investment returns fall simultaneously. A stressed EV scenario must shock both together, not in isolation. This is the tail-risk the RDR premium is partly pricing.

Exam technique — the consistency trap

In any question asking you to "comment on the EV basis" or "identify weaknesses in the EV calculation", always check internal consistency of assumptions. Examiners frequently set up a scenario where, say, a high investment return is assumed alongside low inflation, or where morbidity and lapse assumptions are inconsistent. Spotting this and explaining why the inconsistency overstates or understates the VIF is a high-value technique.

Summary — what you now know

1. EV requires two simultaneous bases: supervisory (for reserves) and projection (for experience).
2. The projection basis uses best estimate assumptions — no margins. Risk lives entirely in the RDR.
3. The RDR is built from: risk-free rate + CAPM equity risk premium + product-specific statistical risk premium.
4. Every assumption (morbidity, lapse, expenses, investment, inflation, mortality, tax) feeds into the annual profit calculation that gets discounted to form VIF.
5. When best estimate assumptions are revised, the change in VIF appears as "operating assumption changes" in the Analysis of EV Movement.
6. Consistency between assumptions is non-negotiable — morbidity, lapses, expenses, investment return and inflation all interact.
7. For short-term products (PMI, HCP), there is no VIF — assumptions only affect the ANW through current reserve adequacy.
