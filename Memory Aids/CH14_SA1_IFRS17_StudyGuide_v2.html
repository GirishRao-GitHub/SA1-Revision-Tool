① The "Why" — Context & Criticisms of IFRS 4

Syllabus Anchors
Chapter 14 covers syllabus objectives 2.3 (regulatory frameworks — financial reporting requirements) and 2.5 (comparing environments across jurisdictions and how they affect practice).

What IFRS 17 replaced — and why IFRS 4 was criticised

IFRS 4 was always an interim standard — it essentially allowed insurers to keep using whatever local accounting practices they already used, provided they met some minimum requirements. This created three major problems that IFRS 17 directly addresses:

IFRS 4 CriticismWhy It Was a ProblemHow IFRS 17 Fixes It
Sensitivity of profits to reserving assumptions Different insurers used different assumption sets; small changes in assumptions caused large swings in reported profit, misleading investors Best estimate assumptions are required, with the RA shown explicitly; the CSM buffers the P&L from assumption changes
Lack of comparability Even within a single country, insurers could report using completely different methodologies — results were non-comparable Single principle-based standard applicable to all insurance contracts globally
Early recognition of premiums & profits for long-term contracts Profit was often recognised at inception when a policy was sold, even though the service (providing coverage) would be delivered over 20–30 years The CSM defers profit to be released over the coverage period as insurance services are provided

The unique challenge of insurance contracts

Insurance contracts are unusual because they combine three features that no single existing accounting standard handled well together:

Financial Instrument
  • Collecting premiums and paying claims are cash flows over time
  • Has time value of money implications
Service Contract
  • The insurer provides a service: coverage (peace of mind)
  • Profit should be earned as the service is delivered
Long-Term Uncertainty
  • Claims may occur far in the future
  • Amounts are unknown — mortality, morbidity, etc.
  • Market and non-market risks both present

IFRS principles underlying all reporting

Both IFRS and US GAAP are principles-based (as opposed to rules-based). The main IFRS principles you must know are:

PrincipleWhat it means
Fair representationStatements must present fairly the financial position, performance, and cash flows of the entity
Going concernAssumed the entity will continue in operation for the foreseeable future (not liquidate)
Accruals basedRevenue recorded when earned; expenses when incurred — regardless of when cash is received or paid
ComparabilityPresentation must be consistent from one period to the next, unless a change is justified
ComplianceIf claiming IFRS compliance, must comply with all IFRS requirements
Reporting periodPresumed at least annual; changes in reporting period must be disclosed
UnderstandabilityUnderstandable to users who have knowledge of business, economic activity, and accounting concepts
OffsettingAssets and liabilities, income and expenses, generally may not be offset
⚠ Exam Trap
The opposite of a going-concern basis is the wind-up (or break-up) basis. Asset values on a wind-up basis are typically lower than on a going-concern basis. The examiner has tested this.

② The Architecture — How IFRS 17 is Structured

The three measurement models

IFRS 17 does not apply one single calculation method to all insurance contracts. It describes three measurement models, and which one applies depends on the nature of the contract:

ModelFull NameWhen UsedIn Scope for SA1?
GMM General Measurement Model (also called Building Block Approach / BBA) Default for all insurance contracts. Most appropriate for traditional long-term life and health business (term assurance, without-profit annuities, IP, CI) ✅ Core — covered in depth
VFA Variable Fee Approach Mandatory for contracts with direct participation features — where cashflows vary significantly with a defined pool of assets (unit-linked, with-profits / DPF) ✅ Covered in this subject
PAA Premium Allocation Approach Simplified model (similar to unearned premium reserve). For very short-term health and care business (typically <1 year coverage) ⚠️ Not considered further in the SA1 subject — per the course notes
⚠ Important Note from Course Notes
The course notes explicitly state: "The Premium Allocation Approach is not considered further in this subject." You should know what it is and when it applies, but do not need deep knowledge of its mechanics.

The GMM Building Blocks

The GMM defines the insurance contract liability as the sum of four building blocks. Visually:

Key Insight — Solvency II Parallel
The four building blocks will feel familiar if you know Solvency II. BEL and RA are calculated similarly. The big new concept is the CSM — this has no Solvency II equivalent. It is the key innovation of IFRS 17.

IFRS 9 — Financial Instruments (Asset Side)

Under IFRS 9, the default measurement basis for financial assets is Fair Value through Profit and Loss (FVPL) — changes in fair value flow through directly to P&L. There are two exceptions:

This is important for health and care insurers because investment portfolios (bonds, equities) must be classified under IFRS 9, and the choice affects how investment income and gains/losses appear in financial statements.

③ The GMM in Depth — BEL, RA & CSM

Building Block 1 & 2: Best Estimate Liability (BEL)

The BEL is calculated using the same methodology as under Solvency II. The steps are:

# BEL Calculation Steps
Step 1: Derive best estimate assumptions (mortality, morbidity, lapses, expenses...)
Step 2: Derive a discount rate from observable market data
Step 3: Project probability-weighted future net cashflows
        (premiums in) minus (claims + expenses out)
Step 4: Discount those cashflows to present value
Step 5: Allow for the cost of any financial guarantees

# Result: BEL can be positive OR negative
# For regular premium contracts, BEL is often NEGATIVE
# (future premiums exceed future outflows — the contract is in profit)
Key Point — BEL can be negative
For a profitable regular premium contract (e.g. a 20-year IP policy), the PV of future premiums exceeds the PV of future benefits and expenses. This makes the BEL negative. The CSM then captures this surplus as "future profit."

Building Block 3: Risk Adjustment (RA)

The RA is analogous to the Solvency II risk margin. It represents the value of non-market risks (e.g. morbidity risk, lapse risk) not captured in the BEL. Three permitted methods:

MethodDescription
Value at Risk (VaR)Amount of extra capital needed to be X% confident of covering the non-market risks
Conditional Tail Expectation (CTE)Expected loss in the worst X% of scenarios (more conservative than VaR)
Cost of Capital (CoC)Cost of holding capital to cover non-market risks — most common in practice (same method as Solvency II)
⚠ Key Exam Point — IFRS 17 does NOT specify the confidence level
Unlike Solvency II (which specifies 99.5% VaR for SCR), IFRS 17 does not specify the confidence level for the RA. This is left to the insurer's choice — but must be disclosed.

The RA is attributed to individual contracts (even though it may be calculated at a higher level of aggregation, allowing for diversification across risk factors).

Building Block 4: Contractual Service Margin (CSM) — The Key Innovation

Core Concept
The CSM is the "future profit" or "unearned profit" on a contract. It is set at inception so that zero profit arises on the day the policy is sold. Profit only emerges over time as insurance services are provided.

Setting the CSM at inception

CSM = (Initial premium − Initial expenses) − (BEL + RA)

# Equivalently:
CSM = Initial net cashflow − BEL − RA

# CSM CANNOT be negative.
# If BEL + RA > Initial net cashflow → LOSS recognised immediately
# If BEL + RA < Initial net cashflow → CSM > 0 (profitable contract)

Why CSM is positive for a profitable regular premium contract

This is a classic exam question (it appeared in the SA1 course notes with a worked solution). For a regular premium contract:

  • The BEL will be negative — because PV(future premiums) > PV(future benefits + expenses)
  • So (BEL + RA) is small, or negative
  • Even though initial expenses may exceed the first premium, the CSM = (net cashflow) − (BEL + RA) is positive
  • The CSM captures all those future profit loadings embedded in future premiums

How the CSM is released (profit emergence)

The initial CSM is written down (amortised) over the term of the contract in line with a chosen "coverage unit" measure. For example:

  • Number of policies in-force
  • Expected claim amounts
  • Number of expected coverage units remaining

This causes profit to emerge steadily over the policy term, rather than all at once. It smooths reported profit.

Annual Cohort Rule
Contracts can be grouped together, but they must remain in annual cohorts — policies written more than one year apart cannot be grouped together. This prevents cross-subsidisation across generations of policyholders.

The CSM as an absorber of assumption changes

This is the second key function of the CSM. When non-investment assumptions change (e.g. morbidity rates are revised):

EventEffect on BEL + RAEffect on CSMEffect on P&L
Non-investment assumption changes (e.g. better morbidity) BEL falls CSM is increased by same amount — absorbs the change No immediate P&L impact — deferred to future periods
Non-investment assumption changes (e.g. worse morbidity) BEL rises CSM is reduced to absorb — but only if CSM is large enough If CSM insufficient → shortfall recognised as loss immediately
Actual experience variances (e.g. more claims than expected) No change to BEL No CSM offset Flows directly to P&L in the period
Key Result
Under IFRS 17 GMM, reported profit in any year is driven more by actual experience and less by assumption changes (because assumption changes are buffered by the CSM). This is much more stable and informative for investors.

Grouping of contracts — onerous vs profitable

IFRS 17 requires that profitable and unprofitable contracts are not offset against each other. At inception, each group of contracts must be categorised as one of:

  • Loss-making at inception — loss recognised immediately (CSM = 0)
  • Profitable with no significant risk of making a loss
  • Any other profitable contracts — those that are profitable at inception but may become loss-making
Asymmetric Treatment
This is deliberate and asymmetric: losses are recognised immediately, profits are deferred and released over the coverage period. A contract cannot be grouped with another contract if doing so would mask an onerous contract within a profitable group.

Transitional Requirements

When IFRS 17 came into effect (1 January 2023), insurers had to calculate a transitional CSM for all in-force policies. Three approaches were permitted:

ApproachAbbreviationWhen usedDescription
Full Retrospective ApproachFRA Must be used if practicable Determines position at transition as if IFRS 17 had always applied throughout the full policy lifetime — requires historical data back to policy inception
Modified Retrospective ApproachMRA If FRA is not practicable Simplified version of FRA using reasonably available information — as close as possible to FRA without excessive cost or effort
Fair Value ApproachFVA If MRA also not practicable Transitional CSM = Fair value of policies less fulfilment cashflows at transition date — effectively treats transition date as day one

For India (Ind AS 117 from April 2026), IRDAI has proposed a phased retrospective application: at least 10 years in FY27, 15 years in FY28, 20 years in FY29.

Disclosure requirements

IFRS 17 requires three key income statement disclosures, separating insurance from investment results:

  • Insurance revenue earned — the amount of CSM released plus other insurance service components
  • Insurance service expenses incurred — claims, expenses, and loss components
  • Insurance finance income and expenses — the unwinding of the discount rate
🎯 Exam Point — Separation of Results
IFRS 17 requires the separation of insurance service results from insurance finance income/expenses. This was a major failing of IFRS 4 — where investment returns and underwriting results were often mingled. This separation is a key objective of the standard.

④ Variable Fee Approach (VFA) & Premium Allocation Approach (PAA)

Variable Fee Approach (VFA)

The VFA is used when a significant proportion of the contract's cashflows vary in line with the value of a clearly defined pool of assets. The key examples are:

  • Unit-linked contracts
  • With-profits / Discretionary Participating Feature (DPF) contracts

The "variable fee" is the fee payable to the insurer, which also varies with the value of the underlying asset pool (e.g. fund management charges as a % of the unit fund). Since the variable fee is a transfer of funds within the insurer, it is not part of the fulfilment cashflows and is not included in the BEL.

The critical difference: GMM vs VFA — how the discount rate and CSM interact

⚠ High-Exam-Value Concept
This distinction is the most testable technical difference between GMM and VFA. Study this carefully.
FeatureGMMVFA
Discount rate basis Based on market observable data (e.g. government bond yields) Calculated with reference to the pool of underlying assets
When discount rate changes BEL and RA change → flows directly to P&L (CSM is NOT unlocked for discount rate changes) BEL and RA change → CSM is unlocked to absorb the change → deferred to future profit releases
P&L impact of interest rate movement Higher volatility — rate changes hit P&L immediately Lower P&L volatility — rate changes absorbed into CSM, released over time
CSM locking CSM is "locked" for discount rate changes (but unlocked for non-investment assumption changes) CSM is "unlocked" for both discount rate changes and non-investment assumption changes
🎯 Worked Example — GMM vs VFA on Interest Rate Rise

Scenario: Interest rates rise. BEL and RA fall (because future cashflows are discounted at a higher rate).

  • Under GMM: The fall in BEL+RA creates a gain. This gain flows directly to P&L — shown as a profit in the period. The CSM is unchanged.
  • Under VFA: The fall in BEL+RA creates a gain. The CSM is increased by the same amount (unlocked), absorbing the gain. P&L is unaffected in the current period. The gain will only emerge as the CSM is gradually released over future periods.

This is the economic logic: under VFA, the assets (which also fell in value when rates rose, or rose in value — depending on the asset) and liabilities move together. The CSM absorbs the net effect, preventing artificial P&L volatility.

Premium Allocation Approach (PAA)

The PAA is a simplified model that produces results similar to the traditional unearned premium approach used by general insurers. It is applicable to:

  • Contracts with a coverage period of 12 months or less (always eligible)
  • Longer contracts where the PAA produces a result that is not materially different from the GMM
  • For health and care insurers: very short-term health and care business
SA1 Scope Reminder
The course notes state the PAA is "not considered further in this subject." Know what it is and when it applies, but do not need to know its detailed mechanics.

⑤ US GAAP — Principles & Comparison with IFRS

Overview

While IFRS 17 is the focus, the SA1 syllabus also requires knowledge of US GAAP for comparative purposes. US GAAP is used by all US-listed companies, governed by the SEC, and guidance is produced by the FASB.

Why US GAAP Matters for SA1
Even if IFRS is not expected to be used by US businesses, US companies may be affected by IFRS through: mergers & acquisitions with non-US companies; non-US stakeholders requiring IFRS information; and non-US subsidiaries of multinational companies.

US GAAP Principles

US GAAP is also principles-based, but has some industry-specific rules. The key principles (some of which don't exist in IFRS) are:

PrincipleDescriptionDifferent from IFRS?

ComplianceMust comply with all US GAAP, but no explicit unreserved statement needed⚠ Yes — IFRS requires explicit compliance statement
Historical costAssets/liabilities valued at acquisition cost rather than fair value — though trend is towards fair values⚠ Yes — IFRS leans more towards fair value
Revenue recognitionRevenue recorded when earned; losses when probable. Augmented by detailed industry-specific rules⚠ Yes — more detailed rules than IFRS
MatchingExpenses matched with revenues when the work makes its contribution to revenueConcept exists in IFRS, less explicit
Full disclosureSufficient information to make a judgement while keeping costs reasonableNo
ObjectivityInformation should be factual and verifiableNo
ConsistencyPresented consistently from period to periodNo
OffsettingGenerally cannot offset. For derivatives: optional under US GAAP vs required under IFRS when stated conditions met⚠ Yes — derivatives treatment differs

Deferred Acquisition Cost (DAC)

The DAC is a concept used under US GAAP to handle the distortion caused by high initial expenses:

  • A notional asset is set up on the balance sheet equal to deferrable acquisition costs (those expected to be recovered from future premiums)
  • This asset is then amortised (written down) over the policy term as profit margins emerge
  • This smooths the initial strain, producing a more even emergence of profit
Important: DAC doesn't change total profit
The choice of how fast to amortise the DAC does not affect the total profit earned — only the timing (pattern) of its recognition. Amortising more slowly → profit recognised faster.

Deferred Profit Liability (DPL)

Used when the premium-paying period is shorter than the insurance period (e.g. a 10-year term policy paid up in 3 years):

  • Without a DPL, all premium income would be recognised quickly while the insurance coverage continues — mismatching income and service
  • A DPL reserve is set up to offset the excess profit being earned during the premium-paying period
  • This reserve is released over the life of the contract
  • Like DAC, the DPL does not affect total profit — only the pattern of recognition

Reporting basis: Best estimate vs Prudent

This is a fundamental distinction that runs through the whole chapter:

BasisDescriptionUsed in
Best estimate Unbiased estimates of expected future experience — no explicit margins IFRS 17, Solvency II BEL — newer profit-focused standards
Prudent Assumptions include a margin for adverse deviation — typically more conservative Older solvency-focused standards, Indian GAAP solvency margins

IFRS 17 requires best estimate cashflows with an explicit risk adjustment shown separately — not buried in the liability. This is the modern approach, consistent with Solvency II.

⑥ Supplementary Value Reporting — Embedded Value

Why supplementary reporting exists

The primary financial statements (under IFRS, GAAP, or local regulations) aim to show "true and fair" profit. However, for long-term insurers, these may still include a level of prudence that understates the true worth of the business for shareholders. This is why supplementary EV reporting developed.

Reason for EV ReportingDetail
DefensiveIf profit and value are understated in primary accounts, the company may be vulnerable to a takeover at a price that doesn't reflect fair value to shareholders
Capital raisingUnderstatement of value compromises the company's ability to raise debt or equity; goes against Stock Exchange principle that investors should have accurate information
Management informationEV gives management a more realistic view of value creation — useful for pricing, product management, and performance measurement

Embedded Value — the formula

Embedded Value (EV) = Free Surplus + Required Capital − Cost of holding required capital + PVIF

where:
PVIF = Present Value of future shareholder cashflows from in-force business
Free Surplus (FS) = Own funds − SCR = MVA − TP − SCR (under Solvency II)

Profit = Change in EV over the period + Profit transfer to shareholders

EV components

ComponentDescription
Free SurplusShareholder own funds in excess of the SCR (or MCR)
Required CapitalCapital the insurer must hold (SCR + potentially risk margin)
Cost of holding capitalThe cost of tying up capital — reduces the EV
PVIFPV of future shareholder cashflows from in-force covered business — the key "franchise value" component

Under Solvency II — EV simplification

Under Solvency II, where BEL is on a best estimate basis, there are no prudential margins to be released in the PVIF. A PVIF component may still exist for:

  • Profits expected beyond Solvency II contract boundaries
  • Difference between best estimate investment returns in EV projection and discount rates in BEL (including matching/volatility adjustment)
  • Release of the risk margin, after allowing for the cost of holding it

If PVIF = 0 and the Solvency II risk margin is used as the measure of cost of residual non-hedgeable risks:

EV ≈ Shareholder 'Own Funds'

EEV and MCEV — Out of scope

SA1 Scope Note
European Embedded Value (EEV) and Market Consistent Embedded Value (MCEV) principles are explicitly stated to be outside the scope of the current SA1 syllabus. Know they exist, but do not study their mechanics.

The future of EV under IFRS 17

With IFRS 17 and Solvency II both moving towards more market-consistent, realistic valuations, the "information gap" that EV was designed to fill is much smaller than it used to be. Many insurers in Solvency II jurisdictions no longer report EV.

Benefits of aligning EV with Solvency II (IAI exam tested this)
  • Efficiencies and resource synergies
  • Reduces manual/spreadsheet-based processes
  • Greater comparability and reconciliation between EV and supervisory balance sheet
  • Opportunity to align pricing and risk management with performance reporting
  • Improvement in quality due to higher data/documentation standards

IAI November 2023 · Q1(iii) IAI
8 marks
The company uses 'shareholder value profit' as its primary measure of profit in its financial statements. Describe how shareholder value profit is measured and how the recognition of profit can be controlled.
Measuring shareholder value profit:
  • Shareholder value profit = change in shareholder value of the long-term insurance fund over the year + this year's transfer to shareholders [1]
  • Assess the value of future transfers during every future reporting period [½]
  • Make estimates of each element of future experience (investment returns, claims, withdrawals, expenses) that would affect transfers to shareholders [½]
  • Project future transfers to shareholders arising on the in-force business using these estimates [½]
  • Discount future transfers to reporting date using the Risk Discounting Rate (RDR) — which reflects the risk inherent to the business [1]
  • Include any shareholder value in respect of free assets in the long-term fund [½]
Controlling profit recognition:
  • Include risk margins in each of the projection assumptions [½]
  • Include a margin for risk in the discount rate used (RDR set higher) [½]
  • Or a combination of both — the margins should have regard to uncertainty and risks of the business [½]

IAI November 2023 · Q1(iv) IAI
8 marks
The regulator has recently allowed companies to use a Deferred Acquisition Cost (DAC) approach in their statutory financial statements. The company is also considering switching to Embedded Value (EV) reporting. Compare the DAC approach with the Embedded Value approach for profit recognition.
DAC approach:
  • Attempts to give a more realistic measure of profit by spreading the effect of initial expenses via a DAC asset [1]
  • Reduces statutory capital strain when a contract is first written, producing more even emergence of profit [1]
  • However, does NOT recognise future profits on in-force business — may still undervalue the true worth of the business, especially if expanding [1]
Embedded Value approach:
  • EV places a value on ALL expected transfers from a contract once written — avoids problems of capital strain [1]
  • Unlike DAC, makes allowance for changes in future expected profits during the year (e.g. due to new sales) [1]
  • Future profits are assessed prudently (RDR set at a higher level) to reflect the risk they may not arise as expected [½]
  • As risk margins unwind, further profit expected to emerge in future — EV does not represent a fully best estimate view at inception [½]
  • Relies on estimates about the future — subject to a degree of judgement [½]
  • EV is regarded as a more realistic measure of profit recognition than the DAC approach [½]

5 marks
Explain why the CSM would be expected to be positive for a profitable regular premium contract, despite initial expenses being likely to exceed the initial premium.
  • The CSM would likely be positive because the BEL would be negative [1]
  • For a regular premium contract, the present value of future premiums would be expected to exceed the PV of future benefits and expenses [1]
  • This is because future premiums include loadings to recover initial expenses and to provide a profit margin [½]
  • So the net cashflow (initial premium − initial expenses) − (BEL + RA) would be positive, since BEL is negative and therefore BEL + RA is small (or negative) [1]
  • As long as the RA is smaller than the discounted value of all profit loadings in future premiums, the CSM will be positive [½]

Practice Q — GMM vs VFA Practice
6 marks
Explain the key difference between the General Measurement Model (GMM) and the Variable Fee Approach (VFA) with regard to the discount rate and CSM, and describe what this means for reported profit when interest rates change.
Discount rate:
  • Under GMM: discount rate is based on market observable data, not linked to any specific asset pool [1]
  • Under VFA: discount rate is calculated with reference to the pool of assets backing the contract [1]
CSM treatment:
  • Under GMM: CSM is NOT unlocked when discount rate changes — the CSM remains unchanged [½]
  • Under VFA: CSM IS unlocked at each period to absorb changes in BEL and RA arising from discount rate changes [½]
Effect when interest rates rise (BEL and RA fall):
  • Under GMM: the fall in BEL+RA creates a gain that flows directly to P&L — recognised as profit in the current period [1]
  • Under VFA: the gain is absorbed by the CSM (CSM is increased) — does not flow to P&L; instead deferred to future periods as CSM unwinds [1]

Practice Q — Transition Practice
6 marks
Describe the three approaches permitted under IFRS 17 for determining the transitional balance sheet, and state when each approach may be used.
Full Retrospective Approach (FRA):
  • Must be used if it is practicable to do so [½]
  • Involves determining the position at transition as if IFRS 17 had applied throughout the full policy lifetime [1]
  • Requires access to historical data back to policy inception [½]
Modified Retrospective Approach (MRA):
  • Used if FRA is not practicable (e.g. lack of historic data for older policies) [½]
  • A simplified version of FRA using reasonable information available without excessive cost or effort [1]
  • Produces a result as close as possible to FRA [½]
Fair Value Approach (FVA):
  • Used if there is insufficient information to carry out an MRA calculation [½]
  • Transitional CSM = fair value of policies less fulfilment cashflows at transition date [1]
  • Effectively treats the transition date as day one for the contract [½]

  • Phased retrospective application: IRDAI has introduced a calibrated retrospective approach — at least 10 years in FY27, 15 years in FY28, 20 years in FY29 — to ease operational burden [1½]
  • Annual cohort upheld: The annual cohort requirement was NOT relaxed despite industry requests — IRDAI confirmed this is a core principle to prevent cross-subsidisation [½]
  • Solvency not impacted: Ind AS adoption does not affect actuarial valuation, surplus determination, or solvency requirements under the Insurance Act 1938 — these continue under existing provisions until Risk Based Capital is implemented [1]
  • Surplus distribution: Distribution of surplus continues to be governed by Section 49 of the Insurance Act — accounting profit under Ind AS 117 does not automatically translate into distributable surplus [1]
  • Independent validation: In the first year, financial statements require independent validation by an auditor from an IRDAI-empanelled list, beyond the statutory audit [½]

# IFRS 17 Insurance Contract Liability (GMM)

# CSM at inception:

⚠ Exam Trap — Surplus Distribution
Ind AS 117 accounting profit ≠ distributable surplus. The CSM releases profit over the coverage period under accounting principles. But the actual dividend/bonus to policyholders is still governed by Section 49 of the Insurance Act — i.e., actuarial surplus from valuation. These two numbers will almost certainly differ. Insurers must disclose a reconciliation between them.

5. Key Policy Choices — IRDAI's Proposed Solutions

5a. Policyholder vs Shareholder Fund Reporting

The tension: IFRS/Ind AS requires entity-level financial statements (no PH/SH split). The Insurance Act §11(2) mandates separate PH and SH fund accounts.

✅ Proposed Solution — Dual Layer
Layer 1 (Ind AS — primary): Balance Sheet, Statement of P&L, Statement of Changes in Equity, Cash Flow Statement — all at entity level.

Layer 2 (Insurance Act — secondary): Separate Revenue Account for the policyholder fund + schedules showing PH/SH fund segregation.

5b. Annual Cohort Requirement for Participating Business

Insurers requested exemption because participating contracts are managed as a unified fund with bonuses declared from overall surplus — tracking at cohort level is operationally complex.

JurisdictionPosition on Annual Cohort
IASB (IFRS 17)No exemption — considered and rejected. Para 22 mandates annual cohorting for all contracts.
European UnionOptional exemption introduced via EU Regulation 2021/2036 for intergenerationally-mutualised contracts — subject to review by 31 Dec 2027.
India (Ind AS 117)NO exemption — insurers must comply with annual cohort requirement. Para B70 flexibility permitted: identify changes at higher aggregation level and allocate systematically to cohort groups.
⚠ Retrospective Cohort Periods — India-Specific
IRDAI's proposed retrospective look-back for annual cohort compliance:
• FY 2026–27 financial statements → at least 10 years retrospective
• FY 2027–28 financial statements → at least 15 years retrospective
• FY 2028–29 financial statements → at least 20 years retrospective

5c. Surplus Distribution in Participating Business

AspectInsurance Act (Section 49)Ind AS 117
Basis of surplus/profitActuarial valuation of assets and liabilitiesAccounting measurement of insurance contract liabilities (CSM release)
PurposeDetermination of distributable surplusFinancial reporting of insurance contract performance
TimingBased on actuarial valuation dateBased on services provided over coverage period

Proposed solution: Section 49 continues to govern actual surplus distribution. Ind AS 117 accounting profit is for financial reporting only. Insurers must provide a reconciliation disclosure between actuarial surplus and Ind AS accounting profit.
