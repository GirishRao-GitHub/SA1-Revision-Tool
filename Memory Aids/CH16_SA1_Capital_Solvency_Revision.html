Why Capital is Required

Three perspectives on capital assessment + two categories of need.

Tested in: March 2022 Q2i–v · March 2021 Q2ii · April 2016 Q3i

Three Perspectives on Capital

Internal

Economic Capital

Internal determination based on the company's own risk profile, risk appetite and ongoing business strategy. Assessed via stochastic modelling and/or stress tests. Most onerous for companies with high risk appetite or aggressive strategies.

Regulatory Floor

Regulatory Capital

Solvency requirements set by the regulator. Defines regulatory values of assets, liabilities and required capital (e.g. Solvency II SCR = VaR 99.5% over 1 year). All companies must calculate and comply. ORSA is a key tool.

Market Access

Rating Agency Capital

Capital adequacy standard required by agencies (S&P, Moody's, AM Best) for a target credit rating. Drives cost of raising capital and market's willingness to transact. Agencies increasingly moving toward economic capital measures.

Key exam point: The most onerous perspective varies by company. A company with an aggressive growth strategy may find economic capital most binding; a less sophisticated insurer may be bound by regulatory capital.

Two Categories of Capital Need

Category 1

Risk Capital

  • Market risk — adverse asset/interest rate movements
  • Credit risk — counterparty default
  • Insurance risk — mortality, morbidity, persistency, expenses
  • Operational risk — outsourcing, terrorism, systems failures
  • Liquidity risk — inability to meet obligations as due
  • Group risk — contagion from group entities
Category 2

Working Capital

  • Fund new business strain
  • Attract intermediaries (demonstrate solvency)
  • Organic expansion — hardware, software, premises, overseas
  • Acquisitions of companies or blocks of business

Exacerbated by: mis-selling fines · flat charging caps · low investment returns · adverse demographics · changing distribution

Solvency Ratio — Definition & Drivers

Solvency Ratio
Solvency Ratio = Available Capital ÷ Required Capital × 100%
A ratio <100% implies insolvency. Regulators typically set thresholds (e.g. 120%) — interventions kick in below these.
Drivers of Available Capital ▾
  • Shareholder equity and retained profits
  • Investment return on free assets
  • Profitability of in-force business and new business
  • Capital injections / subordinated debt
  • Dividends reduce; losses reduce

Depleted by

  • New business strain (capital consumed at point of sale)
  • Adverse claims experience
  • Asset write-downs / fall in investment values
  • Regulatory penalties / mis-selling fines
Drivers of Required Capital ▾
  • Biometric risks: mortality, morbidity, longevity
  • Investment risk: volatility + credit risk
  • Expense risk (incl. expense inflation)
  • Lapse / persistency risk
  • Operational risk
  • Catastrophe / pandemic risk

Reduced by

  • Diversification across risks / geographies
  • Reinsurance (reduces net exposure)
  • Matching assets to liabilities (reduces market risk)

Implications of Borderline Solvency March 2022 Q2ii

Regulatory Actions

  • Restriction / closure to new business
  • Immediate capital injection demand
  • Supervisory intervention / monitoring
  • Potential licence revocation if MCR breached

Commercial Impact

  • Bad publicity → lower sales
  • Brokers not promoting company products
  • Credit rating downgrade → higher cost of capital
  • Drop in share price if listed
  • Expansion plans stalled

Why Expense Management Matters for Solvency March 2022 Q2v

CFO fallacy: "Expenses are irrelevant to solvency — it's just a claims problem."

Why this is wrong: (1) Expense overruns directly eat into available capital. (2) Risk-based capital frameworks explicitly include expense risk and expense inflation as a driver of required capital (SCR expense sub-module). (3) Expense management is a key driver of profitability, which feeds available capital.

Reasons for Projecting Solvency

  • Track evolution of the risk profile over time
  • Assess impact of key business strategy decisions
  • Show new business strain; determine supportable NB volume
Regulatory ORSA and Solvency II requirements ▾
  • Statutory requirement under Solvency II Pillar 2 ORSA
  • Determine risk margin (Pillar 1)
  • May feed into internal model SCR calculations
  • Stress testing and reverse stress testing
Reporting & Value Capital releases, ROC, EV ▾
  • Estimate pattern of capital releases to shareholders
  • Assess return on capital deployed
  • Calculate the cost of holding required capital — used in embedded value

Reserves

Types, purposes, bases, and key exam-tested distinctions for long-term and short-term business.

Tested across multiple sittings · Especially March 2022 Q2iii, March 2021 Q2iii

Reserve Types — Long-Term Business (CI, IP, LTCI, Group LT)

ALR / Policy Reserve

Active Life Reserve

EPV(future claims + expenses) − EPV(future premiums)

Held when future outgo exceeds future income. Separate from disabled life reserves (for policyholders already claiming).

IBNR

Incurred But Not Reported

Claims arising but not yet notified. Special CI risk: policyholders may learn of cover years after the disability event — late notification is increasingly common as brokers become more involved.

RBNS

Reported But Not Settled

Claims notified, in assessment or in payment. Example: IP claims in deferred period; CI claims in assessment period.

Option Reserve

Option Reserve

Set aside when an option "comes into the money." Example: guaranteed conversion options on CI policies; guaranteed renewability options.

Reserve Types — Short-Term Business (PMI, Annual Group)

Retrospective Unearned Premium Reserve (UPR) ▾

Balance of premiums received for unexpired risk. = (100 − m)% × risk-related premium, where m% of risk falls before year-end. Risk-related premium excludes initial expenses (commission etc.) which are earned at inception.

Prospective Unexpired Risk Reserve (URR) ▾

Held when UPR < expected future claims + expenses for the unexpired period. Estimated via projected future loss ratio applied to unexpired premium proportion. URR replaces UPR when UPR is inadequate — never held simultaneously as an addition.

Statistical IBNR, IBNER & Outstanding Claims Reserve ▾

IBNR

Incurred but not reported. Usually small for PMI (short delay between claim event and notification). Calculated via statistical methods (chain ladder triangles).

IBNER

Incurred But Not Enough Reported — adjustment (+ or −) where past case estimates show systematic bias. Separate identification provides management insight and may attract different tax treatment.

Outstanding Claims Reserve

Notified but not yet fully settled. Statistical methods or case estimates per claim.

Smoothing Equalisation / Catastrophe Reserve ▾

Equalisation reserve smooths profits across atypical years. Catastrophe reserve covers future catastrophe events. Legislation and tax treatment vary by territory. May attract favourable tax treatment in some jurisdictions.

Reserving Methods — Case Estimates vs Statistical

Case Estimates — When to Use
  • Small volumes or large/unusual individual claims
  • Low-data situations (new product, new market)
  • Experienced claims manager reviews each file individually
  • Considers: procedure type, hospital, surgeon, age/gender, past history, medical inflation
Statistical — When to Use
  • Large stable portfolio (majority of PMI claims)
  • Homogeneous cohorts; chain ladder run-off triangles
  • Segment by contract / distribution / geography
  • Long-term: stochastic or deterministic models for income duration
In practice: Statistical methods for the bulk of claims + case estimates for large, unusual or outlier claims. The combination is standard.

Solvency II Framework

The three-pillar structure, SCR, MCR, BEL, Risk Margin — with exam-focused depth.

Tested in: March 2022 Q1i–v · March 2021 Q2v · March 2022 Q2i–viii

Overview

Objective: Adequate protection of policyholders; risk-based, harmonised EU-wide approach to capital adequacy, risk management and reporting. Operative from 1 January 2016. Applies to EU insurers with gross premium >€5m or gross TPs >€25m.

Not just a compliance framework — affects capital allocation, product mix, asset mix, corporate structure and M&A decisions. Board-level engagement required.

The Three Pillars

Pillar 1 — Quantitative

Capital Requirements

  • Market-consistent balance sheet
  • Technical Provisions = BEL + Risk Margin
  • SCR — Solvency Capital Requirement
  • MCR — Minimum Capital Requirement
  • Own Funds (Tier 1, 2, 3)
Pillar 2 — Qualitative

Governance & ORSA

  • Board responsible for SII compliance
  • 4 key functions: Risk Mgmt · Actuarial · Compliance · Internal Audit
  • ORSA — own risk & solvency assessment
  • 3–5yr forward-looking solvency projection
  • "Embeddedness" in strategic decisions required
Pillar 3 — Disclosure

Reporting

  • RSR — private to supervisor, annual
  • QRT — quantitative templates, annual + quarterly subset
  • SFCR — public disclosure, annual, within 3–4 months of year-end
  • Contains ORSA summary

Technical Provisions = BEL + Risk Margin

Best Estimate Liability (BEL)

BEL

  • PV of expected future cashflows (benefits + expenses − premiums)
  • Discounted at risk-free yield curve (EIOPA publishes monthly)
  • Best estimate assumptions — no prudential margins
  • Policy-by-policy or grouped by homogeneous product type
  • Can be negative (no floor of zero)
  • SLT (IP, LTCI) and Non-SLT (PMI) treated differently
Risk Margin (RM)

Risk Margin

  • Additional amount so TP = transfer value to another insurer
  • Cost of Capital method: 6% p.a. on projected non-hedgeable SCR, discounted at risk-free rates
  • Covers: insurance risk, operational risk, residual market risk
  • Not a prudence margin — it is a market-consistent transfer price element

SCR vs MCR — Critical Distinction

SCRMCR
Confidence Level VaR 99.5% over 1 year VaR 85% over 1 year
Formula Standard formula (stress tests by risk module, correlation matrix → BSCR + SCR_op + Adj) or internal model Simple linear factor formula based on TPs + premiums/capital at risk
Floor / Cap — Floor: 25% SCR · Cap: 45% SCR · Absolute min: €3.7m (LT) / €2.5m (ST)
Breach consequences Intervention: submit recovery plan within 6 months Ultimate intervention: authorisation may be withdrawn
Capital tier requirements ≥50% Tier 1; <15% Tier 3 ≥80% Tier 1; no Tier 3 allowed

Health Risk Modules under Standard Formula

SLT Health (life-like)

IP · SACI · LTCI

  • Disability / morbidity risk
  • Mortality risk
  • Longevity risk
  • Lapse risk
  • Expense risk
  • Revision risk
Non-SLT Health (non-life-like)

PMI · Annual Products

  • Premium & reserve risk
  • Lapse risk
CAT Risk (both)

Catastrophe Module

  • Epidemic / pandemic scenarios
  • Climate-related events
  • Antimicrobial resistance
  • Mass accident scenarios

Discount Rates — MA vs VA

Matching Adjustment (MA)

Matching Adjustment

  • For LT predictable liabilities held to maturity (annuity-type)
  • Add illiquidity premium to discount rate → reduces BEL
  • Requires regulator approval; ring-fenced assets
  • No surrender risk; assets cannot be sold before maturity
  • MA = insurer-specific (own assets)
Volatility Adjustment (VA)

Volatility Adjustment

  • Where MA not eligible
  • Add % of spread to reduce forced-sale risk in market crises
  • EIOPA publishes by currency/market
  • Subject to liquidity plan + sensitivity analysis
  • VA = market-wide (same for all insurers in that market)
Only one of MA or VA can apply at a time to a given portfolio. MA is insurer-specific; VA is currency/market-wide.

Financial Reinsurance — As a Solvency Tool March 2022 Q2viii

When is FinRe suitable? Key structures ▾

Suitability depends on the nature of the solvency problem and whether genuine risk transfer occurs (for regulatory capital credit).

Lump-sum fund / Surplus relief

  • Reinsurer advances funds → creates assets; insurer repays from future profits
  • Works if the company is profitable and just needs capital timing relief

VIF Monetisation

  • Recognise profit embedded in in-force business through reinsurance
  • Works when there is a large valuable in-force block

Key caveats

  • True risk transfer must occur to get regulatory capital credit
  • Regulator approval may be required
  • FinRe does not fix structural solvency problems — only provides temporary relief
Investigating a Low Solvency Ratio — March 2022 Q2iii
Question: A company's solvency ratio has deteriorated. Outline the areas a Chief Actuary should investigate to understand causes and identify actions. [9 marks]
Available Capital Side

Asset / Admissibility Issues

  • Domiciling of assets / type of assets — admissibility restrictions
  • Fall in investment values (market downturn)
  • Reallocation of assets to more eligible types
  • Unrealised losses on asset portfolio
Liability / Claims Side

Reserve & Claims Issues

  • Reserving assumptions more prudent than required
  • Large claims / catastrophe / pandemic experience
  • Is the adverse claims experience one-off or recurring?
  • IBNR development — late-emerging claims (esp. CI)
Required Capital Side

SCR Movement

  • Has risk profile changed (new products, market expansion)?
  • Have correlation assumptions changed in standard formula?
  • Change in business mix → different SCR modules weighted
  • Capital add-on imposed by regulator?
Structural / Operational

Expenses & Operations

  • Expense overruns eating into available capital
  • New business strain too high — unsustainable growth rate
  • Mismatching of assets and liabilities
  • Reinsurance counterparty failure

Framework Summaries

UK Post-Brexit Position ▾
  • Left EU 1 January 2021. At exit, aligned with Solvency II.
  • UK insurers now third-country undertakings in the EU — cannot passport services.
  • PRA considering reform — potential reduction in capital requirements post-Brexit.
  • Third-country equivalence rules apply. Future alignment with EU uncertain.
USA State-Based System, RBC & PBR ▾

Structure

  • 50 states + DC + 5 territories — each state has own regulator and commissioner
  • NAIC coordinates model laws, standards, and uniformity of reporting
  • Federal Insurance Office (FIO): monitors, no supervisory role
  • Federal Reserve Board (FRB): consolidated oversight of systemically important insurers

Capital: Risk-Based Capital (RBC)

  • Standard formula applied to statutory financial statement balances
  • Covers: asset risk · insurance risk · business risk
  • Designed to identify weakly capitalised companies — closer to MCR than SCR in concept

Reserves: PBR (from 2017)

  • Principle-Based Reserving replaces 150+ years of formula-driven reserves
  • Hold highest of: (a) net premium reserve · (b) deterministic reserve · (c) stochastic reserve
  • Reflects own morbidity, lapses, expenses, option costs

Key Differences from Solvency II

  • RBC ≈ MCR (identifies weak capitalisation); SII also includes SCR for firm-specific risk
  • US reserves historically formulaic + prudential; SII uses market-consistent BEL + explicit RM
  • ORSA is annual but less embedded in strategic decision-making than SII ORSA
China C-ROSS Framework ▾

Regulator

  • CBIRC (China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission)

Three Pillars (similar to SII)

  • Pillar I: Quantitative capital — insurance risk, market risk, credit risk
  • Pillar II: Qualitative: rating system + ORSA equivalent + liquidity indicators
  • Pillar III: Transparency, disclosure, systemic risk monitoring

Key Differences from Solvency II

  • Assets valued at GAAP value, not mark-to-market
  • Discount yield ≈ GAAP liability yield, not risk-free curve
  • No internal models allowed — all insurers use same rules
  • Reflects China's younger, developing insurance market
Australia LAGIC Framework (APRA) ▾

Structure

  • LAGIC (Life & General Insurance Capital) — operative from January 2013
  • Three pillars with strong parallels to Solvency II

Pillar I: PCA (Prescribed Capital Amount)

  • 99.5% VaR over 1 year — same confidence level as SCR
  • Standard method or internal model
  • Covers: insurance + asset + concentration + operational risk
  • APRA can increase or decrease required capital (≈ capital add-on)

Equivalence

  • Provisional equivalence with Solvency II (10-year periods)
South Africa SAM Framework ▾

Regulators

  • PA (Prudential Authority) — licensing, supervision, enforcement
  • FSCA — market conduct, consumer protection, financial literacy

SAM (from July 2018)

  • Solvency Assessment & Management — largely based on Solvency II
  • Three pillars: Pillar I (quantitative), Pillar II (governance/ORSA), Pillar III (reporting/disclosure)
  • 1-in-200 year loss event (99.5% VaR) — same as SCR
  • Standardised formula or internal model
  • SA adaptations: own risk-free rates; market risk calibrated to SA markets
  • Microinsurance: reduced MCR to widen access for lower-income segments
India / IRDAI Indian Framework Context ▾

Regulatory Structure

  • IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India) — sole insurance regulator
  • Formula-based capital requirements (risk-based evolution underway)
  • Solvency margin typically expressed as a required solvency margin (RSM) based on premiums/claims
  • Appointed Actuary (AA) has statutory responsibilities including capital adequacy certification

Statutory Role of Appointed Actuary

  • Products designed, priced and compliant with applicable regulations
  • Policyholder reasonable expectations considered
  • Capital management — insurer remains solvent under stressed scenarios
  • Periodic regulatory reporting of financial condition

Embedded Value

Concept, PVFP, analysis of movement — a major tested area in the March 2022 paper.

Tested: March 2022 Q1i–v (35 marks combined)

EV Concept

Embedded Value Formula
EV = Shareholder Net Assets + PVFP
Where:
Shareholder Net Assets = Market value of assets − Regulatory reserves − SCR (less capital held for other purposes)
PVFP = Present Value of Future (shareholder) Profits on in-force business
Key logic: EV basis is more realistic than the reserving basis → margins release as profit over time. If the EV basis and reserving basis were identical, PVFP = 0. The difference in bases creates the VIF (Value of In-Force).
Without-Profits (IP/CI/LTC)

PVFP Components

  • PV(premiums + investment income − claims − expenses + reserve release)
  • Discounted at risk discount rate reflecting risk profile
Unit-Linked

PVFP Components

  • PV(charges − expenses − excess benefits + non-unit reserve release)
  • Unit funds pass through → shareholder profit comes from charges

Analysis of EV Movement — 7 Heads March 2022 Q1i–ii

#HeadWhat it represents
a Expected return on free surplus Risk-free rate applied to free surplus (= assets − statutory reserve − SCR) at start of year
b Return on in-force — expected return Risk-free rate applied to opening VIF. Called "unwind of discount rate" — profits becoming closer in time, so discounted less.
c Return on in-force — experience variances Actual vs expected experience (claims, lapses, expenses) rolled forward. Separate line per item. Also captures impact on future capital releases.
d Return on in-force — operating assumption changes Best estimate assumptions revised → change in VIF. Includes changes to RM or SCR correlation assumptions; change in cost-of-capital factor.
e New business contribution Increase in VIF + change in net assets at point of sale. Can be negative if new business is loss-making on EV basis.
f Investment return variance Actual investment return vs risk-free assumed return. Includes asset-liability mismatch impact; effect on cost of holding RM and capital.
g Effect of economic assumption changes Change in discount rate / risk-free yield curve. Similar to (f) but forward-looking. Often combined with investment return variance.

EV Movement vs Surplus Analysis March 2022 Q1v

EV Movement Analysis
  • Shows net impact on current surplus AND future profitability (VIF)
  • Takes account of how today's experience changes expected future profits
  • A rise in lapses might show a positive EV impact (removes unprofitable business)
  • Forward-looking view
Analysis of Surplus
  • Shows financial effect of divergence from valuation basis in current year only
  • Does not reflect impact on future profits
  • A rise in lapses would show a negative surplus impact (future premium income lost)
  • Backward-looking view
Exam trap: A single experience item can have opposite signs in EV movement analysis vs surplus analysis. Lapses are the classic example. Make sure you explain why.

Sale Price vs Published EV March 2022 Q1iv

Factors that could push sale price above or below published EV ▾

Could push price ABOVE EV

  • Seller is not in a weak position (not a forced sale) — can negotiate
  • Goodwill / franchise value from expected new business not in EV
  • Synergies that buyer can extract — diversification benefits, expense savings
  • Seller uses more optimistic basis than published EV
  • Competitive bidding process drives price up

Could push price BELOW EV

  • Buyer uses more conservative (prudent) basis
  • Buyer identifies risks not reflected in published EV (adverse experience, model risk)
  • Liquidity premium demanded by buyer
  • Regulatory / capital constraints on buyer → discount for integration costs
  • If EV is based on different asset allocation than buyer intends to hold

Process notes

  • Both parties likely to agree to use EV as starting point
  • Basis for calculations is a negotiation question
  • Independent actuarial review of EV common in M&A transactions

Impact of New Business Strategy on EV March 2022 Q1iii

Scenario: Company decides to focus IP marketing heavily. How does this impact EV and each head of the EV movement analysis?

Key principle: Current EV is unaffected — it does not include future new business. Only future EVs are impacted. Assuming IP is profitable on best estimate basis: future PVFP rises · new business contribution line increases · in-force experience may be affected if growth is so large it creates expense volatility · capital strain from NB strain should be considered.

Solvency Ratio & Capital

March 2022 · Q2i 5 marks · ½ per point
Briefly explain what is meant by solvency ratio and its drivers.
Definition
  • Solvency ratio = Available capital ÷ Required capital × 100%. Regulators require insurers to always maintain a ratio above a minimum threshold.
  • A ratio <100% implies insolvency. There is typically a supervisory threshold (e.g. 120%) — interventions begin below this before outright insolvency.
  • Both available capital and required capital are determined under the applicable regulatory regime. Required capital tends to be more formula-driven; available capital is the market-consistent surplus.
Drivers of Required Capital (Risk Profile)
  • Biometric risks: mortality, morbidity, longevity
  • Investment risk: both in absolute terms and relative to liabilities (mismatch)
  • Expense risk including expense inflation
  • Lapse / persistency risk
  • Operational risk
  • Catastrophe / pandemic risk
  • Counterparty (credit) risk on reinsurance and investments
Drivers of Available Capital
  • Investment return on backing assets
  • Profitability of in-force book and new business written
  • Capital injections from shareholders
  • Asset values (market movements affect the balance sheet)
March 2022 · Q2ii 5 marks · ½ per point
Outline the implications of a borderline solvency ratio to the business of the company.
Regulatory Implications
  • Regulatory intervention — likely to include limiting or closing to new business
  • Immediate demand for capital injection; submission of a recovery plan
  • Increased supervisory oversight and monitoring requirements
  • Risk of licence revocation if MCR is breached
Commercial / Market Implications
  • Bad publicity → negative customer sentiment → lower sales
  • Brokers may stop promoting the company's products
  • Credit rating downgrade → higher cost of raising future capital
  • Drop in share price if publicly listed
  • Any expansion plans likely to be stalled
  • Counterparties (reinsurers, banks) may demand additional collateral
March 2022 · Q2viii 7 marks · ½ per point
Discuss the suitability of financial reinsurance as a solution to a low solvency ratio.
Assessing Suitability
  • Suitability depends on the nature of the solvency problem — what drives the low ratio
  • Also depends on whether genuine risk transfer occurs — required for regulatory capital credit
  • Regulator may need to approve the structure
Lump-Sum Fund / Surplus Relief
  • Reinsurer advances a lump sum → creates assets for the insurer
  • Insurer repays from future profits on the reinsured block
  • Works if the company is profitable but has a capital timing problem
  • Does not work if insolvency is structural / losses are ongoing
VIF Monetisation
  • Recognise profit embedded in in-force business through reinsurance
  • Reinsurer pays for a share of future profits in exchange for assuming the risk
  • Works when there is a large, clearly profitable in-force block with credible experience
Key Limitations
  • FinRe does not fix structural solvency problems — only provides temporary relief
  • If solvency is low due to recurring adverse experience, FinRe is inappropriate
  • True risk transfer must be demonstrable to satisfy regulators
  • FinRe may be expensive (reinsurer requires return for capital deployed)

March 2022 · Q1v 5 marks
Explain the difference between the EV movement analysis and the analysis of surplus.
Core Distinction
  • EV movement shows the net impact on current surplus and future profitability (VIF); surplus analysis only shows divergences in current-year experience from the valuation basis.
  • A single experience item can have opposite signs in the two analyses — this is a critical exam point.
Classic Example: Increase in Lapses
  • Surplus analysis: Negative impact — future premium income is lost; reserve releases may not compensate.
  • EV movement: Positive impact — if the business is unprofitable on EV basis, removing lapsing lives increases VIF (removes negative-VIF policies).
  • Conversely, if business is profitable, higher lapses reduce EV.
What the Surplus Analysis Shows
  • Financial effect of divergences between actual experience and valuation basis (current period only)
  • Financial effect of writing new business in the period
  • If carried out independently of the valuation, it shows the contribution of new business to statutory surplus

Appointed Actuary & Capital

March 2021 · Q2v 11 marks
Outline the statutory responsibilities of the Appointed Actuary and describe how a virtual insurer set-up creates additional challenges for those responsibilities.
Statutory Responsibilities of the AA
  • Products are designed and priced correctly and comply with applicable regulations
  • Policyholder reasonable expectations are considered in all aspects of business
  • Capital management — ensure the insurer remains solvent in reasonably stressed scenarios
  • Periodic regulatory reporting of the insurer's financial condition
  • Certification of solvency returns / financial condition reports to the regulator
Additional Challenges in a Virtual Set-Up
  • AA needs strong data governance — virtual systems create unclear data lineage and accessibility issues
  • Fully virtual operations mean processes are tweaked for digital workflows — AA must understand the controls
  • System upgrades and process changes carry data and control implications that must be assessed beforehand
  • Technology limitations could impact completeness and accuracy of data feeding actuarial models
  • AA and team must understand technology constraints — not just actuarial ones
  • Remote working can impair information flows and sign-off procedures for regulatory submissions
  • Cybersecurity risk affects data integrity — directly relevant to reserving and solvency calculations
March 2021 · Q1v 12 marks
Describe the asset-liability modelling process for a group IP product.
Model Office & Liability Projection
  • Project liabilities using a model office — select representative model points from the portfolio
  • Liabilities for IP typically projected deterministically; stochastic inflation rate may be used for expenses
  • Decision needed: include new business or not — excluding NB is simpler but produces an unrealistic run-off picture
  • Multiple claim states modelled: active / sick / recovered / dead using multi-state (disability) models
  • Key assumptions: inception rates, termination rates (recovery + death), deferred periods, benefit escalation, expenses
Asset Modelling
  • Model the return on assets backing liabilities — fixed income, equities, property as applicable
  • Asset model calibrated to market data (yield curve, volatility surfaces)
  • Stochastic scenarios may be used for equity/property components
  • Typically 1,000 to 10,000 simulations for stochastic runs; time horizon 5–10 years or full run-off
Scenario Testing
  • Duration / term matching tested under key stress scenarios
  • Interest rate stress: parallel shifts up/down in yield curve
  • Morbidity stress: step change in inception rates or termination rates
  • Expense inflation stress
  • Reverse stress testing: identify scenarios that cause insolvency → inform risk management
Output
  • Projected solvency ratio over time — feeds ORSA
  • Assessment of capital needed to support business plan
  • Optimal asset strategy — which asset classes minimise mismatch risk
  • Basis for calculating cost of holding capital for EV purposes
Quick Fire — 2–3 mark questions
March 2022 · Q2v 3 marks
The CFO argues that expense management is irrelevant to solvency — solvency is purely a claims issue. Explain why the CFO is wrong.
Three Reasons Expenses Do Matter for Solvency
  • Direct capital drain: Expense overruns are an outgo item — they directly reduce available capital, exactly as a claim would.
  • Required capital includes expense risk: Risk-based capital frameworks (including Solvency II SCR) explicitly include an expense risk sub-module covering expense levels and expense inflation. Higher uncertainty in expenses → higher SCR → solvency ratio falls from both sides.
  • Profitability link: Expense management is a key driver of profitability. Lower profitability → lower retained earnings → lower available capital over time. Sustainable solvency requires profitable operations, not just claims management.

Explain what "expected return on in-force business" means in the EV movement analysis and why it appears even when there is no change in experience.
Explanation
  • The expected return on in-force (also called "unwind of the discount rate") = risk-free rate × opening VIF.
  • At the start of the year, future profits are discounted to present value using the risk discount rate. At the end of the year, those same profits are one year closer to being received, so they are discounted by one year less.
  • This mechanical unwinding creates a gain in VIF even if nothing changes in the portfolio — it is the expected "passage of time" return built into any discounted value calculation.
  • It is analogous to interest income on a bond — the investor earns the yield simply by holding the bond, without any outperformance.
