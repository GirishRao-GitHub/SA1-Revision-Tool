How to read this balance sheet
▾
1
Start with assets (left side). The dominant asset is investments — ₹3,280 Cr out of ₹4,210 Cr total. IRDAI mandates that insurers park most policyholder money in safe instruments like G-Secs and bonds so claims can always be paid.
2
Look at the largest liability: policy liabilities (₹2,580 Cr). This is money the company effectively "owes" policyholders — it includes IBNR (claims filed or incurred but not yet fully paid) and unearned premium (premium collected for future coverage periods).
3
Net worth = Assets − All liabilities. At ₹1,060 Cr, this is the shareholders' cushion. A growing net worth year-on-year signals the business is generating and retaining profit.
4
Check the solvency ratio (2.1×). IRDAI requires a minimum of 1.5×. This is the primary regulatory health check — if assets aren't comfortably above liabilities, the regulator can intervene.

How to read this revenue account
▾
1
Start at Gross Written Premium (₹2,840 Cr). This is the total premium billed to customers before sharing risk with reinsurers. It's the topline measure of how much insurance business the company wrote.
2
Subtract reinsurance ceded (₹620 Cr) to get net earned premium (₹2,140 Cr). Reinsurance is like insurance-for-insurers — ArogyaShield shares a portion of large or catastrophic claim risk with global reinsurers like GIC Re, Munich Re, etc., paying them a share of premium in return.
3
Net claims (₹1,590 Cr) is the biggest cost. The claims ratio is 65% — for every ₹100 of net premium, ₹65 goes to paying hospitalisation, day-care, and other health claims. IBNR adjustments account for claims incurred but not yet fully reported.
4
Combined ratio = (Claims + Expenses) / Net Earned Premium = 97%. Below 100% means an underwriting profit of ₹70 Cr. Many Indian health insurers run above 100% and rely on investment income — ArogyaShield is in good shape here.
5
Investment income (₹350 Cr) is the real profit engine. Returns from the large mandatory investment portfolio more than 5× the underwriting surplus, bringing the total revenue account surplus to ₹420 Cr — which flows into the P&L.

How to read this P&L and how all 3 statements connect
▾
1
The P&L starts where the Revenue Account ends. The ₹420 Cr surplus flows in as the first line of income — the P&L doesn't repeat all the premium and claims detail; it just picks up the net result.
2
Non-operating items are small but real. Depreciation on IT systems and provisions for uncollectable premiums are deducted here, not in the Revenue Account. They reduce PBT from ₹438 Cr to ₹398 Cr.
3
Tax at ~25% brings PAT to ₹298 Cr. Indian insurers pay corporate tax like any other company. Deferred tax arises from timing differences between accounting and tax treatment of items like IBNR reserves.
4
How it links to the Balance Sheet: Of the ₹298 Cr PAT, ₹60 Cr goes to shareholders as dividend, ₹30 Cr to a catastrophe reserve, and ₹208 Cr is retained. That ₹208 Cr is added to "Reserves & Surplus" on the balance sheet — directly growing net worth year-on-year.
5
The three-statement loop: Revenue Account viability → P&L bottom line → retained earnings → stronger Balance Sheet → more investment assets → more investment income → stronger Revenue Account. Regulators like IRDAI monitor all three together, not in isolation.
