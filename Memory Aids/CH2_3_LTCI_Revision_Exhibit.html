LTCI — Product Overview
SP1 Chapter 3 · Core Reading fundamentals
💡
Exam Lens: LTCI questions frequently test the distinction between pre-funded and immediate needs, ADL triggers, and why cash rather than indemnity benefits predominate. Always link features to their rationale from both insurer and policyholder perspectives.
🏠 Definition & Nature
  • All forms of continuing personal/nursing care and associated domestic services for people unable to look after themselves without support
  • May be provided in the person's own home, day centre, State-sponsored or care home setting
  • Distinct from acute medical care — NOT principally concerned with curing conditions; treats the results of conditions
  • Long-term because health is expected to deteriorate or not improve
  • Two generic types: Pre-funded plans (bought when healthy) and Immediate needs plans (bought when already in need)
  • Responsibility split between State (welfare programme) and individual (insurance / own assets)
💷 Costs of Care
  • Living costs — food, clothing, heating, amenities (insure the extra costs)
  • Housing costs — rent, mortgage, council tax, adaptations (hoists, rails); also called "hotel element"
  • Personal care — all care directly involving the body; includes nursing care and non-nursing personal care
  • Nursing care (narrowest) — requires specific knowledge/skills of a qualified nurse
  • Intermediate care — recuperative services after acute event (e.g. heart attack) to reduce hospital readmission and dependence on ongoing LTC
  • Informal care by family has indirect cost: lost economic activity or cost of replacing that care
🏢 Care Settings
  • Own home — preferred by most; encouraged by government policy; majority of care provided here
  • Homes of relatives — community care outside person's own home
  • Private residential homes — majority of non-home care; can be charity-run (e.g. for veterans)
  • Nursing homes — for those needing higher levels of clinical care
  • Continuing Care Retirement Communities (CCRCs) — lump sum to join; ongoing service charges; tiered care within community
  • Care setting can affect benefit level (some US plans pay different amounts for home vs nursing home)
👨‍👩‍👧 Informal vs Formal Care
  • Informal care — provided by spouse, children, other relatives; often >60 yrs old themselves (those needing care may be 80s/90s)
  • Those over 60 are retired and have time; younger relatives have work/family responsibilities
  • Informal care has an indirect cost — lost economic activity or replacement cost
  • Formal care — professional services from private/public providers; direct cost to individual or State
  • LTCI purchased partly to avoid dependence on family and friends
  • Cash benefits can compensate informal carers; not restricted to formal care spending
❓ Uncertainties for Individuals
  • Probability of needing care — medical advances, genetic predisposition, new conditions
  • Extent of care needed — speed of deterioration, technology advances, level of informal support available
  • Duration of care needed — longevity risk; mortality improvement makes this less predictable
  • All three dimensions make self-funding risky — LTCI pools these risks
  • If underestimated → accumulated wealth insufficient to meet care needs in retirement
🎯 Consumer Needs Met
  • Financial protection against cost of care in old age when unable to look after themselves
  • Independence from reliance on family or State
  • Protection of assets (e.g. family home) from care costs
  • Peace of mind — LTCI provides certainty even if future State provision is uncertain
  • Bought by people who wish to fund care of elderly relatives
  • A lower priority while employed (mortgage, pension take precedence) — awareness grows post-retirement

Pre-Funded Plans — Product Design
Bought when healthy · ADL-triggered · Cash or indemnity benefits
📌
Core principle: The claim trigger defines everything else. ADLs are the dominant trigger; the number failed determines both eligibility and, for tiered products, the benefit level. The overriding cognitive impairment trigger applies regardless of ADL failures.

The 6 Standard Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)

🚿
Washing
Ability to maintain adequate cleanliness and personal hygiene
👕
Dressing
Ability to put on/take off all garments and surgical appliances
🍽️
Feeding
Ability to transfer food from plate to mouth once food is prepared
🚽
Toileting
Ability to manage bowel and bladder function at acceptable hygiene level
🚶
Mobility
Ability to move indoors from room to room on level surfaces at normal place of residence
🛏️
Transferring
Ability to move from lying in bed to sitting in upright chair/wheelchair and vice versa
🔔 Claim Triggers
  • ADL trigger: Unable to perform a specified number of the 6 ADLs alone and without endangering wellbeing of self or others
  • Common requirement: failure of 2 out of 6 for 50% benefit; 3 or more out of 6 for 100% benefit
  • Mental/Cognitive impairment trigger — overriding; payable whether or not any ADLs are failed
  • Cognitive impairment: deterioration in memory, orientation (who/where/when), ability to solve problems, make rational decisions — from organic cause (Alzheimer's, irreversible dementia) — NOT depression or drug side effects
  • ADL definitions are worded carefully for objectivity, but still leave room for value judgements
  • Some plans require additional conditions: e.g. need for night care, or care only providable in nursing home
  • Disability must be continuing (not a single point-in-time assessment)
💰 Benefit Types
  • Indemnity basis: Insurer pays cost of care as incurred, subject to upper limits (e.g. £15,000/year)
  • Cash basis: Fixed amounts specified at policy inception — regardless of actual care cost
  • Most plans provide cash (not indemnity): insurer cannot reliably forecast future care cost inflation
  • Benefit forms: regular income for life (most common), lump sum, annuity certain, or limited benefit payment period
  • Tiered benefits: 50% for 2 ADLs failed; 100% for 3+ ADLs failed — more common than flat benefit
  • Benefits should not depend on place of residence under cash plans (unlike US where home vs nursing home rates differ)
  • Indemnity plans often restricted to maximum (e.g. 3 hrs care/day × 7 days for UK PMI add-on)
⏱️ Deferred Periods & Escalation
  • Deferred periods: 3 months or 12 months — gives time for informal care, adjustment, and assessment
  • Longer deferred period → lower premium; policyholder self-funds initial period
  • Benefit escalation options:
  • → Level (no escalation)
  • → Fixed rate (e.g. 3% or 5% p.a.) — may not keep pace with actual care costs
  • → Linked to inflation index — better match but more uncertain for insurer
  • Care is primarily people-based → costs rise with wage inflation (not just price inflation)
  • Benefit payment period: Lifetime (most comprehensive) or fixed period (e.g. 3 years) — limited period complements State provision
💳 Premium Structures
  • Regular premiums: Level or escalating in line with benefit; usually waived during claim
  • Single premium: Paid at outset; immediate full cover
  • Retrospective payment: Premium funded from equity released on sale of home after death
  • Restricted regular premiums: Stop at a certain age (e.g. 65) or when disability benefit triggers
  • Guaranteed premiums: Fixed for life — expensive contingency loading; not offered in UK or USA
  • Reviewable premiums: Adjusted in light of experience — risk of increase in retirement on fixed income
  • Compromise: premiums reviewable to age 70–75, then guaranteed — limits insurer risk as few survive to receive guarantee
  • Age-dependent guarantee: effectiveness limited at advanced ages (most policyholders near claim or already claiming)
🎁 Additional Benefits
  • Assistive devices benefit: 3–6 months' benefits paid to fund grab rails, bathing seats, stair lifts — helps defer further disability trigger
  • Personal alarms: Helps people remain in own homes
  • Independent care advice: At point of claim; claimant inexperienced in buying care; care manager/advocate
  • Respite care: For policies covering professional care costs — enables non-professional carer breaks
  • Death benefit: Basic plans = none; some return portion of premium (amortised over 5 years on early death → zero after 60 months)
  • Surrender/paid-up: Basic plans = no surrender; after minimum premium-paying period (e.g. 5 years) → reduced paid-up benefit
  • Paid-up alternatives: shorter benefit payment period, or coverage ceasing at e.g. age 85
🏗️ Product Structure Options
  • Long-term vs general (short-term) business — can be structured either way
  • On-shore vs off-shore: Unit-linked plans may be written off-shore (beyond local legislation), often accumulate free of tax; benefits taxed when remitted
  • Stand-alone or as a rider benefit on: IP insurance, CI insurance, PMI, or whole of life assurance
  • LTCI rider on CI: good — many critical illnesses (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, stroke) lead to LTC need
  • LTCI rider on PMI: natural — PMI covers acute; LTC rider covers chronic disability
  • LTCI rider on IP: less natural — no link between IP benefit level (based on salary) and LTC benefit needed
  • LTCI rider on whole of life: LTC benefits seen as accelerated death benefits — good conceptual fit

Tiered Benefits — Advantages & Disadvantages

✅ Advantages of Tiered Benefits
  • Earlier care at mild disability level → slows deterioration → better quality of life and lower long-run cost
  • Better fit to customer needs → more likely to sell well
  • Seems better value → increases appeal
  • Differentiates from competitors
  • Reduces incentive to exaggerate disability (partial benefit available at mild stages)
❌ Disadvantages of Tiered Benefits
  • Increased administration costs for insurer
  • More complex to price (data scarce for each severity level)
  • Higher cost of waiver of premium (more trigger events)
  • More difficult to sell — sellers may prefer simpler products
  • Risk of disputes over which tier applies
⚡
Immediate Needs Plans
Single-premium impaired life annuity · Bought when in need of care
🔑
Key Contrast: For pre-funded plans, poor health increases the premium (shorter premium-paying period, earlier claims). For immediate needs plans, poor health decreases the premium (shorter expected annuity payment period — less total benefit expected).
📋 Core Features
  • Purchased by people already in need of LTC — not a pre-funded product
  • Protects against uncertain survival duration — longevity risk
  • Single premium → guaranteed lifetime income
  • Premium individually calculated based on health status of applicant
  • Effectively an impaired life annuity (not indemnity — provides fixed cash amounts)
  • Extended from nursing homes to domiciliary care in modern markets
  • Policyholder always receives some benefit — no risk of zero benefit (unlike pre-funded)
  • No surrender value — cannot exit the contract
💸 Benefit Structure
  • Level benefit or escalating at fixed rate (often 5% p.a.) or linked to inflation index + perhaps 2% p.a.
  • Care is primarily staff-based → costs should rise with average earnings over long term
  • Some plans pre-agreed escalation with specific nursing homes — insurer not responsible for quality of care or care home failure
  • Benefits can differ for nursing home vs home care
💀 Death Benefit Options
  • Minimum payment period: Benefits paid for minimum period even if PH dies (e.g. 2 years)
  • Amortising the single premium: Premium spread over N years; death benefit = unreturned portion (e.g. 4-year spread → 25% returned if dies after year 3)
  • Capital protection: Part of premium returned on death (e.g. unit-linked: proportion of premium not yet used as benefit returned on death)
  • Higher death benefit selected → less sensitive to health status at inception (benefit payable regardless of survival duration)
  • Trade-off: more death benefit = less of the premium available to fund annuity income
🏗️ Product Structures
  • Not just simple impaired-life annuities — structures include: pure endowments, purchased life annuities, disability covers
  • Structure chosen to maximise tax efficiency and minimise capital requirements for the insurer and policyholder
  • Life annuity provides continuing benefit on survival; series of pure endowments also achieves this
  • Disability cover (like IP with different definition) also meets the need
  • Actuary's role: design product that meets needs at lowest cost by exploiting tax reliefs and minimising capital

Worked Example: Alice's Immediate Needs Solution (SP1 Ch3 Q3.2)

Alice, aged 78, post-stroke, needs £18,000 pa net. She has £75,000 capital. What risk does she face and what solution is appropriate? ▶
Risk Identification
  • Immediate residual care cost: £25,000 (nursing home) − £7,000 (State + occupational pension) = £18,000 p.a.
  • £75,000 invested at 5% = £3,750 income p.a. — far below £18,000 needed
  • Solving: 18,000 × ä₇₈ = 75,000 → ä₇₈ ≈ 4.17 → capital runs out in approximately 4.75 years
  • Probability of Alice (post-stroke) surviving 5+ years ≈ 30% → significant longevity risk
  • Risk: living too long so capital exhausted, unable to maintain lifestyle, may need cheaper care home, burdens family
Immediate Needs Solution
  • Using impaired mortality (ELT15 + loadings: +0.35 in year 1, +0.10 thereafter) → ä₇₈ ≈ 3.75
  • After insurer loading of ~10%: £10,000 single premium buys ≈ £2,425 p.a. annuity
  • £75,000 buys approximately £18,200 p.a. — neatly covering the £18,000 shortfall
  • This eliminates longevity risk — Alice's income is guaranteed for life regardless of how long she survives
  • Consider adding modest escalation to allow for care cost inflation
⚠️
Common Objection & Response: "Buy fixed-interest bonds directly and cut out insurer expenses/profit — then leave remainder to heirs on death." Rebuttal: This ignores the core purpose of the annuity — pooling longevity risk. Without insurance, Alice bears the risk of outliving her capital. No assets are left to bequeath if care continues. The insurer's bulk-buying power on bonds may also give better returns than individual investment, and the individual may not have the expertise to select optimal bonds.

Unit-Linked LTCI Plans

💡
Design Rationale: People would buy savings plans but avoided thinking about LTCI. Solution: sell it primarily as a flexible investment (often an inheritance plan held in trust) — with LTCI protection as a secondary feature. Death benefit makes it more attractive than pure protection plans where all premiums are "lost" on death.
⚙️ How Unit-Linked LTCI Works
  • Single premium (most common) → allocated to unit fund
  • Monthly risk charges deducted from unit fund → non-unit fund → covers LTC protection cost
  • Also: fund management charges, policy admin charges
  • Death/surrender: bid value of units from unit fund
  • LTC benefits: primarily from non-unit fund (protection element); may also use unit fund (depending on protection level selected)
  • Often placed in discretionary trust — inheritance plan structure: trust beneficiaries receive fund on death; policyholder has contingent claim for LTC
🛡️ Fund Protection Options
  • Full fund protection: Entire fund (initial + growth) protected; LTC benefits paid entirely from non-unit fund; minimum fixed deferred period applies; highest risk charges
  • Initial investment protection: Only initial premium protected; growth is available to meet some LTC benefit before non-unit fund used
  • No protection (exhaustion): Unit fund pays LTC benefits first until exhausted; non-unit fund only kicks in after fund exhausted; lowest risk charges
  • From insurer's view: fund protection choice = varying the effective deferred period
📐 Effective Deferred Period Calculation
  • Effective deferred period = fixed minimum deferred period + extra period while fund covers benefit
  • Example: Single premium £10,000; benefit £300/week; fund grown to £35,200; initial investment protected
  • Available for benefit: £35,200 − £10,000 = £25,200
  • Fund pays for: 25,200 / 300 = 84 additional weeks
  • Effective deferred period: 84 + 26 (fixed) = 110 weeks
  • Risk charges increase as policyholder ages and as benefit escalates (fund may be exhausted faster than expected)
🔒 Guarantees on Unit-Linked Plans
  • No guarantee: If fund exhausted before death and before claim, cover lapses (no more risk charges can be deducted) unless policyholder tops up
  • Guarantee from fixed age (e.g. 90): If fund positive at age 90, no further risk charges — insurer accepts ongoing longevity risk
  • Full guarantee with restricted investment: Insurer accepts fund exhaustion risk; investment restricted to managed/with-profits fund; moves toward middle ground between stand-alone and investment solution
  • Complexity is a significant barrier to sales — considerable ongoing financial advice required

Pension-Based Solutions

🏛️ Rationale for Pension Link
  • LTCI has natural link with pensions — both address post-retirement needs
  • Standard pension annuity may be inappropriate for extreme old age — fixed income doesn't recognise extra costs of care
  • Pension annuity problem: surviving spouse pension often reduced; further eroded by inflation
  • Original occupational pension designed for ~10 years of retirement with level income — not today's 30+ year retirements with potential LTC needs
  • UK: pensioners no longer required to purchase annuity → they may have a capital pot for LTCI purchase
🏗️ Pension-LTCI Design
  • Pseudo-pension annuity solution: Conventional immediate annuity from pension fund + rider LTCI benefit that increases pension income at disability trigger
  • Benefit = impaired deferred life annuity — deferred period ends at triggering event (disability/age)
  • LTC rider premium: from tax-free lump sum (single premium) OR from ongoing pension income (regular premium)
  • Regular premiums stop at earlier of: death, or triggering of LTC benefit
  • Challenge: initial pension may be reduced to fund LTC cover, relative to pre-retirement salary

Pricing & Assumptions
Key assumptions · Rating factors · Asset-liability matching
⚠️
Data Warning: LTCI suffers from severe data scarcity. Disability incidence, recovery rates, cognitive impairment rates and care cost inflation are all uncertain. This means large contingency loadings are required — making guaranteed premium products expensive and potentially unmarketable.
📊 Key Pricing Assumptions
  • Morbidity (disability incidence): Probability of failing required number of ADLs by age — scarce data; must use population statistics plus reinsurer data
  • Mortality: Mortality of both healthy and disabled lives — life table adjustments for impaired lives; also affects premium payment period
  • Recovery rates: Probability of recovering from LTC (improving → re-entering healthy state); relevant for multi-state models
  • Benefit escalation: Rate at which benefits increase (fixed vs linked); care cost inflation highly uncertain
  • Expenses: High admin costs; claims assessment costs; care management services
  • Investment return / discount rate: For discounting benefit stream; matching adjustment may be available for immediate needs block
  • Lapse/persistency: Voluntary lapses; potential for anti-selective lapsing if premiums rise
⚖️ Rating Factors (Pre-Funded)
  • Age at entry — primary factor; older → shorter premium-paying period, earlier expected claim
  • Health status / functional ability — medical questionnaire; pre-existing conditions
  • Benefit amount — larger benefit → higher premium proportionally
  • Deferred period — longer → lower premium
  • Benefit escalation rate — higher escalation → higher premium
  • Benefit payment period — lifetime vs fixed term
  • Policy term — whole life vs to fixed age
  • Guarantee type — guaranteed vs reviewable premiums
  • Gender — if permitted; women have longer expected disability duration
🧮 Modelling Approaches
  • Multi-state models — most appropriate: Healthy → Mildly disabled → Severely disabled → Dead; allows transitions between states and recovery
  • Transition intensities estimated from experience data at each age
  • Deterministic cashflow model: Project benefit payments by duration, applying probability of being in each state
  • Stochastic model: Allows for uncertainty in incidence, duration, recovery rates
  • Sensitivity analysis essential — assumptions are very uncertain
  • For immediate needs: simpler — project annuity cashflows using impaired mortality table
🏦 Asset-Liability Matching (IAI 1119 Q1iv)
  • Pre-funded liabilities (premium-paying phase): Long duration — fixed-interest bonds of matching term; cash for short-duration liabilities
  • Benefits increasing at 3% p.a. (fixed): Index-linked gilts; provides real return matching benefit escalation
  • Benefits linked to general inflation: Index-linked gilts (inflation linkers); or equities/property for long-run real return (higher risk/volatility)
  • Expense liabilities (staff-based): Assets linked to wage inflation
  • Currency matching: Assets in same currency as liabilities; overseas policies may require overseas investments
  • Immediate needs block: Fixed-interest bonds for level benefits; index-linked for escalating; matching adjustment may be available (Solvency II) if cash flows predictable and assets ring-fenced
📐 Matching Adjustment (IAI 1222 Q1iv)
  • Conditions for MA: (1) Regulator approval; (2) Long-term predictable liabilities — no surrender; (3) Key risks: longevity and expenses only; (4) Assets ring-fenced; (5) No early redemption; (6) Assets matched by nature, term, currency
  • Calculation example: 50% of assets earn +1.50% additional yield; default risk 0.50%
  • MA = 50% × (1.50% − 0.50%) = 0.50% p.a.
  • LTCI immediate needs business is well-suited to MA: fixed predictable cash flows, no policyholder surrender, longevity risk pooled
📉 New Business Strain
  • Even for single premium LTCI (immediate needs), new business strain can arise if supervisory reserves are on a stronger basis than the premium basis
  • Solvency capital requirement adds to strain beyond reserves
  • For regular premium pre-funded: strain arises because initial expenses (commission, underwriting, admin) exceed first premium
  • Reviewable premium products carry less capital strain than guaranteed premium products
  • Capital strain particularly important for new/small LTCI insurers with limited free assets

EV for Unit-Linked LTCI (IAI 1222 Q1v)

EV methodology for acquired unit-linked LTCI block ▶
  • Net assets: Total assets in excess of technical provisions + SCR (Solvency II). 100% of net assets included (shareholder-owned).
  • PVIF (Present Value of In-Force): PV of future shareholder cashflows = release of risk margin + release of SCR, less frictional costs (tax, agency costs).
  • Projection model: Must project both unit fund AND non-unit fund cashflows (non-unit cashflows depend on unit fund size at claim trigger).
  • Assumptions needed: Investment returns, morbidity (disability incidence/recovery), mortality, expenses, lapses, inflation, tax.
  • Top-up premiums: Treated as in-force (if reasonable to assume ongoing contributions) or as new business (if uncertain). Contract boundary applies under Solvency II but EV treatment may differ.

Underwriting & Claims Management
Acceptance · ADL assessment · Ongoing monitoring
📋 Pre-Funded: Initial Underwriting
  • Proposal form — age, health status, functional ability, family history of dementia/long-term conditions
  • Further medical evidence if any adverse indicators: GP report, specialist report, medical examination
  • Financial underwriting: Benefit level should not exceed expected costs of care (prevents over-insurance windfall)
  • If benefit level much higher than actual care costs → temptation to claim → financial underwriting required
  • Key aim: ensure premium reflects risk; prevent adverse selection by those already experiencing early deterioration
  • Guaranteed premium products require most stringent underwriting (no opportunity to revise)
  • Reviewable premium products allow more lenient initial underwriting
⚡ Immediate Needs: Underwriting
  • Detailed individual medical evidence collected — health status directly determines premium
  • Functional ability tests / ADL assessment to establish level of disability
  • Indicative quote first (based on brief questions), then full underwriting if proposal proceeds
  • Decision must be made quickly — applicant is already in need of care; delays are unacceptable
  • Medical examinations should be kept to a minimum — unnecessary cost and inconvenience at a difficult time
  • GP/consultant report used to confirm health status and likely prognosis
  • Adverse health → lower premium (shorter expected payment period)
🩺 Claim Assessment
  • Assessment by independent assessor (not insurer's own staff) — preserves objectivity; policyholder confidence
  • ADL assessments: functional ability tests — structured for objectivity but subjective elements remain (e.g. no specification of exact stair count)
  • Cognitive impairment assessed separately — standardised tests; overriding trigger if satisfied
  • Important to assess: cannot perform the activity vs chooses not to perform it — moral hazard risk
  • Waiting period in claim definition: disability must persist for specified period (not one-off event) before benefit triggers
  • Care manager / advocate may be appointed to help policyholder navigate care choices
🔄 Ongoing Claims Monitoring
  • Periodic reassessment of ADL status — frequency depends on nature of disability
  • Some conditions are clearly permanent (e.g. severe Alzheimer's) — less frequent reassessment
  • Others may improve (e.g. post-stroke) — regular monitoring required
  • Care manager reviews care plan and expenditure (for indemnity policies)
  • Monitor for: recovery (claim termination), change in disability level (tiered benefit adjustment), death
  • Insurer may offer rehabilitation services to slow deterioration
  • Assistive devices provided early to defer progression to higher care levels

Risks to the Insurer
Longevity · Morbidity · Inflation · Political · Moral hazard
📈 Longevity Risk
  • Policyholders surviving longer than expected → more benefit payments than priced
  • Critical for immediate needs plans: annuity payments continue longer than expected single premium covers
  • Mortality improvements are hard to predict — especially at very old ages (80+) where improvements are accelerating
  • Pooled across portfolio but systematic longevity improvement cannot be diversified away
  • Reinsurance can transfer longevity risk; longevity swaps also available in some markets
🧬 Morbidity / Disability Risk
  • More policyholders claiming than expected (higher disability incidence)
  • Claims lasting longer than expected (lower recovery rates)
  • Progression to higher ADL failure levels faster than expected
  • Cognitive impairment rates rising with ageing population
  • Data is scarce — largely population-based, not insured-population-based
  • Medical advances can work both ways: cure some conditions (↓ incidence) but extend life with disability (↑ duration)
💷 Care Cost Inflation Risk
  • Care costs are primarily staff-based → driven by wage inflation, not just price inflation
  • Rising demand for carers (ageing population) + limited supply → wage growth in care sector may exceed general earnings growth
  • New care technologies and practices may increase cost per care episode
  • Cash benefit policies: insurer bears fixed cash risk (no direct exposure to care inflation) but policyholder bears this risk
  • Indemnity policies: insurer bears full care inflation risk — very difficult to price and reserve for
  • Key reason most UK insurers offer cash rather than indemnity benefits
🏛️ Political / Regulatory Risk
  • State may increase its provision → demand for LTCI falls; existing policyholders may lapse
  • State may decrease provision → claims on insurer increase if policyholders expected State to supplement
  • State could charge for nursing care (previously free) → insurer faces unexpected additional claims cost
  • Regulator may insist insurer provides more than intended (e.g. interpret "care" broadly) → margin required in pricing
  • Tax treatment changes (premiums or benefits) → affects demand and benefit levels
  • Contract design must be robust to changes in State provision
🎭 Moral Hazard
  • ADL definitions leave room for value judgements — policyholders may exaggerate disability
  • High benefit → reduced incentive to stay active or attempt recovery
  • Cash benefits used for non-care purposes (holiday, gift to relatives) — insurer has no control
  • Family may exploit cash payments; inadequate care provided to elderly person
  • Over-insurance: benefit exceeds actual care costs → windfall effect → incentive to claim
  • Managed by: independent assessment, financial underwriting, benefit limits, deferred periods
🎯 Anti-Selection
  • Those expecting to need care soon (early deterioration not yet diagnosable) may select in
  • Selective lapsing: if premiums rise, healthier lives lapse; sicker lives retain → portfolio worsens → further premium rises needed → spiral
  • Managed by: underwriting at inception, capping premium increases, actively-at-work criteria
  • Reviewable premiums create anti-selection spiral risk if market allows switching
  • Voluntary group schemes carry higher anti-selection than compulsory schemes
💾 Data & Pricing Risk
  • Limited insured-lives experience data globally — must rely on population statistics
  • Published morbidity tables may be out of date rapidly (changing care patterns, new conditions)
  • Transition intensities between health states particularly sparse
  • Reinsurer assistance is critical — provides mortality/morbidity data and pricing support
  • Large contingency loadings required → makes guaranteed premiums expensive → limits marketability
  • Data problem worse for new markets (e.g. developing countries launching LTCI)
💼 Insurer Operational Risks
  • Systems risk: Complex multi-state models, ongoing claim monitoring, ADL tracking
  • Reputation risk: If cash benefits perceived as inadequate for care costs → policyholder dissatisfaction, complaints
  • Mis-selling risk: Policyholders believe they are buying "care" not "cash" → unrealistic expectations
  • Provider risk: For indemnity plans, care home quality/failure affects claims
  • Expense risk: LTCI has high ongoing admin costs vs other H&C products
  • Investment risk: Mismatch between assets and long-duration, inflation-linked liabilities

SP1 Ch3 Q3.1
Comment on the advice: "Buy fixed-interest investments directly instead of an immediate needs annuity — cut out insurer expenses; bequeath remainder on death."
▾
Main Rebuttal — Longevity Risk
  • An annuity is insurance against the financial consequences of living too long. Without it, the individual bears all longevity risk
  • A person in LTC cannot supplement income when savings run out — they may have to change to a cheaper care home at the worst possible time
  • Rather than leaving money to relatives, you may end up relying on relatives to fund your care
Additional Points
  • Insurer's admin expenses and profit are offset by pooling of longevity risk — those who die early subsidise those who live long
  • Individual buying bonds directly may have higher dealing costs and less expertise in selecting optimal bonds
  • Insurer may hold a proportion in equities/property/index-linked gilts (not just fixed-interest) — potentially offering better returns than individual bond investment
  • On death, fixed-interest bonds may have positive value — but if you're alive and funds exhausted, there's nothing left
SP1 Ch3 Q3.2
Alice, aged 78, post-stroke, needs £18,000 p.a. extra. She has £75,000 savings. Describe her risk and suggest an immediate needs solution.
▾
Alice receives £7,000 p.a. net (State + occupational pension). Nursing home costs £25,000 p.a. Assume: post-stroke mortality loading +0.35 (year 1) and +0.10 (thereafter) above ELT15; discount rate 5%.
Risk Quantification
  • Annual shortfall: £25,000 − £7,000 = £18,000 p.a. needed
  • £75,000 at 5% = £3,750 income p.a. — far insufficient
  • Self-funding: capital runs out in ~4.75 years
  • Using impaired mortality from ELT15 (Female): survival probability after 5 years ≈ 30% → significant longevity risk
Immediate Needs Solution
  • Impaired life annuity using enhanced mortality: ä₇₈ (impaired) ≈ 3.75
  • After 10% expense/profit loading: £10,000 buys ~£2,425 p.a.
  • £75,000 buys approximately £18,200 p.a. — matches the shortfall
  • This eliminates longevity risk completely for Alice
  • Consider modest escalation to match care cost inflation
SP1 Ch7 Q7.5 13 marks
Design a pre-funded LTCI product covering home and nursing-home care, meeting the needs of all stakeholders. Cover benefit structure, premiums, and claim definitions.
▾
Benefit Structure
  • Main benefit: indemnity-based covering actual care costs subject to specified limits (e.g. max £X per year per ADL failure level)
  • Cash alternative: fixed regular income — avoids exposure to care cost inflation but transfers risk to policyholder
  • Two benefit levels: lower for home care; higher for nursing home care
  • Benefits for life subject to ongoing disability; alternatively fixed 3-year period complementing State provision
  • Benefit escalation linked to inflation index; insurer may accept or limit this
  • Additional: assistive devices benefit (fixed months), independent care advice at claim, respite care for informal carers
  • Death benefit: possible; adds cost; single premium return or minimum payment period
  • Guarantee vs reviewable: guaranteed from advanced age (e.g. 75) — limits additional cost while providing policyholder security
Premiums
  • Regular premiums with waiver during claim; option for single premium
  • Reviewable premiums most likely given uncertain claims experience
  • Premiums linked to benefit escalation rate; stop at e.g. age 85
  • Actuarially fair rating by age, health, benefit level, deferred period, escalation, payment period
Claim Definitions
  • Clear, unambiguous criteria — e.g. inability to perform N out of 6 ADLs (washing, dressing, feeding, toileting, mobility, transferring)
  • Mental impairment overriding trigger (Alzheimer's, organic dementia)
  • Number of ADLs required for benefit to trigger: calibrate to limit windfall and ensure genuine need
  • Assessment by independent body (not insurer) — builds policyholder trust
  • Consistent with competitors to limit anti-selection and regulatory scrutiny
SP1 Ch6 Q6.2 5 marks
Give an example of LTCI with indemnity benefits and a similar product with non-indemnity benefits. How can indemnity be limited while maintaining appeal to consumers?
▾
Indemnity vs Non-Indemnity Examples
  • Indemnity: Policy pays for the cost of care in an approved nursing home, covering whatever care is assessed as needed (subject to limits) — restores policyholder to pre-claim position financially
  • Non-indemnity: Policy pays £500 per week when claim conditions are met — fixed regardless of actual care costs incurred. May over- or under-compensate
Limiting Indemnity While Remaining Attractive
  • Deferred period: No benefits for initial 3–12 months — policyholder funds this; still seen as reasonable since initial care can be informal/own resources
  • Maximum benefit period: 3–5 years of full care benefits, then State provision assumed — acceptable if probability of longer need is perceived as low
  • Annual monetary cap: Indemnity up to e.g. £30,000/year — partial indemnity; still valuable protection against most scenarios
  • Reviewable premiums: Maintains affordability by adjusting to experience
  • Limiting coverage age: Benefits cease at e.g. 85 — reduces tail risk for insurer
