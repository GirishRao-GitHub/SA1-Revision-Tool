① Data Criteria & General Considerations
4 Core Criteria for Data
  • Relevant — must relate to a similar class of lives and policy conditions to those expected for the product in question
  • Credible — sufficient volume of data to enable precise estimates with relatively little sampling error
  • Available — accessible to the actuary in time for the exercise
  • Reliable — fit for the purpose intended; trustworthy source
Also consider: cost of obtaining & analysing data · suitability of format
Judgement & Adjustment
  • Combining sources: actuary uses judgement to compile statistics from one or more sources that together meet the criteria
  • Adjustments always needed: chosen data body will likely require adjustment for the specific purpose — not a precise methodology; broad-based ratios initially
  • Improves with experience: more accurate allowances possible once insurer has significant own experience data
  • New products / markets: relevant experience data typically scarce; US PMI is the exception (very well established)
Actuary projects data forward using skill & judgement to estimate future claim experience
Key Point: The most important premium component is the pure risk cost (anticipated claims). Getting data right is therefore critical. For health & care products, this is harder than for most life insurance products — greater reliance on external sources is the norm, not the exception.
② Projection to Mid-Point of Insurance Usage
Why & How Far to Project
  • Database is historic — assumptions must stay valid across the full life of policies written under the current pricing basis
  • Two horizons to bridge: (1) the shelf-life of the rates (how long before rates are next revised), and (2) the expected policy duration to termination or next premium review date
  • Project forward to the average mid-point — i.e. ½ × [shelf-life of proposed premium rates] + ½ × [expected duration to termination / review date]
  • Greater uncertainty → larger margins needed in assumptions; longer projection horizon = more uncertainty = higher cost
The actuary must gather information on all local market trends & developments to support projections
Projection by Assumption Type
  • Claim inception rates — vary by age and calendar year; project using annual improvement/deterioration factors applied to each policy year
  • Claim termination rates — vary by duration from claim commencement; allow for improvement or deterioration over time
  • PMI average claim size — function of medical inflation, treatment protocols, hospital charges, State provision; reviewed annually → much shorter projection horizon needed
Short review cycles benefit: smaller uncertainty margins → cheaper premiums → more competitive; smaller reserves → less capital required. Contract should allow future review where there is significant uncertainty.
③ Own Data
Why Own Data is Best
  • Relevance is the key advantage — reflects the insurer's specific approach to underwriting, claims and distribution, which external data cannot capture
  • No cost or format problems — data is held internally
  • Company's own experience is generally the best base for prediction of future experience
Own data = high relevance, typically low credibility — the opposite of population data
5 Relevance Factors (unique to each insurer)
  • Underwriting & acceptance: strictness determines health profile of accepted lives
  • Policy conditions: definition of claim, benefit triggers, exclusions differ across companies
  • Claims management: approach to investigating, paying and contesting claims
  • Distribution method & channels: IFA vs direct vs bancassurance attracts different customer types
  • Target market: occupational class mix, geographic distribution, age profile
Credibility Problem & Workarounds
  • Volume insufficient for most H&C lines, especially at individual risk-cell level
  • Workaround 1: express assumption as a simple adjustment to a standard (external) table — fit adjustment parameter statistically to own data
  • Workaround 2: weight low-credibility cells using market data published at the same level of detail
  • New insurer: totally reliant on external sources for years; credibility builds slowly; high-level analysis only initially
④ Population Data
What It Is & Credibility
  • Source: national government periodic analyses of healthcare experience for the population
  • Degree of sophistication varies enormously by country; availability is not universal
  • Credibility: HIGH — large numbers involved; often the only available first estimate for some risk cells
  • Cost: free and readily available in many territories
For some risk cells, population data may be the only base from which a first estimate of future costs can be established
Drawbacks: Format & Accuracy
  • Accuracy & reliability questionable — especially in H&C where definitions are subjective and double-counting is significant. E.g. CI: heart attack + bypass surgery = 1 claim but 2 population records — overlap must be removed
  • Format problems — data may not be available electronically or in appropriate format for actuarial use
  • Out-of-date — population data often takes years to compile and publish; may be stale on arrival
Relevance Issues & Adjustments
  • Class of lives: national experience ≠ insured lives (different health profiles, demographics). Improve by splitting data by socio-economic group and region — insurer's policyholders' occupations link to socio-economic class; regional distribution affects morbidity rates
  • Claim definition: population 'sickness' definition will differ from the insurance policy's claim trigger — requires careful adjustment before use
  • No universal adjustment rule — the appropriate adjustment depends on the specific product, market and insurer's practices
Anti-Selection Nuance (Exam Favourite)
Underwriting effect: insured lives are healthier than the general population — declined or loaded lives removed → expect better experience than population data suggests.
Countervailing effect: if the policy is non-essential, people choose to buy insurance partly because they expect to claim (adverse selection / moral hazard). This offsets the underwriting improvement — particularly evident in CI cover in many markets.
  • Actuaries may apply only a small discount to population data; validity depends on sales, marketing, underwriting & claims philosophy
⑤ Reinsurers' Data
Why Reinsurers Provide Data
  • Service motivation: reinsurer's profits under a treaty depend on the accuracy of the insurance premiums → strong incentive to help price correctly
  • Payment mechanism: normally provided in return for a share of the business (reinsurance treaty) rather than a direct fee
  • Treaty is typical: it is almost invariably the case that a reinsurance treaty is set up — this is the usual means by which the insurer pays the reinsurer for data services received
Contrast with actuarial consultants: similar data access but charge a fee and do not share in the risk
Relevance & Adjustments Needed
  • Assess divergence from company's own likely future experience, even if most risk is ceded
  • Adjust for: company's specific underwriting approach and claims management practices — these may not be known to the reinsurer
  • But: if the reinsurer is taking on a proportion of the risk, it will be very interested in the underwriting and claims process (directly impacts its own risk)
Credibility & Classic Use Case
  • Credibility: reinsurers draw from broad market involvement — excellent position to assist product launches
  • New products: credibility is low initially; grows as the market for that product develops and reinsurers accumulate own treaty experience
  • Classic example — CI: reinsurers drove CI development in many territories in the 1980s; no insured experience existed → used weighted overseas insured data + local population data
  • Established products: reinsurers increasingly rely on own treaty experience → more accurate data

Insured Lives Data — Detail (e.g. CMI in UK)
  • Mechanism: agreement between companies to pool claims & policy statistics → industry-wide data more credible than any individual insurer's data
  • Output: standard morbidity tables (used as base reference for experience monitoring & assumption-setting)
  • Standard tables infrequent: great deal of preparation involved; pooled experience expressed as adjustment to most recent standard until a new one is warranted
  • Key advantages: represents insured experience · credible volume · reflects local market practices · compiled by experts (removes company-specific glitches)
  • Key limitation: market average only — not specific to any one company; actuary must consider how own experience differs and adjust accordingly
  • Underwriting translation caution: strict life UW ratios may not translate directly to morbidity ratios for IP/CI business
PMI insured lives data: very limited except at a high level (unlike CI and IP)
Overseas Data — Bridging Method & Caveats
Bridging formula (broad approach):
Estimated home insured experience ≈ overseas insured experience × (home population rates ÷ overseas population rates)
  • Always less relevant than equivalent home territory data, even where insured
  • Key differences to allow for: culture · State healthcare provision (directly affects insurance experience) · market practices · legislation · policy conditions
  • Starting point only — further adjustments almost certainly required
Actuarial Consultants vs Reinsurers: similar data access; key difference is that consultants charge a direct fee and do not share in the insurance risk. Reinsurers are compensated via the treaty.
