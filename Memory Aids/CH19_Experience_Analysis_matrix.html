2 · Mortality & Morbidity Rates
Mortality In-claim vs. pre-claim; impaired lives; improving trends; impact on reserves
  • Investigate mortality of claimants in payment (affects claim termination) and pre-claimants (affects exposure)
  • Economic conditions and changes in interest rates may affect value of claims-in-payment reserves
Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Improving mortality means more lives reaching age of potential CI claim → increased incidence exposure
  • Longer survival post-claim possible as treatment improves; may affect reinsurance cost
Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
  • Impaired mortality rates (in-claim) and mortality rates pre-claim both require investigation
  • Improving mortality = more lives reaching care age AND longer claim durations; impaired mortality may also reduce, extending claims
  • Estimation of future mortality trends required (Country B likely has improving mortality)
  • Guaranteed return-of-premiums on death may have been much higher than expected
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Mortality not a primary driver; however, catastrophic events (flooding, pandemic) can cause temporary spikes
  • Severe flooding may worsen existing conditions, indirectly affecting claim frequency
Sep 2023 Q1(ii)
Morbidity – inception rates Claim triggers; deferred period; incapacity definition; economic cycle effects; anti-selection
  • Investigate changes in incapacity definitions and deferred periods as key drivers of inception rate
  • Recession → people more likely to bring forward a claim
  • Changes in cause of claim (e.g. rise in mental health/stress) can increase inception and reduce recovery
  • Investigate early claims / claims by notification date to detect anti-selection
Sep 2024 Q3(i,iii) · Sep 2024 Q1(ii)
  • Disease X pandemic may bring forward CI inception (acceleration effect) or cause increased incidence
  • Introduction of new or expanded conditions may alter observed inception rates
  • Only 50–60% of CI claims result in payment; poor definitions = low effective incidence
  • Anti-selection: non-disclosure of smoker status / lifestyle worsens incidence
Apr 2021 Q2(ii) · Apr 2025 Q1(ii)
  • Morbidity incidence rates central to investigation; must be analysed by age, gender, condition and care setting
  • Changes in policyholder behaviour and claims eligibility criteria (e.g. ADL definitions) can alter incidence
  • Introduction of screening programmes may bring forward claims
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Increased flooding frequency causes spikes in PMI claim incidence, with strong regional concentration
  • Disruption to state healthcare (longer waiting times) shifts demand to PMI, raising incidence
  • New disease, pandemic or catastrophe can cause unexpected incidence surge
Sep 2023 Q1(ii) · Apr 2022 Q2(i)
Morbidity – termination / recovery rates Recovery; mortality in-claim; rehabilitation; cause mix; managed care interventions
  • Lower recovery rates a key driver of worsening IP loss ratio; investigate cause of claims (mental health → lower recovery)
  • Claims management team intervention can improve return-to-work and reduce duration
  • Partial, proportional and rehabilitation benefits affect effective termination rate
  • Recovery rates may correlate with sick-pay absence data in policyholder's geographic region
Sep 2024 Q3(i,ii,iii) · Sep 2022 Q2(v)
  • CI is a lump-sum benefit; no in-payment termination rate as such, but survival post-claim affects any linked benefit
  • Time-to-pay claims increased significantly (14 days → 72 days); operational not biometric, but affects policyholder outcome
Apr 2025 Q1(ii)
  • Recovery rates likely immaterial for LTCI (care needs rarely reverse); may not even be recorded
  • Impaired mortality rate in-claim effectively determines termination; investigate trends carefully
  • Cultural shifts (home care → care homes) change average claim cost and duration
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • PMI claims are acute episodes; termination is natural end of treatment
  • Claims may take longer to settle during flooding as complexity increases
  • Provider supply disruption (flood-damaged facilities) can extend treatment duration and cost
Sep 2023 Q1(ii)
3 · Claims Amounts & Costs
Average claim amount Indemnity vs. fixed benefit; benefit inflation; care cost inflation; medical inflation
  • Higher claim amounts driven by claim escalation rates; investigate whether escalation assumptions hold
  • Replacement ratio limits and benefit maximums affect actual amount paid
  • Higher inflation increases salary-linked benefit amounts and long-term liability
Sep 2024 Q3(i) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Fixed lump-sum benefit means short-term claim amount is fixed, but real value erodes with inflation
  • Maximum CI benefits grew at ~1.3% p.a. real over 2005–2024; premium growth has lagged behind
  • Commission increases have pushed up premiums beyond inflation, reducing value to policyholders
Apr 2025 Q1(ii)
  • Indemnity-based LTCI: average claim amounts vary with cost of care, which must be investigated separately
  • Cost of care inflation (staff salaries, facility costs) is a key driver
  • Average claim cost also affected by mix of care settings (home vs. residential vs. nursing)
  • Investment income in part funds claim costs; lower-than-expected returns increase net cost
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Medical inflation higher than expected is a primary driver of worsening PMI experience
  • Higher medical salaries, new treatment protocols and new procedures push up cost per claim
  • Fixed fee schedule with providers limits cost variance and inflation risk
  • Aggregation risk: large individual claims (e.g. cancer, evacuation) can distort averages
Apr 2022 Q2(i,ii)
Claims inflation Medical vs. general inflation; provider pricing power; regulatory changes; treatment protocols
  • Higher general inflation increases salary-linked benefits and expenses; deferred period products more exposed over the long term
  • If risk mitigation (e.g. inflation swaps) has a limited duration, tail exposure remains
Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Inflation affects premium sustainability; where premiums are guaranteed, insurer bears the full risk
  • High inflation may trigger selective lapses of CI (healthy lives exit; impaired lives stay)
Sep 2024 Q1(i,ii)
  • Care cost inflation is distinct from general inflation; must be investigated separately
  • Reduction in state provision or means-testing thresholds increases private care demand and cost
  • Expense inflation also relevant to investigate
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Medical inflation higher than expected is the most common PMI adverse experience driver cited
  • Changes in treatment protocols, introduction of new procedures, reduction in provider competition all feed into medical inflation
  • Renegotiation of preferential hospital tariffs at higher rates is a discrete inflationary shock
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Sep 2019 Q3(ii)
4 · Lapse & Persistency Experience
Lapse rates Anti-selective lapsing; duration; premium size; economic cycle; distributor churning; surrender values
  • Selective lapses worsen if product no longer competitive; investigate by duration and cause
  • Recession: policyholders may lapse due to affordability; investigate whether healthy or impaired lives are lapsing
  • Lapse correlates with absence/sick-pay data by region (geographic approach)
Sep 2024 Q3(i) · Sep 2022 Q2(v)
  • Anti-selection: healthy lives more likely to lapse CI in economic downturn, leaving worse pool
  • Lapse and re-entry: policyholders may lapse to buy cheaper cover, incurring new underwriting
  • Distributor churning increases commission costs and creates lapses irrespective of policyholder need
  • Investigate lapse rates by duration in force, premium size, distribution channel
Apr 2022 Q1(i,ii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Lapse experience should be investigated by duration in force, premium payment method and premium size
  • Healthier lives lapsing worsens remaining pool; early lapses impair expense recovery
  • Guaranteed return-of-premium requests before eligible claim period have been higher than expected
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Lapse during waiting period and by policy duration are key metrics for a new PMI product
  • Non-renewal behaviour investigated as equivalent to lapse for annually-renewable PMI
  • Underwriting change that attracts different risk mix can interact with lapse behaviour
Sep 2022 Q3(v)
5 · Business Mix & Underwriting
Business mix Age; gender; occupation; socio-economic group; group vs. individual; distribution channel; geographic concentration
  • Demographic trends (age, gender, smoker) and occupation mix are key drivers of IP experience
  • Scheme size for group business affects free cover limits and risk concentration
  • Significant decline in new business raises average age and risk of remaining book
  • Loss of large group schemes with favourable experience distorts overall loss ratio
Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Mix change driven by new distributors, economic conditions or competitor actions can alter expected experience substantially
  • High advisor commission → churning → unstable business mix
  • Inflation may shift new business mix toward more affluent (lower risk) policyholders
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Apr 2025 Q1(ii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Profile of new business and volume of new business must be tracked; deviations from expected mix affect pricing adequacy
  • Distribution model change (e.g. shift to lower socio-economic area) worsens expected health outcomes
  • Geographic analysis: cost of care and exposure concentration vary by region
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Older lives, different occupation category, or higher group vs. individual proportion can all worsen experience
  • Selling through new distributors with a different client mix is a frequent cause of adverse PMI claims
  • Higher lapse/non-renewal by better risks leaves a more adverse pool
  • Geographic concentration (e.g. flood-prone regions) creates accumulation exposure
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Sep 2023 Q1(ii)
Underwriting experience Changes in underwriting standards; cost-benefit analysis; anti-selection; market comparison
  • Review underwriting criteria by occupation; tighten health underwriting; reduce free cover limits on groups
  • Review disability definitions; ensure clarity to limit disputes
  • Increase claim underwriting: require additional documentation or second opinions
  • Investigate how underwriting tool is implemented in practice; cost/benefit of changes
Sep 2024 Q3(ii) · Apr 2022 Q3(i,ii)
  • Underwriting levels adjusted during the year (e.g. loosened) leads to higher and earlier claims
  • Compare underwriting category against claims experience to assess if current policy is effective
  • Market investigation to ensure underwriting practice is not out of line with competitors
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Sep 2023 Q3(iv)
  • Underwriting policy may have weakened to keep up with competitors, increasing anti-selection risk
  • Genomic testing may allow anti-selection without disclosure; investigate any change in risk profile at inception
  • Medical practitioners and HR professionals useful sources for understanding occupational/medical trends
Apr 2021 Q3(iii) · Sep 2023 Q3(iv)
  • Investigate frequency of GP referrals and pre-authorisation requirements; these influence claim acceptance rate
  • Carry out cost/benefit analysis of current underwriting policy; check frequency of tools used (e.g. doctor's reports)
  • Declined claims provide information on where policy is unclear or affecting customer experience
  • Reinsurers or consultants may have market data for comparison
Sep 2023 Q3(iv) · Sep 2019 Q3(ii)
6 · Expenses & Investment
Expenses Acquisition; administration; claims management; one-off items; expense inflation
  • Setting up a claims management team: estimate staff numbers based on current and future claims; include salaries, bonuses, pension, NI, systems, offices
  • Specialist external opinion costs for complex cases
  • Higher general inflation increases fixed and variable operating expenses
Sep 2024 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Advisor commission has increased significantly; may drive churning and higher new business expenses
  • Increased lapsing raises admin costs for processing and retention activity
  • Fixed costs spread over fewer policies if book shrinks; economies of scale lost
Apr 2025 Q1(ii) · Apr 2022 Q1(i)
  • Divide expenses into: acquisition, pre-claim administration, claim assessment, claims management
  • Consider whether one-off expenses should be excluded from the experience basis
  • Expense inflation must be investigated as a separate assumption
  • Lower new business volume increases per-policy expense loading
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Claims management expenses (pre-authorisation, case management, provider negotiation) are a significant PMI cost
  • Provider tariff renegotiation and managed care programmes affect total cost but also operational overhead
Sep 2019 Q3(ii)
Investment income / returns Asset matching; interest rate sensitivity; inflation linkage; reinsurance changes
  • Changes in interest rates may affect value of claims-in-payment reserves
  • High inflation may erode investment returns; inflation swaps or matched assets (index-linked bonds) may mitigate
Sep 2024 Q3(i) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Market volatility in recession reduces asset values; may require disinvestment at unfavourable prices to meet surrenders
  • Investment strategy review triggered by reduced premium income from lapsing policyholders
Apr 2022 Q1(viii,ix)
  • Investment income partly funds the cost of pre-funded LTCI claims; lower-than-expected returns require higher premiums
  • Recession, changes in investment strategy or economic shocks have been cited as causes of LTCI rate increases
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • PMI is typically short-tail so investment income is less material; however, counterparty risk on reinsurance must be monitored
Sep 2024 Q1(i)
7 · Reinsurance & External Benchmarks
Reinsurance Pricing changes; treaty renegotiation; counterparty risk; data availability from reinsurer
  • Reinsurer data and consultants may provide market benchmarks for loss ratios and claim durations
  • Introduce or review reinsurance if claim size or frequency experience is adverse
  • Get advice from reinsurer on claims management best practice
Sep 2024 Q3(ii) · Sep 2023 Q3(iv)
  • Reinsurance premiums reduce if book shrinks from lapses, but reinsurer may reprice per policy for selective lapse risk
  • Introduce reinsurance if affordable to cap large CI claim impact
Apr 2022 Q1(i) · Apr 2022 Q2(ii)
  • Reinsurer data, market data and population data are key supplementary sources when own LTCI data is limited
  • Reinsurer may adjust rates upward based on emerging LTCI market experience
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Reinsurance can be introduced to cap large or aggregated PMI claims
  • Counterparty risk on reinsurance must be monitored, especially if reinsurer is exposed to same geographic risks
Apr 2022 Q2(ii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
Comparison to expected & market benchmarks Actual vs. pricing assumptions; industry data; market comparison; identifying reasons for deviation
  • For all factors, actual experience should be compared with pricing assumptions and reasons for any difference identified
  • Compare loss ratio against competitor norms; industry claim ratios provide a guide to expected benefit of interventions
Sep 2024 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Claims payout ratio (50–60%) far below market expectation; investigate whether definitions or claims management explain the gap
  • Regularly check competitor products to ensure terms and premiums are not out of line with market
Apr 2025 Q1(ii) · Apr 2022 Q1(ii)
  • Compare actual morbidity incidence, claim costs and lapse rates to pricing assumptions
  • Use reinsurer, market and population data as external benchmarks where own data is insufficient for credibility
Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Claims ratio (claims paid / gross premium) tracked against expected; analyse reasons for deviation across frequency, amount and mix dimensions
  • Experience analysis used to determine whether actions on product design, underwriting or provider management are needed
Sep 2022 Q3(v) · Apr 2022 Q2(i,ii)
8 · Actions Following Experience Review
Product & pricing actions Rate increases; benefit redesign; deductibles; co-payments; reviewable vs. guaranteed premiums
  • Increase premium rates or implement more granular rating
  • Amend terms: reduce maximum replacement ratio; increase waiting/deferred periods; introduce partial or rehabilitation benefits
  • Review and tighten policy wording; improve fraud detection
  • Introduce rehabilitation services to reduce claim duration
Sep 2024 Q3(ii)
  • Consider reviewable premiums to allow ongoing pricing response
  • Add in supplementary benefits (cheap to provide, valued by customers) to reduce lapse incentive
  • Reduce or remove surrender benefits to minimise lapse incentives
  • Price in a payment holiday option; offer reduced sum assured to retain policyholders
Apr 2022 Q1(ii)
  • Substantial premium rate increase may be required; consider phased approach or amended benefit terms to limit reputational impact
  • Review care cost inflation assumptions; review investment strategy to improve return
  • May need to amend policy terms for existing business (subject to legal review)
Apr 2021 Q3(iv)
  • Adjust product design: introduce or increase excesses, co-payments, deductibles
  • Limit number of treatments (e.g. physiotherapy visits); cap indemnity with monetary limits
  • Restrict hospitals covered; exclude high-frequency conditions from standard product
  • Offer a waiting list plan where state services could be used
Sep 2019 Q3(ii) · Apr 2022 Q2(ii)
Claims management actions Managed care; provider negotiation; case management; fraud detection; pre-authorisation
  • Set up dedicated claim management / rehabilitation team to improve return-to-work rates
  • Estimate costs vs. benefits of team carefully: current claim numbers, mix, duration and cause determine team size and skill mix
  • Long-term benefit: arrest rising loss ratio; once-off benefit from existing large reserves with return-to-work potential
Sep 2024 Q3(iii)
  • Review claims processes to reduce time-to-pay (currently 72 days vs. 14 days in 2005)
  • Tighten claim definitions; ensure wording is clear and consistently applied
Apr 2025 Q1(ii)
  • Claims management may have weakened (e.g. if outsourced company did not uphold standards)
  • Review provider fee schedules; negotiate tariffs that vary by risk segment
  • Legal challenges may have given rise to unexpected claims; ensure wording is robust
Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
  • Improve claims management processes: ensure GP referrals in place; review benefit utilisation
  • Adopt a managed care approach for higher-risk or chronically ill patients
  • Negotiate fixed fee schedule with providers; require pre-authorisation for all treatment
  • Review protocols with providers to ensure procedures are appropriate and medically necessary
Apr 2022 Q2(ii) · Sep 2019 Q3(ii)
