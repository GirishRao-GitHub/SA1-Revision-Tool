1. Claim cost projection methodology
  • Frequency × average cost per claim; separate IP and OP rates
  • Utilisation rate per 1,000 lives × average cost per admission / visit
  • Day-care, inpatient, OPD modelled separately
  • Seasonal and geographic adjustment to base rates
  • Incidence rate per condition × sum insured (fixed benefit — no cost component)
  • No direct medical cost exposure; claim cost known at design stage
  • Multi-pay: weight incidence by tier-specific partial payment
  • Inception rate × expected claim duration × monthly benefit
  • Separate inception and termination (recovery / death) rate tables
  • Claim reserve = present value of future payments on in-force claims
  • Care inception rate × expected care duration × daily cost of care
  • Multi-state model: healthy → dependent → deceased
  • Long projection horizon (20–40 years) — actuarial modelling essential
  • Home care and residential care cost modelled separately
  • Package price = negotiated hospital cost + insurer margin
  • Not frequency/severity based — margin model, not loss model
  • Procedure mix × package price per procedure type
  • Hospital bears cost overrun above package price
  • Scenario-based: probability of declared event × aggregate payout
  • Return-period modelling (e.g. 1-in-50 year pandemic)
  • Pool adequacy = accumulated levy vs expected worst-case payout
  • Not individual policy pricing — fund-level projection

  • Core pricing risk — India medical inflation running 10–15% p.a.
  • Technology-led cost push: new treatments, diagnostics, drugs
  • Utilisation trend: increasing claim frequency over time
  • Mix shift: proportionately more expensive procedures over time
  • Must be explicitly projected — flat assumptions will under-price
  • No direct exposure to medical cost inflation (fixed benefit)
  • Indirect: sum insured adequacy erodes if inflation not reflected in benefit increases
  • Medical advances may reduce incidence (e.g. better cardiac outcomes)
  • No direct medical cost exposure — fixed income benefit
  • Salary inflation affects benefit amount (if salary-linked benefit)
  • Rehabilitation / case management costs subject to service inflation
  • Care cost inflation is the dominant long-term pricing risk
  • Residential and domiciliary care costs inflate above general CPI
  • Wage inflation in care sector adds to cost pressure
  • 20–40 year inflation projection is highly uncertain — scenario range essential
  • Package price renegotiated with hospital periodically — inflation managed at repricing
  • Frequency of package repricing is the key inflation control mechanism
  • Between repricing events, insurer bears medical cost inflation risk
  • Healthcare delivery cost relevant if benefit is cost-reimbursement based
  • Less relevant if pool pays fixed per-capita benefit on trigger
  • Levy level reviewed periodically to reflect cost changes

  • Procedure mix shift risk: more complex procedures sought over time

  • At-work clause for group new joiners limits selection at entry

  • Occupational mix shift — more manual / high-risk workers in portfolio
  • Longevity improvement: more people living long enough to reach care need
