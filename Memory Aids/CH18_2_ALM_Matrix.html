① Investment Principles
Why Invest?
  • Meet contractual benefit & expense payments as they fall due
  • Generate returns to benefit SH (profits/dividends) and/or PH (lower premiums)
  • Support the insurer's commercial success, financial results and long-term security
  • Investment importance varies by product — critical for pre-funded LTCI; minor for PMI (low reserves, short-tail)
  • Unit-linked PH benefit directly from good asset performance in the unit fund
What is the Core Principle?
  • Maximise investment return, subject to meeting all contractual and TCF obligations, recognising uncertainties involved and the overall risk that SH, Reg and PH are prepared to tolerate
  • High returns and low volatility conflict — higher expected return = higher variance
  • Available capital = MV(assets) − market-consistent value(liabilities)
  • Solvency cover ratio (own funds ÷ SCR) — insurers typically target 150–200%
  • The insurer must balance return and security given the nature of its liabilities
How to Balance Risk and Return?
  • Closer matching of assets to liabilities (nature, term, currency) → lower investment risk
  • Free assets provide a cushion allowing departure from a matched position
  • Greater free assets → greater freedom to invest in higher-returning (more volatile) assets
  • Appetite for risk depends on capital strength, diversification and regulatory environment
  • Extent of divergence from matched position = the company's "risk budget"
② Aim of Asset-Liability Management
Why ALM?  What does it deliver?
  • Derive an investment strategy that reflects the insurer's attitude to market risk — the extent to which it is prepared to suffer losses due to adverse economic/investment market conditions
  • Attitude to risk varies with size of available capital and level of diversification
  • Sets the "risk budget" — how much to deviate from cashflow matching and how much capital to expose in doing so
  • Examines alternative investment strategies (physical + derivative) for suitability and risk level
  • Decide whether to hedge only best-estimate liabilities or to seek further balance sheet stabilisation
  • Even "perfect" cashflow matching doesn't necessarily stabilise the reported liabilities (e.g. Solvency II volatility adjustment based on representative portfolio, not actual holdings)
  • Solvency II: P&L attribution is a regulatory requirement for internal model approval
How is ALM Governed?
  • Choose a quantitative measure of success, e.g. probability that free capital drops below zero over the next 5 years
  • Investment strategy not developed in isolation — optimised jointly with risk management strategy and new business strategy
  • Model office projections are part of overall financial management — identify, explore and monitor risks; integral to ORSA under Solvency II
  • ALM team activities: strategic asset allocation + hedging strategy + ongoing risk management (measuring, monitoring, rebalancing)
  • Movements in full balance sheet can occur even with good cashflow matching — due to movements in SCR or risk margin
③ Effect of Liabilities on Asset Selection — The Four Liability Types
Liability Nature, Term & Currency Drive Asset Choice
Net liability outgo = benefit payments + expenses − premiums · Must match nature AND term AND currency
① Guaranteed in Monetary Terms
e.g. Fixed CI lump sum · fixed IP benefit · premiums (fixed)
Match with: Fixed-interest (govt) bonds, chosen to match term of outgo
Problem: Available bond terms often shorter than liabilities; reinvestment rate risk; surplus premiums must be reinvested at unknown rates
Alternative: Immunisation (second-best) — protects against small interest rate changes; zero-coupon bonds if available
With large numbers of policies, cashflows statistically predictable → best match approach feasible
② Guaranteed vs Index (Prices / Earnings)
e.g. IP benefit linked to RPI/CPI · expenses (move with wages/prices)
Match with: Index-linked government bonds (same index, matched term)
If unavailable: Real assets (equities, property) — provide real return over long term but volatile short-term
Equities limitation: Return not guaranteed; default risk; poor short-term match; better match for earnings indices than price indices
NAE matching: No bonds linked to NAE; equities better long-term but short-term volatility problematic
Inflation caps/floors: Particularly challenging — may use inflation-based derivatives
③ Indemnity
e.g. PMI — amount depends on actual treatment costs incurred
Match with: Cash and short-term fixed-interest bonds (short-tail business, minimal reserves)
Rationale: Premium income broadly sufficient to pay claims → reserves very small → investment income negligible
For persistent reserves: Amounts unknown at setting; costs escalate with medical inflation (often > RPI) → index-linked bonds or real assets
Medical inflation often exceeds general price inflation — monitor claims cost trends carefully
④ Investment-Linked
e.g. Unit-linked LTCI · unit-linked with-profits health
Match with: The actual assets underlying the investment link / unit fund
In many markets legally required to hold the underlying assets exactly
Deliberate mismatch: Only if insurer expects alternative assets to outperform — risk of loss falls entirely on insurer (not PH)
Matching requirements less of an issue — vast bulk of assets and liabilities exactly matched
Term & Currency Rules
Term: Match asset proceeds to when liability outgo falls due — assets too long = exposed to rising yields (capital loss); assets too short = exposed to falling yields (reinvestment risk).
Currency: Liabilities in a given currency matched by assets in the same currency. Overseas assets can be held with currency options to hedge FX risk. Alternatively, invest overseas for diversification / attractive returns and hedge the currency mismatch.
④ Mean Term, Inflation & Liquidity — Matching Constraints
Why Mean Term Matters
  • Assets too long: If long-term yields rise, MV of assets falls more than MV of liabilities → possible insolvency on paper (even if not actually insolvent until liability falls due)
  • Assets too short: If interest rates fall, reinvestment at lower rates → insufficient income to meet long-term liabilities
  • Current solvency: statements only fully hold if liabilities discounted at market-responsive rates
  • Future solvency: threatened whether or not liabilities are discounted — depends on future investment returns
  • Insurer can depart from matched term if: (a) large free assets; (b) new business premiums expected to meet shorter liabilities — but relying on future premiums is dangerous
What Are the Inflation Risks?
  • Benefits expressly linked to an inflationary index → must hold index-linked assets
  • Benefits that indemnify medical expense costs (PMI, LTCI care costs) → subject to medical/care inflation, often exceeding RPI
  • Expenses of settling claims → mainly wage inflation
  • Short-term liabilities: Treat as fixed in monetary terms — not enough time for inflation to deviate materially
  • Long-term liabilities: Inflation is a major consideration — assets should provide real return
  • National indices (RPI, CPI, NAE) relevant but imperfect matches for specific claim types
How to Manage Liquidity
  • Writing classes with widely fluctuating claims levels → maintain access to liquid funds
  • Portfolio contracting (closed to NB) → can't fund claims from incoming premiums → higher liquidity requirement
  • Reinsurance reduces variability of claims outgo → indirect benefit to investment strategy (less liquid assets possible) — but gross claims paid far in advance of RI recoveries
  • Default of reinsurer or broker → sharp cashflow interruption; if shortfall not immediate, assets can be realised in orderly way
  • High claims variability → cautious strategy with higher proportion of liquid assets
  • Regulatory constraints: Restrictions on asset types, solvency admissibility, currency matching, exposure limits, mismatching reserves, valuation methods, custodianship
⑤ The ALM Process & Modelling
Asset-Liability Modelling Process  ·  Steps & Tools
Why Model? Projections of assets and liabilities become particularly useful when their relationship is examined together. Used to test investment strategies, estimate insolvency probability, determine free assets needed, and enable advance planning for condition changes.
① Project Liability OutgoModel all relevant cashflows on a year-by-year basis for the entire policy lifetime. Liability projection system models benefits, expenses, premiums. Decide level of detail to incorporate.
② Project Asset ProceedsSeparate asset projection model feeds into liability system, OR build single combined model. Stochastic investment models project future income and capital values.
③ Compare & AssessCompare asset proceeds vs liability outgo each period. Excess of assets over liabilities must comfortably cover solvency capital required. Test at year-end or continuously.
④ Re-run AssumptionsRun under different scenarios (sensitivity testing for deterministic; probability distributions for stochastic). Identify which assumptions are most critical.
⑤ Assess AppropriatenessStatistical distribution of surplus each year → estimate probability of future insolvency for each strategy. Also calculate aggregate profitability measure (e.g. PV shareholder distributions).
⑥ Investigate AlternativesRepeat for different (more or less risky) strategies until target insolvency probability achieved. Identify strategy with equal insolvency risk and highest profitability.
Deterministic vs Stochastic

Deterministic: Pre-set values for all variables based on a single set of assumptions. Run using different sets (sensitivity testing). Simpler but gives no probability distribution.

Stochastic: Key parameters randomly selected from defined probability distributions. Generates distribution of outcomes — e.g. probability of free capital < 0 over next 5 years. Requires more assumptions (ESG calibration, asset model choice).
Economic Scenario Generators (ESG)

What: Model producing numerous simulations of possible future economic outcomes (interest rates, inflation, equity/property/credit returns). Power comes from modelling joint behaviour of economic variables coherently.

Calibrations: Real-world (for ALM — project asset returns, calculate insolvency probabilities) vs Risk-neutral/market-consistent (for liability valuation at future points). ALM needs both.

Also: Time zero stress tests assess immediate risks; risk exposures change significantly as assets and liabilities mature.
Allowing for solvency requirements: Model must incorporate regulatory asset values and liability values to assess solvency at each future period. Compare against SCR at each point. Insurer likely to place further, more stringent, class-specific solvency capital requirements on itself. Allowance for future new business needed to monitor ongoing progress.
⑥ Derivatives in ALM
Derivatives — Purposes, Types & Risks
Why Many health and care insurers now use derivatives as an integral part of their ALM strategies.
① Reduce Risk of Loss
Alternative to physical assets when unavailable/impractical. e.g. Equity put options limit loss from equity market fall where assets and liabilities unmatched. Futures on index gain exposure without buying every stock.
② Increase Available Capital
Reducing regulatory capital requirements. e.g. Holding put options may reduce the SCR under Solvency II → frees up capital for deployment elsewhere.
③ Stabilise Balance Sheet
Hedge SCR or risk margin under Solvency II. Risk margin is sensitive to interest rate changes. Construct portfolio matching any movement in liability value from interest rate changes → reduces balance sheet volatility.
④ Reduce Tax / Costs
Exchange-traded derivatives cheaper to deal than underlying physical assets. Particularly relevant for gaining broad market exposure.
⑤ Efficient Acquisition / Disposal
Going short equity futures provides immediate reduction in equity exposure whilst selling actual assets more slowly when conditions are optimal. Avoids market impact of large physical transactions.
Static vs Dynamic Hedging
Static: Derivative held to protect against specific severe adverse scenario in limited circumstances. Set and forget.
Dynamic: Portfolio of derivatives held and frequently rebalanced to continue matching liability risk sensitivities as conditions change.
Residual Risks of Derivative Strategies   Counterparty risk Liquidity risk Operational risk Basis risk (index ≠ actual portfolio)  · Local regulatory restrictions on use of derivatives must also be considered.
⑦ Effect of Free Assets on Asset Selection
Why Free Assets Matter Free assets = excess of assets over liabilities. Enable insurer to depart from matching strategies to improve overall return — benefiting PH (lower premiums) and SH (higher dividends). Some must be held securely to cover minimum solvency capital; excess available for higher-returning assets. Freedom exercised depends on actual level relative to statutory solvency requirements.
What Can Free Assets Back? Minimum SCR portion: short-dated domestic government bonds (security + low capital volatility). Excess free assets: long-term equities (domestic + overseas), direct property (if portfolio large enough for diversification). Free assets measured relative to: supervisory reserves, premiums written in last year, ultimate liabilities from recent claims, absolute liability size, regulatory capital requirements.
How Much Freedom? Greater free assets → greater permissible mismatch and higher variance of return acceptable. Constrained by: acceptable risk level · regulatory restrictions on admissible assets · MCR requirements. Why hold above statutory minimum? (1) Buffer so minimum not breached after adverse movement; (2) Authorities expect it; (3) Appear financially strong to brokers/PH/SH; (4) Statutory minimum insufficient for inherent business risks.
⑧ Estimating Investment Income in Future Time Periods
Asset Class What Income Stream Estimation Approach Key Nuances
Fixed-Interest (Govt / Corp) Known coupon + redemption proceeds if held to maturity Income & redemption proceeds known; adjust for default risk by reducing amounts progressively (e.g. ½% yr 1, 1% yr 2, 1½% yr 3...) Default allowance depends on avg quality of non-gilt portfolio. Some gilts have spread of redemption dates at govt option. Possibility of sale introduces capital uncertainty.
Index-Linked Securities Coupon + redemption, both increased by index changes Same as fixed-interest but increase payments by estimated future index changes (e.g. RPI forecast for index-linked gilts) Income not as volatile as capital value. Capital value has more volatility than conventional gilts, especially long-dated, in both real and monetary terms.
Equities Dividend income + capital growth Take current income stream; assume annual increases linked to inflation + economic growth prospects. Directors smooth dividends; well-diversified portfolio = stable income stream despite individual company cuts. Capital value potentially very volatile. If equities not ever to be realised: treat as infinite dividend stream. If to be sold: need assumptions about future market values at disposal dates. Input from economists/investment managers recommended.
Property Rental income + capital growth Project rental income + growth; allow for specific lease terms and rent review dates for individual properties. Very conservative capital value assumptions due to illiquidity. Rental stream more certain than equities short-term (fixed for term of lease), but uncertainty post rent review. Diversified portfolio: low proportion with zero rent. Investment expenses much higher than equities; allow for obsolescence/refitting.
Cash & Money-Market Interest income at short-term rates Option A — Income stream: Current short-term rates + yield curve shape to project future rates. Option B — Lump sum now: Total cash available at maturity date (best if cash held to meet claims within months). Option C — Hybrid: Cash notionally used to meet immediate liability outgo; balance rolled up at short-term rates. Capital value: virtually no volatility. Income: can change significantly year to year. Short-term interest rates can vary considerably over time.
Overseas Investments As equivalent domestic asset class Treat like corresponding domestic assets but add assumptions about future exchange rates consistent with liability projections All domestic asset volatility characteristics apply plus additional currency volatility. Greater default risk in some cases. Diversification benefit may justify holding despite FX risk.
