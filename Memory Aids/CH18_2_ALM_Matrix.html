① Investment Principles  ·  Why / What / How
Why Invest?
  • Meet contractual benefit & expense payments as they fall due
  • Generate returns to benefit SH (profits/dividends) and/or PH (lower premiums)
  • Support the insurer's commercial success, financial results and long-term security
  • Investment importance varies by product — critical for pre-funded LTCI (high reserves); minor for PMI (low reserves, short-tail)
  • Unit-linked PH benefit directly from good asset performance in the unit fund
What is the Core Principle?
  • Maximise investment return, subject to meeting all contractual and TCF obligations, recognising uncertainties involved and the overall risk that SH, Reg and PH are prepared to tolerate
  • High returns and low volatility conflict — higher expected return = higher variance of that return
  • Available capital = MV(assets) − market-consistent value(liabilities)
  • Solvency cover ratio (own funds ÷ SCR) — insurers typically target 150–200%
How to Balance Risk & Return?
  • Match assets to nature, term and currency of liabilities → reduces investment risk
  • Free assets provide a cushion — allow departure from matched position in pursuit of higher return
  • Greater free assets → greater freedom to invest in higher-returning (more volatile) assets
  • Risk appetite depends on capital strength, diversification and regulatory environment
  • Extent of divergence from matched position = the company's "risk budget"
② Aim of Asset-Liability Management  ·  What / Conditions / Trade-offs
What does ALM deliver?
  • Investment strategy aligned to the insurer's attitude to market risk — willingness to suffer losses due to adverse economic/investment market conditions
  • Sets the risk budget: how much to deviate from cashflow matching and how much capital to expose
  • Examines alternative strategies (physical + derivative) for suitability and risk level
  • Ongoing ALM risk management: measuring, monitoring and rebalancing positions
  • Model office projections: integral to ORSA under Solvency II; allow risks to be identified, explored and monitored
Conditions that shape ALM
  • Capital strength and diversification → determines risk tolerance
  • SCR target typically 150–200%; internal model approval requires P&L attribution
  • Solvency II volatility adjustment based on representative portfolio not actual holdings → even perfect cashflow matching doesn't fully stabilise reported liabilities
  • Movements in SCR or risk margin cause full balance sheet movements even with matched cashflows
  • Quantitative success measure needed: e.g. probability free capital < 0 over 5 years
Trade-offs
  • Hedge best-estimate liabilities only vs seek full balance sheet stabilisation (SCR + risk margin)
  • Investment strategy not developed in isolation — optimised jointly with risk management strategy and new business strategy
  • Return maximisation vs capital efficiency (lower SCR via derivatives)
  • ALM hedging may avoid unpalatable management actions — but if those actions already built into projection model, need for matching may be masked
③ Effect of Liabilities on Asset Selection  ·  What / Key Nuances
The Four Liability Types & Their Matching Assets
Net liability outgo = benefit payments + expenses − premiums  ·  Match nature AND term AND currency — always all three
What is each type, what matches it  ·  Key Nuances — where matching breaks down
① Guaranteed in Monetary Terms
e.g. Fixed CI lump sum · fixed IP benefit · fixed premiums
Match: Fixed-interest government bonds, chosen to match term of outgo

Nuances:
· Available bond terms often shorter than liabilities
· Surplus premiums must be reinvested at unknown future rates
· Net liability may be a cash inflow in early years — no asset can match that directly
· Immunisation: second-best alternative; protects only against small rate changes
· Zero-coupon bonds eliminate reinvestment risk but may not exist in the market
② Guaranteed vs Index (Prices / Earnings)
e.g. IP benefit linked to RPI/CPI or NAE · expenses (wages + prices)
Match: Index-linked government bonds (same index, matched term)

Nuances:
· IL gilts: time-lag in index used for coupon calculation prevents perfect match
· If IL gilts unavailable: real assets (equities/property) — good long-term match, poor short-term
· NAE matching: no bonds linked to NAE; equities better long-term but volatile short-term
· Inflation caps/floors: particularly challenging — inflation-based derivatives may help
· Expenses: link to earnings index broadly adequate even if not strictly comparable
③ Indemnity
e.g. PMI — amount depends on actual treatment costs incurred
Match: Cash and short-term fixed-interest bonds

Nuances:
· Rationale: PMI is short-tail; premium income broadly covers claims; reserves minimal → investment income negligible
· Where persistent reserves exist: amounts unknown; costs escalate with medical inflation
· Medical inflation often exceeds RPI — index-linked bonds only partially match
· For longer-tail indemnity reserves: equities/property provide real return but volatility must be accepted
④ Investment-Linked
e.g. Unit-linked LTCI · unit-linked with-profits health
Match: The actual assets underlying the unit fund / investment link

Nuances:
· Often a legal requirement to hold the underlying assets exactly
· ALM matching requirements are less of an issue — assets and liabilities move together
· Deliberate mismatch: only if insurer expects alternative assets to outperform — any underperformance falls entirely on insurer (not PH)
· Sensitivity analysis / scenario testing still needed for charges, capital requirements
Key Nuances — Term & Currency Rules Term: Match asset proceeds to when liability outgo falls due. Assets too long → exposed to rising yields (capital loss on sale). Assets too short → exposed to falling yields (reinvestment risk). Departure acceptable if: (a) large free assets; (b) new business premiums can bridge gap — but relying heavily on future premiums is dangerous.
Currency: Match currency of assets to currency of liabilities. Currency options can hedge FX risk on overseas assets. Overseas investment also justified for diversification or access to assets unavailable domestically.
④ Mean Term, Inflation & Liquidity  ·  What / Conditions / Trade-offs
What is Mean Term Mismatch?
  • Assets too long: Long-term yields rise → MV of assets falls by more than MV of liabilities → possible insolvency. Example: liabilities 1 week, assets 20 years — a 20%+ fall in asset values possible from yield spike
  • Assets too short: Interest rates fall → reinvestment at lower rates → insufficient income to meet long-term liabilities. Example: cash at 15% pa → rates cut to 6% → insolvency over 2-year horizon
  • Current solvency statements fully hold only if liabilities discounted at market-responsive rates
  • Future solvency threatened whether or not liabilities are discounted — depends on future investment returns
What Are the Inflation Risks?  Conditions
  • Benefits expressly linked to index → hold index-linked assets
  • Indemnity benefits (PMI, LTCI care costs) → subject to medical/care inflation, often exceeding RPI
  • Expenses → mainly wage inflation, linked to earnings index
  • Short-term liabilities: Treat as fixed in monetary terms — not enough time for inflation to deviate materially; also unlikely to deviate much from expected
  • Long-term liabilities: Inflation is a major consideration — assets must provide real return
  • National indices (RPI, CPI, NAE) are relevant benchmarks but imperfect matches for specific claim types
What Drives Liquidity Need?  Trade-offs
  • Writing classes with widely fluctuating claims → maintain liquid funds
  • Portfolio contracting / run-off → cannot fund claims from incoming premiums → higher liquidity requirement
  • Reinsurance reduces claims variability (→ less liquid assets possible) but gross claims paid far in advance of RI recoveries (→ tension with matching)
  • Default of reinsurer/broker → sharp cashflow interruption; if shortfall not immediate, realise assets in orderly way to minimise loss
  • Trade-off: Matching may suggest longer-term equities/property, but liquidity need may force shorter-term investments even if liability nature hasn't changed
  • Regulatory constraints: Restrictions on asset types, solvency admissibility, currency matching, exposure limits, mismatching reserves, valuation methods, custodianship
⑤ The ALM Process & Modelling  ·  Why / What / How
Asset-Liability Modelling Process  ·  Steps, Tools & Model Types
Why Model? Test investment strategies · Estimate probability of insolvency · Determine free assets required to support a strategy · Enable advance planning for changes in conditions or strategies. Integral to ORSA under Solvency II.
What are the steps?
① Project Liability OutgoModel all relevant cashflows year-by-year for entire policy lifetime. Decide level of detail to incorporate. Liability projection system models benefits, expenses, premiums.
② Project Asset ProceedsSeparate asset projection model — feeds into liability system, or built as combined model. Stochastic investment models project future income and capital value changes.
③ Compare & AssessCompare asset proceeds vs liability outgo per period. Excess of assets over liabilities must comfortably cover required solvency capital. Test at year-end or continuously.
④ Re-run AssumptionsRun under different scenarios. Sensitivity testing (deterministic) or full probability distributions (stochastic). Identify which assumptions are most critical to results.
⑤ Assess AppropriatenessStatistical distribution of surplus each year → estimate probability of future insolvency per strategy. Calculate aggregate profitability (e.g. PV shareholder distributions).
⑥ Investigate AlternativesRepeat for different strategies until target insolvency probability achieved. Identify strategy with equal insolvency risk and highest profitability. Repeat with different free asset allocations.
How — Deterministic vs Stochastic Deterministic: Pre-set values for all variables. Run with different assumption sets (sensitivity testing). Simpler, no probability distribution output.

Stochastic: Key parameters randomly selected from defined probability distributions. Hundreds/thousands of sample paths generated. Outputs: probability of insolvency, confidence intervals for surplus. Requires more assumptions — ESG calibration, asset model choice, parameter consistency critical.
How — Economic Scenario Generators (ESG) What: Model producing numerous simulations of future economic outcomes. Power comes from modelling joint behaviour of variables (interest rates, inflation, equity/property/credit) coherently.

Calibrations: Real-world — for ALM: project actual asset returns, calculate insolvency probabilities. Risk-neutral/market-consistent — for liability valuation at future time points. ALM needs both.

Also: Time zero stress tests assess immediate risk exposures. Risk exposures change significantly as assets and liabilities mature over time.
⑥ Derivatives in ALM  ·  Why / What / How
Derivatives — Purposes, Types & Risks
Why Many health and care insurers now use derivatives as an integral part of their ALM strategies — held for the following distinct purposes:
Why ① Reduce Risk of Loss
Alternative to physical assets when unavailable or impractical. e.g. Equity put options limit loss from equity market fall where assets and liabilities unmatched. Index futures provide exposure without buying every constituent stock.
Why ② Increase Available Capital
Reduce regulatory capital requirements. e.g. Holding equity put options may reduce the SCR under Solvency II → frees capital for other uses or reduces cost of holding capital.
Why ③ Stabilise Balance Sheet
Hedge SCR or risk margin under Solvency II. Risk margin sensitive to interest rate changes. Construct portfolio matching any movement in liability value from rate changes → reduces balance sheet volatility.
Why ④ Reduce Tax / Costs
Exchange-traded derivatives cheaper to deal than underlying physical assets — particularly relevant for gaining broad market exposure quickly or at scale.
Why ⑤ Efficient Acquisition / Disposal
Going short equity futures: immediate reduction in equity exposure whilst selling actual assets more slowly when conditions are optimal. Avoids market impact of large physical transactions.
How — Static vs Dynamic
Static: Held to protect against a specific severe adverse scenario; set and hold. Dynamic: Portfolio rebalanced frequently to continue matching liability risk sensitivities as conditions change — e.g. duration of liabilities changes as policies age.
Key Nuances — Residual Risks  Counterparty risk Liquidity risk Operational risk Basis risk — derivative based on index ≠ actual portfolio held  · Local regulatory restrictions on use of derivatives must also be considered · Derivative strategies often imperfect by design.
⑦ Effect of Free Assets on Asset Selection  ·  What / Conditions / Trade-offs
Free Assets — Definition, Conditions & Trade-offs
What are free assets? Free assets = excess of assets over liabilities. An insurance company will have free assets (also termed "free reserves" in short-term companies).
  • Some must be held securely to cover minimum solvency capital requirements (MCR) — short-dated domestic government bonds: relative payment security (government guarantee) + low capital volatility (near maturity)
  • Excess available to invest for long-term return — equities (domestic + overseas), direct property (if portfolio large enough)
  • Free assets measured relative to: supervisory reserves · premiums written in last year · ultimate liabilities from recent claims · absolute liability size · regulatory capital requirements
Conditions — when can you mismatch?
  • Greater free assets → greater permissible mismatch → higher variance of return acceptable
  • Extent of freedom constrained by: acceptable risk level · regulatory admissibility restrictions · MCR/SCR requirements
  • Why hold above statutory minimum?
  • Buffer so minimum not breached after adverse asset/liability movements
  • Regulatory authorities expect it (even if not legally required)
  • Appear financially strong to brokers, PH and SH — competitive signal
  • Statutory minimum may be insufficient for inherent risks of the lines written
Trade-offs
  • Using free assets to support mismatch means those assets are at risk of loss — SH may prefer to reserve free assets for NB strain or product development
  • Proprietary company: duty to PH (invest wisely; meet liabilities) AND to SH (sustain and enhance return on assets above MCR). These can pull in different directions
  • Greater freedom of investment = greater responsibility — TCF obligations limit how far from matching the insurer can stray on reviewable products
  • Unit-linked mismatch: any excess return accrues to insurer; any shortfall also borne by insurer — asymmetric risk profile
⑧ Estimating Investment Income by Asset Class  ·  What / Key Nuances
Asset Class What — Income Stream & Estimation Key Nuances — Where estimates get complicated
Fixed-Interest (Govt / Corp) Income and redemption proceeds known if held to maturity. Reduce for default risk progressively: e.g. ½% yr 1, 1% yr 2, 1½% yr 3 (reduction factor depends on average portfolio quality). Possibility of sale introduces capital uncertainty. Some gilts have spread of redemption dates at government option. Reinvestment of proceeds at unknown future rates if bonds mature before liabilities.
Index-Linked Securities Same as fixed-interest, but increase all payments by an estimate of future index changes (e.g. RPI forecast for index-linked gilts). Allow for default risk similarly. Income is not volatile but not certain. Capital value more volatile than conventional gilts — especially long-dated — in both real and monetary terms. Time-lag in index used for payment calculations.
Equities Take current income stream (dividends); assume annual increases reflecting likely dividend growth — linked to inflation and economic growth prospects. Directors smooth dividends; diversified portfolio gives fairly stable income. Capital value potentially very volatile. If equities are ever to be sold to meet liability outgo: need assumptions about future market values at disposal dates — input from economists/investment managers recommended. Treating as infinite stream only valid if equities back free assets entirely.
Property Project rental income and growth, allowing for specific lease terms and rent review dates. When allowing for realisation: very conservative capital value assumptions due to illiquidity and poor marketability. Rental stream more certain short-term (fixed for lease term) but uncertain post rent-review. Diversified portfolio: low proportion with zero rent. Investment expenses much higher than equities (maintenance, management). Allow for obsolescence and periodic refitting.
Cash & Money-Market Option A — Income stream: Current short-term rates + yield curve to project future rates.
Option B — Lump sum now: Total cash available at maturity (best if cash held to meet claims within months).
Option C — Hybrid: Cash notionally meets immediate liability outgo; balance rolled up at short-term rates.		 Capital value: virtually no volatility. Income: can change significantly year to year — short-term rates vary considerably. Option B most appropriate when cash held specifically for imminent claim payments. Option A suitable if cash forms a permanent "float" on the balance sheet.
Overseas Investments Treat like corresponding domestic asset class. Add assumptions about future exchange rates consistent with those used in the liability outgo projections. All domestic volatility characteristics apply plus additional currency volatility (extra volatility in sterling/domestic currency terms). Greater default risk in some cases. Diversification benefit may justify holding despite the FX complexity.
