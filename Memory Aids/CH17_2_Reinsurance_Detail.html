② Facultative vs Treaty
Facultative
Each risk individually negotiated. Cedant free to place anywhere; reinsurer free to accept or reject.
AdvantageFlexibility to seek best terms per risk · Can cover risks outside treaty scope (large/unusual)
DisadvantageTime-consuming & costly · No certainty of cover availability · Delay before insurer can accept business → reduced market standing
Used for: individual proposals outside normal treaty range — eg unusually large SI, unusual UW features
Treaty
Formal pre-agreed arrangement. Risks within scope placed automatically. Three obligation structures:
StructureCedantReinsurer
Fac / FacOptionalOptional (informal)
Fac / ObOptionalObliged to accept
Ob / ObMust cedeMust accept (norm)
AdvantageCertain cover within terms · Efficient (automatic) · Helps control solvency & growth · Marketability
DisadvantageInflexible once set · Terms may restrict insurer · Takes time to set up · Less optimal terms for large/unusual risks
Treaty must specify: coverage scope · exclusions · financial terms (premiums, commission, timing) · obligations of both parties · arbitration clause
③ Proportional Reinsurance — Basis
Original Terms (Coinsurance)
  • Reinsurer shares original premium & claims in same fixed proportion. Applies to long-term AND short-term.
  • Cedant provides its premium rates to reinsurer so RI can check adequacy of pricing.
  • Commission paid by reinsurer to cedant: covers cedant's initial expenses on the ceded portion. Amount reflects RI's view of expected experience.
  • Profit commission possible if portfolio meets profitability criteria.
  • Deposit back: RI deposits its share of reserve with cedant → cedant holds full reserve, earns investment return, RI avoids investing in unfamiliar market. Also mitigates RI default risk.
Risk Premium
  • Reinsurer sets its own premium rate independently of cedant's premium → cedant free to change rates without altering RI terms.
  • Applied to reinsured benefit (% of SI or % of sum-at-risk).
  • Level risk premiums: reinsurer spreads cost level over policy term (like cedant might for policyholder).
  • Yearly increasing: each year's premium = expected claims cost in that year (rises with age/duration).
  • Either type may be guaranteed or reviewable.
  • Sum-at-risk variant: reinsure only the excess of benefit over reserve. Only useful where SI is lump sum and reserve is material (mainly CI). Not practical for IP/LTCI (claim duration unknown → SAR indeterminate).
Long-term health: QS or Surplus on Original Terms or Risk Premium basis.  |  Short-term health: Quota Share on Original Terms basis only.
④⑤ Quota Share vs Surplus — The Two Proportional Structures
Proportional Structure Comparison
QS = same %; Surplus = targeted monetary retention
QUOTA SHARE Fixed R% of every risk
MechanismFixed R% of every risk ceded, regardless of size. All claim payments split R% to RI. Simplest to administer — just list totals. Always by treaty.
PurposesSpread/reduce parameter risk · Write larger portfolios · Reciprocal business · Improve solvency ratio · Satisfy statutory capital · Finance NBS
AdvantagesAdministratively simple · Automatic commission for financing · Straightforward solvency relief · Good for new products/territories
DisadvantagesSame % regardless of size: cedes too much on small risks, not enough on very large ones (less efficient than Surplus at targeting risk) · Cedes share of profit
Reciprocity: Insurer A cedes 40% QS to B; B cedes 40% QS to A. Both diversify without diversifying geographically.
SURPLUS Fixed monetary retention; variable % per policy
MechanismInsurer selects monetary retention. Cede = SI − retention (if positive). % ceded varies per policy. Policies below retention: 0% ceded. Long-term fixed-SI only (not PMI).
ExampleRetention = £75k. SI £150k → 50% ceded. SI £100k → 25% ceded. SI £50k → 0% ceded. More logical targeting of large exposures.
AdvantagesAccept larger risks (more effective than QS) · Targets large risks precisely → reduces claims volatility · Better aligned with risk management objective
DisadvantagesLess protection against parameter risk (net retained proportion varies → harder to predict experience) · Less suitable for financing · More admin complexity
Inflation: Treaty specifies whether monetary retention remains fixed or proportion remains fixed when SI increases over time.
⑥ Non-Proportional (Excess of Loss) Reinsurance
Excess of Loss — Three Types
Primary aim: accept risks with potential large claims · Stabilise technical results · Reduce variance of claims outgo
Risk XL
  • Individual policy claims · e.g. PMI where one large treatment claim occurs
  • Cover expressed as: £X in excess of £Y (amount of layer in excess of excess point)
  • Multiple layers can be stacked with no gaps between them
  • Top layer may be unlimited
  • Proportional within XL: RI covers 90% of layer → insurer retains incentive to control large claims (moral hazard)
  • Indexed limits: stability clause indexes excess point and upper limit to inflation index → extra premium if not indexed
Aggregate XL / Stop Loss
  • Covers total portfolio claims over 12 months (whole account)
  • In health context: aggregate XL = stop loss (morbidity = only peril — no point distinguishing)
  • Excess point & upper limit expressed as % of earned premiums (e.g. 110% to 130% claims ratio)
  • Using % prevents insurer gaming limits by writing more volume
  • Especially important for group health (non-independent risks → aggregate accumulation)
  • Rarely warranted for long-term individual insurance (not volatile enough)
  • ⚠ Often unavailable at affordable price — reinsurers reluctant: limited control over cedant UW & claims · Historically loss-making
Catastrophe XL
  • Single event / common cause trigger: workplace explosion, epidemic, pandemic
  • Multiple individual claims all from same cause aggregate to exceed retention
  • Event-type cover (e.g. chemical explosion): generally available
  • ⚠ Pandemic cover: risk too uncertain → normally unavailable at affordable price. Severe epidemic: government may step in.
Pros of all XL types
  • Accepts risks with very large potential claims
  • Stabilises annual profits / reduces claims variance
  • More efficient capital use (lower reserves needed)
Downside: RI premium in long run > expected recoveries. Post-bad-years, XL premiums spike as RI restores solvency.
⑦ Financial Reinsurance
Risk Premium Fin Re — Disguised Loan
  • Reinsurer pays large upfront commission = disguised loan to cedant
  • Cedant repays via higher-than-market RI premiums in future years
  • Reinsurer assumes lapse risk: if policies lapse, cedant cannot repay → treaty includes clawback provisions
  • Uses: finance new business strain · new distribution channel / IT systems · M&A
  • Loan is "off balance sheet" for cedant (no standard liability reservation needed) → improves apparent solvency position
Contingent Loan (Surplus Relief) — VIF-Based
  • Reinsurer advances funds to cedant; repayment contingent on future VIF profits emerging
  • If VIF profits don't emerge → reinsurer absorbs loss (= capital gift). Cedant need not reserve for contingent repayment.
  • Result: assets increase without increase in liabilities → improves free asset position (under non-realistic regimes)
  • EV impact: assets ↑ by loan amount BUT PVIF ↓ by PV(repayments) → broadly neutral to EV
  • Risk reduction: VIF profit converted to certain cash → reduces EV uncertainty
Solvency II / Realistic Regimes: Financial RI ineffective — balance sheet already credit for future profits; loan repayment must be shown as liability. Both effects cancel out.

Risk Management & Overseas / IT
Risk Management
  • Underwriting: UW manuals (acceptance limits, evidence requirements, exclusions, loading calc, referral rules) · IT-based UW systems (80% straight-through acceptance) · Auditing cedant UW practice
  • Claims control: Initial assessment of claims · Training in claims handling · Monitoring of experience · Soft: PMI moratoria/blanket exclusions reduce UW need; Hard: IP/CI — UW mistakes very costly
  • Policy wording: Regular review → highlight defects that produce unintended claims → incorporate into next policy draft
Overseas & IT
  • Overseas: Local risk stats · Contacts (distributors, hospitals, consultants, TPAs) · Local policy documents/translations · Legal & regulatory advice (professional legal still needed)
  • IT software: Underwriting systems · Pricing software (multi-state models for H&C) · Claims acceptance/checking · Reserving systems · Internet-hosted → always up-to-date
⑨ Reinsurance Impact (SA1 Ch.17 §5)
Reinsurance Impact — Financial, Market & Relationship Effects
Tax Arbitrage
If tax basis differs between insurer and reinsurer, both parties can improve their after-tax result by structuring the treaty appropriately. Quota share popular here. Rules may change — treat as opportunity not permanent feature.
Capital (Regulatory) Arbitrage
Reinsurer needs less solvency capital per unit of risk (eg not subject to same regime) → cost of capital lower → can offer cheaper RI rates. Saving shared with cedant = lower premiums or higher commission.
New Markets Benefit
Insurer: gains experience & in-force book in new area it couldn't have entered without RI support. Reinsurer: gains quota share of (hopefully) profitable new business + experience + market credibility + portfolio diversification.
Consultancy Fees vs Risk Share
Two ways RI is compensated: (1) risk share (preferred — profit-aligned, especially for new uncertain product) or (2) consultancy fees (justified where RI work is disproportionate to risk taken). Not mutually exclusive.
Predictability of Results
Health & care less predictable than life → RI smoothing has extra "peace of mind" value for smaller insurer. Net financial gain may be zero (self-balancing) but volatility reduction has real worth to shareholders & regulators.
Difference of Interests
Insurer: quantity (volume of business written). Reinsurer: quality (profitable claims experience). Conflict visible when: (i) high quota share → RI scrutinises claims insurer would pay quickly; (ii) RI may increase premiums or exit at renewal. Long-term relationship — open conflict rare but tension always exists.
Badging — Alternative to Reinsurance (SA1 Ch.17 §6)
Badging — Generalist Fronts, Specialist Insures
What it is
Generalist insurer (large brand, no H&C experience) distributes product. Specialist H&C insurer issues the policy, handles all UW, claims, administration & payments. Specialist carries all insurance risk.
Why vs Reinsurance
Reinsurance: issuing company retains some risk, then cedes part. Badging: all risk stays with specialist from outset — generalist takes no insurance risk, only brand/distribution role. Strictly not "reinsurance" but achieves some same goals (TA, risk transfer).
Commercial Terms
Both gain: specialist gets distribution; generalist gets product range. Net balance of benefit determines who pays whom. Often structured as profit-sharing arrangement. Legal requirement: specialist's name may have to appear in product literature (customer must know who actually insures). Poor specialist service → damages generalist's reputation.
⑩ Determining Retention Level
Two Approaches
ApproachMethodOutcome
Ruin ProbabilityStochastic model: simulate claim rates + asset/liability projections under various retentions. Find retention s.t. P(insolvency) < threshold (e.g. 0.5% = 1-in-200).Retention level that keeps solvency at target confidence level.
Cost MinimisationTotal cost = (a) risk experience fluctuation reserve cost + (b) RI premium cost. Retention ↑ → (a) ↑, (b) ↓. Find retention minimising (a)+(b). Simulation needed for (a).Economically optimal retention balancing self-insurance vs external RI.
Key Factors & Product Differences
  • More reinsurance (lower retention) when: greater uncertainty about future claims · lower acceptable P(ruin) · higher variance of benefit distribution · smaller free assets
  • Free assets: key driver of both risk tolerance (XL) and financial RI need. Large insurer may not need XL for normal policies — only catastrophe and exceptional SI.
  • LT fixed-benefit (IP, CI): model claim incidence stochastically (benefit amount known)
  • ST indemnity (PMI): model both claim incidence AND claim amounts stochastically
  • RI availability: reinsurers may refuse cover for new/inexperienced cedants (e.g. CI without stringent UW) or charge very high premiums — limiting cedant's options.
Once retention is determined, treaty terms must be reviewed at each renewal — claims experience of the ceded portfolio will affect future RI premium rates (especially for XL).
