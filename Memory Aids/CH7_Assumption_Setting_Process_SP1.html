A · Morbidity – Inception Rates (IP, CI, LTCI, PMI)
Morbidity – Inception Rates
SP1 Ch12 & Ch14
  • Collect dates of birth, entry, exit, diagnosis, first stopping work, notification, start and end of claim, and reason for exit
  • Subdivide by risk cell: age, gender, smoker status, occupation, benefit size, class of product (standalone/accelerated), individual/group, distribution source, region, duration from entry, underwriting status
  • For CI: collect by disease/procedure type separately; include RBNS and IBNR allowances
  • For LTCI: collect transitions between each care state separately; dates of transition to higher benefit level
  • For PMI: count policies taken out or renewed each year by age (Nₓ); count claim incidences by age and benefit category (Iₓᵏ)
  • Investigation period typically covers 3–4 years to balance data volume against heterogeneity risk
IFoA Exams
  • Collect morbidity experience data since the last pricing or premium review date
  • For LTCI: also collect by care setting (home vs. residential vs. nursing)
  • Investigate early claims and claims by notification date to detect anti-selective behaviour
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
SP1 Ch12
  • Accuracy, reliability and format of data must be assessed; data may be missing, inaccurate or biased
  • For CI: ensure Iₓʲ excludes claims diagnosed before the investigation period starts; includes RBNS and IBNR claims
  • For IP: claim inceptions beginning in the first d weeks of the investigation period must be excluded (deferred period adjustment); include inceptions up to d weeks after period end
  • Check for double-counting in population data (e.g. heart attack and bypass surgery counted as two events but one CI claim)
IFoA Exams
  • Carry out systematic data checks on all extracts; reconcile policy data against admin system control reports
  • Check for outlier/abnormal claims that distort crude incidence rates
  • Errors in data lead to incorrect assumptions — verify before any analysis
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) · Apr 2022 Q2(i)
SP1 Ch12
  • Construct central exposed-to-risk (EₓH) for healthy policyholders in the investigation period
  • Only include lives insured within the investigation period; adjust on a life-by-life basis using dates of entry and exit for partial exposure
  • IP inception rate: î = Iₓ / EₓH (where Iₓ = inceptions, EₓH = healthy central exposed-to-risk)
  • CI: î ˣʲ = Iₓʲ / EₓH for each disease j separately; combine individual rates to produce global incidence rate
  • LTCI: construct exposed-to-risk separately for each care state; estimate transition intensities between states
  • PMI: iˆₓᵏ = Iₓᵏ / Nₓ for each benefit category k
IFoA Exams
  • Adjustments to exposed-to-risk needed where life's exposure covers only part of the period
  • As data is largely computerised, adjustments can be made on a life-by-life basis
  • Ensure claims and lapses in the period are correctly reflected
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch12 & Ch14
  • Compare crude rates to a standard table (where one exists — UK has tables for CI and IP but not LTCI or PMI)
  • Express assumption as a simple adjustment to the standard table: e.g. iₓHD = a·iₓHD(S) + b; fit constants a and b to own data
  • Credibility: if own data lacks credibility for particular risk cells, weight towards market/reinsurer data
  • Industry (insured lives) data is more relevant than population data but may be heterogeneous due to different underwriting, claims management, policy conditions and distribution channels among insurers
  • Population data: can show trends but may double-count and may be out-of-date before published
IFoA Exams
  • Monitor actual claims against expected for each homogeneous group
  • For a small market (e.g. LTCI), credibility is low; supplement with reinsurer, market and population data
  • Compare resultant rates with competitor pricing as a final reasonableness check
Sep 2020 Q3(iii,iv) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
SP1 Ch12 & Ch14
  • Project rates forward to the midpoint of the insurance usage period (shelf-life of rates + average duration of policy)
  • Incidence rates vary by both age and calendar year; project for each future cohort year separately
  • Adjust for expected future changes: target market, underwriting standards, claim controls, policy wording, medical advances, diagnostic trends, economic conditions
  • For CI: project each disease incidence rate separately, then recombine; allow for potential overlap between conditions
  • Data underlying investigation is historic — actuary must apply judgement on future trends from market information
  • Consistency: morbidity inception assumptions must be consistent with investment return (both affected by economic climate) and with claims expense assumptions
IFoA Exams
  • Revise best estimate assumptions based on investigation results
  • Allow for shifts in cause mix (e.g. rise in mental health claims for IP); changes in ADL definitions for LTCI; introduction of screening programmes bringing forward claims
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
SP1 Ch13
  • Margins depend on: (1) degree of risk/uncertainty in the parameter; (2) financial significance of adverse outcomes; (3) company's attitude to risk; (4) level of free assets; (5) competitive pressures on pricing
  • Health and care insurance has a wider "funnel of doubt" than life insurance — volume of relevant statistics is less credible; claim outcomes are more subjective and influenced by social and economic factors
  • Approaches to incorporating margin: adjust the expected value directly; use a stochastic model; increase the risk discount rate
  • Reviewable premiums reduce the need for large embedded margins; guaranteed premiums require greater margins
  • For reserving: margin must also satisfy supervisory regulations; commercial considerations (competition) are irrelevant
IFoA Exams
  • For new/uncertain product (e.g. new disease, limited LTCI data): add larger margins; reduce as credibility builds
  • Assumptions approved by senior colleagues (e.g. Chief Actuary); process must be clearly documented
  • Independent review by reinsurer or external party may validate assumptions
Apr 2019 Q1(v) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii) · Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
B · Morbidity – Termination / Recovery Rates (IP & LTCI)
Morbidity – Termination / Recovery Rates
SP1 Ch12 & Ch14
  • For IP: collect dates of claim start, recovery, death in claim; split by duration since sickness onset, deferred period, duration since claim payments started, and cause of claim
  • Recovery rates typically decrease with increasing claim duration (less serious cases resolve first); may exhibit "spikes" at certain durations (e.g. change in benefit level, further claims management)
  • Longer deferred periods → higher probability of recovery before claim begins; also selects for longer-duration sicknesses
  • For LTCI: collect transitions to higher benefit levels and deaths in each claim state; no return transitions normally occur
IFoA Exams
  • Distinguish recovery (return to work), mortality in-claim and withdrawal from claim for IP
  • For LTCI: recovery rates likely immaterial; focus is on mortality in-claim as the principal termination decrement
  • Collect cause-of-claim data as a key driver of termination patterns
Sep 2024 Q3(i,iii)
SP1 Ch12
  • Validate claim termination dates; check for missing recovery notifications
  • For IP: claim inceptions beginning in the first d weeks of investigation must be excluded from recovery rate calculations to avoid bias
IFoA Exams
  • Reconcile claims in payment at start and end of period against reserves
  • Check for duplicate or incomplete claim records
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
SP1 Ch12
  • Construct central exposed-to-risk for claiming policyholders (EₓC) separately from healthy lives
  • IP recovery rate: ρ̂ = Rₓ / EₓC where Rₓ = number of recoveries
  • IP in-claim mortality: μ̂ = Mₓ / EₓC where Mₓ = deaths from claiming state
  • LTCI: for each transition from state i to state j: λ̂ᵢʲ = Dᵢʲ / Eᵢ where Dᵢʲ = transitions and Eᵢ = exposed-to-risk in state i
  • Termination rates occurring at claim durations over two years can typically be ignored for short-term IP analysis
IFoA Exams
  • Exposed-to-risk calculated separately for claiming lives; adjustments made for partial periods
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch14
  • Compare actual recovery rates to expected under current basis; investigate if any changes in deferred period mix, claims management or cause mix explain the deviation
  • Adjust for expected differences in target market, underwriting, product design features, and economic anomalies in past data
  • Use reinsurer short-term accident data or industry standard tables to supplement where own data is limited by deferred period
IFoA Exams
  • Investigate if cause mix has shifted (e.g. more mental health claims → lower recovery rates)
  • Assess whether claims management or rehabilitation interventions have materially affected observed rates
Sep 2024 Q3(i,iii)
SP1 Ch12 & Ch14
  • Project termination rates forward; allow for expected changes in: deferred period mix, medical advances in rehabilitation, changes in claims management intensity
  • Experience improvement or deterioration over time should be allowed for in projected rates
  • Average claim termination rates may be used for group pricing, weighted by expected business mix (age, gender, etc.)
IFoA Exams
  • Adjust recovery rate assumptions for projected changes in cause mix
  • For LTCI: project impaired mortality trends; improving general mortality extends claim durations
  • Allowance for partial/rehabilitation benefits affects the net termination rate assumption
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(ii)
SP1 Ch13
  • Recovery rate assumptions are subject to significant uncertainty from human behaviour, economic conditions and medical developments — margins must reflect this
  • Consistency required: termination rates must be consistent with claims expenses (volume of in-payment claims determines claim management cost)
IFoA Exams
  • If cause-mix shift is uncertain, margins in termination rates should reflect that uncertainty
  • Document rationale for any departure from prior assumption; sign off at appropriate level
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii)
C · Mortality (Pre-claim & In-claim)
Mortality
SP1 Ch14
  • Standard mortality tables typically exist and are used as the basis; morbidity and mortality parameters are usually expressed as an adjustment to a standard table
  • Collect deaths from healthy state (Dₓ) and deaths from claiming state (Mₓ) separately
  • For CI accelerated: death is a trigger for payment — must be included in claim count
  • For CI standalone: death releases reserve; must be counted in decrement data
IFoA Exams
  • Investigate pre-claim mortality (affects exposure) and impaired in-claim mortality (affects termination) separately
  • For LTCI: impaired mortality is the primary in-claim decrement
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
SP1 Ch12
  • Reconcile death counts to policy records; check for missing death notifications which would inflate the exposed-to-risk and understate mortality rates
  • Ensure deaths within the deferred period are correctly excluded from IP claim inception counts
IFoA Exams
  • Reconcile death claims against insured lives data; check for missing or duplicated notifications
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
SP1 Ch12
  • Pre-claim mortality: μ̂ₓ = Dₓ / EₓH (deaths from healthy / healthy central exposed-to-risk)
  • In-claim mortality for IP: μ̂ₓ = Mₓ / EₓC (deaths from claiming / claiming central exposed-to-risk)
  • In some cases, exposed-to-risk techniques used; in others, adjustment to a standard table may be applied directly without full graduation
IFoA Exams
Not specifically covered in exam solutions
SP1 Ch14
  • Compare actual deaths to expected under standard table; derive adjustment factors (A/E ratios) by age group
  • Standard table comparison enables easy comparison with industry average and signals when pricing basis needs updating
  • Population data is an important source for mortality trend evidence, even if not directly applicable to insured lives
IFoA Exams
  • For LTCI: improving general mortality means more lives reach care age AND longer in-claim durations — both adverse
  • Assess whether improving mortality trend is continuing using population and CMI data
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
SP1 Ch14
  • Project mortality improvements forward; in a well-developed country, continued improvement is expected
  • Adjustment to standard table: e.g. mortality at age x = a·qₓ(standard) + b; project a and b allowing for future improvements
  • Consistency: investment return and inflation assumptions affect the discount applied to future mortality cashflows
IFoA Exams
  • Project pre-claim and in-claim (impaired) mortality separately for LTCI
  • Guaranteed return-of-premium on death (LTCI) may be more costly than expected — review assumption
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
SP1 Ch13
  • Mortality improvement uncertainty compounds over long policy durations (LTCI, IP) — margins must reflect projection risk
  • For CI accelerated: mortality is a claim trigger, so adverse mortality directly increases claim cost; margin needed accordingly
IFoA Exams
  • Mortality improvement uncertainty requires higher margins for long-term products
  • Document basis of improvement projection (e.g. CMI model, expert judgement) and have it signed off
Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
D · Lapse & Persistency
Lapse Rates
SP1 Ch14
  • Analyse the company's own recent experience split by: policy type (standalone/accelerated), series, sales channel, sales agent, policy duration, premium size, benefit size, premium frequency, payment method, age, gender, smoker status
  • Lapse rates are highly dependent on distribution methods and after-sales service — own data is strongly preferred; market data is much less useful than for demographic assumptions
  • No population equivalent of lapsing exists — population data cannot inform lapse assumptions
  • PMI: track non-renewal at each annual renewal as the equivalent of lapse
IFoA Exams
  • Also split by: whether premiums are guaranteed or reviewable; distribution channel; individual vs. group
  • For PMI: track non-renewal rates; also lapse during the waiting period and by policy duration for new products
Apr 2021 Q3(ii) · Sep 2022 Q3(v)
SP1 Ch14
  • Check for duplicate lapse records; ensure lapses and surrenders are correctly distinguished in data
  • Assess whether any special factors (adverse economic conditions, competitor product launches) have distorted recent experience
  • Market data sources are much less useful for lapse rates than for other demographic assumptions due to high heterogeneity between insurers
IFoA Exams
  • Verify lapse dates align with premium payment records
  • Ensure lapses and surrenders are correctly distinguished
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
SP1 Ch14
  • Lapse rates are calculated from own data: number of lapses in each duration band ÷ exposed-to-risk in that band
  • Key focus: duration from policy entry — determines whether acquisition costs have been recouped before lapse
  • For clawback: compare early lapses against policies exposed to clawback at each duration; split by product type (credibility may be an issue)
IFoA Exams
  • Lapse investigation: compare number of lapses in each period to the relevant portfolio exposed-to-risk to produce lapse rates
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch14
  • Consider factors influencing policyholder choice to lapse: economic (affordability), political (state benefits), commercial (competitor products), awareness (perceived product necessity)
  • PMI lapse/non-renewal is strongly affected by premium increase size, quality of state healthcare (waiting lists) and economic confidence
  • Selective lapsing: healthier lives more likely to lapse → remaining pool deteriorates → morbidity worsens — consistency check required with morbidity assumptions
IFoA Exams
  • Investigate whether selective lapsing is occurring (healthy lives exiting, impaired lives remaining)
  • Assess whether distributor churning or economic downturn is driving observed lapses above expected
Sep 2020 Q3(iii,iv) · Apr 2022 Q1(i) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
SP1 Ch14
  • Adjust lapse rates derived from historical analysis to reflect expected future conditions: economic outlook, competitiveness of product, changes in distribution channel or after-sales service
  • Differences in characteristics between data used and future new policyholders must be adjusted for
  • Consider the anti-selection effect: higher lapse rate → worse remaining pool → higher morbidity rates; assumptions must be made consistently
  • For LTCI in economic downturn: non-trivial premiums → increased lapses expected; CI standalone: low premiums → little incentive to lapse
IFoA Exams
  • Set best estimate allowing for: expected economic conditions, competitiveness, product features (surrender penalties, payment holidays)
  • Regular experience analysis on lapses ensures adequacy of loading over time
Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Apr 2022 Q1(ii)
SP1 Ch13 & Ch14
  • Lapse rates are among the most uncertain assumptions — human behaviour is difficult to predict; margins must reflect this
  • Effect of lapses on profit: early lapses cause loss where insufficient premiums received to recover initial strain; later lapses (without surrender value) benefit the insurer
  • Consistency: lapse assumption must be consistent with morbidity (selective lapsing), volume (fixed expense loading) and clawback (commission credit risk)
IFoA Exams
  • In economic downturn or high-inflation environment, add margins to lapse assumptions
  • Commission clawback and surrender penalties reduce lapse risk; reflect in governance framework
Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
E · Expenses & Expense Inflation
Expense Rates & Inflation
SP1 Ch15
  • Reflect expected expenses from: initial acquisition, initial medical underwriting, initial administration, renewal administration, renewal commission, investment management, withdrawal, claim administration, termination
  • Distinguish direct (fixed and variable) vs. indirect (overhead) expenses
  • PMI has the highest renewal administration costs of all product types (premium review, policyholder communications and re-rating each year)
  • PMI investment expenses are unimportant (reserves small; short duration)
IFoA Exams
  • Divide expenses into: acquisition, pre-claim administration, claim assessment, claims management
  • Identify and separately record one-off/non-recurring expenses to exclude from ongoing basis
  • Commission is collected and excluded separately from the expense investigation
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
SP1 Ch15
  • Reconcile total expenses to management accounts or audited accounts
  • Identify and exclude one-off (non-recurring) expenses that would not be expected to recur
  • Split fixed vs. variable: variable expenses depend on business volume; fixed costs must be apportioned
IFoA Exams
  • Carry out spot checks; reconcile to management accounts
  • Ensure all data extracts are subject to systematic checks (number of items, total premiums per product)
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
SP1 Ch15
  • Express expense assumptions as: per-policy initial expense, per-policy renewal expense, per-claim expense — loaded into premium as % of premium, % of sum insured, or fixed amount per policy (or combination)
  • For pricing: fix initial expense assumption at a forward-looking level (e.g. if basis to apply for 4 years, set to expected expense in year 2 or 3, not today's figure)
  • Consider both inflation of expenses during policy term AND inflation between now and future policy issue dates
IFoA Exams
Not specifically covered beyond the SP1 material
SP1 Ch15
  • Monitor actual expenses against expected, allowing for inflation since last analysis
  • Expense inflation is a blend of price inflation (office costs, IT) and earnings inflation (staff salaries); weight historically and project the blend forward
  • Consider: current price and earnings inflation rates, expected future rates, differential between fixed and index-linked gilt yields
IFoA Exams
  • Assess whether fixed costs are adequately recovered at current policy volumes
  • Check whether expense loading method (by premium, sum insured, fixed per policy) remains appropriate for the business mix
  • Expense loading by sum insured best reflects risk profile for CI and IP targeting higher socio-economic segments
Sep 2022 Q1(iii,iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(v)
SP1 Ch15
  • Project future expense levels allowing for expected new business volumes; lower volumes → higher per-policy fixed expense loading
  • Consistency: expense inflation must be consistent with investment return assumption (real return = investment return minus expense inflation)
  • New business volume and mix assumption (covered in Ch16) directly affects whether fixed costs will be recovered
IFoA Exams
  • In high-inflation environment, both fixed and variable expense components increase
  • If volumes are lower than expected, per-policy loading must increase to recover fixed costs
Sep 2024 Q1(i,v) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch13
  • Expense assumptions linked to new business volumes — volume risk creates uncertainty; margins must reflect this
  • Consistency principle: expense margins must be consistent with new business volume and lapse margins (both affect per-policy expense loading)
IFoA Exams
  • Ensure expense loadings are sufficient to cover anticipated costs; higher margins if volumes uncertain or inflation elevated
  • Regular experience analysis on expenses ensures the loading remains adequate given observed lapse experience
  • For new/uncertain products: greater need for margins to reflect approximations and unknowns
Apr 2019 Q1(v) · Sep 2022 Q1(iv)
F · Benefit Amount & Claims Inflation (PMI & LTCI)
Benefit Amount & Claims Inflation
SP1 Ch15
  • For long-term products (IP, CI, LTCI): benefit level fixed at outset; average benefit size is part of the business mix assumption, not a separate claim amount assumption. Exception: LTCI indemnity cover requires separate claim cost modelling
  • For PMI: forecast number of claims AND average claim amount for each benefit category k: ĉₓᵏ = Cₓᵏ / Iₓᵏ (total amount / number of claims)
  • PMI benefit inflation driven by: changes in medical treatment protocols, hospital charging structures, future age profile of portfolio — not just general CPI
  • IP benefit inflation: best linked to national earnings index (or occupation-specific earnings). CI: sum insured usually fixed. LTCI: link to nursing/residential home cost index; insurers often cap rate of benefit inflation
IFoA Exams
  • Collect average claim amounts by condition, treatment type, provider and region
  • For LTCI: collect cost of care data separately by care setting; distinguish care cost inflation from general inflation
  • For PMI: track claim amounts at the level of individual procedures to detect protocol or tariff changes
Apr 2021 Q3(ii) · Apr 2022 Q2(i)
SP1 Ch15
  • Best starting point for PMI claim amounts: analyse insurer's own recent experience, or that of a similar insurer if own experience is insufficient
  • Adjust average claim amount for: differences in policy conditions; differences in negotiated hospital fee schedules; future provider capacity and charging trends
  • Alternatively, use treatment price lists for private hospitals as an independent cross-check
IFoA Exams
  • Identify large individual claims that may be outliers distorting averages
  • Reconcile total claims paid to management accounts; check for double-counting or missing claims
Apr 2022 Q2(i)
SP1 Ch12
  • PMI: record total claim amount paid from each cohort by benefit category k (Cₓᵏ); compute average cost per claim: ĉₓᵏ = Cₓᵏ / Iₓᵏ
  • Include all relevant IBNR and RBNS in both Iₓᵏ and Cₓᵏ; exclude claims incurred in previous years of the investigation
IFoA Exams
Not specifically covered beyond SP1 material
SP1 Ch15
  • PMI: hospital deals with specific providers make claim costs more predictable; presence or absence in past experience vs. future must be explicitly allowed for
  • If provider capacity reduces, treatments become more expensive — this is benefit inflation, not just protocol change
  • Industry data for PMI is generally not available due to insurers' reluctance to disclose data in sufficient detail
IFoA Exams
  • Compare actual average claim amounts and total costs to expected; decompose into frequency and average cost movements
  • Investigate whether medical inflation, new protocols, new procedures or tariff changes explain the deviation
  • Assess whether preferred hospital fee schedules have been renegotiated at higher rates
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
SP1 Ch15
  • PMI: project average claim cost forward using a medical cost inflation assumption that incorporates protocol changes, hospital charging trends and age profile shift — not just CPI
  • A consumer price index is often used as an approximation for medical cost inflation, but does not capture salary inflation in medical staff or new technology costs above general inflation
  • For all products with inflating benefits: model explicitly in cashflow projection; whichever benefit increase method is chosen (fixed % or index-linked), include in model
IFoA Exams
  • Set claims inflation assumption reflecting specific drivers: staff salaries, protocols, provider pricing power
  • Where a fixed fee schedule with providers exists, inflation assumption is lower and more predictable
  • For IP and LTCI with salary-linked or care-cost-linked benefits, project benefit inflation separately from general CPI
Apr 2022 Q2(i,ii) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
SP1 Ch13 & Ch15
  • Medical cost inflation is highly uncertain and subject to social, political and economic influences — one of the wider elements of the "funnel of doubt" for H&C insurance
  • Consistency: benefit inflation assumption must be consistent with investment return (real return = investment return minus benefit inflation)
  • If benefits are inflation-linked, index-linked bonds provide the best matching asset — and should be assumed in the investment return assumption
IFoA Exams
  • In high-inflation environment: add margins on claims inflation, especially for guaranteed-premium products
  • Reviewable premiums reduce need for embedded inflation margins; guaranteed premiums require larger ones
  • Document rationale relative to market data and provider tariff evidence; obtain sign-off
Sep 2024 Q1(i,vi) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii)
G · Investment Return
Investment Return
SP1 Ch15
  • Consider the likely mix of assets that will back the contract: fixed interest bonds for fixed benefits; index-linked bonds for inflation-linked benefits; possible equity component for LTCI (to mirror real care cost increases)
  • For CI: investment assumption is not very important — reserves small relative to cashflows
  • For pre-funded LTCI (especially single premium): investment return is a key cashflow item — very important
  • For PMI: policies are one year duration; claims settled quickly; investment return is not important
IFoA Exams
  • Collect actual investment income (after capital movements) split by investment class: dividends, interest, realised and unrealised capital gains
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch15
  • Investigate returns that assets of the intended mix are yielding now (and recently)
  • Assess extent of reinvestment risk: if assets can be matched to liabilities (e.g. single-premium LTCI with matched bonds), investment return can be locked in and uncertainty is reduced
IFoA Exams
  • Reconcile investment income to audited accounts; check market values against Bloomberg or equivalent
  • Check credit ratings and currency of assets against independent sources
Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
SP1 Ch15
  • For market-consistent valuation: set investment return as the risk-free rate irrespective of actual assets held (risk-neutral calibration); stochastic approach still requires volatility and correlation assumptions for actual asset types
  • Traditional pricing basis: use expected earned return on the actual investment mix backing the contract
IFoA Exams
Not specifically covered beyond SP1 material
SP1 Ch15
  • The first step is to establish how sensitive the results are to the investment assumption — if sensitivity is low, the exact value chosen is not critical
  • Sensitivity increases with: size of reserves (relative to cashflows), duration of policy, onerousness of guarantees
IFoA Exams
  • Monitor actual investment income against expected; note that for short-tail products (PMI) this is less material
  • Assess impact of interest rate changes on value of claims-in-payment reserves (IP)
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
SP1 Ch15
  • Predict future returns: consider current yields → apply judgement on future economic environment → allow for reinvestment risk
  • Consistency: investment return assumption must be consistent with expense inflation and benefit inflation assumptions; and with the risk discount rate (risk-free rate + risk premium)
  • Risk discount rate: based on the insurer's required profit margin at the original pricing date; risk-free component should reflect current economic conditions
IFoA Exams
  • In high-inflation environment: consider whether ALM (inflation swaps, index-linked bonds) is in place; if not, carry more uncertainty in the investment return assumption
  • Investment return, expense inflation and risk discount rate must be mutually consistent in the profit test
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
SP1 Ch13 & Ch15
  • Size of investment return margin depends on: financial significance of the assumption; extent of guarantees; availability of matching assets
  • For Solvency II reserves (Best Estimate Liability): use risk-free rate; Risk Margin uses 6% cost-of-capital approach applied to present value of projected non-hedgeable risks
  • If matching assets available (e.g. for single-premium LTCI), margins can be smaller; if reinvestment risk significant, larger margins needed
IFoA Exams
  • Risk Margin uses 6% cost-of-capital applied to projected non-hedgeable risks; verify drivers are appropriate
  • Document investment strategy change and its impact on the net assumption basis; obtain sign-off
Apr 2019 Q2(i,iii) · Sep 2019 Q1(viii)
H · Reinsurance Costs & Recoveries
Reinsurance
SP1 Ch12
  • Reinsurer's data: more credible than own data but may need adjustment for insurer's specific underwriting and claims management practices
  • Reinsurer typically provides data in return for a share of the business; useful especially for new products or where own data lacks credibility
  • Reinsurer is also a useful source of market benchmark data for claim rates and trends
IFoA Exams
  • Collect actual reinsurance premiums paid and recoveries received by product line and treaty
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
SP1 Ch15
  • In-house commission and clawback parameters may be compared to market figures and those provided by reinsurers or consultants as a reasonableness check
IFoA Exams
  • Regular (e.g. quarterly) review of reinsurance premiums and reinsured sum at risk
  • Check reinsurance recoverables; allow for expected counterparty default
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
SP1 Ch12
  • For credibility weighting purposes: reinsurer data is generally more credible than own data, but less relevant (adjusted for specific underwriting and claims practices)
IFoA Exams
Not covered in provided material
SP1 Ch13
  • Reinsurance is not part of the contractual arrangement between insurer and policyholder — profit/loss on reinsurance should not be included in the premium review exercise itself
  • For Solvency II: reinsurance recoverables assessed separately from gross BEL; allow for expected counterparty default
IFoA Exams
  • Reinsurance excluded from premium review cashflows; counterparty default included in Solvency II BEL calculation
  • Seek independent advice from reinsurers on whether they are also considering premium rate changes
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
SP1 Ch12
  • As the insurer's own data credibility grows over time, reliance on reinsurer data and the level of reinsurance can be reduced
  • For new product: total reliance on reinsurer or consultancy data; monitor and refine as own experience emerges
IFoA Exams
