Approaches to Regulation — Health & Care Insurance
SA1 Ch.10 · IFE 2024 CONSOLIDATED STUDY MATRIX
① Regulatory Objectives
Why Insurance is Regulated
  • Policyholders pay premiums in advance — must trust insurer to pay future claims
  • Insurer may collapse before claim arises; policyholders cannot easily monitor solvency
  • Danger from unscrupulous or incompetent operators
  • Controls needed over: classes written, people running company, financial strength, published information
  • Compensation schemes exist if individual companies fail
Regulator Objectives (Examples)
  • Promote safety & soundness of supervised companies
  • Secure appropriate degree of consumer protection
  • Ensure appropriate consumer outcomes
  • Promote effective competition in consumers' interests
  • Protect & enhance integrity of the financial system
  • Promote development of the insurance industry
The Balancing Act & Drivers of Change
  • Too little regulation: higher risk to policyholders, potential losses
  • Too much regulation: ↑ compliance costs → ↑ premiums, ↓ competition, ↓ coverage
  • Indirect costs: changing consumer behaviour, reduced product innovation (hard to quantify)
  • Drivers of change: Economic environment (2008 crisis → US reform; Solvency II), desire to harmonise across jurisdictions, advances in risk management
  • Supra-national bodies (IAIS, EIOPA) set global standards
② Types of Regulatory Responsibilities — Overview
Regulatory Responsibilities — Health & Care Insurers
Produced via rulebooks + supervisory statements / guidance notes
Responsibility What it covers Key Point
Authorisation Permission requirements & process for writing specific classes; separate applications per class EU: authorised in one member state → passport to write business across all EU states under home-state supervision
Business Standards Day-to-day conduct: senior management systems & controls; market conduct; TCF Some generic (all companies); some insurance-specific. Affects every aspect of operations.
Supervisory Reporting Form, frequency & content of financial and management reports to regulator Standardised templates + possible additional management reports. Balance sheet, revenue account, business plan (3+ year forward look), stress tests.
Monitoring Regular assessment visits; governance review; supervisory reviews of regulatory reports Degree of scrutiny ∝ inherent risk of company. High-risk firms get more attention.
Valuation & Capital Rules for asset valuation, liability calculation, assumptions, capital requirement levels & triggers Minimum levels required + supervisory intervention levels; may be fund-specific (LT fund vs shareholder fund)
Fit & Proper Vetting senior position holders; ongoing assessment; withdrawal of approval Criteria: integrity, competence & experience, qualifications & training
Actions by Regulator Powers where insurer acts inappropriately Proportionate to nature, scale & complexity. Range: fines → revoking permission → criminal prosecution.
Transfer of Surplus Rules on moving surplus from LT insurance fund to shareholders' fund Regulator may also assess quality/liquidity of capital remaining post-transfer
Transfer of Liabilities Takeovers, mergers, internal group restructuring — protecting all three policyholder groups UK: Court approval required + Independent Expert report. USA: indemnity vs assumption reinsurance.
③ Authorisation & Business Standards in Detail
Authorisation
  • Authorisation required for each class of business (e.g. health & care separately from annuity)
  • New product types may require additional authorisation applications — can take time
  • Some jurisdictions: new product must be pre-approved before sale to consumers
  • Other jurisdictions: no pre-approval but expect discussion of high-risk or novel products
  • EU passporting: authorised in one member state → can write in any EU state, supervised by home-state regulator
Senior Management Systems & Controls
  • Compliance framework
  • Risk management systems
  • Internal systems & controls
  • Outsourcing governance
  • Appointment of fit & proper persons + statutory actuarial roles (see §⑤)
  • Regular meetings with senior management at the firm
  • Regulatory supervisory & disciplinary processes
Market Conduct — Treating Customers Fairly (TCF)
Rules require firms to act honestly, fairly and professionally in accordance with client best interests. Interpretation of TCF varies significantly by jurisdiction.
TCF Area Key Requirements Notable Detail
Communications All communications: clear, fair, not misleading Specific rules on use of past performance in promotions. Cold calls & distance marketing (internet/email) have separate rules.
Remuneration Disclosure Disclose firm's nature, services & remuneration method at first contact Commission or 'commission equivalent' basis must be explained. Some jurisdictions ban commission on some/all products → fee-based system. Details still communicated.
Product Information Key features document at or before point of sale Customer-specific expenses & charges; projected values at multiple investment returns (e.g. 3%, 6%, 9% at 5/10/15 yrs for unit-linked); impact of charges shown as reduction in yield (RIY). Fixed rates in some jurisdictions.
Compensation Rules for dealing with complaints and paying compensation to customers Typically an independent complaints body / ombudsman exists alongside regulator
Other Suitability assessment; record-keeping; deal execution; right to cancel; claims handling; transaction-related info; unit-linked underlying assets All items in best interests of firm anyway — reduce risk of bad publicity from unhappy policyholders
④ Valuation, Capital Requirements & Regulator Actions
Valuation & Capital Requirements
Given significant uncertainty in asset and liability values, regulators specify rules and/or principles for valuation and capital requirement derivation.
Asset Valuation Market value vs book value; treatment of unlisted assets; models used; restrictions on asset types (admissibility limits)
Liabilities Types of business included; treatment of discretionary increases (bonuses); treatment of options and guarantees embedded in contracts
Assumptions Discount rate derivation (best estimate, risk-free or prudent); demographic assumptions; persistency; options/guarantee valuation method; whether reinsurance credit allowed
Reinsurance How to allow for reinsurance recoveries; risk of reinsurer default — may limit credit taken or require net-of-RI liability floor
Capital Requirements Derivation method / principles; minimum levels required; levels triggering supervisory intervention; fund-specific requirements (LT business fund vs shareholder fund)
Transfer of Surplus LT insurance fund → shareholder fund. May allow any disclosed surplus, or may require a minimum capital buffer to be maintained. Post-transfer liquidity assessed by regulator.
Actions Available to Regulators (where insurer acts inappropriately)
Require insurer to remedy situation Public censure Fines Require purchase of reinsurance Require additional capital to be held Revoke permission to carry out regulated activities Revoke managers' ability to hold senior positions Require insurer to pursue merger or acquisition Refuse to approve transfer of liabilities Criminal prosecutions Proportionality applies: actions should match the nature, scale and complexity of risk. Some jurisdictions: rule-based triggers. Others: regulator has discretion. Key factors: proximity of failure, confidence insurer will act on instructions, potential impact on policyholders & financial system stability.
⑤ Fit & Proper Persons & Statutory Actuarial Roles
Fit & Proper  ·  Statutory Actuarial Roles
Covered by regulatory rulebooks and 'fit & proper' persons regulations
Fit & Proper Assessment Criteria
  • Personal characteristics — e.g. integrity, honesty
  • Level of competence & experience — appropriate industry seniority
  • Qualifications & training — relevant professional credentials
Assessment includes: background checks, relevant industry experience review, possible interview with regulator's staff. Ongoing assessment required — approval can be withdrawn.
Statutory Actuarial Roles — by Jurisdiction

UKWith-Profits Actuary, Reviewing Actuary — see APS L1
Other JurisdictionsAppropriate Actuary or Appointed Actuary
Tasks similarregardless of jurisdiction or title — see responsibilities below
Statutory Actuarial Responsibilities (examples)
Ensuring adequacy of technical provisions
Ensuring appropriateness of methodology & assumptions for technical provisions
Assessing sufficiency & adequacy of data used
Expressing opinion on adequacy of reinsurance arrangements
Firm's Obligations to the Statutory Actuary
Keep actuary informed of business plans; seek advice on implications for policyholders
Pay due regard to the actuary's advice
Provide adequate resources, data and systems as reasonably required
Holders cannot fulfil other roles causing a conflict of interest, or must declare such roles to the regulator. Roles covered by 'fit & proper' persons regulations.
⑥ Other Legislation & Professional Guidance
IFoA Actuarial Professional Standards (APSs)
APS L1 Duties & Responsibilities of Life Assurance Actuaries. Applies to Chief Actuaries, Small Insurer Chief Actuaries, With-Profits Actuaries, Appropriate Actuaries, Reviewing Actuaries — in UK authorised insurers & friendly societies.
APS L2 FSMA 2000 (Communications by Actuaries) Regulations 2003. Applies to AFHs, With-Profits Actuaries, Appropriate Actuaries of UK authorised insurers. Also applies to Chief Actuaries. Triggers for mandatory regulator communication: legislation contravention; assets may be insufficient; policyholder interests not considered; inadequate insurer–actuary relationship.
APS X1 Applying Standards to Actuarial Work (eff. Jul 2017). Principles for determining which standards apply. Applies to IFoA members outside UK too.
APS X2 Review of Actuarial Work. Applies to all members — extent to which independent peer review is required for actuarial work on which recipients are entitled to rely.
APS X3 The Actuary as Expert in Legal Proceedings. Applies to actuaries appointed as expert witnesses in courts, tribunals or similar.
Other Guidance Other professional actuarial bodies may issue jurisdiction-specific guidance. Must be followed alongside IFoA guidance in accordance with APS X1. All IFoA members covered by The Actuaries' Code regardless of location.
⑥ Consumer Legislation — Fair Contract Requirements
Features of a Fair Contract
  • Drafted so customers can easily understand terms
  • Cannot contain unfair penalties (e.g. cancellation — only recover losses reasonably incurred)
  • Must have clearly defined cancellation rights
  • Must have clearly defined and explained charges
  • Must have a clear definition of how the contract may be changed or varied
  • Must provide a clear explanation of how premiums for a policy could be varied
Examples of Unfair Terms
  • Company can change service characteristics after customer is bound by contract
  • Customer required to pay for unused services when they end the contract
  • Customer required to pay disproportionate charges when ending contract
  • Company has discretion over price after customer is bound
  • Customer not allowed to cancel the service under any circumstances
'Unfair' = contrary to good faith, causes significant imbalance in parties' rights & obligations to consumer's detriment. Consumer protection body may be independent of insurance regulator.

⑦ Equality Legislation
Protected Characteristic General Position What is Permitted Key Insurance Implication
Age Cannot discriminate on grounds of age (UK: 2010 Equality Act). Both direct and indirect discrimination unlawful unless justified. Age bands OK if based on appropriate data about risks. Age limits permitted with signposting to alternative provider. Differential treatment OK if 'proportionate means to a legitimate aim'. Insurance prices may still vary by age where it genuinely reflects risk or cost. Differences in treatment resulting from maternity/pregnancy costs are unlawful.
Disability UK: disability = physical or mental impairment with substantial, long-term adverse effect on normal daily activities. HIV, cancer, MS & some visual impairments also protected. Decline or impose special terms if based on information that is relevant to the risk, from a reasonable source, and the decision is reasonable given that information. Key data quality issue: decisions must be justifiable by evidence. Special terms can be imposed — but only with sufficient, relevant, accurate data. Illustrates importance of good underwriting data.
Gender EU Gender Directive: equal treatment of men and women in access to goods & services. Originally had opt-out for insurance — removed by ECJ from 21 December 2012. None for new business from Dec 2012 in EU. Reviewable premiums not treated as new business. Some products already required unisex rates (e.g. annuities with protected rights). Cannot use gender as rating factor for new business. Proxy factors highly correlated to gender also not permitted (indirect discrimination). Contingency loadings needed for gender mix uncertainty. Significant for annuities and protection products where mortality/morbidity differs materially by gender.
Other characteristics Race, religion & belief, sexual orientation, gender reassignment, marriage & civil partnership, pregnancy & maternity — all protected Indirect discrimination may be justified if proportionate means to a legitimate aim (except pregnancy & maternity — no justification) Anti-discrimination laws may be overseen by body independent of insurance regulator. Exact features of fair vs unfair treatment differ between jurisdictions.
⑧ Regulatory Approach by Jurisdiction
Aspect UK USA China Australia South Africa
Regulator(s) PRA (prudential) + FCA (conduct). Both statutory duty to co-ordinate. State-level regulators (50 states). NAIC co-ordinates. FIO (monitoring) + Federal Reserve (systemic). CBIRC — established April 2018 by merger of CBRC + CIRC. Responsible for banking & insurance supervision and enforcement. APRA — Australian Prudential Regulation Authority. Est. 1 July 1998. Funded by supervised industries. PA (Prudential Authority) + FSCA (Financial Sector Conduct Authority). FSB split Apr 2018. Twin peaks model.
Approach Type Principles-based (14 PRA/FCA principles for business). Risk-based supervision — high-risk firms get most scrutiny. Prescriptive rules + overall asset adequacy analysis. Recent inclusion of some principles-based elements. Mix of detailed rules and principles. Significant changes via C-ROSS framework. Risk-based supervision — targets areas of greatest risk to entity and financial system. SAM framework (risk-based). FSCA aims to enhance market integrity, fair customer treatment, financial education.
Capital Framework Solvency II (currently under UK Government review post-Brexit — assumed to continue at time of writing). Risk-Based Capital (RBC) standards. NAIC sets model laws; significant uniformity of reporting/valuation/capital across states. C-ROSS (China Risk Oriented Solvency System). Similar to Solvency II but reflects specific Chinese insurance market circumstances. LAGIC (Life & General Insurance Capital standards). Strong parallels to Solvency II; 3-pillar structure. Implemented 1 Jan 2013. SAM (Solvency Assessment & Management). SII-based. Implemented 1 July 2018. Part of broader Twin Peaks financial sector reforms.
International Links Works with EIOPA; participates in IAIS. Left EU Jan 2021 — no longer part of EU passporting. FIO advises on international matters. Federal Reserve has consolidated oversight of systemically important insurers. Large, rapidly growing market. Higher growth than most western countries since insurance industry reopened in 1980s. APRA mission: establish and enforce prudential standards ensuring financial promises are met in stable, efficient and competitive system. PA responsible for licensing, ongoing supervision and enforcement across banks, insurers, financial conglomerates and market infrastructure.
Key Feature FCA: product lifecycle concern from design stage; can ban products. PRA: not required to ensure no insurer fails — appropriate degree of protection only. NAIC provides guidance & model laws but has no enforcement power over states. Each state implements its own regulations. C-ROSS intended to reflect Chinese specifics, not be a direct copy of Solvency II. Covered in more detail in Chapter 13. Identified limitations of earlier regime (risk-based capital, risk mgmt, disclosure). LAGIC developed to address these — covered in Ch. 13. Similar Twin Peaks model to UK. One regulator for prudential (PA ≈ PRA), one for market conduct (FSCA ≈ FCA).
Exam Note · You are not expected to know jurisdiction-specific details for the SA1 exam. Focus on the ideas and concepts each jurisdiction illustrates — principles-based vs rules-based, twin peaks model, risk-based capital, passporting, harmonisation rationale, etc.
