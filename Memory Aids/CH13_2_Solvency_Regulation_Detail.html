① United Kingdom — Post-Brexit Solvency Position
Background & Brexit Impact
  • Left EU: 1 January 2021 — no EU-wide arrangement for financial services regulation at point of exit.
  • Prior to exit, UK was part of EU and Solvency II applied fully to UK insurers.
  • At exit, UK was aligned with Solvency II. Post-exit alignment remains uncertain.
  • UK insurers can no longer passport services into EU — become third-country undertakings.
  • Third-country equivalence rules (covered in Ch.11) now apply.
Reform Considerations (PRA)
  • UK government considering reform of UK insurers' capital rules.
  • Aim: potentially reduce capital requirements on UK insurers post-Brexit.
  • Ongoing discussions with EU on financial services; outcome still uncertain (as at May 2023).
  • This section will be updated in future editions as reform details emerge.
② USA — State-Based System, RBC, PBR, ORSA & Key EU Differences
Regulatory Structure
  • State-based system: 50 states + DC + 5 territories, each with own insurance department and commissioner. Ultimate solvency responsibility rests with each state.
  • NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners): coordinates model laws, standards and guidance. Supports uniformity of reporting, valuation and capital across states.
  • Federal Insurance Office (FIO): monitors insurance sector; advises on international & national matters. No supervisory role.
  • Federal Reserve Board (FRB): consolidated oversight of systemically important insurers.
  • Insurer solvency requires: adequate reserves + capital + surplus at all times. Conservatism is pervasive.
Risk-Based Capital (RBC) & PBR
  • RBC: Standard formula applied to statutory financial statement balances. Produces capital requirement from which regulators set intervention thresholds.
  • Factors cover: asset risk · insurance risk · business risk. Considers size and risk profile of insurer.
  • Intended to identify weakly capitalised companies — similar to MCR under Solvency II, not SCR.
  • PBR (Principle-Based Reserving) from 1 Jan 2017 (new contracts): replaces 150+ years of formula-driven reserves.
  • Three reserve calculations — hold highest of: (a) net premium reserve (prescribed assumptions) · (b) deterministic reserve (single prescribed scenario) · (c) stochastic reserve.
  • Reflects own morbidity, lapses, expenses, option costs — "right-sizes" reserves per product complexity.
  • Existing business continues on previous valuation basis.
SMI, ORSA & Key Differences vs EU
  • SMI (Solvency Modernisation Initiative, 2008): critical self-examination of US framework. Covered capital, governance, group supervision, statutory accounting, reinsurance.
  • ORSA: Annual self-assessment of risk management adequacy and current/prospective solvency. Annual ORSA Summary Report to lead state commissioner. Complies with IAIS ICP 16.
  • Insurer must: conduct ORSA at least annually · document internally · report to regulator.
  • vs EU — Approach: Similar purposes; different determination of reserves and capital.
  • vs EU — Capital: RBC identifies weak capitalisation; SII requires higher capital reflecting specific firm risks. SII more responsive to firm's own portfolio.
  • vs EU — Reserves: US historically formulaic with prudence margins; SII requires market-consistent BEL + explicit risk margin.
③ China — C-ROSS Framework (Three Pillars & Differences to Solvency II)
C-ROSS: Three-Tiered Regulatory Framework
Administered by CBIRC (China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission). C-ROSS has similarities to Solvency II but reflects the characteristics of the Chinese market — which is still relatively young vs US/EU.
Pillar I
Quantitative Capital Requirements
Factors applied to premium, reserve and catastrophe risk by line of business. Covers quantitative risks for life business: insurance risk, market risk, credit risk.
Pillar II
Qualitative Capital Requirements
Three components: (1) Rating system to determine level of regulatory intervention. (2) Explicit risk management process assessment — equivalent to ORSA (called Solvency Aligned Risk Management Required Assessment). (3) Series of liquidity risk indicators.
Pillar III
Transparency & Disclosure
Covers unsupervisable risks including systematic risk of entire market. CBIRC aims to build mechanism to improve transparency and communication across market participants.
Under Solvency II ORSA gives the insurer more room to use internal models. Under C-ROSS, all insurers follow the same rules — no internal models allowed.
Key Differences vs Solvency II
  • Asset valuation: C-ROSS uses GAAP accounting value (directly from balance sheet, minor amendments). Solvency II marks assets to market.
  • Discounting: C-ROSS discount yield ≈ GAAP liability yield. Solvency II uses current risk-free curve.
  • Internal models: Not allowed under C-ROSS. Solvency II permits internal models giving firm-specific capital.
Challenges for Health & Care Insurers
  • Complex production process for results
  • Regular & timely C-ROSS reporting = significant additional workload
  • Pillar II: substantial effort to establish risk & capital management frameworks meeting CBIRC requirements
  • Need to streamline & automate reporting to reduce time and operational risk
④ Australia — LAGIC & APRA
LAGIC Framework — Three Pillars
APRA (Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) takes a risk-based approach — targeting regulatory resources at highest-risk areas. LAGIC (Life and General Insurance Capital) project began 2009; effective 1 January 2013.
Pillar I
Prescribed Capital Amount (PCA)
99.5% VaR over 1 year. Calculated using APRA Standard Method (insurance + asset + concentration + operational risk) or APRA-approved internal model. ≈ SCR under Solvency II. APRA can apply supervisory adjustment (↑ or ↓) — similar to SII capital add-on.
Pillar II
Governance & Risk Management
Good risk management and governance requirements. Includes Individual Capital Adequacy Assessment report — similar to ORSA under Solvency II.
Pillar III
Disclosure
Regular disclosure requirements by insurer. Promotes market transparency.
Equivalence with Solvency II
  • LAGIC has strong parallels to Solvency II — three-pillar structure, risk-based capital, ORSA equivalent.
  • At time of writing: LAGIC has provisional equivalence with Solvency II.
  • Provisional equivalence = granted for a 10-year period, with possible extension for further 10-year periods.
  • Also exists: full equivalence and temporary equivalence (limited period ending 31 Dec 2020 or date prudential regime deemed equivalent, extendable one further year).
  • Equivalence under Solvency II covered in Chapter 11.
Supervisory adjustment: APRA can increase or decrease required capital if methodology does not produce appropriate outcome — analogous to the capital add-on under Solvency II.
⑤ South Africa — SAM Framework
SAM — Solvency Assessment and Management
Implemented July 2018 by the Prudential Authority (PA). Largely based on Solvency II, with adaptations for the South African market and economic environment.
Pillar 1
Quantitative Requirements
SAM balance sheet + capital requirements. Main capital requirement = Own Funds to survive a 1-in-200 year loss event (≈ SCR under SII). Standardised formula or internal model. Promotes financial soundness from regulatory perspective.
Pillar 2
Qualitative Requirements
Sound governance and risk management. Supervision of insurers' qualitative controls and risk frameworks.
Pillar 3
Reporting & Disclosure
Regulatory reporting (private to PA) + public disclosures to market. Promotes transparency.
Regulators & SA-Specific Adaptations
  • PA (Prudential Authority): licensing, ongoing supervision, enforcement of banks and insurers.
  • FSCA (Financial Sector Conduct Authority): market conduct regulation; efficiency & integrity of financial markets; consumer protection; financial literacy.
SA Adaptations to Solvency II Base
  • Risk-free rates calibrated to the South African context (not EU risk-free curve).
  • Market risk calibrated to the South African market environment.
  • Microinsurance: reduced minimum capital requirement for new microinsurers — encouraging wider access to insurance for low-income populations.
  • Guiding principle: SAM should comply with Solvency II third-country equivalence criteria from the outset.

Most country regulators in Asia are building more robust regulatory and solvency frameworks, though sophistication varies widely by jurisdiction. The approach ranges from Solvency II equivalency status to much simpler minimum capital requirements.

⑦ Latin America — Solvency II-Type Adoption
Country-by-Country Developments
  • Mexico: First country in Latin America to adopt a Solvency II-like regulatory framework.
  • Brazil: Granted provisional Solvency II equivalence from 1 January 2016 for ten years. Introduced its own equivalent of ORSA — companies must comply.
  • Chile: Developing a risk-based capital approach modelled after Solvency II. ORSA equivalent also introduced.
  • Many other countries: introducing aspects of Solvency II such as risk-based capital calculations — significant change from previous regulatory models.
  • Consequence: Introduction of risk-based capital requirements in Latin America likely to lead to a greater focus on risk management across the region.
Why the Shift to Solvency II-Type Frameworks?
  • EU's Solvency II has had a significant impact on insurance solvency regulation globally — many jurisdictions have used it as the reference point for reform.
  • Risk-based capital more accurately reflects the actual risk profile of insurers vs flat formulaic approaches.
  • Equivalence status with Solvency II supports cross-border reinsurance and group supervision arrangements.
  • IAIS Insurance Core Principles (ICPs) provide a global baseline — many reforms align to these, which themselves are influenced by Solvency II thinking.
  • ORSA requirement is broadly consistent with IAIS ICP 16 (Enterprise Risk Management) — adopted across US, EU, China, Australia, South Africa and Latin America.
