SA1 Ch.5 §2 · Distribution
Methods of Sale, Remuneration & Regulation
5 Main Channels ① Brokers (commission/fee) · ② Tied agents / ARs (commission) · ③ Own salesforce / DSF (salary+commission) · ④ Direct marketing (no remuneration) · ⑤ Worksite marketing

Channel affects: demographic experience · product pricing · withdrawal rates. Complex products suit advised channels; simple products suit direct/online.

SA1 Ch.5 §3 · Consumer Protection
Mis-selling Risks by Product
IP Issues Income replacement <100% · Benefit may not keep pace with income growth · No cover for unemployment · Benefits reduced if income drops (ABI Best Practice)
CI Issues Only listed conditions covered · Insurer definitions more restrictive than medical · Perceived as covering "all critical illness" · Confusion between CI and IP
LTCI Issues Cash not care — fixed benefits may not cover actual care costs · Poor investment performance on unit-linked policies · Misnomer "long-term care insurance"
PMI Issues Restricted vs comprehensive — NHS waiting list trigger not understood · Acute vs chronic confusion — cover ceases when condition becomes chronic
Tech Distribution Risk Insurtech: online sale without advice → higher product confusion risk · Algorithm-driven "nudging" toward unsuitable products
Poor practice → fines, compensation, operational costs, regulatory change. Quality of selling process is the primary defence.

SA1 Ch.5 §4–5 · Stakeholders
Role of the State & Role of Employers
State–Insurer Interplay IPAugments subsistence benefits CINo State counterpart — private fills gap LTCIMeans-tested — insurer targets assets above threshold PMIComplements NHS; reduces waiting times
Means Testing Targeted State spending on those most in need · Introduction driven by: demographic change, low-tax environment, competing public expenditure demands
Future State Role Political party changes create uncertainty · Government can affect products via: legislation, taxation, State benefit changes, national health system changes, communication
Employer Benefits Attract & retain staff · Tax efficient (premium as business expense) · Group cheaper per head than individual · Economies of scale + experience rating · MHD basis for PMI
Future State role is crucial to insurance strategy planning. Group schemes often compulsory — reduces anti-selection for insurer.

SA1 Ch.6 §1–2 · Underwriting
Medical Underwriting & Genetics
Front-End UW Full medical info at proposal stage · Exact exclusions known upfront · Disadvantage: long forms, personal disclosure deterrent · Responses: loaded premium, deferral, decline, PEC exclusion, different product, reinsure, facultative
Back-End UW Moratorium approach — immediate cover, check at claim · No UW at entry, PEC excluded · Moratorium period typically 5 yrs pre-entry; covered after 2 yrs treatment-free · UK PMI & ASU predominant use
Psychosocial Factors IP claims driven by non-medical factors: poor work attendance, life events, stress · Predictive flags: GP visit frequency, work attendance record, personal circumstance changes
Genetics Anti-selection: tested individuals seek higher cover → insurer premium shortfall · Information asymmetry: insurers want test results; many countries prohibit use · UK: no genetic UW for IP/CI/LTCI (exception: Huntington's for high-value life)
CI most at risk from genetic testing (cancer & heart disease genetically influenced). PMI least at risk (annual reviewable premiums). "Once only" group IP UW: full evidence upfront, no re-UW on benefit increases.

SA1 Ch.6 §3–4 · Environment
Counterparties & External Influences
Key Counterparties Outsourcers (SLA governs) · Brokers (brokers' balances = credit risk) · Hospitals & consultants (usual & customary fees) · Independent investigators (IP claim validation) · Medical evidence gatherers
Demographic Aging population → fewer taxpayers, State strain, demand for LTCI/PMI · Living longer → more PMI claims, longer IP claims, LTCI cost rise · Fewer children → aging accelerated, fewer new policyholders
Medical & Economic Medical advances: ↓CI incidence but ↑early detection claims · New diseases · PMI: new expensive treatments · Business optimism drives group scheme uptake · Employment security affects claim rates (presenteeism / claim whilst you can)
Political, Social & Pandemics Govt changes → uncertainty in State provision · ↑Claim consciousness (less stigma, aware of cover) · ↑Litigation · COVID-19: excess mortality, Long COVID morbidity impact · Pandemics affect capital adequacy — stress testing required
Mental health: 1 in 4 adults affected annually. Was UK's top IP claim cause in 2017. Climate physical/transition/liability risks all relevant to health insurer balance sheets.

SA1 Ch.6 §5 · Medical Conditions
Key Medical Conditions — Mortality, Morbidity & Regional Variation
Top Mortality Causes (Global 2019) Heart disease (16%) · Stroke (combined CVD = 15m deaths) · COPD (3.3m) · Lung cancer (1.8m) · Diabetes (1.7m) · Dementia (7th, doubled since 2000) · Life expectancy rose 66.8→73.4 yrs (2000–2019)
Specific Diseases — Insurance Impact Heart disease: risk factors (smoking, obesity, BP) · Cancer: improving survival → longer CI/PMI claims · Stroke: leading disability cause in UK · COPD: UK premature deaths 2× EU avg · Liver disease: only rising cause in England — preventable
Regional Variations Africa: HIV/AIDS & respiratory infections lead · Americas: violence 2nd · High-income: neoplasms & CVD dominate · Low-income: communicable diseases still leading but declining · Mental disorders top 4 in high/high-middle income
Insurer Implications Changing survival → historic data less credible · Need better / more data: wearables, big data, cohort studies · IADLs as early disability indicators · Long-term care cost prediction increasingly difficult
Non-communicable diseases caused 70% of global deaths. NCDs rising in low-income countries. 78% of absolute NCD deaths in low- and middle-income countries.
