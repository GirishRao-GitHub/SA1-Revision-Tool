P — Purpose of underwriting 4 A's
Remember: "4 A's" — Anti-selection · Allocation · Avoidance of over-insurance · Alignment with pricing
  • Anti-selection prevention — stops high-risk lives getting cover at standard rates; both claim frequency and average claim cost are affected
  • Allocation to homogeneous risk groups — classification so the expected experience of each group matches its premium; ensures fairness across policyholders
  • Avoidance of over-insurance — financial underwriting caps benefit relative to pre-claim income; prevents incentive not to return to work (IP)
  • Alignment of actual experience with pricing assumptions — the underwriter enforces the risk group boundaries set by the pricing actuary; keeps claims close to what was priced
Exam tip: framing the purpose before describing the process earns marks. Lead with "Underwriting achieves fair risk classification by…"
A — Approaches (3 types) Full · Moratorium · MHD
Think of a traffic light — strictest to loosest. Full = red (check everything). Moratorium = amber (check at claim). MHD = green (no check).
Full medical UW
Evidence at entry. Clear certainty for policyholder. Most common for long-term products (IP, CI). Costliest per policy. Outcomes: accept / load / exclude / decline.
Moratorium
No UW at entry. PECs excluded for first 2–3 consecutive clean years (5-yr lookback). Check at claim stage. Suited to PMI and group CI. Cheaper upfront.
MHD (disregarded)
No exclusions for pre-existing conditions. Greatest anti-selection risk — priced accordingly. Most common: group PMI. Also offered on transfer from group to individual PMI.

Moratorium mechanics (exam favourite)

  • Lookback period: conditions existing in the ~5 years before inception are excluded
  • Clean period: exclusion is waived after ~2–3 consecutive years with no treatment or symptoms
  • Clock resets if treatment is received — another consecutive clean period must run
  • Advantage: immediate cover offered, lower per-policy UW cost; disadvantage: policyholder uncertainty, reputational risk from claim refusals

PMI renewal UW terms

  • No Worse Terms (NWT) — new insurer accepts on terms at least as comprehensive as old policy
  • CPME — NWT with only the original exclusions carried forward
  • Switch — changing insurer at renewal, sometimes with simplified UW (declaration of good health only)
C — Collection of evidence PMMS + F
Remember: PMMS + F — Proposal form, Medical attendant report, Medical examination, Specialist tests + Financial underwriting
  • Proposal form — factual questions only (within the proposer's knowledge); age, gender, occupation, residence, smoker status, BMI, medical & family history
  • Primary Medical Attendant Report (PMAR) — report from the applicant's GP; required above certain sum-at-risk thresholds (non-medical limits)
  • Medical examination — independent examination by a doctor/nurse appointed by the insurer; used for larger or higher-risk cases
  • Specialist tests — e.g. HIV test, blood tests; triggered by specific risk factors or very large benefit amounts
  • Financial underwriting — salary/earnings evidence; caps benefit to a replacement ratio (prevents over-insurance and removes incentive not to return to work)

Non-medical risk factors also collected

Occupation / class Leisure pursuits Country of residence Overseas travel plans

Non-medical limits (when to escalate)

  • Thresholds vary by age and benefit type; e.g. IP sum-at-risk <£X → proposal form only; £X–£Y → PMAR; >£Y → full examination
  • Higher sum-at-risk = more UW effort; also varies by product (CI thresholds often at £100k+)
  • Consent of proposer required before accessing sources 2–4
Psychosocial factors (IP) — Predictive flags that go beyond reported medical conditions: GP attendance patterns inconsistent with known problems; poor work-attendance history; adverse life events; poor resilience to common but unexplained symptoms (fatigue, headaches). Some insurers ask "how much time did you take off?" rather than listing conditions.
E — End decisions (outcomes) LEAD + P
Remember: LEAD + P — Load, Exclude, Accept standard, Decline, Postpone
  • Load — accept with a higher premium to reflect elevated risk; most common outcome for sub-standard but insurable risks in long-term products
  • Exclude — accept but with a named condition excluded from cover; most common for PMI where specific PEC exclusions apply
  • Accept at standard terms — the desired outcome for most cases; insurer aims to keep loaded/declined cases to a minimum to avoid bad feeling and distributor deterrence
  • Decline — risk too high to quantify or price; generates bad feeling and extra admin; a last resort
  • Postpone — risk is elevated but temporary (e.g. recent operation, ongoing treatment); review at a later date

Additional decision tools

Reduced benefit Alternative product 100% facultative reinsurance (zero retention)

Misrepresentation at claims stage — 3 categories

  • Innocent (honest & reasonable) → pay claim in full
  • Careless (insufficient care, not deliberate) → proportionate remedy (e.g. reduce payout)
  • Deliberate / reckless → void policy from inception; most severe cases only
Non-contestability clause: after a defined period (often 2 years) on risk, the insurer cannot void a claim for careless or innocent misrepresentation — only outright fraud. Simplifies claims management for both parties.
D — Differences: group vs individual FAME
Remember: FAME — Free Cover Limit, Actively at work, Moratorium/MHD preferred, Experience assessed at group average
Group UW features
Free Cover Limit — no individual UW below threshold; FCL higher for larger/compulsory schemes
"Actively at work" on day cover starts — cheap, verifiable from HR records
MHD or moratorium more common than full medical UW
UW done at group level — some unhealthy lives acceptable if balanced by healthy ones
Take-over terms for schemes transferring from another insurer
Individual UW features
Full medical UW standard for long-term (IP, CI, LTCI)
Moratorium standard for individual PMI
Greater anti-selection risk — customer chooses insurer and benefit level
Non-medical limits trigger escalating evidence requirements
Psychosocial factors more relevant (especially IP)

Anti-selection controls unique to group

  • Minimum scheme size and minimum take-up rate (voluntary schemes)
  • Benefits linked to salary formula (reduces adverse selection vs. self-chosen amounts)
  • Compulsory membership — greatest protection against anti-selection
  • "Once only" UW on group IP — full UW at entry, no further UW on future benefit increases

Exam-specific angles (frequently tested)

Online / simplified UW
Fewer questions → speed & cost savings but higher non-disclosure risk, anti-selection, reinsurer renegotiation, and morbidity deterioration. Must still allow adequate risk assessment.
Automated / tele-UW
Speed, consistency, cost savings. Risks: no human judgement, underwriters lose skill on routine cases, periodic sampling vs human decisions needed.
Claims underwriting
Even with full UW at entry, insurer must check at claim stage for non-disclosure and consistency with original proposal data. Liaison between UW and claims teams is critical.
Genetics restrictions
Cannot require genetic tests or use results in most markets. Creates anti-selection risk. Insurer must price or exclude broadly. Ban reduces UW accuracy — may force broader exclusions or higher premiums.
Data science in UW
Banking / wearables / social media data for risk classification. Benefits: automated UW, better risk selection. Risks: data privacy, regulatory constraints, algorithmic bias, ethics.
Reinsurer UW support
UW manuals, training, facultative capacity for large cases, claims control. Particularly vital for new products or markets where insurer lacks own experience data.
