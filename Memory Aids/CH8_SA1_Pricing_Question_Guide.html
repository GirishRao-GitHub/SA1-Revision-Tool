S1
Define Benefit Design & Scope
What exactly are we pricing?
Always include
▾
Benefit boundaries
  • State precisely what the benefit covers — inpatient only, or IP + OP + day-care; lump-sum vs. income; full ADL or partial ADL trigger
  • Define exclusions, waiting periods, deferred periods, and survival periods — each one directly reduces the risk premium
  • Identify sub-limits, co-pays, excesses, and caps — these must be removed from the gross claim cost before loading
  • Specify whether benefits are fixed-term or whole-of-life; indemnity or fixed benefit — affects the pricing framework chosen
  • Clarify reinstatement features, floaters, and family floater design — these are separate pricing streams
Product-specific triggers
  • PMI/Indemnity: IP vs. OP vs. day-care boundary; cashless vs. reimbursement; network tier
  • CI: condition list, survival period, whether multi-pay or single; own-condition vs. accelerated definition
  • IP: own-occupation vs. any-occupation definition; deferred period options (1, 3, 6, 12 months); salary definition
  • LTC: ADL failure threshold (2 of 6 vs. 3 of 6); home care vs. residential vs. nursing — each is a separate state
  • Group IP: whether cover is compulsory (removes anti-selection) or voluntary; link to employer sick-pay scheme
The examiner expects you to show the product design affects the risk premium formula — not just to list features. Always connect scope to pricing mechanics.
S2
Data Collection & Sources
Internal, industry, reinsurer, international
Always include
▾
Internal data
  • Own claims experience — incidence rates, average claim amounts, claim duration by age/gender/occupation
  • Expense data — split by initial (acquisition), renewal (admin), and claims handling
  • Lapse and persistency data by policy duration — critical for long-tail products (IP, LTC)
  • Investment return on reserves — more material for long-tail (IP, LTC) than short-tail (PMI)
External & industry data
  • IRDAI / GIC pooled industry statistics — aggregate claims rates, loss ratios by product class
  • Reinsurer morbidity tables (Swiss Re, Munich Re, RGA) — often most granular source available, especially for CI and IP in India
  • International benchmarks (UK CMI, US SOA, Australian data) — adjusted for Indian demographics, healthcare cost level, and product design differences
  • ICMR cancer/cardiac incidence data — cross-check for CI condition-specific rates
  • Medical literature on disease progression, survival rates, improving outcomes — impacts trend assumptions
  • Market research: consumer willingness to pay, take-up rate estimates, competitor pricing survey
New product / new market challenges
  • If own experience is nil: rely entirely on reinsurer data and international benchmarks — apply conservative margins
  • Adjust international data for: different disease prevalence, healthcare cost level, underwriting stringency, claim definition differences
  • COVID-19 years (2020–21) should be excluded or treated carefully — utilisation was atypically suppressed then elevated
IP / Group note For group IP: employer's own absence records are a primary source. Sector-specific data (mining vs. white collar) materially changes the incidence rates. In a new IP market, reinsurer guidance is the only credible source — apply a higher risk margin.
LTC note No meaningful India LTC experience data exists. Use UK, US, and Australian tables as a starting point; adjust for Indian longevity, cultural caregiving norms, and public provision. Data uncertainty is the single biggest pricing challenge for LTC.
S3
Data Analysis & Credibility
Deriving rates from raw experience
Always include
▾
Experience investigation
  • Derive claim incidence rates by age, gender, and key risk factor — actual-to-expected (A/E) analysis against pricing basis
  • Calculate average claim amounts / average claim duration — separately by cause/benefit class where possible
  • Identify medical cost inflation trends — India medical inflation running 10–15% p.a., significantly above CPI
  • Stratify by underwriting method, duration from entry, NCD level, group size, industry — A/E varies significantly across these dimensions
  • Investigate new procedures, drugs, and technology — these shift treatment patterns and claims costs (technology-led cost push)
Credibility weighting
  • Credibility formula: RP = Z·A + (1−Z)·E — blend own experience (A) with book/market rate (E) using credibility factor Z
  • Z = 0 for new/small schemes (100% book rate); Z → 1 as experience volume grows
  • In practice, PMI group given full credibility at lower data volume than IP — commercial pressure
  • Stochastic fitting of severity distribution — particularly important for high-cost top-up products and LTC tail
Burning cost
  • Burning cost = accumulation of past claims adjusted for: changes in membership, changes in cover, medical inflation
  • Must include IBNR (incurred but not reported) and RBNS (reported but not settled)
  • For IP: capitalise future expected payments from past claim inceptions (EPV of in-force claims) — do not just use paid claims
Examiners reward candidates who note WHY data is limited (new product, new market, novel design) and what practical steps are taken (reinsurer data, conservative margins, frequent review).
S4
Assumption Setting
Best estimate + margins for uncertainty
Always include
▾
Core morbidity assumptions
  • Incidence rate — by age, gender, occupation/deferred period; distinguish sickness inception from claim inception (= sickness inception × P(surviving deferred period))
  • Termination rates — recovery rate and mortality rate by duration on claim; critical for IP and LTC average claim duration
  • Medical cost / benefit inflation — separate from general CPI; must be projected forward explicitly (not assumed flat)
  • Persistency / lapse rates — by policy duration; selective lapse (healthier lives lapse more) worsens remaining pool
  • Selection effect — underwriting creates lighter early-year experience that grades to ultimate over ~5 years; avoid over-estimating
Other assumptions
  • Expense assumptions — initial (per-policy acquisition), renewal (per-policy p.a.), claims handling (per-claim or % of claims)
  • Investment return — more material for IP/LTC (long-dated reserves) than PMI (short-tail)
  • New business volume assumption — drives expense per policy; volume shortfall kills digital / packaged product economics
  • Anti-selection loading — explicit proportion assumed to purchase because of pre-existing impairment (individual voluntary product)
  • Risk discount rate (RDR) — uplift for novel/uncertain products to reflect higher parameter uncertainty
Documentation & sign-off
  • All assumptions must be documented, with sources, justification, and sensitivity to adverse deviation stated
  • Actuarial opinion / sign-off required; regulatory may require approval of assumptions (e.g. IRDAI product filing)
S5
Risk Premium Calculation
Product-specific formulae
Always include
▾
Formulae by product type
PMI / Indemnity: RP = Σk (ik × ACk) [incidence × avg claim cost by benefit class] CI (stand-alone): RP = ix × P(survive survival period) × SI CI (accelerated): RP = [ix(CI) + (1 − kx)·qx] × SI IP (inception/ann): RP = Σt [ survival · ix^d · ä_x · v^t · benefit ] IP (multi-state): track: Healthy → Sick → Claimant → Recovered → Dead LTC: RP = Σ care levels: inception × annuity per level × daily cost
Adjustments to gross cost
  • Apply deductibles, co-pays, sub-limits — reduce gross incidence rate and/or average cost
  • Apply underwriting loading above free cover limit for group business
  • Apply NCD adjustment — model long-term portfolio effect, not just year-1 discount
  • Unisex pricing (PMI): weight male/female rates by expected gender mix — anti-selection risk if actual mix differs
  • Inertia adjustment for cash plans — reduce expected claims for under-claiming (customers forget to claim small amounts)
Group-specific adjustments
  • Book rate lower than individual: less anti-selection (compulsory membership) + lower expenses (economies of scale)
  • Rate cells by age, gender, occupation, group size — larger scheme → more weight on experience, less on individual factors
  • Deposit and adjustment premiums for groups where exact membership is unknown at policy start
Always state which pricing approach you are using (inception/annuity vs multi-state) and why. IP and LTC use the same two frameworks — CI and PMI have product-specific formulae. Get the formula right for the product asked about.
S6
Loadings — from Risk Premium to Office Premium
Expenses, contingency, capital, profit
Always include
▾
Expense loadings
  • Initial expenses: commission / acquisition cost, system setup, underwriting cost, policy issue
  • Renewal expenses: policy admin, premium collection, regulatory reporting
  • Claims handling: TPA cost (typically 3–5% of PMI premium); case management cost for IP/LTC; medical verification for CI
  • Digital / new product: system development + mobile app maintenance = significant upfront cost to be amortised over expected in-force volume
  • Fixed expenses create economies of scale — low volume → high per-policy cost → pricing pressure in early years
Risk & contingency margin
  • Loading for adverse deviation — especially material where experience data is limited or product is novel
  • Guaranteed premium structure: higher contingency margin required (no future repricing option to correct mispricing)
  • Parameter uncertainty margin — reflect in higher RDR or explicit %-of-premium loading; document and justify the margin chosen
Profit & capital loading
  • Target profit margin to meet required ROC / WACC — expressed as % of PV premiums or % of initial commission (NPV basis)
  • Capital (SCR) loading: opportunity cost = (IRR − asset yield) × SCR locked in per annum
  • Long-tail products (IP, LTC) lock up capital for extended periods → cost of capital loading is significantly higher than for PMI
  • Reinsurance reduces SCR — net capital cost loading reflects net-of-reinsurance capital requirement
Other loadings
  • Premium tax / GST — added as a regulatory requirement (not part of actuarial loading but must appear in final premium)
  • Reinsurance cost (net of commission) as a separate loading — EPV of reinsurance premiums paid less EPV of recoveries
S7
Profit Testing & Sensitivity Analysis
Validate profitability and resilience
15+ marks
▾
Profit criteria
  • NPV (primary criterion): discounted profit signature at RDR; expressed as % of PV premiums or % of initial commission — allows comparison across products
  • IRR (secondary): intuitive concept but not unique if profit signature changes sign; cannot be directly related to market share
  • Discounted payback period (secondary): useful for capital-constrained firms; ignores cashflows after payback date — use alongside NPV
  • NPV positive ⟹ product meets target RDR; NPV as % PV premiums ties profitability to volume — scalable benchmark
Sensitivity & scenario testing
  • Sensitivity on all key assumptions — identify which drives greatest profit impact (typically: morbidity for CI/IP, medical inflation for PMI)
  • Stress scenario: medical inflation 2–3% above base; pandemic / mass hospitalisation event; economic recession → mental health / stress claims spike
  • Maximum probable loss (MPL) scenario — confirm capital adequacy for worst-case year
  • Low-volume scenario: if actual new business is 30–50% of projected, per-policy fixed costs rise sharply — test break-even volume
  • Stochastic simulation for high-severity, low-frequency products (LTC tail, pandemic pool) — deterministic stress is insufficient
Iterative loop
  • Profit test → if fails: revisit product design, distribution, profit requirement, or abandon product
  • Rate adjustment → re-test → repeat until competitiveness and profitability are simultaneously achieved
NPV is the preferred primary criterion — always state this and explain why IRR is a secondary measure (not unique, cannot be related to market share). This is a common examiner catch point.
S8
Reinsurance Considerations
Risk sharing, capital, expertise
15+ marks
▾
Seven roles of reinsurance (in pricing context)
  • Technical expertise — reinsurer provides incidence tables, pricing benchmarks, and product design guidance
  • Risk sharing / smoothing profits — reduces volatility of insurer's profit signature
  • New business strain financing — quota share provides upfront commission to fund initial expenses
  • Capital arbitrage — reinsurer holds less capital → lower total industry cost → lower net premium possible
  • Tax arbitrage — may allow pricing net of reinsurance for competitive advantage
  • Enabling larger risks — XL essential for large CI sum insured; makes individual large-risk cover viable
  • Regulatory approval — quota share reinsurer often co-approves premium tables and retains right to vary commission if rates change
Pricing impact
  • If reinsurance cost > benefit: net cost (EPV premiums paid − EPV recoveries) is a loading in the office premium
  • If arbitrage available: price net of reinsurance → competitive advantage
  • Reinsurer benchmark rate vs internal model: material divergence signals a modelling error or data problem
  • Very limited LTC reinsurance in India — insurer retains full long-tail risk; major constraint on product launch
S9
Premium Reviewability & Regulatory Filing
Guaranteed vs reviewable; IRDAI compliance
15+ marks
▾
Guaranteed vs reviewable premiums
  • Reviewable: allows repricing as experience develops; lower initial premium (no cost-of-guarantee loading); better marketability for new products with uncertain experience
  • Guaranteed: sales certainty; no reputational risk of increases; but requires heavier reserves, higher capital, and larger contingency margin — removes ability to reprice
  • For new markets (e.g. IP Country B scenario): launch with premiums guaranteed for initial period (e.g. 5 years), then reviewable — balances marketability with repricing need
  • Lapse risk on review: selective lapsation — high-risk lives retain cover on increase; healthy lives exit → adverse selection feedback loop must be modelled in profit test
  • Basis for review must be set out clearly in product literature; customers must understand when and why premiums may change
Regulatory requirements
  • File under IRDAI Use & File or File & Use procedure (IRDAI Insurance Products Regulations 2024) as applicable
  • Guaranteed renewability rule — insurer cannot refuse renewal (IRDAI health product regulations)
  • Over-insurance prevention: benefit-not-to-exceed-salary rule for group IP; insurer must verify against other income sources
  • Rating factor restrictions — check whether gender discrimination is permitted; regulatory ban must be reflected in pricing (community rating)
  • Novel products (packaged surgical, pandemic pool) — regulatory classification may be uncertain; IRDAI jurisdiction question must be resolved before launch
S10
Competitiveness, Anti-selection & Market Positioning
Market-rate benchmarking; anti-selection exposure
20+ marks
▾
Competitiveness check
  • Benchmark final rates against market — if uncompetitive: revisit product design, distribution, or profit requirement before abandoning
  • Monitor competitors' rating factors — finer differentiation → cherry-picking of lighter risks → anti-selection against broader pricer
  • Fixed expenses → volume matters → economies of scale → large players have cost advantage
  • Loss leaders: pricing below cost to gain market share — risk is adverse mix (loss leader over-sold; subsidising product under-sold)
Anti-selection management in pricing
  • Individual voluntary products: higher-risk individuals disproportionately purchase — price must explicitly reflect this loading
  • Compulsory group cover: anti-selection at entry is eliminated — lower rate justified; compulsory participation assumption must hold
  • Waiting periods, survival periods, at-work clauses — reduce adverse selection from imminent claimants at entry
  • CI: individuals with family history or early symptoms more likely to buy; full medical underwriting for individual removes selection at entry
  • LTC: adverse selection is highest of any H&C product — those nearest to care need are most likely to buy; long waiting periods and full medical underwriting essential
S11
Ongoing Monitoring & Premium Review
Actuarial control cycle in operation
20+ marks
▾
Monitoring framework
  • Actual-to-expected (A/E) analysis for all key assumptions: morbidity incidence, average claim cost, medical inflation, lapses, expenses, new business mix
  • Stratify monitoring: by age, gender, duration, NCD level, underwriting method, network, provider — focus on segments where A/E deviates most
  • Watch for new procedures, drugs, or technology shifting claims costs (technology-led cost push)
  • Expense monitoring: actual vs expected split by initial, renewal, and claims — adjusted for inflation and volume
  • Three types of premium review: (1) new business basis monitoring, (2) reviewable premium adequacy testing, (3) loss-leader reserve adequacy check
Premium review process
  • Collect experience since last pricing/review date; full experience investigation before deciding on any rate change
  • Early-year distortions (underwriting selection effect) must be understood before acting — avoid premature rate increases
  • Revise best-estimate assumptions; re-run profit test from review date; assess whether rate change is needed and at what level
  • Model impact of rate change on future volume and overhead coverage — higher rate → some lapses → fewer policies absorbing fixed costs
  • Assumption change need not force premium change — test significance first; communicate basis of review clearly with adequate notice
S12
Options & Guarantees Pricing
Additional benefit options; stochastic valuation
25 marks only
▾
Types of option relevant to H&C products
  • Purchase additional cover without medical evidence (e.g. at marriage, birth of child)
  • Renew policy at term end (e.g. CI at end of 10-year term) — guaranteed renewability
  • Buy-back: reinstate life cover after accelerated CI payout
  • All options: only exercisable when not currently claiming — to limit anti-selection
Option cost & valuation
  • Option cost = proportion exercising × average extra morbidity/mortality over standard rates at exercise date
  • Higher take-up → lower average impairment of exercisers (healthier lives dilute); lower take-up → worse health of exercisers — monitoring essential
  • Conventional method: assume 100% take-up → ultimate mortality (conservative; simple); extra benefit + extra premiums at standard select rates + admin + reserves pre/post exercise
  • North American method: realistic take-up rates; harder to obtain data; more accurate but more assumptions needed
  • Stochastic modelling: simulate proportions and claim experience in each risk sub-group → distribution of option cost → statistical adequacy level
Guarantee pricing
  • Resilience testing: can the premium cover outgoings under adverse scenarios over the guarantee period?
  • Additional reserves in early years (when risk is lower) to fund guaranteed rate through adverse periods
  • Disclosure of guarantee cost may drive policyholders toward reviewable contracts — marketability trade-off
  • Regulatory approval of the valuation technique and guarantee reserves required before launch
Options questions often ask for both the conventional and North American methods. Know the difference. Health options differ from financial options — some policies may always be "in the money" (e.g. someone with deteriorating health will always exercise). Restricting exercise dates reduces anti-selection.
