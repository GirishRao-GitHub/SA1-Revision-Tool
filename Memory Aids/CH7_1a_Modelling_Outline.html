Objectives & Basic Features of a Health Insurance Model
Prime Objective Enable actuary to advise company so it is run in a sound financial way
Model Points Data records representing a policy or group · Number influenced by: computer power, contract complexity/variability, stochastic vs det., purpose, time available, sensitivity of results
Operational Requirements Well documented · Clear & communicable · Independently verifiable · Not overly complex · Capable of development · Range of implementation methods
H&C Specific Features All cashflows inc. options/guarantees · Reserve & solvency margin cashflows · State-based cashflow projection · Asset–liability interactions (dynamic links) · Stochastic capability where needed

Deterministic vs Stochastic Models
Deterministic Fixed parameters → point estimate → "one question, one answer" · Sensitivity testing available
Stochastic Parameters have distributions → many simulations → probability distribution output · "distribution-in, distribution-out"
When Stochastic is Invaluable Assessing guarantees · Stable/predictable distributions (e.g. investment returns) · Year-on-year volatility · Identifying worst-case scenarios
Stochastic Disadvantages Complex & time-consuming · Results hard to explain · Dynamic links essential · Spurious accuracy risk
Calibration Risk-neutral (market-consistent): valuation, replicates market prices · Real-world: projections, realistic long-term expectations
H&C more volatile than pure life → stochastic especially important here

Sensitivity Analysis
Two Sources of Sensitivity Model point error (choice of model points) · Parameter mis-estimation (actual ≠ assumed)
Scenario Testing Multiple correlated parameters changed together (e.g. low yields + low expense inflation)
Key Parameters to Test Claim inception/recovery/transfer rates · Mortality · Lapses/non-renewals · Avg claim size (PMI) · Expenses & inflation · Benefit inflation · Investment yield · NB mix & volume · Tax
Uses Set pricing margins · Trigger product redesign · Validate model behaviour · Quantify profit/RoC variance · Determine capital/global reserves

Formula / Equation of Value Approach
Principle PV(income) = PV(outgo) → solve for premium · Numerator = PV(claims + expenses − inv. income) · Denominator = PV(1 unit premium)
Components Valued Claims (inc. inflation) · Premiums (inc. escalation, persistency, waiver) · Expenses · Investment income · Tax · Profit margin
Drawbacks No proper event timing · Reserves ignored · No capital needs · Negative cashflows ignored · No separate cashflow inspection · Fixed discount rate · No assumption variation over time · Complex products unsuited
Best suited to short-term contracts (e.g. 1-year PMI) where drawbacks are immaterial

Cashflow Approach
Process Select model points → project all cashflows (inc. reserves & capital) → discount at risk discount rate → find premium meeting profit criterion
Cashflow Items (Conv.) Premiums · Expenses (initial/renewal/claim) · Commission · Claims · Reserve contributions · Capital contributions · Interest · Tax
Additional (Unit-Linked) Unit allocations · Bid-offer spread · Excess claims cost · Risk charges · Unit/policy charges · FMC · Unit fund growth · Fund payouts on claim/surrender
Profit Criterion Per model point OR in aggregate (accepts cross-subsidy) · Risk discount rate = required return + statistical risk loading
Further Uses Profitability of existing business · Return on capital · Capital requirements (new business model / full model office)
Gold standard for long-term pricing · Not needed for 1-year PMI

Multi-State Modelling
When Used Policyholders exist in multiple states each with different cashflows · Most relevant to IP & LTCI
IP States Healthy ↔ Sick (claiming) → Dead / Lapsed · Deferred period handled by adjustment (not a separate state)
Transition Intensities μijx = annual rate of moving from state i to j at age x · Applied each period to project sub-cohort counts
Pricing Logic EPV(claims) = sick sub-cohort × benefit amount · EPV(premiums) = healthy sub-cohort × premium · Solve for P
Practical Limits Many sub-cohorts → spurious accuracy · Data availability determines feasibility · Combine cohorts, use fewer reliable intensities · Same framework used for reserving (different assumptions)

Model Office — Main Components
Policy Liability Model Projects cashflows (physical & notional) · Rolls forward in-force portfolio · Calculates supervisory reserves & capital at each year-end
Expense Model Full office: all company expenses · Profit test: policy-related only · Fixed overheads: modelled globally in full office only
Asset Model Simple (profit test): expected total return only · Complex (inv. strategy): stochastic, by asset class, income + gains, dynamic asset mix · Must be consistent with liability model (esp. inflation)
Consolidation & Verification Add up outputs → manual adjustments (un-modelled business, tax, contingency reserves) → verify vs supervisory valuation, vs prior results, vs simplified model
