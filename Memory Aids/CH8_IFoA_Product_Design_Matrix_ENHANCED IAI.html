01Benefit Structure
  • PMI can be structured on an indemnity basis, reimbursing the actual cost of eligible treatment, or as a fixed cash benefit paying a pre-agreed sum regardless of cost incurred — the choice affects pricing, risk exposure, and policyholder expectations.
  • The product must precisely define the scope of inpatient, outpatient, and day-care cover, as ambiguity over which care setting applies to a given treatment is a leading cause of disputes at claim stage.
  • Sub-limits (e.g. per-night room rate, per-procedure cap) and annual monetary limits are used to control maximum claim exposure and maintain premium affordability, but must be set at a level that still delivers meaningful cover.
  • Excesses, co-insurance, and co-payment mechanisms share cost with the policyholder, reducing claims frequency and severity while also acting as a deterrent to over-utilisation of covered services.
  • PMI policies must specify whether policyholders must use restricted network providers (giving the insurer cost control) or may access any licensed provider (giving the policyholder wider choice at higher cost).
  • The cashless settlement model (insurer pays provider directly) differs from the reimbursement model (policyholder pays upfront and claims back) — cashless is administratively complex but improves policyholder experience.
  • Waiting periods before certain conditions become eligible for cover (e.g. 9 months for maternity, 2 years for chronic conditions) are a key anti-selection and claims control mechanism in both individual and group PMI product design.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • IRDAI classification distinguishes health insurance into indemnity-based (compensates actual loss) and benefit-based (pays fixed amount) products — general and health insurers may offer both, but life insurers may only offer benefit-based products; life insurers are also permitted to offer health products on a unit-linked platform. [May 2024 Q3]
  • Product types specific to India include hospital cash / surgical cash benefits, AYUSH, homeopathy and acupuncture coverage, and overseas/domestic travel health products (only general/health insurers). [Nov 2024 Q2a; May 2024 Q3]
  • No Claim Bonus (NCB) vs premium discount is a key design distinction in the Indian market: NCB rewards claim-free years by increasing sum insured or reducing premium, whereas a wearable-linked health product offers premium discounts based on tracked health parameters regardless of claim history — these two mechanisms have different policyholder behaviour and anti-selection implications. [Mar 2017 Q1i; Sep 2016 Q2ii]
  • Inflation-indexed sum insured products automatically increase the sum insured in line with a medical inflation index each year (irrespective of claims), addressing the problem of inadequate cover due to rising medical costs — a design feature more prominent in the Indian market context where medical inflation runs well above CPI. [Sep 2016 Q2ii]
  • Overseas treatment top-up is a benefit structure where a basic domestic group PMI plan is supplemented with a rider paying for treatment abroad when domestic options are exhausted — design features include regional geographical tiers (e.g. Asia-only vs worldwide), companion and travel coverage, and cashless access to overseas provider networks. [Nov 2020 Q1v]
  • Unit-linked hospital cash / surgical benefit invests net premiums in unit funds, deducting morbidity charges, policy expenses and taxes from the fund — an unusual hybrid structure in India combining investment and health coverage; the investment risk is borne by the policyholder. [May 2024 Q2ic]
  • Prepaid surgical bundle (hospital-sold fixed-price product): a patient pays a once-off prepaid fee for a specific defined surgical procedure or medical package — removing price uncertainty and out-of-pocket shock; this is not a traditional insurance product and has no recurring premium. [May 2025 Q1a]
  • COVID-specific standalone product: benefit design decisions unique to pandemic products include whether quarantine benefits (home vs institutional) are covered, whether family floater is appropriate given infection transmission risk, accumulation risk across policies, and whether policy term should be less than 12 months. [Nov 2020 Q2ii]
  • Dental insurance product design in India requires deciding between comprehensive vs restrictive cover, free check-up inclusions (cost vs claim reduction benefit), unrestricted renewability clauses, and portability with other insurers — all regulated under IRDAI frameworks. [Mar 2018 Q1i]
  • Critical illness cover typically pays a lump sum on diagnosis of a specified condition; alternatively a severity-based tiered structure pays different percentages of the sum assured depending on the clinical stage or severity of the illness at time of claim.
  • Because CI pays a fixed benefit unrelated to actual treatment cost, the insurer has no exposure to medical inflation or the rising cost of specific procedures — this is a key pricing advantage over indemnity PMI products.
  • Children's cancer extensions and additional condition riders (beyond the standard CI list) are optional benefit add-ons that increase the product's appeal to families, though they introduce conditions where claims data may be sparse.
  • A Return of Premium (ROP) option refunds all or part of premiums paid if no claim arises before a specified trigger event — this reduces lapse incentive and enhances the product's savings appeal, primarily for individual policies.
  • Mental health conditions are increasingly included in CI definitions, but defining measurable severity thresholds (e.g. hospitalisation for a minimum number of days) is essential to maintain claims consistency and control.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Multi-pay CI with Indian product context: when designing a multi-pay CI product for India, incidence rates must be split by first and subsequent occurrences, and the inter-linkages between covered conditions must be modelled — the frequency of claims will be higher than standalone CI but claim severity per event may be lower as early-stage conditions are caught. [Apr 2016 Q2iii_a]
  • Capital requirements for CI are higher than traditional CI when launching multi-pay variants — the availability of sufficient capital to write expected volumes must be confirmed before product launch. [Apr 2016 Q2iii_a]
  • Risks of new product launch specific to IAI curriculum: product substitution risk (new CI variant cannibalises profitable existing product), over-sales capital strain, under-sales expense non-recovery, product design failure (does not meet customer needs), and product costing failure (parameter error in morbidity assumptions). [Apr 2016 Q2ii]
  • Gender-targeted marketing without using gender as a rating factor: in markets where gender is prohibited as a pricing variable (e.g. post EU Gender Directive), CI product differentiation can be achieved through gender-coded advertising (magazines, TV programming breaks), affinity marketing to gender-skewed occupations, highlighting gender-specific conditions covered, and varying sales team communication style. [Nov 2023 Q2iv]
  • The income protection benefit is typically expressed as a percentage of pre-disability earnings (e.g. 60–75%), known as the replacement ratio — this is capped to ensure the policyholder retains a financial incentive to return to work.
  • The deferred (waiting) period — the gap between onset of incapacity and commencement of benefit — is a key product parameter that determines premium, claim frequency, and coordination with state or employer sick-pay provisions.
  • The maximum benefit period determines how long benefit is paid (e.g. 2 years, 5 years, to age 65) and is a major driver of premium; linked claims provisions define when a new claim period starts for a recurring or related condition.
  • A stepped or proportional benefit structure pays a reduced benefit when the policyholder is working but earning less than their pre-disability salary, encouraging gradual return to work and reducing the cliff-edge cessation of benefit.
  • Rehabilitation benefit and phased return-to-work provisions are increasingly integral product features, recognising that early intervention reduces total claim duration and cost, benefiting both the insurer and the policyholder.
  • Benefit cessation triggers include recovery to pre-disability capacity, reaching the maximum benefit period, retirement, or death — clearly defining these in the policy wording is critical to avoid claims disputes.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Occupation-based definition for group IP: the IAI curriculum emphasises that in Indian group IP, an own occupation definition is typically adopted initially, potentially switching to a less restrictive definition (e.g. any occupation or ADL-based) after 2 years — the policy must specify this change clearly. [Mar 2021 Q1ii]
  • Stepped benefit structure (e.g. 100% of salary for first 6 months, then 50%) and benefit indexation linked to a wages index during claim are specific product design features tested in IAI group IP scenarios — the IFoA matrix mentions proportional benefit but not these specific step-down designs. [Mar 2021 Q1ii]
  • Early notification requirement (e.g. halfway through deferred period): requiring employers to notify the insurer of sickness absence before the deferred period expires enables early rehabilitation intervention — a design feature that reduces claim duration and is prominent in IAI group IP scenarios. [Mar 2021 Q1ii]
  • Accident-only IP variant: a product covering only accidental disability rather than both accident and sickness — advantages include simplified underwriting, more objective claims assessment, reduced epidemic/pandemic risk; risks include the blurring of accident/sickness causation, anti-selection by high-accident-risk occupations, and selective lapsation from full IP into the cheaper accident-only variant. [Dec 2022 Q2iii]
  • Key features of individual IP in the IAI context include: waiver of premium during claim, benefits that escalate differently in and out of claim, activity-of-daily-living (ADL) based definitions as an alternative to occupation-based, and counselling/rehabilitation services — the IAI papers present these as a descriptive feature list for a developing market. [Dec 2022 Q2i]
  • Micro-insurance IP design implications: less stringent definitions of incapacity, simpler claims triggers, policy documentation translated into local languages, monthly premium mode linked to variable incomes (e.g. weather/crop dependent), and collective/community enrolment mechanisms — fundamentally different operational design vs standard urban IP. [Mar 2017 Q2iv]
  • Long-term care benefits can be structured as a lump sum (providing flexibility but requiring the policyholder to manage the funds) or as a regular income (providing a predictable income stream aligned with ongoing care costs).
  • An indemnity LTC benefit reimburses actual care costs (subject to a cap), while a fixed benefit pays a set amount regardless of actual costs — the indemnity approach better reflects true care costs but exposes the insurer to care cost inflation.
  • Fixed-term benefit duration (e.g. 3 or 5 years) limits the insurer's maximum liability, whereas a whole-of-life benefit duration provides more comprehensive cover for the policyholder but creates very long-tail liabilities for the insurer.
  • The benefit level is typically linked to the degree of disability measured by Activities of Daily Living (ADL) — for example, benefit may be payable when the claimant is unable to perform 2 or more of 6 standard ADLs.
  • LTC products must distinguish between home care (care provided in the policyholder's own home) and residential or nursing facility care, as costs and triggers differ significantly between these settings.
  • A pre-funded LTC product is purchased in advance (often at age 50–65) with a deferred onset period, while an immediate-needs annuity is purchased at the point care is already required — these serve different customer needs and carry different risk profiles.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Pre-funded vs Immediate Needs — detailed feature comparison: IAI papers provide a granular feature list not present in the IFoA matrix: pre-funded policies may be unit-linked or conventional; a paid-up benefit may be available after a minimum premium period; disability benefits are tax-free; immediate-needs annuities may offer a capital guarantee (purchase price less benefits received paid on death); and if qualifying as an 'Immediate Needs Annuity', payments made directly to a registered care provider are not taxable in the hands of the insured. [Nov 2019 Q1ii]
  • Policyholder advantages/disadvantages not in matrix: pre-funded — premiums may be relatively modest if policy taken out young; limited premium payment term (e.g. premiums cease at age 85) helps budgeting; risk of no benefit if lapsed without surrender value. Immediate-needs — insured may struggle to find the large single premium; cost depends on interest rates at time of purchase; insured has no control over timing of need. [Nov 2019 Q1ii]
  • State and social insurance health products are designed with standardised, simplified benefits to ensure broad population access, avoiding the complexity of commercial PMI which may be inaccessible to lower-income groups.
  • Mandatory cover design (i.e. cover that citizens cannot individually opt out of) is the foundation of community-rated or social insurance health schemes, since voluntary participation leads to adverse selection and unsustainable risk pools.
  • Per-person caps and aggregate pool limits are built into state-backed schemes to control total public liability and ensure the scheme remains fiscally sustainable even under adverse experience.
  • Microinsurance products are designed specifically for low-income populations in emerging markets, characterised by simple, low sum-assured benefits and affordable premiums where commercial health insurance is unaffordable.
  • Co-insurance and co-payment provisions require the beneficiary to share part of the treatment cost, reducing moral hazard (over-use of free services) and limiting the total fiscal burden on the state.
  • A fixed cash sum for hospitalisation (regardless of actual cost) is a common feature of state and microinsurance products, providing a simple, verifiable trigger that is easy for claimants to understand and for administrators to process.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • India's Universal Health Coverage (UHC) programme: when the government introduces a UHC scheme covering ~50% of the population (means-tested), private health insurers are impacted through: increased general health awareness driving demand for top-up products; pressure on supply-side capacity in the short term pushing up medical costs; need to revise underwriting restrictions and benefit design as baseline coverage shifts; and potentially relaxed exclusions/waiting periods to complement state cover. [Mar 2018 Q2iii]
  • Government treatment cost controls: governments implementing UHC may introduce standard treatment protocols and caps on cost of procedures/equipment — this constrains medical inflation but may also restrict access to advanced treatments. [Mar 2018 Q2iii]
02Cover & Claim Definitions
  • A clear and precise boundary between inpatient, outpatient, and day-care treatment must be established in the policy wording, since ambiguity over which category applies to a given procedure is a primary source of claim disputes.
  • The policy must define which treatments and procedures are eligible for cover — generally based on medical necessity, excluding experimental or unproven treatments and cosmetic procedures.
  • A comprehensive exclusion list — covering standard exclusions (e.g. self-inflicted injuries), special exclusions (applied at underwriting for known conditions), and condition-specific exclusions — is essential to control adverse claim experience.
  • The pre-existing condition definition determines which conditions known to the policyholder at inception are excluded; the exclusion period (e.g. 2 years under moratorium) must be clearly stated to avoid disputes at claim stage.
  • The territorial scope of cover specifies whether treatment is covered only domestically, within a region, or worldwide — international cover significantly increases the insurer's exposure to high-cost healthcare systems.
  • The policy must define eligible providers (e.g. registered hospitals, licensed specialists) to prevent claims under cover for non-medical settings and to maintain quality standards within the network.
  • Maternity cover, congenital conditions, and chronic disease management each require specific definitional treatment as they raise particular adverse selection, ongoing utilisation, and claims control challenges.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • IRDAI pre-existing disease waiting period rules: under IRDAI (Insurance Products) Regulations 2024, waiting period for pre-existing diseases disclosed at proposal is capped at a maximum of 36 months of continuous coverage (reduced from the earlier 48-month norm in many products) — insurers are encouraged to offer lesser waiting periods. [May 2024 Q3]
  • Moratorium post-60-month rule (IRDAI): after 60 continuous months of coverage (including portability and migration), no policy or claim may be contested by the insurer on grounds of non-disclosure or misrepresentation — this is a moratorium provision specific to the Indian regulatory framework (previously 8 years, now reduced to 5 years). [May 2024 Q3]
  • Mental illness — mandatory coverage (India): the Indian regulator mandated that insurers cover mental illness under indemnity health policies — the IAI papers specifically analyse the challenge of defining covered conditions, the difficulty of establishing whether mental illness pre-existed at inception (no prior proposal questions), the need to reach agreements with specialist providers for cashless settlement, and the requirement to update claims and underwriting manuals. [Jun 2019 Q2vi]
  • Portability of cover definitions: under Indian regulation, 'portability' means the right to transfer credit for pre-existing condition waiting periods, time-bound exclusions, and NCD from one insurer to another — this creates definitional complexity as the porting insurer must honour waiting period credits only up to the previous policy's sum insured, creating tiered exclusions for the excess amount. [Sep 2016 Q1ii]
  • Front-end vs back-end underwriting and its effect on claim definitions: in India the dominant practice is back-end underwriting (limited front-end checks; pre-existing conditions assessed at claim stage) — this creates definitional ambiguity at claim stage; introducing optional front-end medical tests with a moratorium waiver is a specific IAI exam design proposal tested in detail. [Jun 2019 Q2v]
  • The CI condition list must balance breadth (covering more conditions to maximise sales appeal) against the need to maintain a manageable claims pay-out ratio — adding low-frequency conditions may be low risk but increases product complexity.
  • Severity thresholds for tiered or partial payment (e.g. early-stage vs advanced cancer, mild vs severe heart attack) must be precisely defined, typically by reference to objective medical criteria, to ensure consistency of claim decisions.
  • A standard CI definition requires a survival period of 28 days post-diagnosis before a claim is paid — this prevents claims arising from diagnosis immediately preceding death where the condition, not the claim, is the primary event.
  • Inclusion of mental health conditions in the CI definition requires careful specification of what constitutes 'serious' severity, since mental health diagnoses are inherently more subjective and prone to claims inflation.
  • The definition of 'child' for children's CI benefit extensions (e.g. up to age 18 or 21) must be clearly stated, as must the conditions covered, since children's conditions differ meaningfully from adult CI conditions.
  • Independent medical verification requirements — specifying which medical professional must diagnose the condition — are an important claims control mechanism, particularly for conditions with subjective diagnostic criteria.
  • The distinction between own occupation (incapable of performing the policyholder's specific job) and any occupation (incapable of performing any work) is the most fundamental definition in an IP policy, affecting both claim eligibility and premium.
  • An Activities of Daily Working Life (ADWL) approach assesses disability by reference to a standardised set of physical and cognitive tasks required for work, providing a more objective basis for claim assessment in some occupation classes.
  • The approach to pre-existing conditions — whether full medical UW exclusions or a moratorium — determines the nature and extent of exclusions in force at claim stage and is relevant to both individual and group IP design.
  • The salary definition — whether it includes only basic salary or total compensation (bonuses, benefits in kind) — directly affects the replacement ratio calculation and hence the maximum benefit amount.
  • The linked claims period specifies the minimum gap between two periods of incapacity for them to be treated as separate claims — without this, a policyholder could return to work briefly to 'reset' their deferred period and re-claim on the same condition.
  • Exclusions for hazardous activities (e.g. extreme sports) and certain occupational risks must be clearly defined; failure to exclude these appropriately can result in claims the insurer did not price for.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Group IP policy conditions — IAI depth: IAI papers test detailed group IP policy wording including: compulsory medical re-certification every 6–12 months (to be checked by insurer, not employer); a minimum waiting period of employment (e.g. 12 months) before eligibility; benefit limitation formula set as a percentage of pre-disability income less all sources of continuing income (not just state benefits); pre-disability income averaging method over a defined prior period (e.g. 1 year) to handle bonuses; and linkage of expiry ages to the employer's pension scheme retirement ages. [Mar 2021 Q1ii]
  • Accident vs sickness claim boundary: for accident-only IP, the IAI specifically tests the definitional challenge where accident and sickness become blurred (e.g. falling because of a sickness episode, road accident caused by fatigue from illness) — the policy wording must clearly define 'accidental' cause and the insurer must be prepared for disputes and ombudsman/court challenges. [Dec 2022 Q2iii]
  • The ADL trigger — specifying which activities (e.g. washing, dressing, feeding, transferring, continence, mobility) and how many must be failed — is the central claim trigger in LTC insurance and must be precisely defined to avoid disputes.
  • A separate cognitive or mental impairment trigger (e.g. Alzheimer's, dementia) is included in most LTC policies alongside the ADL trigger, recognising that cognitively impaired individuals may retain physical ability to perform ADLs.
  • The policy must specify whether disability assessment is based on a single point-in-time event or requires the inability to be ongoing and persistent, since the latter significantly reduces claim frequency.
  • Care setting definitions — distinguishing home care, assisted living, residential care, and nursing home care — determine what level of benefit applies in each setting and which costs are covered.
  • LTC underwriting typically excludes pre-existing cognitive conditions diagnosed or symptomatic before inception; the precise exclusion wording is critical to avoid claims arising from conditions the insurer did not knowingly accept.
  • At claim stage, requiring assessment by an independent qualified ADL assessor provides a more objective and consistent basis for determining whether the claim trigger has been met.
  • State and social insurance schemes require simple, unambiguous claim triggers to accommodate claimants with low financial literacy — complex eligibility criteria are a barrier to accessing cover and undermine the scheme's social purpose.
  • A minimum hospitalisation stay threshold (e.g. at least one overnight stay) is used to define an eligible inpatient episode, distinguishing it from day-care or outpatient treatment.
  • Government or regulator mandated standardised condition lists ensure consistency of cover across all providers in a regulated market, making it easier for consumers to compare products and for regulators to monitor fairness.
  • Exclusion of cosmetic and elective procedures is standard in state health schemes, ensuring public funds are directed to medically necessary treatments rather than procedures driven by personal preference.
  • A catastrophe or pandemic trigger based on an objectively declared event (e.g. government emergency declaration) enables rapid mass pay-outs without individual claim assessment during a systemic event.
03Product Features & Options
  • Guaranteed renewability ensures the policyholder cannot be cancelled due to deteriorating health, while continuity provisions address how pre-existing conditions and waiting periods are treated when a policyholder moves between schemes.
  • A no-claims discount (NCD) or experience bonus rewards policyholders who do not claim in a given year with a premium reduction, incentivising low utilisation and helping the insurer retain good risks.
  • Modular add-ons — including dental, optical, maternity, outpatient wellness, and mental health riders — allow customers to build a personalised PMI package while the insurer prices each module based on its own risk profile.
  • Reviewable premiums — where the insurer can increase premiums at renewal based on claims experience — are common in PMI, but the frequency and basis of review must be disclosed clearly to policyholders.
  • An open referral option (allowing policyholders to choose their own specialist rather than being referred by a GP) is a premium differentiator that adds value for customers who want greater control over their healthcare.
▾ Individual
  • Continuity of cover provisions for individuals moving between insurers — recognising pre-existing conditions on a moratorium basis rather than re-applying exclusions — are critical to avoid leaving policyholders with no effective cover.
▾ Group
  • A top-up option over a base employer group scheme allows employees to purchase additional individual cover beyond what their employer provides, filling gaps without the need for full fresh underwriting.
  • Portability on leaving a group scheme allows an employee to continue PMI cover individually (often without fresh underwriting) when they leave their employer — an important feature for the departing employee and a retention tool for the insurer.
  • Self-funded vs insured scheme options allow larger employers to choose whether the employer bears the claims risk directly (with the insurer providing administration and stop-loss) or transfers full risk to the insurer via an insured scheme.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • IRDAI pricing incentive mechanisms: IRDAI regulations require that any incentive or mechanism to reward policyholders (for early entry, continued renewals, favourable claims experience, or wellness/preventive habits) must be disclosed upfront in the prospectus and policy document — and the scope of wellness/preventive habits must be clearly defined in every product. [May 2024 Q3]
  • Wearable-linked wellness discounts: a product feature whereby the insurer tracks health parameters via a wrist-worn device (step count, heart rate, sleep pattern) and offers premium discounts at renewal for healthy behaviour — distinct from NCB; challenges include device authentication, continuous engagement, and premium differentials attractive enough to overcome data-sharing reluctance. [Mar 2017 Q1i]
  • Free health check-up feature (India): offering annual free dental/health check-ups as a product feature — the IAI specifically tests cost-vs-benefit: the cost of providing check-ups against reduced morbidity experience and improved persistency from healthier detected-early lives. [Sep 2016 Q1iv_c; Mar 2018 Q1i]
  • Portability as a regulatory product feature: in India, portability is a mandated product feature — the insurer must allow policyholders to port to another insurer while carrying forward credits for pre-existing condition waiting periods and NCD up to the previous sum insured — practical challenges include system complexity, pricing difficulties (porting lives cannot be priced differently despite different expected morbidity), and initial expense non-recovery when healthy lives port out early. [Sep 2016 Q1ii]
  • COVID-19 rider vs standalone design decision: a key product feature decision is whether to offer pandemic cover as a rider (linked to a base product, with shared underwriting and easier cross-sell but harder to discontinue post-pandemic) or as a standalone product (simpler to design for the specific benefit, easier to stop selling, but higher mis-selling risk). [Nov 2020 Q2i]
  • Outpatient (OP) cover add-on: adding OP cover to an existing IP/PMI product in India requires specifying product design features including: co-pay/deductible structures; sub-limits for physiotherapy, pharmacy, and dental/optical; pre-authorisation thresholds; and the pharmacy generic vs branded drug approach — these are IAI-specific design details not present in the IFoA matrix. [Jul 2022 Q2iv]
  • Reviewable vs guaranteed premiums is a fundamental design choice: reviewable premiums are lower at outset but transfer repricing risk to the policyholder, whereas guaranteed premiums provide certainty but command a higher initial premium.
  • Optional additional condition riders (beyond the core CI list, e.g. diabetes, osteoporosis) allow the insurer to extend the product's appeal while keeping the core product simpler and avoiding the need to reprice the full product.
▾ Individual
  • A unit-linked CI structure combines a reviewable morbidity charge with a fixed fund allocation to an investment element — meaning the policyholder retains investment risk while the morbidity charge is subject to periodic review.
  • A Return of Premium (ROP) feature specifies the trigger events (e.g. death without prior CI claim, policy expiry) under which premiums paid are refunded — this reduces lapse incentive and enhances the product's savings appeal for individual policyholders.
  • A multi-pay CI option reinstates the full sum assured after a first claim, allowing the policyholder to claim again on a different specified CI condition — this is a significant product enhancement that reduces the 'one-shot' nature of standard CI.
  • CI can be offered as a standalone individual product or as an accelerated benefit rider attached to a life policy — the latter pays the CI benefit against the life sum assured, reducing the death benefit by the amount paid.
▾ Group
  • Group CI is typically structured as a benefit multiple of salary (e.g. 2× or 4× annual salary) rather than a personally chosen sum assured, making the scheme simple to administer and align with other group death-in-service benefits.
  • In a group CI scheme, the employer can negotiate standard terms for all members without individual selection — the scheme design reflects the employer's objectives rather than individual policyholder preferences.
  • A partial or proportional disability benefit option pays a reduced benefit when the policyholder is working but earning less than their pre-disability salary, encouraging gradual return to work and reducing the cliff-edge cessation of benefit.
  • Rehabilitation services (e.g. physiotherapy, occupational therapy, vocational retraining) provided as an integral product feature reduce total claim duration and cost, benefiting both the insurer and the policyholder.
  • Reviewable vs guaranteed premium structures present the same risk-transfer trade-off as in other H&C products — reviewable premiums are lower initially but expose the policyholder to future increases.
▾ Individual
  • Deferred period options (typically 1, 3, 6, or 12 months) allow the individual policyholder to align the start of benefit with the point at which their own savings or any employer/state sick pay is exhausted.
  • Giving individual policyholders a choice between own occupation and any occupation definition provides flexibility at inception — the cheaper any occupation option means the policyholder accepts a higher bar before benefit is payable.
  • A return-to-work incentive — such as a tapered benefit that reduces gradually as earned income increases — encourages policyholders to return to work without facing an immediate cliff-edge loss of IP benefit.
▾ Group
  • Group IP benefit structures are frequently differentiated by employment grade or salary band — for example, senior staff receiving a higher replacement ratio or longer maximum benefit period — reflecting the employer's relative valuation of different workforce segments.
  • Auto-enrolment and minimum participation requirements in group IP prevent the scheme from being disproportionately selected by employees who already have health concerns, maintaining average risk quality.
  • HR and payroll system integration — automating premium collection and salary data updates — is a standard operational feature of group IP schemes, reducing administration costs and improving accuracy.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Overseas treatment continuation option: when a group IP member leaves employment, offering the option to continue the top-up overseas treatment cover on an individual basis — a retention feature specific to the Indian group health market where job mobility is high. [Nov 2020 Q1v]
  • Monthly premium mode aligned to payroll: for group products in India, allowing monthly premium deduction directly from payroll (rather than annual premium) is a specific product feature that increases affordability and reduces lapse due to premium non-payment. [Nov 2020 Q1v]
  • Waiting period of employment before scheme eligibility (e.g. 12 months from joining date): IAI tests this as a specific group IP feature that reduces both anti-selection and administrative costs from short-tenure employees cycling in and out of the scheme. [Mar 2021 Q1ii]
  • A surrender value — the amount refundable if the policyholder cancels before claim — must be designed consistently with the reserving basis; a generous surrender value increases anti-selection risk as unhealthy lives are less likely to surrender.
  • An inflation-linked benefit escalation option increases the benefit amount in line with a care cost index or general inflation, protecting the real value of the LTC benefit over a potentially decades-long duration.
  • A death benefit during the pre-funding phase — typically a return of premiums paid — provides value to policyholders who die before making a claim, removing the 'use it or lose it' perception.
  • Premium waiver once a claim commences is a standard LTC feature, recognising that the policyholder should not be required to continue paying premiums in order to receive the benefit they have already qualified for.
  • Offering pre-funded LTC and immediate-needs LTC annuities as separate product variants allows the insurer to serve both the planning market (younger purchasers) and the at-point-of-need market.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Paid-up benefit option: in a pre-funded LTC product, after a minimum premium payment period, the policyholder may opt for a paid-up benefit (cease paying premiums, accepting a reduced benefit amount) — an IAI-tested feature not explicitly in the IFoA matrix. [Nov 2019 Q1ii]
  • Capital guarantee on immediate-needs annuity: some immediate-needs LTC annuities offer a capital guarantee whereby any excess of purchase price over total benefits received is paid out on death — this increases purchase price but reduces the policyholder's longevity risk concern. [Nov 2019 Q1ii]
  • Tax treatment of LTC benefits (India-specific): disability-triggered LTC benefits are tax-free; for immediate-needs annuities, payments made directly to a registered care provider are not taxable in the hands of the insured — these tax features are IAI-specific and not covered in the IFoA matrix. [Nov 2019 Q1ii]
  • Mandatory or auto-enrolment design — eliminating individual opt-out — ensures universal participation, which is the prerequisite for community rating and the pooling of risk across the full population.
  • Premium reviewability built into the scheme structure, with regulator approval required for changes, allows the scheme to remain financially sustainable as claims experience and demographic trends evolve.
  • A waiting period (e.g. 3 months from enrolment before cover activates) serves as an anti-selection control in lieu of medical underwriting, preventing individuals from joining specifically because they are about to incur a claim.
  • A government backstop or guarantee for catastrophe events provides the insurer with protection against systemic aggregate risk that no single insurer could absorb commercially.
  • Tax incentives — such as tax relief on premiums or employer contributions — are commonly used by governments to stimulate demand for private cover, reducing the total fiscal burden on the public health system.
04Target Market & Customer Needs
  • The primary demand driver for PMI is the gap between what state healthcare provides and what patients actually need — including shorter waiting times, choice of specialist, higher-quality facilities, and access to treatments not state-funded.
  • An ageing population generates sustained demand for PMI as healthcare utilisation increases significantly with age, and age-related chronic conditions become more prevalent.

05Underwriting Approach
  • Community rating has the advantage of providing broad access and simple administration, but creates adverse selection risk (healthier lives exit to cheaper risk-rated products) and requires mandatory participation or risk equalisation to be financially viable.
  • Insurers may tighten underwriting standards in response to adverse claims experience — for example, by introducing stricter pre-existing condition exclusions — which can improve risk selection but may restrict access.
▾ Individual
  • Individual PMI can be underwritten on a full medical underwriting (FMU) basis, a moratorium basis, or on a community rating basis — the choice is largely determined by regulatory requirements and has profound implications for adverse selection, affordability, and fairness.
  • Moratorium underwriting reduces friction at the point of sale but can lead to disputes at claim stage when the moratorium period has not yet expired for a relevant condition.
▾ Group
  • In group PMI, a free cover limit (FCL) means members below a certain benefit amount are admitted without individual evidence of health.
  • An at-work clause requires group members to be actively at work on the effective date of their cover.
  • Experience-based underwriting adjustments at group renewal allow the insurer to reflect actual risk in the group without individual re-underwriting.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Factors influencing underwriting approach — IAI-specific list: range and size of benefits (sum insured level); type of product (hospital cash vs indemnity vs CI); distribution channel (socio-economic profile varies by channel); target market (design UW to match perceived risk of that segment); reinsurer's recommended UW approach; competitors' existing UW practices; level of competition; regulatory constraints (e.g. prohibition of genetic testing); and cost-vs-benefit of underwriting depth. [Mar 2018 Q2v]
  • Back-end underwriting as the dominant Indian PMI practice: limited front-end UW, with pre-existing conditions assessed only at claim stage — allows rapid policy issuance but creates significant information asymmetry and increases claims disputes; IAI papers analyse this as a feature distinct from the IFoA matrix's more balanced UW discussion. [Jun 2019 Q2v]

The regulatory framework in India splits the cost of medicals between insurer and insured. IAI papers assess the advantages (better risk understanding, more competitive pricing) and challenges (expensive tests, lengthy issuance, high decline rates, possible adverse reputational impact). [Jun 2019 Q2v]
  • Group PMI underwriting for large groups (1000+ members): IAI tests specific considerations including mandatory vs optional cover; industry/occupation risk profile; group size effect on expense economies of scale; system upgrades needed; and the potential for experience-based pricing adjustments even for large group renewals. [Sep 2016 Q1iv_c]

[Apr 2016 Q4]
  • Underwriting for COVID-19 product: specific considerations include the appropriate questionnaire design (short form vs declaration of good health), questions about lifestyle diseases (diabetes, hypertension as co-morbidity risk), treatment of past COVID incidence, and whether pre-existing condition exclusion applies — all specific to a pandemic-context product. [Nov 2020 Q2ii]
  • AI and algorithmic underwriting tools can process large volumes of applications faster and more consistently than manual UW, but may introduce unintended bias if trained on non-representative data.
  • The UW approach adopted affects lapse behaviour — policyholders who were fully underwritten and have known exclusions applied may be more or less likely to lapse than those on moratorium.
▾ Individual
  • Full medical underwriting is the standard approach for individual CI policies, with the extent of underwriting scaled to the sum assured.
  • Raising or lowering acceptance criteria is a tool for changing the business mix and managing aggregate morbidity risk.
▾ Group

  • An at-work clause for group new joiners is a simple but effective anti-selection control.
  • New joiners to a group scheme after the initial enrolment window may be subject to individual evidence even below the FCL.
  • AI and automated UW tools in IP can enable faster decisions but may compress risk margins if healthier lives are identified and charged lower premiums, leaving a more adverse residual pool.
  • The over-insurance risk is controlled by enforcing a replacement ratio limit at UW stage, requiring salary verification.
  • Periodic medical review during a long-duration IP claim serves as both a claims management tool and a check on continued eligibility.
▾ Individual

06Pricing Considerations
  • Medical inflation — the tendency for healthcare costs to rise faster than general price inflation — is the single most critical ongoing pricing risk in indemnity PMI.
  • Claims frequency and severity assumptions by category of treatment are the foundation of PMI pricing, requiring credible internal or industry data and regular updating.
  • Expense loadings — covering distribution costs, policy administration, and claims handling — must be included in the premium.
  • When entering a new market, a lack of credible local claims data forces reliance on reinsurer tables, international benchmarks, or industry averages.
▾ Individual
  • The choice between community rating and risk-rated individual premiums is largely determined by regulatory requirements.
▾ Group
  • Experience rating at group renewal adjusts the renewal premium to reflect the actual claims experience of the specific group.
  • For small groups with insufficient claims credibility, a blended rate is used.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • PMI analysis of surplus vs long-term insurer: a key IAI-specific pricing/monitoring topic is that PMI profit analysis is performed more frequently (quarterly rather than annually) because the insurer can reprice at renewal — key items to monitor include incidence rates, average cost per claim, loss ratio by treatment category, persistency by cohort, and selection effect by duration. [Mar 2022 Q1vi]
  • Burning cost pricing method: the IAI curriculum explicitly teaches the burning cost approach (frequency × severity) for pricing outpatient cover, requiring separate estimates for: major risk factors (age, gender, network, occupation, location); dental/optical/maternity as separate cost components; underwriting loading for sub-standard lives; initial product development and system setup costs to be recouped; target loss ratio incorporating profit loading; and iterative profit testing and market testing. [Jul 2022 Q2iii]
  • Inflation-indexed product pricing complexity: pricing a sum-insured-inflation product requires projecting the index value to mid-projection period, applying to a banded sum insured structure, and reassessing the premium at each policy year as the index moves — significantly more complex than pricing a fixed sum insured product. [Sep 2016 Q2ii]
  • Pricing a new PMI insurer with no own data: IAI papers specifically test the data challenges for a new SAHI (Standalone Health Insurer) including: reliance on IRDAI industry data (may lack granularity); reinsurer data; distribution partner data; published medical research; need to adjust for anti-selection from moratorium underwriting; medical inflation significantly above CPI; and the break-even volume required to cover fixed expenses. [Mar 2017 Q1iv_a]
  • Premium for overseas PMI/group product: pricing steps specific to IAI include obtaining data from IRDAI, local regulators, overseas medical journals, overseas hospitals directly, competitor quote portals, and reinsurers with multi-national data; adjusting Indian morbidity data for expatriate profile; and bottom-up expense estimation for the overseas operational model. [May 2023 Q2iib]
  • COVID-19 product pricing review checklist: IAI tests a specific review of whether: best estimate assumptions are justified; average claims cost varies by network/non-network hospital; sensitivity testing has been performed on key assumptions; maximum probable loss and capital adequacy have been assessed; reinsurance is available; volume estimates are reasonable; and whether pricing is appropriate for the level of underwriting applied. 10Anti-selection & Lapse Risk
  • Anti-selection under community rating occurs when healthier individuals exit the pool.
  • Mandatory participation is the most effective remedy.
  • A risk equalisation mechanism allows multiple insurers to operate in a community-rated market without competitive disadvantage.
  • Waiting periods and exclusions prevent individuals from joining PMI only when they need treatment imminently.
  • A no-claims discount (NCD) rewards and retains good risks.
◆ IAI-specific additions
  • Portability-induced selective lapsing: under the Indian PMI portability regulation, when a portfolio has adverse experience requiring a premium increase, healthy policyholders can port to another insurer without losing accumulated waiting-period credits — creating selective lapsing that worsens the remaining pool; the porting insurer cannot price differently for porting lives despite their different expected morbidity. [Sep 2016 Q1ii]
  • Wearable product — authentication anti-selection: a health monitoring device linked to premium discounts creates a specific anti-selection risk if a healthy person wears the device for an unhealthy policyholder — device authentication mechanisms are required to identify if the device is being worn by someone other than the insured. [Mar 2017 Q1i]
  • COVID product family floater anti-selection: offering a family floater COVID product raises accumulation and anti-selection risk — if one family member contracts COVID the probability of others being affected is high, creating within-policy correlation; the product design must consider limiting family floater or building in specific accumulation controls. [Nov 2020 Q2ii]
  • Micro-insurance IP — higher anti-selection and fraud risk: in micro-insurance IP, the population covered is often less financially literate, claim investigations are harder (remote locations, high travel cost), fraud is more difficult to detect (large volumes of small policies), and non-disclosure risk is higher due to lack of understanding of disclosure obligations — all anti-selection amplifiers specific to the micro-insurance context. [Mar 2017 Q2iv]
