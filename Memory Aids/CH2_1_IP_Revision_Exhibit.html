IP — Product Overview
SP1 Chapter 1 · SA1 Chapter 2 · Definition, nature, aims, customer needs
💡
Exam Lens: IP questions test your ability to link product features to their rationale, distinguish IP from CI and PMI, and apply knowledge to group schemes, new markets, and monitoring scenarios. Always explain why a feature exists, not just what it is. Moral hazard and return-to-work incentives are the dominant thread.
📘 Definition & History
  • Modern name for Permanent Health Insurance (PHI) — renamed to better reflect coverage and attract customers
  • PHI name emphasised insurer must pay a valid claim as long as conditions met (claimant sick and below retirement age)
  • Also known as: Disability Income Insurance (USA), long-term sickness insurance
  • Name change partly to avoid confusion with PMI — PHI pays a regular income, not medical costs
  • Provides the insured with regular income during periods of incapacity (inability to work due to illness or injury)
  • Benefits are in the form of a temporary annuity — continuing until policyholder recovers, dies, or policy expires
⚖️ IP vs CI vs PMI
  • IP vs CI: IP requires inability to work; CI pays on diagnosis regardless of work capacity. IP = income stream; CI = lump sum
  • IP vs PMI: PMI reimburses actual treatment cost (indemnity); IP replaces lost earnings. IP is long-term; PMI is annually renewable
  • IP premiums usually level (unlike PMI which rises with age); inflation-linking common
  • IP = long-term product; multiple separate claim periods possible without policy ceasing
  • IP benefit: policyholder decides use — mortgage, living costs, pension contributions, locum cover
  • Not all illness/injury covered — unemployment, drug abuse, self-inflicted injury excluded
👥 Customer Needs
  • Employers — transfer long-term sick pay liability to insurer; statutory sickness benefit covered; encourages staff retention and morale; tax-deductible premiums; benefits treated as business expense
  • Self-employed — no employer safety net; actively purchase IP; locum cover for professional practices (e.g. doctors)
  • Individuals — income replacement especially for those with dependants; loan/mortgage servicing; premium waiver (continues pension contributions during incapacity)
  • Where State disability benefits are low, IP fills a clear gap
  • IP may also cover MPPI — mortgage payment protection during incapacity
⚙️ Product Cycle Highlights
  • IP fits the full product cycle: design → pricing → marketing → underwriting → claims → monitoring → valuation
  • Product design is complex — admin systems must record claim start dates, cessation dates, reasons, and causes
  • Pricing must reflect practices across the cycle (e.g. weaker claims management = higher premiums)
  • Underwriting and claims management are more complex for IP than most life products
  • Experience monitoring complicated by actuarial difficulties in measuring morbidity risk
  • IP may be actively bought by the self-employed; others need active marketing
Group IP — Why Employers Buy
📌
Tested in IAI Mar 2021 Q1(i) and IFoA Sep 2020 Q2(i). Always distinguish between employer's financial motivations (statutory sick pay liability transfer, tax relief) and HR/welfare motivations (staff retention, morale, return-to-work incentives).
💼 Financial & Tax Reasons
  • Insures liability for statutory sickness payments and any discretionary sick pay (custom and practice)
  • Premiums paid by employer qualify for tax relief; benefits treated as business expense
  • May also pay for a replacement employee — IP protects against some of this cost
  • Long-term cost could be lower than recruitment fees if key staff retained
🤝 HR & Welfare Reasons
  • Enhances total reward package — attracts and retains better staff, reduces turnover
  • Particularly valued by employees — creates loyalty, especially among senior key staff
  • Employees who fall sick are more able to return — living standards maintained during incapacity
  • Rehabilitation benefits encourage faster return to work
  • Access to counselling and advice services to manage sickness absence
  • Employer has a moral obligation to support workforce wellbeing

Policy Conditions & Benefit Design
Replacement ratio, deferred period, linked claims, escalation, exclusions
📌
Core exam principle: Every benefit design feature in IP has an underlying rationale — usually controlling moral hazard, matching employer sick pay, or preserving return-to-work incentive. Always state the why.
Benefit Amount Design
FeatureDefinition / MechanismKey Rationale / Exam Point
Replacement Ratio Post-claim net income ÷ pre-claim net income. Insurers limit benefit to 50–75% of pre-claim gross earnings A ratio ≥100% removes all financial incentive to return to work. A 1% rise in replacement ratio may imply ~1% higher expected claim cost. Core IP pricing concept.
Over-insurance Benefit + all other income sources must not exceed full pre-claim income Offsets employer sick pay, State benefits, other insurance. Moral hazard control. Can be hard to enforce if claimant earns informal income.
Escalating Benefits Benefits increase in line with RPI / NAEI index annually in and out of claim Protects real value of benefit; subject to maximum (e.g. 5% p.a.). Premium also index-linked. Reduces "benefit erosion" by inflation.
Proportionate / Rehabilitation Benefit Reduced benefit when partially incapacitated or returning to part-time work Incentivises rehabilitation and partial return to work. Benefits both insurer (lower claim cost) and insured (income during recovery).
Waiver of Premium Premiums waived during a valid claim; usually after short deferred period (3–6 months) Prevents lapsing during incapacity — insured shouldn't lose cover when most in need. Adds cost; short deferred period on WOP reduces moral hazard.
Tax Treatment If benefits taxed as income, effective replacement ratio is lower than nominal Group IP benefits typically taxed. Individual IP benefits often tax-free. Affects real net replacement ratio and incentive to return to work.
Benefit Timing Features
FeatureHow It WorksPurpose / Key Points
Deferred Period Period of sickness before benefit payments begin. Common: 4, 13, 26, 52 weeks (UK). Up to 2 years possible. Integrates with employer sick pay. Avoids short-term/trivial claims. Reduces premium and moral hazard. Shorter deferred period = higher claims inception rate. Major rating factor.
Waiting Period Period at start of policy during which no benefits paid at all (now uncommon) Reduces anti-selection. Initial underwriting now preferred over waiting period. Uncommon in modern products.
Split Deferred Two benefit levels, e.g. £250/week after 13 weeks rising to £500/week after 26 weeks Integrates IP benefits with a stepped employer sick pay scheme. More precisely matches customer need at different claim durations.
Early Notification Clause Policyholder must notify insurer (e.g. after 1 month of sickness) even if deferred period is longer Enables early rehabilitation support before benefit commencement. Enables thorough claim assessment. Reduces overall claim cost if recovery aided early.
Linked Claims Deferred period waived if sickness recurs within (e.g.) 26–52 weeks from the same cause Encourages return to work — claimant doesn't fear "resetting" the deferred period if relapse occurs. Reduces disincentive to attempt recovery.
Expiry Age / Term Benefits cease at expiry age (usually retirement age: 50, 55, 60, or 65) or at end of fixed term Minimum 5-year term ensures classification as long-term business. Must align with employer's pension scheme retirement ages for group IP.
Benefit Limitation Formula (Claims Management)
IP Benefit Limitation — Typical Structure
Benefit = min( X% × Gross Pre-disability Income − All Continuing Income, Maximum Cap )
X% < 100 — ensures financial incentive to return to work is preserved
Continuing income offsets include: employer sick pay, State disability benefits, other IP policies, investment income
Monetary maximum cap: prevents excessive replacement ratios for very high earners
Pre-disability income: averaged over prior period (e.g. 1 year) to include bonuses and irregular payments
Challenge: claimants may earn undeclared informal income — hard for insurer to detect
Common Exclusions
🚫 Standard Exclusions
  • Unemployment, redundancy, or voluntary early retirement
  • Alcohol or drug abuse
  • Self-inflicted injury or attempted suicide
  • War, civil commotion, or terrorism
  • Failure to follow prescribed medical advice
  • Hazardous sports or recreational activities
  • Criminal acts
  • Pre-existing conditions (within moratorium or with exclusion clause)
⚠️ Policy Condition Design Principles
  • Conditions are the most complex part of IP design — must cover lapse, claim, recovery, partial recovery, retirement, and death scenarios
  • Unclear conditions risk being interpreted by courts in favour of the claimant
  • Simple conditions help policyholders understand at sale → accurate proposals → better underwriting
  • Must give cushion against adverse events (e.g. war exclusions) while remaining fair

Incapacity Definitions
Own occ, any occ, suited occ, ADW, ADL, changing definitions
📌
Definition of incapacity is key to a sustainable, profitable product and normally applies throughout the contract (SP1 Ch7). Heavily tested in IAI Apr 2024 Q1(i) and Dec 2022 Q2(i). Always link definition choice to cost, moral hazard, and occupational suitability.
Occupational Definitions
DefinitionStandardWho pays?Cost to InsurerMoral HazardTypical Use
Own Occupation Unable to perform the specific duties of the policyholder's own job Most generous to claimant Highest Highest — subjective; easy to maintain claim Professional/white-collar workers; individual policies
Suited Occupation Unable to perform occupations for which reasonably suited by education, training, or experience Intermediate Intermediate Intermediate; dispute risk at claims stage Common in group IP; balances cost vs. generosity
Any Occupation Unable to perform ANY occupation for remuneration Most restrictive Lowest Lowest — very hard to remain unable to do anything Rarely used in individual policies; manual/high-risk workers
Activities of Daily Work (ADW) Unable to perform a specified number of specific work-related activities Functional; objective Moderate Lower — objective measurable tasks Higher-risk occupations where occupational definitions are impractical
Non-Occupational Definitions
🏠 Activities of Daily Living (ADL)
  • The 6 ADLs: Washing, Dressing, Feeding, Mobility (walking/transferring), Continence, Toileting
  • Typically requires inability to perform 2 of 6 ADLs (unaided)
  • Used for LTCI and sometimes IP for non-workers (e.g. homemakers)
  • Not occupation-dependent — applicable at any age, including post-retirement
  • Less subjective than occupational definitions — objective functional assessment
  • Also used in extension of group IP to family members (adapted tasks: e.g. travelling on public transport, dealing with money)
🔄 Changing Definitions
  • Policy may start on own occupation definition for first (e.g.) 2 years of incapacity
  • Then switch to any occupation or suited occupation after the initial period
  • Reduces moral hazard for long-term claims — less generous definition after initial period provides return-to-work incentive
  • Commonly used in group IP for manual workers
  • Example wording: "own occupation for first 24 months; thereafter unable to perform any occupation suited by education or training"
  • Clinically: most long-term conditions — the condition itself doesn't change, but capacity to do alternative work exists
Occupation Classes — Impact on Rating
🔢
Occupation class is the most critical IP rating factor — affects both probability AND duration of claim. Insurers group occupations into classes (e.g. Class 1 = professional/office; Class 4/5 = manual/hazardous). Manual workers have higher inception rates AND longer claim durations than office workers — doubly more expensive.

Group IP — Design Considerations (IAI Mar 2021 Q1ii)
Policy Conditions for Group IP ›
  • Offer range of deferred periods (e.g. 1 month to 1 year) — short deferred period covers statutory sickness benefits risk; longer = lower premium
  • Require early notification of sickness absence (e.g. halfway through deferred period) — enables counselling/advice to prevent claim coming into payment
  • Own occupation definition initially appropriate — may switch to less generous after (e.g.) 2 years
  • Require regular medical certification and re-certification every 6–12 months
  • Enforce benefit limitation (X% of pre-disability income minus all continuing income, X < 100%) — preserves return-to-work incentive
  • Pre-disability income averaged over defined period (e.g. 1 year) to include bonuses/irregular pay
  • Linked claims period (e.g. 3–6 months) — encourages return to work
  • Proportionate benefits on partial return to work or return in lower-paid role
  • Waiting period before new employees are eligible (e.g. 12 months) — limits anti-selection and admin costs
  • Compulsory scheme membership (or high participation rate) — reduces anti-selection
  • Stepped benefit structure possible (e.g. 100% salary for first 6 months, then 50%)
  • Benefit expiry age aligned with employer pension scheme retirement age
Group IP Underwriting ›
  • Free Cover Limit (FCL): set based on group size, proportion joining, industry, location — all employees below FCL accepted without individual UW
  • Higher FCL if membership compulsory or participation rate high
  • Employees above FCL: individually underwritten using standard individual procedures (medical info, tests, specialist reports)
  • Actively at work criterion: all new members continuously at work with no absence for (e.g.) 3 months prior to joining — directly related to events causing claims
  • Specific exclusions may apply for chronic disease (e.g. severe asthma) — though broad exclusions may be unacceptable to employer
  • Consider time in dangerous environment, overseas work, driving, as underwriting risk factors
Group IP Claims Administration ›
  • Telephone helplines: advise on State benefits, policy conditions, and part-time working options
  • Counselling/advice service: ensure claimants aware of all help available; may train employer's HR department
  • Regular evidence of continuing incapacity required — minimises fraudulent claims
  • Regularly check other income sources to be offset against maximum claim entitlement
  • Claims payments made by employer via payroll (offset against premium due) — reduces insurer admin costs
  • Portfolio transfer risk: if insurer takes over existing group scheme from another insurer, may need to take on responsibility for existing as well as future liabilities
Workers vs Managers Design Differences (IAI Mar 2021 Q1iii)
💼 Managers' Scheme
  • Benefits typically higher — employer values retention of senior staff more
  • Wide income variation — free cover limit more impactful; more individual UW cases
  • Own occupation definition appropriate — professional roles; wide suited alternatives available
  • More comprehensive ill-health retirement benefits likely to be available from pension scheme
🔨 Workers' Scheme (Differences)
  • Premiums higher — manual workers have higher inception rates AND longer claim durations
  • Less income variation — FCL less important (unless overtime/bonuses included)
  • Slightly incapacity triggers full claim (physical jobs) — possibly less restrictive incapacity definition needed
  • After 24 months: switch to inability to perform "any suited occupation" — retraining to less physical work may be appropriate
  • Different employer sick pay terms — different range of deferred periods needed
  • Claims smaller but more frequent — relatively more expensive per £ to administer
Family Member Extension of Group IP (IAI Mar 2021 Q1iv)
Family Extension — Design, UW, Pros & Cons ›
Design Challenges
  • Not all family members in employment — occupational definition problematic; ADL-based or personal capability assessment needed
  • Benefit often set as % of employee's salary (e.g. 50% of employee benefit) — links benefit to family financial impact
  • Or flat rate benefit (e.g. ₹1,00,000 p.a.) — simpler but less precise
  • Spouses may earn other income — must be offset against maximum entitlement
  • Long deferred period (6–12 months) reduces admin cost and compensates for limited underwriting
Underwriting & Pricing
  • If near-universal coverage: additional premium = simple % loading on main premium (based on average family size)
  • Voluntary election by employees → adverse selection (large families more likely to join)
  • Medical underwriting of family members possible but costly relative to savings
  • Alternative: moratorium on pre-existing condition claims (2 years free of symptoms)
Advantages for Insurer
  • Increased premium income → greater profits
  • Novelty in market — brokers more likely to consider it in reviews
  • Perceived value much greater than additional premium → strong selling point
Disadvantages for Insurer
  • Data collection for underwriting more complex — employer won't have family member data
  • More expensive to administer — personal capability assessments vs occupational criteria
  • Very limited statistical data to cost the additional risk
  • Premium collection more complex unless flat % addition used

Pricing Approach — Inception / Disabled Life Annuity
IP Pricing Formula — Inception / Disabled Life Annuity Approach (SP1 Ch17)
PV(Claims) = Σₜ p(survive to t+½) × v^(t+½) × iₓᵈ × äᵢₗ
iₓᵈ = Claim inception rate: probability that a claim becomes payable to life aged x, having become sick d weeks earlier and remained sick through deferred period
Note: Sickness inception ≠ Claim inception — sickness inception = first unable to work; claim inception = end of deferred period when benefit commences
Claim inception rates derived from sickness inception rates × probability of remaining sick through deferred period
äᵢₗ = Disabled Life Annuity: PV of benefit payments to a life currently a claimant (allows for recovery and death)
This approach ignores recoveries leading back to further sickness — a simplification of the full multi-state model
Multi-State Modelling (SP1 Ch17)
States, Transitions, and Modelling Complexities ›
5 Model States
  • State 1: Healthy premium payer
  • State 2: Sick within deferred period (no benefit yet)
  • State 3: Claimant (benefit in payment)
  • State 4: Recovered — returned to healthy premium payer
  • State 5: Dead
Transition Rates Required
  • Sickness inception rate (1→2)
  • Recovery during deferred period (2→1)
  • Claim inception rate (2→3)
  • Recovery from claim (3→4/1)
  • Death rates from both healthy and sick states
  • Lapse rate from healthy state
  • Recovery rates must vary by duration of claim (reduce as duration increases) AND by cause of incapacity
  • Sales volumes hard to estimate: depend on economic/political factors; IP markets typically under-insured
  • Data limitations: published IP inception tables have limited credibility; US statistics plentiful but limited relevance due to different UW, wordings, claims management
  • Economic morale: recession → prolonged claims; self-employed less motivated to return to work
  • Government welfare provision: high State benefits reduce IP demand AND return-to-work incentive
  • Tax: premium taxes reduce sales; tax relief increases sales
Premium Review Process (IAI Dec 2022 Q2vii)
Step-by-Step Premium Review — Full Process ›
Step 1 — Experience Investigation
  • Collect morbidity experience since last review; split at least by: gender, age group, occupational class, deferred period, smoker status, region, income replacement ratio, type of incapacity definition
  • Study claim inception rates and termination rates separately — necessary actions differ depending on which is the problem
  • Termination rates: split by reason (death or recovery) and by claim duration
  • Mortality experience: also an important component
  • Expense analysis: actual vs expected, split by initial/renewal/claims expenses, adjusted for inflation
  • Lapse investigation: by duration in force, distribution channel, gender, age, benefit level
  • Exposure calculation: ensure lives insured in investigation period only; adjust for withdrawals (lapse, cancellation) on life-by-life basis
  • A vs E: actual vs expected by issue year (UW implications) and calendar year (general claims environment)
Step 2 — Assumption Revision
  • Revise best estimate assumptions based on experience, allowing for: credibility of own data; future trends in inception/termination rates
  • Risk discount rate: based on required profit margin at original pricing; risk-free rate consistent with current economic conditions
  • Investment return assumption: consistent with risk discount rate and expense inflation
Step 3 — Profit Testing
  • Project cashflows forward from review date to end of policy term; discount at risk discount rate
  • Compare profitability criterion under latest assumptions with original pricing criterion
  • Decide if rates should be adjusted — consider reducing rates if excess profits expected
Step 4 — Communication & Behavioural Risk
  • Give policyholders sufficient notice to decide whether to continue cover
  • Rate increase is last resort — first explore UW/claims/process improvements
  • Weight risk of selective withdrawal — healthier lives more likely to lapse; sicker pool remains
  • Monitor impact on volume — fewer policies = higher per-policy overheads
Investment Policy for IP Liabilities (IAI May 2025 Q3d)
📈 Level / Fixed Benefits → Fixed Interest Assets
  • Benefits guaranteed in money terms → match with fixed-interest bonds and cash
  • Assets in same currency as liabilities
  • Short-dated gilts and cash suitable for backing capital requirements — limits SCR market risk
📊 Escalating Benefits → Index-Linked Assets
  • Benefits linked to salary inflation (NAEI) → index-linked gilts best match
  • Alternatively: equities or property (expected real return) — but higher volatility; requires large free surplus and investment expertise
  • Growing book: incoming premiums can be used to pay claims (liquidity less critical) — but future premium income not guaranteed
  • Highly variable claims → retain higher proportion of liquid assets

Underwriting & Claims Management
Individual UW, group free cover, benefit limitation, rehabilitation, monitoring
Underwriting Approach — Individual IP (IFoA Apr 2024 Q1)
📌
IP is more complex to underwrite than other health and care products. Back pain and stress-related incapacity are IP-specific risks not relevant for CI or PMI. The insurer must assess the likelihood that a person will take prolonged time off work — not just current health status.
✅ What Good IP Underwriting Achieves
  • Identifies applicants with substantially higher risk of prolonged incapacity than assumed in standard rates
  • Classifies applicants into broadly homogeneous risk groups → appropriate pricing
  • Reduces anti-selection — thorough UW upfront limits adverse selection spiral
  • Fewer disputes at claims stage — conditions already assessed at inception
  • More accurate case reserving for individual policies
  • Allows more nuanced decisions (rating, exclusion, decline) — minimises flat declines
⚠️ UW Challenges Specific to IP
  • Many conditions that keep people off work (back pain, stress) are not clinically measurable at UW stage
  • Subjective incapacity definitions create moral hazard that medical UW cannot fully control
  • Automated UW tools may not capture IP-specific factors adequately
  • Labour-intensive process → expensive; prone to human error; can take many weeks for complex cases
  • Applicant may need to disclose sensitive personal health/lifestyle information
  • Occupation class assessment is critical — must verify actual job duties, not just title
Three Key Claims Management Components (SA1 Ch7)
1️⃣ Benefit Limitation
  • Required so claimant retains financial incentive to return to work
  • Formula: X% of gross pre-disability income minus all continuing income (X < 100%)
  • Continuing income includes: employer sick pay, State benefits, other IP policies, investment income
  • Monetary maxima; percentage limitations; overall maximum cap
  • Stringent limits for high earners
  • Difficult to enforce — claimants may earn undeclared informal income
2️⃣ Proportionate / Partial Benefit
  • Payable when claimant returns to original occupation in reduced capacity or lower-paid role
  • Calculated same way as benefit limitation — preserves return-to-work incentive
  • Enables "stepping down" from full claim without abrupt income loss
  • Benefits employer (gets worker back part-time) and insured (supports recovery income)
3️⃣ Rehabilitation
  • Counselling — positive attitude towards return, awareness of alternatives to full employment
  • Rehabilitation via counselling yields significant cost reductions
  • Physiotherapy, vocational retraining, psychological support — especially for mental health claims
  • Early notification clause enables intervention before benefit commences
  • Reducing claim duration is as valuable as reducing inception rate
  • Most cost-effective claims management tool for IP
Monitoring Experience (IAI May 2025 Q3b, Q3c)
Why Monitor — Key Reasons ›
  • Identify divergences in actual experience from pricing assumptions (actuarial control cycle)
  • Assess whether contract is proving as profitable as expected — provides shareholders acceptable return
  • On in-force guaranteed-rate IP: limited scope to change pricing; can improve claims underwriting
  • On reviewable-rate IP: use results to change premiums or benefits — subject to TCF and competitive constraints
  • Revise assumptions in embedded value calculations — recognise profits/losses appropriately
  • Use results to re-price new business where necessary
  • Monitor business volumes and mix — cross-subsidies between policies are likely; mix monitoring essential
  • Check appropriateness of supervisory valuation assumptions for regulatory reporting
Monitoring Methods for IP Business ›
  • Analysis of surplus — identifies items of experience drifting from assumptions
  • Model office approach — alternative to surplus analysis
  • Morbidity investigation (3–5 year period): split by gender, age group, occupation class, deferred period, smoker status, region
  • Study inception AND termination rates separately — action differs depending on which is problematic
  • Termination data: split by reason (death vs recovery) and duration from claim commencement
  • Large volumes → more cells in analysis while retaining credibility
  • Expense investigation: actual vs expected by initial/renewal/termination/claims; allow for inflation since last analysis; exclude one-off expenses
  • Lapse investigation: by duration in force, distribution channel, gender, age, benefit level, payment frequency — volatile; linked to economic conditions
  • Monitor by individual salesperson/broker — identify problematic sales outlets
  • Monitor underwriting and reinsurance limits — still appropriate for developing portfolio?
  • Monitor economic factors and competition — external influences on future experience
Investigating a High Loss Ratio (IFoA Sep 2024 Q3i, Q3ii)
Possible Causes and Actions to Improve Loss Ratio ›
Investigate These Causes
  • Higher claim inception rates; lower recovery rates; higher claim amounts; lower premium income
  • Changes in business mix: age, gender, smoker, occupation, scheme size
  • Product terms: incapacity definitions, deferred periods, benefit limits, replacement ratios
  • Pricing discounts to win market share → lower premium income
  • Decline in new business → average book age rising → higher risk pool
  • Economic recession → more claims, worse recovery rates
  • Selective lapses → healthier lives leaving
  • Mental health / stress claims (lower recovery rates, harder to manage)
  • Anti-selective early claims or abnormal large group schemes
  • Ex-gratia claims, legal challenges, fines
Actions to Improve Loss Ratio
  • Increase premium rates; introduce more granular rating
  • Reduce maximum replacement ratio
  • Increase deferred periods; strengthen waiting periods
  • Introduce partial / rehabilitation benefits if not present
  • Tighten underwriting: limit high-risk occupations; increase health UW; reduce group FCL
  • Review disability definitions — ensure they are clear to limit disputes
  • Tighten claims assessment practices; require additional documentation / second opinions
  • Introduce rehabilitation services to reduce claim duration
  • Review and tighten policy wording
  • Improve fraud detection processes
  • Consult reinsurer for advice and data
  • Introduce loyalty benefits to reduce selective lapses

Key Risks in IP
Moral hazard, anti-selection, mental health, external factors, propensity to claim
Moral Hazard — The Central IP Risk
🧠
Moral hazard is unique in IP compared to CI or PMI. IP claimants may prolong claims because they receive replacement income. This cannot be eliminated — it can only be controlled through product design, claims management, and benefit limitation. The examiner expects you to address this for every IP design question.
📊 Moral Hazard Drivers
  • Replacement ratio ≥ 100% → zero financial incentive to return to work
  • Long claim duration: as claim extends, psychological barriers to return increase
  • Mental health and stress conditions: subjective; hard to assess; long duration; increasing proportion of claims
  • Self-employed: economic incentive to claim if business performing poorly
  • Recession: unemployment risk encourages prolonged IP claims as alternative to redundancy
  • High State benefits: reduce return-to-work incentive (State alternative to IP claim)
🛡️ Moral Hazard Controls
  • Benefit capped at X% of pre-disability income (X < 100%)
  • Offset all continuing income sources against maximum benefit
  • Own occupation → suited/any occupation after 2 years (reduces incentive for prolonged claims)
  • Proportionate / rehabilitation benefit — incentivises partial return
  • Counselling and rehabilitation services — address psychological barriers
  • Regular medical certification and re-certification
  • Target occupations with strong work ethic in marketing / underwriting
  • Claims monitoring — regular check on other income sources
Anti-Selection & Selective Lapsing
🎯 Anti-Selection at Inception
  • Unhealthy lives self-select into IP — moral hazard controls AND underwriting required
  • Insufficient UW questions → adverse risk pool builds progressively
  • If market tightens UW and one insurer does not, that insurer attracts all the adverse risks
  • Mental health conditions: hard to detect at UW; significant anti-selection risk
  • Early claims (within 1–2 years of inception): indicator of anti-selection; investigate separately
📉 Selective Lapsing Risk
  • On premium review / rate increase: healthier lives more likely to lapse (re-entry to market at lower rate)
  • Sicker lives remain → deteriorating risk pool → further premium increases → spiral
  • Lapse and re-entry cycle: healthy lives lapse and take new policy at lower premium in competitive market; only healthy lives can do this (others can't pass new UW)
  • Impact: per-policy expense loading rises; remaining risk pool worsens
  • Mitigation: loyalty benefits; carefully calibrated rate increases; TCF communication

Factors Influencing Individual Propensity to Claim (IAI Mar 2018 Q2vi)
👤 Individual Factors
  • Current state of health — healthy individuals likely to claim only for infectious diseases or accidents
  • Personal medical history — prior chronic disease history increases propensity
  • Family history — diseases with genetic correlation (cancer, diabetes) increase risk
  • Age — older individuals more likely to claim; age increases both inception and duration
  • Gender — different morbidity patterns; females higher incidence of some conditions
  • Smoking — smokers more likely to develop health conditions earlier
🌍 External & Behavioural Factors
  • Financial circumstances — financial stress increases propensity (claim as income replacement)
  • Occupation — manual/hazardous workers much more likely to claim than sedentary workers
  • Leisure/hobby activities — risky hobbies (mountaineering, scuba diving, motorsport) increase risk
  • Area of residence / access to healthcare — urban areas: better access → more diagnosis → more claims
  • Replacement ratio — higher ratio reduces incentive to return to work (duration risk)
  • Definition of incapacity — own occupation: easier to maintain claim; any occupation: very hard
Risk of Entering New Market with IP (IAI Dec 2022 Q2iv)
Key Considerations Before Launching IP in a New Market ›
Demand & Competition
  • Sufficient demand to achieve required volumes? Conduct surveys to assess customer awareness and affordability
  • Any personal tax incentives for IP? Consumer confidence in brand and product?
  • Why haven't other insurers entered? If existing insurers not profitable, why should the new entrant be?
Data & Regulatory
  • Availability of relevant data for pricing: disability incidence, termination rates, split by age, gender, occupation, income
  • Licensing requirements; capital/solvency requirements; expense overruns/new business strain
  • Restrictions on product design, rating factors, and investment
  • How evolved are insurance ombudsman and legal dispute systems?
Distribution & Infrastructure
  • Availability of qualified salespeople; distribution channel options (bancassurance, direct, IFAs)
  • Availability of service partners (disability rehabilitation, counselling services)
  • Healthcare infrastructure: hospitals and doctors for medical reports and UW referrals
  • Willingness of reinsurers to support the business
Profitability & Other
  • Demographics and demand projections; regulatory limitations on profitability; repatriation of profits; corporate tax
  • Cultural impacts on propensity to claim and lapse
  • Potential for fraud
  • Would acquiring an existing local insurer be a better alternative?
