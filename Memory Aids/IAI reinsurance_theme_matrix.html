1. Reasons for reinsuring
  • Protect actual solvency against uncertainty in incidence / duration trends
  • Increase capacity — write more business with same capital; accept larger and impaired-life risks
  • Smooth experience fluctuations — protect against blips in surplus emergence
  • Reduce new business strain via reinsurance commission; reduce capital requirements for new entrants
  • Access reinsurer's technical expertise: underwriting, pricing, claims, IT systems, rehabilitation
  • Reinsurance terms act as a check on the insurer's own premium rates
  • Reduce valuation reserves via reinsurance credit
May23-Q1i

  • Established insurer writing individual IP already — requirement for RI likely small but useful for new group product
  • Catastrophe RI if concerned about accumulation risk from a single employer

  • Protect against claims volatility on new product with uncertain experience
  • Obtain technical assistance: pricing data, underwriting support

2. Treaty structure — proportional
  • Quota-share (QS): cedes fixed proportion of premiums and claims; simpler to administer; reinsurer involved in operations
  • Original terms QS common for CI/IP — level of cover relatively stable across policies
  • Proportion reinsured may reduce over time as experience develops
  • Individual Surplus: proportional but policy-by-policy based on sum at risk above a retention line; suited to fixed benefit / large face amount products
May23-Q1i; Sep21-Q1iii
  • Original terms QS probably best for group IP — simple, level of cover doesn't vary greatly (60–75% of salary)
  • Proportion ceded may reduce over time as experience develops
  • Quota-share preferred for OP extension: protects against underpricing while giving solvency relief; cost-benefit analysis of reinsurance outgo vs capital efficiency required
May24-Q2ib; Jul22-Q2iv
  • Quota-share on original terms likely for new LTC/annuity product in early years
  • Proportional arrangement allows reinsurer to be hands-on in underwriting support
May24-Q2ia
  • QS: cedes same proportion of premiums and claims; provides solvency capital relief and technical support
  • QS does not address catastrophic/pandemic risk — insurer still exposed to retained proportion of a large event
  • Individual Surplus: workable for IP above certain benefit size; ineffective for group MR indemnity
  • QS preferred when company is expanding to new geographies/risks or seeking to write more business without more capital
Sep21-Q1iii; Nov20-Q1iii
  • Stop-loss is aggregate non-proportional; different from proportional treaties
  • For PMI/health: QS dampens catastrophe exposure as it shifts high-frequency low-severity risk to reinsurer proportionally
Dec22-Q1i–iii
  • Proportional treaty types: Quota Share, Surplus — both reported in bordereaux to reinsurer
  • Proportional treaties: claims development patterns may differ from non-proportional; data more aggregated
May25-Q2a–b
  • First-dollar QS can fund capital strain via ceding commission
  • However, IRDAI expense regulation does not allow credit for reinsurance commissions against expense compliance
  • High QS implies fronting role rather than genuine risk taking

3. Treaty structure — non-proportional (XL / stop-loss)
  • Per person XoL: covers individual claims above an attachment point; limited applicability if pandemic risk is high-frequency rather than high-severity
  • Aggregate XoL: covers all losses above a threshold in a period — most appropriate for catastrophe / pandemic concern
Sep21-Q1iii
  • Excess of loss less relevant for OP cover — emphasis is on capacity and technical support, not single large claims
  • QS preferred over XL for OP; XL more relevant for protecting against accumulation from single employer group
Jul22-Q2iv
  • XL less common for annuity/LTC — risk is longevity and care cost escalation, not single large event
  • Aggregate cover possible for LTC if concerned about population-wide longevity improvement
  • Per person XoL: covers claims above attachment per person per year; lower attachment akin to QS — reinsurer won't accept without operational involvement
  • Per occurrence XoL: covers claims arising from a single event above threshold; appropriate if pandemic defined as one occurrence; depends critically on treaty wording of "occurrence"
  • Aggregate XoL: covers all losses above a threshold over a period; most appropriate for pandemic; likely more expensive and limited capacity; insurer still exposed beyond aggregate limit
  • XL preferred when insurer is more experienced, confident in taking risk, and concerned about ceding too much profit under QS
Sep21-Q1iii; Nov20-Q1iii
  • Stop-loss (aggregate XL): reinsurer pays claims in excess of attachment; pure premium depends on probability of exceeding attachment and expected severity above it
  • Layer structure: XS 70 cr; XS 100 cr; XS 90 cr capped at 25 cr — each has different premium ranking by product
  • Neither burning cost alone nor analytical approach alone is sufficient; combine both and use skilled underwriter judgment
  • Adjustments needed for inflation, changes in cover, and business volume growth
Dec22-Q1i–iii
  • Per risk XoL: data issues — greater tendency for claims to develop upwards, particularly for liability classes
  • Non-proportional: development patterns can be very different from direct insurance benchmarks
May25-Q2b
  • XL preferred when insurer is experienced and concerned about ceding too much profit under QS
  • Cost and availability of XL capacity influences choice between QS and XL
Nov20-Q1iii; Jun19-Q1vi
4. Alternate risk transfer
  • Not a primary topic for individual CI/IP; recapture is the main ART-adjacent issue
  • Not a primary topic for group IP
  • Not a primary topic for LTC/annuity
  • Catastrophe bond: insurer sponsors SPV; SPV issues bonds to investors; if catastrophe occurs insurer uses invested capital to pay claims; low counterparty risk; consider risks and regulatory scrutiny of SPV structure
  • Participating in a pool: share catastrophic claims results with other insurers; not very useful as all pool members are simultaneously affected by pandemic
  • Own captive: set up captive reinsurance in favourable jurisdiction; inappropriate for catastrophe — captive is risk financing, not risk transfer; insurer still responsible for claims in a catastrophe
Sep21-Q1iv
  • Not a primary topic for stop-loss pricing
  • Not a primary topic for inwards RI reserving
  • Financial reinsurance structures: lump sum fund in return for future profit; VIF monetisation; commission financing; asset restructuring to reduce inadmissible assets
  • Overall suitability depends on nature of solvency problem, regulatory acceptability, and cost vs benefit
  • FinRe less likely to help medium-size health insurer — VIF monetisation and commission financing better suited to large blocks of long-term business

5. Impact on reserves and valuation
  • Reinsurance credit = discounted value of future expected excess RI recovery over RI premiums; highest in year 1, reduces to zero by end of term
  • Net reserve = gross reserve less reinsurance credit; follows same pattern as gross reserve
  • For a mature in-force book (12 years, stable volumes): aggregate gross reserve, RI credit and net reserve all expected to be broadly stable year on year
  • If RI premium = pricing/BE mortality rate: no gain/loss from arrangement; reserves reduced by prudence margin on ceded share; capital requirement reduced
  • RI premiums paid in advance, recoveries in arrear — small reduction in investment income
May23-Q1iiia–b, Q1ivb
  • Valuation for OP cover must accommodate higher claim volume and different seasonality in IBNR
  • Claim reporting lag for OP typically shorter than IP (less pre-authorisation); affects IBNR methodology
  • IBNER behaviour differs: IP has large initial estimate refined at billing; OP tends to be stable
Jul22-Q2iv
  • Proportional RI reduces liability reserves by ceded proportion of BEL
  • For Solvency II: net BEL reflects only retained portion; RI recoverables recognised as assets
  • RI arrangements disclosed in EV: PVIF reflects net of reinsurance cashflows
May24-Q2ia
  • QS: ceded proportion of reserves held as RI recoverables
  • Aggregate XoL: contingent recoverable recognised only if aggregate claims likely to exceed attachment
  • RI counterparty risk must be recognised in Solvency II as a separate risk charge
Sep21-Q1iii
  • Stop-loss premium ranking by product depends on distribution of aggregate claims (mean, SD, shape)
  • For reserving: actual recoveries under stop-loss reduce net reserves; prospective premium depends on future distribution, not just past burning cost
  • LTCI has very low SD (5 cr) — stop-loss less useful and cheapest to buy
Dec22-Q1i–iii
  • Longer reporting delays for inwards RI: insurers submit bordereaux quarterly → IBNR must allow for this
  • Heterogeneity of exposures: different cedants, contract terms, reserving philosophies — makes standard methods less reliable
  • IBNR gross of retrocession; retrocession recoveries calculated separately to get net IBNR
  • Industry benchmarks less applicable due to heterogeneity of RI portfolios
  • Reserving margin level: depends on company reserving policy
May25-Q2a–b, Q2f
  • FinRe lump sum: assets created, no liability — improves available solvency margin
  • VIF monetisation: recognises embedded profit via reinsurance — effective only for large profitable long-term blocks
  • Commission financing: reduces new business strain; most effective for products with high acquisition costs
  • Asset restructuring: reduces inadmissible assets in ASM calculation
Mar22-Q2viii
6. Recapture considerations
  • Recapture triggered by recapture clause in treaty — methodology usually specified therein (typically VIF-based)
  • Recapture fee validated by projecting reinsurance cashflows discounted at risk-free rate plus risk margin; sensitivity tests performed before finalising
  • Key assumptions: morbidity from treaty experience, commission per treaty terms, expenses from reinsurer's share, persistency of cedant, investment return consistent with discount rate, cost of capital per solvency requirements, allowances for profit share / deposit-back / collateral
  • Morbidity benefit: high-value claims previously covered internally — experience may worsen; depends on treaty type (QS vs stop-loss)
  • Technical support lost: may need to employ consultants; reduced ability to price innovative products
  • Investment income: more profit retained; higher free assets available for riskier investments but more capital required in approved assets
  • Lapses: reinsurer has no direct policyholder contact — recapture unlikely to affect lapses directly

7. Reinsurance level & monitoring
  • Monitor experience of both company and reinsurer: identify divergence from pricing assumptions as part of actuarial control cycle
  • Reinsurance terms depend on experience of reinsured portfolio — changes by reinsurer will impact insurer
  • Monitor to inform re-pricing of new business and revision of valuation assumptions for EV
  • If terms are reviewable: use monitoring results to revise premiums or benefits
Nov19-Q1iii
  • Monitor RI relationship continuously; reinsurer advice on group-specific underwriting and data
  • Proportion ceded may reduce over time as the insurer gains experience in group business
May24-Q2ib
  • Monitor to check if RI terms remain appropriate as experience develops for this new product type
  • Proportion ceded expected to reduce over time as in-house expertise builds
May24-Q2ia
  • CFO concern: net claim ratio growing faster than gross — RI may appear to profit at insurer's expense
  • Explanation: RI premium paid annually in advance; claims recovered over following year; for growing book this creates a timing distortion — earned basis analysis clarifies the picture
  • Lower retention level reduces variance and upside; review in line with risk appetite before reducing RI
  • Consider: availability of alternatives; existing treaty commitments; impact on technical dependency
Jun19-Q1vi

  • Zero past recovery does not imply zero future probability — analytical approach needed to supplement burning cost
  • Monitor claims development relative to assumed distribution shape used in stop-loss pricing
Dec22-Q1i–iii
  • Monitor claims development patterns relative to reserving assumptions
  • Check whether changes in cedant mix or contract terms invalidate past development patterns
  • Monitor retrocession recoveries separately from gross IBNR
May25-Q2f

  • Financial RI suitability depends on whether regulator accepts the structure
  • QS ceding commission does not count as expense relief for IRDAI expense compliance purposes

8. Inwards RI — data & reserving issues
  • Not a primary topic for individual CI/IP direct writing
  • Not a primary topic for group IP direct writing
  • Not a primary topic for LTC/annuity direct writing
  • Not a primary topic for catastrophe/pandemic context
  • Burning cost approach: average past actual recoveries if cover had been in place; simple but unreliable for high-layer / infrequent recovery covers
  • Analytical approach: fit distribution (log-Normal, Gamma, etc.) to claims and compute pure premium for each layer analytically; then combine with experienced underwriter judgment
  • Key adjustments: inflation, changes in cover, business volume growth, business cycle effects
Dec22-Q1ii–iii
  • Grouping factors for IBNR: type of contract (facultative/treaty); type of cover (QS/surplus/per-risk XoL); basis (loss occurring/risk attaching/claims made); line of business; claim type; cedant type; data homogeneity
  • Reporting delays longer: insurers submit bordereaux quarterly — IBNR must allow for extended reporting lag
  • Heterogeneity: greater mix of contract types, terms and cedant reserving philosophies — standard industry benchmarks less reliable
  • Information less detailed than for direct: claims reported on aggregated basis, not case-by-case
  • IT systems may not perfectly capture all contract features and terms
  • Non-proportional: greater tendency for upward claims development, especially for liability class
  • IBNR calculated gross of retrocession; retrocession recoveries calculated separately
  • Issues before finalising: claims development percentages derived from local direct business may not apply to inwards reinsurance from different territory; check earned premium calculation if CI policy terms differ from assumed; assess whether margin level aligns with company reserving policy; check uniformity of risk earning assumption

9. Regulatory & capital interaction
  • Reinsurance credit reduces net reserve → reduces regulatory capital requirement
  • Reinsurance counterparty credit risk is a separate Solvency II risk module
May23-Q1i, Q1ivb
  • QS provides solvency capital relief — shifts proportion of RSM to reinsurer
  • Regulatory restrictions may limit maximum proportion of business that can be ceded
May24-Q2ib; Nov20-Q1iii
  • QS reduces capital requirement on new product; especially important in early years with limited experience
  • Capital partly depends on regulatory reserving and solvency capital requirements under Solvency II / IRDAI framework
May24-Q2ia
  • QS provides solvency capital relief — most reliable RI structure for regulatory capital purposes
  • Aggregate XoL: contingent recoverable — regulatory capital treatment less certain
  • Cat bond: off-balance-sheet SPV; regulatory scrutiny required; low counterparty risk
  • IRDAI may impose limits on reinsurance cession level
Sep21-Q1iii–iv; Nov20-Q1iii
  • Stop-loss reinsurance: recoverable reduces net claims in the regulatory accounts if attachment point exceeded
  • Contingent recoverable on prospective stop-loss not recognised in capital until attachment is breached or very likely to be breached
Dec22-Q1i–iii
  • Retrocession recoveries reduce net capital requirement for the inwards RI book
  • IRDAI requirements on cession ratios apply to Indian direct insurers; reinsurers face different requirements
May25-Q2f
  • Financial RI: must be acceptable to IRDAI to count towards solvency improvement
  • Ceding commission under QS does not reduce expense ratio for IRDAI compliance purposes — regulatory arbitrage limited
  • Captive: favourable tax/capital treatment in jurisdiction — but no genuine risk transfer

10. Pricing & underwriting support from reinsurer
  • Reinsurer's acceptance terms provide a benchmark check on the insurer's own rates
  • Reinsurer provides software and data for pricing impaired lives — particularly where insurer's own data is limited
  • Technical underwriting support: especially for scarce risk classes (certain impaired lives, rare conditions)
May23-Q1i
  • Reinsurer provides pricing data and free cover limit guidance for group IP — critical for a new entrant to the group market
  • Underwriting guidance on group-specific risk factors (occupation, size, geography)
May24-Q2ib
  • Reinsurer provides data for pricing new impaired life / LTC product where insurer has no own experience
  • Underwriting support: helps assess applicant impairment levels; reduces anti-selection risk from inaccurate pricing
May24-Q2ia
  • Reinsurer involvement in QS provides ongoing pricing and underwriting support — important for pandemic / new disease risk where insurer lacks experience
  • Per occurrence or aggregate XoL: reinsurer less involved operationally; insurer must maintain own underwriting capability
Sep21-Q1iii
  • Stop-loss pricing: reinsurer combines burning cost and analytical distribution to set premium
  • Skilled health underwriter provides judgment layer over quantitative analysis
  • Cedant must provide claims data by accident/underwriting year in sufficient detail for reinsurer to fit loss distribution
Dec22-Q1ii–iii
  • Inwards RI: reinsurer receives less detailed claims data from cedants — development of own pricing benchmarks required
  • Heterogeneity of cedant portfolios means reinsurer cannot rely on industry benchmarks for individual treaties
May25-Q2b
  • Information required from ceding company before quoting: demographic data; claims history and volatility; stop-loss retention level; claims control quality; risk appetite of own company; available capital; reinsurance availability and terms in market; competing quotations
  • Ceding company should not bear a loss before the RI cover attaches — otherwise insurer has no incentive to manage claims quality
