Healthcare Market & Key Demand Drivers
SA1 Ch.5 §1.1–1.3
Six Key Drivers of Demand
  • Wealth & sophistication — richer populations expect and pay more for better healthcare
  • Demographics — aging populations, longer lives, comorbidity; more chronic conditions
  • Economic conditions — low interest rates ↓ investment-type products; health products benefit. Demand tracks economic cycle.
  • State provision level — UK NHS (comprehensive) suppresses demand vs USA (limited State) which creates demand
  • Employer-provided benefits — major market driver in many territories for IP and PMI
  • Technology — aggregator sites, wearables, AI — change how products are bought and priced
Other Factors Influencing Demand
  • Competition level and range of products available
  • Pricing & perceived value — influenced by competition, required profitability, capital requirements
  • Public attitude to risk and awareness of health issues
  • Overall perception of insurance industry (scandals, mis-selling)
  • Quality of customer service — sales, administration, claims
  • Willingness of brokers to sell products
  • Political drivers — uncertainty over State long-term care or legislative changes
  • State healthcare perception — publicised failures raise demand for private cover
IP Insurance
Covers lost income due to illness/injury. Over 100 years old. Purchasers: self-employed + informed middle-earners. Must be sold, not bought. UK: 75% of IP cover via group employer schemes.
CI Insurance
Lump sum on diagnosis of listed condition. Demand > IP in individual markets: more flexible, easier to sell alongside mortgage, fear of critical illness > fear of inability to work, higher broker commission.
LTCI
Product everyone needs but few buy. UK immediate needs annuity market: ~£100m premium p.a. USA: 7.2m covered in 2014; fallen to <12 providers by 2020. Pension benefit legislation affects demand at retirement.
PMI
Heavily influenced by national healthcare system & State policy. UK: "double pay" perception. USA: natural large market. China: growing rapidly as public systems become inadequate for aging urban population.
Key Principle — Anti-selection & Non-Disclosure

Non-disclosure: customers fail to correctly answer underwriting questions (deliberate or innocent). Insurer pays unless wilful non-disclosure proven. Anti-selection: customers with higher expected claims self-select into buying — the "buy when you think you'll claim" effect. Both inflate claims above pricing basis. Careful underwriting is the control for both.

Customer Perception, Competition & Lessons from Other Markets
SA1 Ch.5 §1.4–1.6
Customer Perception Factors
  • Public understanding of financial products — socio-economic & geographic divides exist; emerging markets have little experience
  • Tax incentives: UK PMI attracts 12% IPT (Insurance Premium Tax) — not tax-deductible. Ireland & Germany offer partial relief. Tax relief increases attractiveness.
  • Adverse media coverage: corporate failures, mis-selling scandals, compliance breaches, poor investment returns, credit crunch association — all damage industry perception
  • Financial literacy — government campaigns and media exposure matter; Rwanda example (teenagers teaching younger children)
Competition Dynamics
  • Developed markets: small number of specialist insurers — competition exists but consolidation risk is real
  • State-sponsored insurers in some markets create barrier to entry for commercial insurers
  • Local legislation may require foreign insurers to partner with local company
  • UK PMI market: dominated by few insurers → CMA investigation 2012 found high barriers to entry for private hospitals, weak competitive constraints in local markets → higher prices for self-pay patients
  • Leading PMI players require: appropriate systems, effective distribution, experienced staff, third-party relationships, trusted brand
Lessons from Other Markets
  • Products travel: CI insurance originated in South Africa as "dread disease" product (lump sum for fund treatment or deal with financial consequences). Now global.
  • Major Medical Expenses (MME) — also from South Africa — less successful because: no indemnity cover gap, customers don't appreciate medical cost levels
  • Dovetailing: products must complement local State benefits — know the territory's welfare system
  • Regulatory changes in one country → lessons for others (e.g. USA ACA/Affordable Care Act)
  • China example: three largest domestic insurers dominate; international groups have acquired stakes. Most plans are supplementary to public insurance.

Technology, Data Science & Climate Change
SA1 Ch.5 §1.7–1.9
Technology Applications Across the Value Chain
  • New business: AI qualifies leads, targets on-demand products online, tailors adverts by age group
  • Underwriting: new third-party data sources, telematics (motor analogy — activity monitoring), mobile device data
  • Claims: machine learning reads handwritten forms, voice risk analysis detects potentially false claims, mobile platforms for microinsurance
  • Customer: price comparison sites (most active in UK; growing in Europe, Asia, LatAm), online self-service, fund switching, policy amendments, renewal quotes
  • Wearables: consumers share activity data for premium discounts. Evidence on claims impact mixed — may attract already-healthy lives (selection effect)
Data Science — EIOPA 2018 Review Findings
  • Strong trend toward data-driven business models across insurance value chain
  • Traditional sources (demographic, exposure data) combined with new (social media, telematics) → finer granularity on consumer behaviour & lifestyles
  • Biggest area: pricing and underwriting stage
  • Advantages: step-change in risk analysis, new products, reduce market friction, fairer outcomes, inclusivity
  • Potential issues — Data: quality, completeness, consent (personal sensitive data), harmonisation of sources, growing dataset → harder to maintain quality
  • Mis-selling: "nudging" techniques to push unwanted products
  • Data security: cyber-attack costs (lost data, reputational damage, business interruption); 91m records stolen from 3 US healthcare providers May 2017
  • Legacy systems: poor quality historic data; high update costs
  • Ethics: cross-subsidy declines as risk segmentation increases → unhealthy lives priced out. Views on "ethical" data use will evolve.
Climate Risk — Physical
  • ↑ Morbidity from wildfires, air pollution → ↑ health insurance claims
  • Heat waves → excess mortality (cardiovascular strain in elderly) → early LTCI termination
  • Extreme weather → ↑ claims, post-event morbidity from loss of housing/water
  • Vector-borne diseases (malaria, dengue) spreading geographically via ↑ temperature & humidity
  • Sea level rise → ↑ morbidity/mortality
  • Crop failure → food insecurity → ↑ ill health
Climate Risk — Transition
  • Policy changes reducing fossil fuels → investments in carbon-intensive industries losing value
  • Consumer preference for "greener" products & companies
  • Investment in carbon-intensive industries → reputational damage → harder to attract customers & staff
  • For health & care insurer: mainly affects asset values and changes in demand for certain products
  • Transition to low-carbon economy may reduce demand for occupational pension plans where employer base is carbon-intensive
Climate Risk — Liability & IFoA Guidance
  • Injured parties seek compensation for climate change impacts (first-order physical or second-order transition)
  • New links between air pollution and health conditions → latent claims class
  • Firms not accounting for investment decision impact on climate → direct claims for damages
  • IFoA Risk Alert 2017a: actuaries must communicate the extent to which climate risks are taken into account in relevant decisions, calculations or advice
  • Insurers should: evaluate impact of changing temperature & disease patterns on mortality; consider climate risk implications on invested assets; consider alterations to pricing/morbidity assumptions

Commission vs Fee-Based Remuneration
  • Commission concern: product bias — advisers recommend products with higher commission rather than best-fit for client
  • Fee-based: more transparent, demonstrates independence. Initial commission "rebated" to client → effectively reduces premium
  • Practical limit: fees only viable for corporate clients and high net worth individuals
  • UK precedent: commission banned for savings products in 2013 (Retail Distribution Review)
  • Commission still dominates in most jurisdictions for health products
Regulation — Areas of Impact on Distribution
  • Types of products that can be sold by insurers
  • How advice is provided and paid for
  • Level and form of charges on products
  • Caps on product charges
  • Requirements for distributors — certain products only via certain channels
  • Extent to which market is open to external competition
  • How mis-selling issues are dealt with
  • Brokers additionally provide for corporate clients: market research, administration of client records, employee assistance programmes

Mis-selling Risks by Product
SA1 Ch.5 §3
Regulatory Context

Consumer protection is a primary concern for insurance regulators globally. Poor practice can include inappropriate advice or selling unsuitable products. Consequences: fines, required compensation to policyholders, substantial operational costs, and proactive regulatory changes to prevent future recurrence.

IP — Mis-selling Risks
  • Replacement ratio intentionally <100% (to maintain return-to-work incentive) — not always understood
  • Benefits may not keep pace with income growth over time
  • Name "Income Protection" implies full & total cover — doesn't cover unemployment
  • Insurer can restrict benefits if income drops (e.g. career change to lower pay) — ABI Statement of Best Practice governs
  • Benefit escalation capped — may fall short if salary grows faster
CI — Mis-selling Risks
  • Only listed conditions covered — consumer may assume "any critical illness" is covered
  • Insurer's policy definition may be more restrictive than medical definition (e.g. "severe" heart attack)
  • Different insurers use different definitions — hard to compare without standardisation
  • UK move to simplified definitions (cardiologist diagnosis = pay) — but some insurers moving away from ABI standard
  • UK 2001 survey: significant number of CI policyholders believed they had IP insurance
  • CI prohibited in some US States due to regulatory concern about consumer understanding
LTCI — Mis-selling Risks
  • LTCI is a misnomer — provides cash, not care. Fixed benefit may not cover actual care costs.
  • ADL-triggered cash payment → misperception that costs will be fully covered
  • Unit-linked LTCI: poor investment performance forces additional contributions at worst time
  • If care costs exceed benefit limits — litigation risk arises
  • Inflation risk — indexed benefit may still fall short of real care cost growth
  • Solution: ensure selling process makes cash-not-care nature explicit; or redesign as indemnity (expensive)
PMI — Mis-selling Risks
  • Restricted products (e.g. NHS waiting list trigger) vs comprehensive — buyer may not appreciate limitations
  • Acute vs chronic confusion: PMI covers acute (treatable) conditions; cover ceases when condition becomes chronic — extremely upsetting for customer
  • Industry-agreed definitions for acute/chronic help but not perfect
  • Some insurers pay for chronic conditions on moral grounds — but must allow for financial implications
  • Restrictions on indemnification / approved hospital lists not always understood at sale
Insurtech & Technology Distribution Risk (§3.6)
  • Consumers increasingly self-directed — using multiple digital sources for research and purchase
  • Insurtech: tech start-ups partnering with existing insurers — AI-driven underwriting decisions online up to a maximum sum assured for CI
  • Examples: policy recommendations from social media profile; digital broker/insurer management tools bypassing legacy systems
  • Risk: complex products sold without adequate advice → product confusion → complaints, claims disputes, regulatory action
  • Developing markets: digital mobile channels reach unbanked/unconnected populations (mobile microinsurance)
  • Insurtech = derived from "insurance" + "technology"

Role of the State & Role of Employers
SA1 Ch.5 §4–5
State–Insurer Complementarity by Product
  • IP: State provides subsistence-level disability income. IP augments this. UK group schemes = ~75% of IP coverage. State benefits are time-limited or definition-changing. Clear role for insurance.
  • CI: No State lump-sum benefit on disability. CI insurance solely fills this gap in UK and most markets.
  • LTCI: State means-tested — England threshold: assets ≤ £14,250 (fully covered), £14,250–£23,250 (partial), >£23,250 (full self-funding). LTCI targets those above threshold. May be forced to sell family home.
  • PMI: UK NHS provides free comprehensive care but with waiting times. PMI value proposition: faster access, choice of consultant, private rooms, convenience. "Double payment" accepted by financially aware customers.
Future Role of State — Key Considerations
  • IP: Government may invest in prevention (vaccination, anti-obesity) rather than benefits. Stricter qualification rules to reduce claimants — UK 2008 benefit restructure.
  • CI: No State lump-sum likely — private sector expected to continue providing.
  • LTCI: Major debate globally. Increasing longevity and care demand → public funding strain. Some countries publicly fund significant portion. Countries with heavy public funding find it very difficult to shift to private LTCI.
  • PMI: Ageing population + rising costs → government may: prioritise spending, raise taxes, target resources by group. Private care fills any emerging gaps.
  • Government tools: legislation & regulation · taxation (premiums, benefits, investments) · State benefit changes · national health system changes · public communication criticising insurers
Employer Benefit Feature Detail & Implications
Valued BenefitsPMI aids early return to work; IP demonstrates continuing financial responsibility during prolonged sickness. Both used to attract, retain and reward staff. Dependants sometimes covered (though this has become less common due to cost).
Tax EfficiencyPremiums can be a business expense (varies by jurisdiction). IP benefits often paid gross to employer, then through payroll — taxed as earned income. PMI can be tax-efficient for employer. Tax efficiency for employees affects voluntary take-up rates.
Relative CostGroup cover cheaper per person due to: economies of scale, experience rating, less underwriting, lower commission, less anti-selection (compulsory membership preferred by insurer), employer takes active claims management role. However not always cheaper — elderly workforce or single unit rate schemes may be exceptions.
MHD (Medical History Disregarded) BasisLarge group PMI schemes can offer cover for pre-existing conditions that would be excluded individually. Usually heavily experience-rated or cost-plus (self-insured) — most/all risk borne by employer.
Free Cover LevelIP group schemes: cover up to free limit without individual UW ("once only" UW approach). Employees in poor health can access cover they may not obtain individually.

Medical Underwriting & Genetics
SA1 Ch.6 §1–2
Six Ways Medical Underwriting Manages Risk

① Protects against severe anti-selection (unquantifiable risks) · ② Identifies substandard risks for special terms · ③ Determines appropriate special terms for substandard risks · ④ Ensures all risks rated fairly (classification) · ⑤ Ensures actual morbidity does not depart far from pricing basis · ⑥ Financial underwriting reduces over-insurance risk

Front-End Underwriting
Back-End (Moratorium) Underwriting
  • Full medical info gathered at proposal stage
  • Applicant allocated to risk class; specific exclusions applied upfront
  • Insured knows exactly what is and is not covered
  • Additional sources: doctor's reports, medical examination, specialist tests, supplementary questionnaires (asthma, epilepsy, anxiety)
  • Non-medical limits in UK IP: age 40 → £10k report required; £20k exam required
  • Advantage: certainty for both parties
  • Disadvantage: lengthy forms, personal disclosure burden, can deter applicants
  • Immediate cover on all new conditions (arising post-application)
  • No medical history check at entry; no specific exclusions applied
  • PEC check performed at claim stage — if condition (or related condition) pre-dated application → claim refused
  • Moratorium period: typically disallow claims from conditions in past 5 years; covered after 2 years treatment-free
  • Predominant in UK PMI, ASU, group CI
  • Advantage: immediate, simple entry — no disclosure burden
  • Disadvantage: uncertainty about what is covered; harder to establish pre-existence at claim; ombudsman concerns in UK 1990s
Psychosocial Factors in IP Underwriting (§1.6)
  • Back pain & stress-related incapacity remain largest IP claim causes; underlying psychosocial factors are as important as the medical condition itself
  • Predictive flags for long-term disability: GP attendance rate inconsistent with known problems · poor work attendance record · changes in personal circumstances · adverse reactions to normal stressful life events
  • New UW approach: ask "how much time did you take off work for this illness?" rather than just listing diagnoses
  • Applicants demonstrating resilience to diagnosed conditions may get better terms than those who only list diseases without context
Genetics — Key Issues (§2)
  • Anti-selection: tested individual knows predisposition → seeks higher cover → insurer premium shortfall. BRCA1/2 gene → CI premium ~40× standard.
  • Information imbalance: one principle of insurance = all material facts disclosed. Genetic info creates imbalance — many argue insurer has no right to it.
  • Regulatory position:
    — UK: no use of genetic info for IP, CI, LTCI. Exception: Huntington's for high-value life insurance only.
    — USA: health/care insurers cannot use predictive genetic tests.
    — NZ/Australia: insurers can ask for disclosure of known test results and use them.
    — Many countries treat any use as discriminatory/illegal.
  • Insurance most at risk from genetics: CI (cancer/heart genetically linked) → then LTCI (dementia gene) → then IP → least: PMI (annual renewable premiums)
  • Future scenario: If genetic tests widespread → government may need to provide social insurance on compulsory basis → end of private insurance as known

Counterparties & External Influences
SA1 Ch.6 §3–4
Key Counterparties & Their Risks
  • Outsourcers (SLA governs): SLA must specify: validity dates · party contacts & responsibilities · service details & frequency · payment terms & conditions · other obligations · service monitoring method · failure penalties · cancellation ("get out") clauses · dispute resolution · governing jurisdiction
  • Brokers' balances: premiums held by intermediary in transit to insurer → credit risk + lost investment return. Insurer may hold capital against this risk.
  • Hospitals & consultants: PMI insurers have semi-official fee scales ("usual & customary"). Pre-authorisation required before treatment. Challenge to hospital if >10% above scale — but usually pays in full to avoid customer shortfall. "Blacklisted" providers warned to customer.
  • Independent investigators / assessors: used in IP to validate claim and monitor health periodically. Reputational risk — perceived as "hard-nosed detective". Should be presented as rehabilitation help, not surveillance.
  • Medical evidence gatherers: at underwriting (doctor's report, exam) and at claim (degree of impairment). Reputational risk if not sensitive — full references required before employing.
Demographic External Influences (§4.1)
  • Aging population: fewer working-age taxpayers → State strain → ↑ demand for private health products. China: 15.5% over 60 now; 25% by 2050.
  • Living longer: medical advances reduce incidence (IP/CI ↓) but survivors live longer in disability (IP/LTCI ↑). PMI: more claims as people live longer with multiple health issues.
  • Fewer children: lower fertility raises individual wealth but accelerates aging. Key life event (having children) that triggers insurance purchase becomes less common → smaller future policyholder base. UK fertility falling since 2012.
  • Medical advances impact by product:
    — IP: net ↓ incidence expected but longer survival in disability
    — LTCI: surviving at older ages ↑ claim cost
    — CI: early detection via screening may ↑ claim incidence (claim before former death)
    — PMI: new expensive treatments ↑ cost per claim; faster recovery ↓ hospital stay

Key Medical Conditions — Mortality, Morbidity & Regional Variation
SA1 Ch.6 §5
Global Mortality — Top Causes (WHO 2019)
  • Heart disease: #1 globally, 16% of all deaths. Combined CVD (heart + stroke) = 15m deaths. Leading cause for 20 years.
  • Stroke: #2 in CVD group. Leading cause of disability in UK. Risk factors: high BP, diabetes, heart disease, smoking, age, gender.
  • COPD: 3.3m deaths. UK premature deaths ~2× EU average in 2008. Main cause: smoking (also work-related fumes). Asthma premature mortality 1.5× higher in UK vs EU.
  • Lung cancer: 1.8m deaths (6th leading cause). Preventable — smoking, diet, alcohol, obesity account for ~1/3 of all cancers.
  • Diabetes: 1.7m deaths (true burden higher — complications & underreporting). Type 2 significantly underdiagnosed. Kidney disease (#10, 1.3m deaths) is a main complication of poorly controlled diabetes.
  • Dementia: 7th leading cause globally. Deaths doubled between 2000 and 2019. Alzheimer's gene test already exists.
  • Life expectancy: 66.8 years (2000) → 73.4 years (2019) globally. UK improving due to falls in coronary heart disease, lung disease, some cancers.
Specific Diseases — Insurance Implications
  • Cancer: survival rates improving significantly. UK prostate cancer: 25% 10-year survival (1971–72) → ~80% (2023). Breast cancer: 40% → ~75%. Impact: policyholders survive CI claim, may pay premiums longer than priced for. PMI: more treatable cancers → ↑ cost per claim. Early diagnosis via screening can ↑ CI claim incidence (claim on policy that would have lapsed before diagnosis previously).
  • Heart disease risk factors: smoking, overweight/obesity, high BP (primary). Secondary: high cholesterol, physical inactivity, diabetes, family history, ethnic background, age & gender.
  • Liver disease: only major cause of mortality increasing in England while declining in European peers. All three causes — obesity, undiagnosed infection, alcohol — are preventable. Scotland minimum unit alcohol pricing introduced May 2018.
  • Respiratory (COPD & asthma): work-related exposure an important cause alongside smoking. Early identification, diagnosis and intervention is UK government focus.

Insurer Data Challenge — Key Takeaway

Changing longevity and morbidity rates make historic experience data less credible. Insurers need better data via: wearable technology, big data analytics, cohort studies, expert medical advice, reinsurer data, international comparisons. IADLs (Instrumental Activities of Daily Living) — shopping, cooking, laundry — serve as early indicators of disability progression before standard ADL failures occur. LTCI particularly data-poor in UK given small market size.
