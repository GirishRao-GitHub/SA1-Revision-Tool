Capital Requirements — Three Perspectives
Economic Capital Internal · Risk profile & appetite · Ongoing business strategy · Most onerous for some companies Regulatory Capital Prescribed solvency rules · Defines asset/liability values · All companies must meet · Most onerous for others Rating Agency Capital Credit rating standards · Drives cost of raising capital · Market willingness to deal Available Capital Assets − Liabilities · Definition of both must be clarified (BEL vs TP vs prudential) Required Capital Assets in excess of liabilities → claims met with high certainty
All three perspectives link policyholder protection to the company's broader risk appetite. Most onerous varies by company.

Why Capital Is Required — Two Categories
Risk Capital Cover day-to-day risks: Market · Credit · Insurance (mortality, persistency, expenses) · Operational (outsourcing, terrorism, systems) · Liquidity · Group Working Capital Fund new business · Attract intermediaries · Overheads & organic expansion (hardware, software, product dev, premises, overseas) · Acquire companies/blocks Exacerbated by Mis-selling fines · Flat charging caps · Low investment returns · Adverse demographics (↑longevity → ↑LTCI) · TCF implications · Changing distribution (internet) ORSA Solvency II / similar regimes require all risks assessed → companies increasingly aware of capital employed & return on it

Reasons for Projecting Solvency
Monitor Risk Profile Understand & track evolution of risk profile over time Strategy Decisions Assess impact of key decisions · Show new business strain · Determine supportable NB volume Capital Releases Estimate pattern of releases · Assess return on capital · Calculate cost of holding capital for embedded value Statutory Requirement Solvency II Pillar 2 ORSA · Determine risk margin (Pillar 1) · May feed internal model SCR Risk Management Stress testing · Reverse stress testing (known outcome → identify causes) · Effective risk measurement
Projections are not just about the probability of insolvency — they support capital planning, embedded value and regulatory compliance.

Available Capital — Sources & Market-Consistent Assessment
Proprietary Initial + subsequent shareholder capital · Retained profits (incl. 100% with-profits surplus) · Reduced by dividends & losses Mutual Initial capital injections · Historic under-distribution of with-profits surplus · 'Entity' approach — not earmarked for current policyholders Market-Consistent Valuation Assets = market value (directly observed) · Liabilities valued in line with traded assets they resemble (zero-coupon bonds, options) Realistic Capital Market value assets − market-consistent liabilities · Best measure from policyholder & shareholder perspectives · Captures asset-liability interactions No-Arbitrage Condition Choice of backing assets must not affect liability value · Option pricing + interpolation/extrapolation used Non-Market Risk Mortality/lapse risks use best estimate + risk margin (compensation for uncertainty) → higher liability than BEA alone
Approaches: certainty equivalent · risk-neutral valuation · state-price deflators (SPD = stochastic discount factors, real-world presentation)

Assessing Ongoing Solvency — Economic Capital
Definition Internal assessment: assets required in excess of liabilities, allowing for all risks & ongoing business strategy, so claims met with high certainty Stochastic Modelling Realistic capital across many real-world economic scenarios · Used for market & credit risks · Also for insurance risks where distribution available (stochastic morbidity models for CI · multi-state models for IP) Stress Tests Used where risk distribution is less well understood · Applied to realistic capital · Inter-dependencies between risks must be assessed Time Horizon One-year period OR full run-off of in-force book · Shorter if risks can be hedged via capital markets · Longer if risks cannot be hedged · Calibration must be consistent with chosen horizon ORSA Requirement Solvency II companies must assess economic capital via ORSA · Many jurisdictions outside EU also require ORSA

Regulatory & Rating Agency Capital · Modelling Practicalities
Regulatory Floor Must meet regardless of economic capital result · May target different policyholder security level · ORSA may overlap with economic capital assessment Rating Agency Financial strength rating drives cost of raising capital & market willingness · Standards moving toward economic capital & ORSA · Understand agencies' frameworks Nested Calculations Problem Outer model = real-world stochastic (investment, rates, inflation, claims) · Inner model = market-consistent (value options/guarantees at future date) · 10,000 × 10,000 = enormous computation Proxy Models (Light Models) Simplification of full 'heavy' model · Techniques: polynomial/curve-fitting · kriging (Gaussian multi-dim interpolation) · replicating portfolios Closed-Form Alternative Black-Scholes for options/guarantees instead of stochastic inner model Calibration Expert judgement scenarios → heavy model run → least-squares or optimisation fit · Validate on out-of-sample scenarios · Re-calibrate periodically (goodness-of-fit deteriorates) Overfitting Warning Too many factors → poor out-of-sample performance · Tail fit critical for SCR/VaR · Re-calibration required regularly
Polynomial example with 2 risk factors x, y: f(x) = a₁x + a₂x² + a₃y + a₄y² + a₅xy + … · In practice 10–20 risk factors, up to 4th order

The Relationship Between Capital, Risk & Economic Value
Inextricably Linked Risk → determines capital needs (function of tail of risk distribution) → value creation affected by frictional cost of holding capital Risk Drives Capital Higher risk requires more capital if policyholders are to remain attracted · Risk also drives shareholders' required return directly Frictional Cost Capital locked into H&C company may not be tax-efficient · Reduces shareholder value creation · Holding less capital (if adequate) increases value Optimise Portfolio of Risks Economic capital analysis helps identify opportunities to release capital without compromising policyholder risk tolerance Concentration Reduction High morbidity concentration → reinsurance → ↓economic capital but ↑credit risk & ↓morbidity profits · Expense risk → outsource → ↑operational & counterparty risk Key Principle Optimising capital ≠ minimising capital · Must also consider how risk decisions affect economic value creation overall

Methods of Managing the Capital Position
Equity Proprietary: external share issue (needs credible business plan) · Mutual: demutualisation (merger/sale or stock exchange float) · Group: reallocate group capital · Lowest ranking → highest cost Subordinated Debt Capital markets · Repaid after policyholder claims (TCF) · ↑free assets (PH view) but liability (SH view) · Counts as 'own funds' under Solvency II Securitisation Convert EV of a block into tradable instrument · Payment now; repay as surplus emerges (contingent loan) · Most useful in regimes not crediting future profits · Less effective under Solvency II Reinsurance Financial RI: crystallise future profits (less effective under SII) · Traditional RI: ↓insurance risk SCR (offset by ↑credit risk) · Regulatory arbitrage if reinsurer in lower-SCR regime Derivatives Reduce SCR · Put options on equity index (domestic & foreign) · OTC for long-dated liability guarantees (price = market risk + bank cost of capital + profit) · Static hedge ALM Review asset-liability matching → smaller reserves and/or capital requirements · Reduce cost of options/guarantees · Reduce interest rate risk
Two broad strategies: (1) Raise additional available capital · (2) Reduce liabilities and/or required capital. Securitisation & financial RI are most effective in regimes that do NOT credit future profits in the balance sheet.
