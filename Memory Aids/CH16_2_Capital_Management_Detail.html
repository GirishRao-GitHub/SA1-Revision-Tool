① Capital Requirements — Three Perspectives & Two Categories
Three Types of Required Capital Assessment
All companies must meet regulatory minimum · Most onerous varies by company
Economic Capital Internal · Syllabus 4.2.1
DefinitionInternal determination based on company's own risk profile, risk appetite and ongoing business strategy
Who drives it?Management / Board — informed by economic capital models and ORSA
Key characteristicMost onerous for companies with high risk appetite or aggressive strategies; allows for premiums beyond contract boundaries
Assessment methodStochastic modelling and/or stress tests on realistic capital
Regulatory Capital Prescribed · Minimum floor
DefinitionSolvency requirements set by the regulator, defining the regulatory value of assets, liabilities and required capital
Who drives it?Regulator — e.g. Solvency II SCR (Value-at-Risk 99.5% over 1 year)
Key characteristicAll companies must calculate and cover. May target a different level of policyholder security than internal measures
ORSA overlapAny ORSA requirement may include a solvency capital projection equivalent to economic capital
Rating Agency Capital Credit rating · Market access
DefinitionThe capital adequacy standard required by rating agencies for a company to achieve or maintain a particular credit rating
Who drives it?External rating agencies (e.g. S&P, Moody's, AM Best) — publish methodology details
Key characteristicDrives cost of raising additional capital and market's willingness to do business with the company
Recent trendAgencies moving toward economic capital measures and internal assessments (e.g. ORSA)
② Risk Capital — Day-to-Day Risk Cover
  • Market risk — adverse movements in asset values / interest rates
  • Credit risk — counterparty default
  • Insurance risk — mortality, morbidity, persistency, expenses
  • Operational risk — outsourcing, terrorism, malicious damage, systems failures
  • Liquidity risk — inability to meet obligations as they fall due
  • Group risk — contagion from other group entities
② Working Capital — Support Business Strategy
  • Fund new business — including attracting intermediaries (need free capital to demonstrate solvency)
  • Organic expansion — hardware, software, product development, premises, overseas initiatives
  • Acquisitions — other companies or blocks of business
  • Exacerbated by: mis-selling fines · flat charging caps · low investment returns · adverse demographics (↑longevity → ↑LTCI) · TCF · changing distribution patterns
  • Solvency II / ORSA — companies increasingly aware of capital employed and need to manage the return on it

Entity Approach
Capital belongs to the corporate entity — not to any particular generation of policyholders. Not earmarked for distribution to current policyholders. Available capital may vary over time due to smoothing.
Assessing Available Capital — Market-Consistent Valuations & Realistic Capital
Realistic CapitalMarket value of assets minus market-consistent value of liabilities. Best measure from both policyholder and shareholder perspectives.
Market-Consistent ValuationLiability cashflows valued in line with traded assets they most closely resemble (zero-coupon bonds, option pricing). No-arbitrage ensured — choice of backing assets must not affect liability value.
Non-Market RisksMortality/lapse risks: best estimate assumptions + risk margin (compensation for uncertainty). Appropriate because anyone accepting such a liability will require compensation if experience worsens.
Policyholder PerspectiveCaptures all net assets to meet claims (BEA liabilities). Distribution of realistic capital = best measure of downside risk exposure. Requiring claims met with probability p ≡ requiring realistic capital is positive with probability p.
Shareholder PerspectiveDefines capital actually locked in the company. Realistic capital is negative only in circumstances when shareholders would potentially allow default.
Approaches in PracticeCertainty equivalent valuation · Risk-neutral valuation · State-price deflators (SPD) — stochastic discount factors allowing real-world presentation rather than risk-neutral presentation.
⑤ Assessing Ongoing Solvency — Economic Capital
Economic Capital: Two Approaches to Calculation
Realistic capital calculated across a number of scenarios · Interdependencies between risks assessed for overall ECap requirement
Stochastic Modelling
  • Realistic capital calculated across a large number of real-world economic scenarios
  • Commonly used for market and credit risks — distribution of possible outcomes well-understood
  • Can also be used for other risks where a distribution is available: stochastic morbidity models for CI · multi-state models for IP
  • UK mortality: significant research → stochastic mortality models increasingly used (e.g. for immediate needs annuities)
  • Advantage: captures full distribution of outcomes including tail events
Stress Tests
  • Used where the distribution of a particular risk is less clearly understood
  • Apply a defined set of stresses to realistic capital and measure the impact
  • Includes reverse stress testing: start from a known adverse outcome (e.g. regulatory capital breach) and identify what events could cause it
  • Advantage: simpler to implement; useful for operational, liquidity, and other non-market risks
  • Interdependencies between risks must be assessed to determine the overall ECap requirement
Time Horizon Considerations
One-year period — common in Solvency II (SCR basis). If risks can be managed/hedged through capital markets, shorter horizon appropriate.
Full run-off — assesses capital needed until all in-force business terminates. Better for non-hedgeable, long-tailed risks.
No single correct answer — calibration of stresses and scenarios must use a consistent time horizon and time step frequency.
ORSA obligation — under Solvency II all companies must assess economic capital through ORSA. Many non-EU jurisdictions also require an ORSA.
⑥ Modelling Practicalities — Nested Calculations & Proxy Models
The Nested Calculations Problem: To calculate probability of insolvency, stochastic inner simulations (market-consistent, to value liabilities at a future date) must be nested within each step of a stochastic outer simulation (real-world, to project future conditions). Example: 10,000 outer scenarios × 10,000 inner simulations = 100 million calculations — computationally infeasible. Short Solvency II reporting timescales compound this problem.
Proxy Model Types ('Light Models') — Simplifications of the Full 'Heavy' Model
Polynomial / Curve-Fitting
Fit a polynomial function to the heavy model output as a function of key risk factors. Example (2 risk factors x, y): f(x) = a₁x + a₂x² + a₃y + a₄y² + a₅xy + a₆x²y + a₇xy² + a₈x²y²
In practice: 10–20 risk factors; up to 4th-order terms. Risk factors: interest rates, equity index level & volatility, inflation, morbidity, etc.
Kriging
A form of multi-dimensional interpolation modelled by Gaussian processes. Allows the proxy model to flexibly represent complex, non-linear relationships between risk factors and capital outcomes.
Particularly effective when the relationship between risk factors and the heavy model output is not well approximated by polynomial functions.
Replicating Portfolio
Assets are selected to closely replicate the behaviour of the liabilities. The replicating assets are then modelled instead of the liabilities directly.
Becoming less popular — difficult to find assets that replicate liabilities sufficiently well, especially for non-market risks (morbidity / longevity).

⑦ The Relationship Between Capital, Risk & Economic Value
Risk, Capital & Value Are Inextricably Linked (Figure 16.1, Core Reading)
Risk
  • Exposure determines capital needs
  • Required capital = f(tail of risk distribution)
  • Market price of risk is key driver of value creation
  • Price depends on overall characteristics of risk distribution
Capital
  • Capital utilisation has a frictional cost
  • Frictional cost reduces value creation
  • E.g. H&C company may not be tax-efficient place for capital
  • Optimise portfolio of risks → release capital without compromising policyholder risk tolerance
Economic Value
  • Risk drives shareholder value directly
  • Market price of financial risk = core driver of shareholders' required return
  • Value creation = profits net of frictional cost of capital held
  • Optimising ECap ≠ minimising ECap
Morbidity concentration → Reinsurance: Paying risk transfer premium + taking on credit risk. Reduction in required ECap and frictional cost should more than compensate — but at cost of reduced potential morbidity profits.
Expense risk → Outsourcing: Third-party administrator reduces expense risk. Trade-off: increases counterparty and operational risk.
Key principle: Companies must consider how risk decisions affect economic value creation overall — not just capital reduction. Impact on overall company value is the correct objective.
⑧ Methods of Managing the Capital Position
Method Mechanism Constraints / Disadvantages Solvency II / Regulatory Notes
Equity Proprietary: external share issue (needs credible business plan). Group: reallocate group capital. Mutual: demutualisation (merger / sale / stock exchange float) Lowest-ranking capital → highest required return for investors. Demutualisation is time-consuming and complex. Counts as Tier 1 own funds under Solvency II
Subordinated Loan Stock Issue subordinated debt in capital markets. Repayment guaranteed only after policyholder claims met (with TCF allowance). Increases free assets from PH perspective. Is a liability from shareholder perspective. Ranking vs policyholders in wind-up must be considered. Can normally be held as 'own funds' under Solvency II (Tier 2/3 depending on terms)
Securitisation Convert embedded value of a block into a tradable instrument (contingent loan). Receive cash now; repay from future profits of that block as they emerge. Repayment is contingent on profit emerging. Issuer must pay interest. Less effective where future profits already credited in regulatory balance sheet. Less effective under Solvency II (BEL credits future profits). Possible to construct arrangements crediting profits beyond contract boundary.
Reinsurance Financial RI: crystallise future expected profits — improves regulatory balance sheet. Traditional RI: reduces insurance risk component of SCR. Financial RI: less effective under SII. Traditional RI: offset from increased counterparty risk. Cedes potential profit. Regulatory arbitrage possible if reinsurer operates under a lower-SCR regime — lower cost of capital passed on as more attractive premiums.
Derivatives Reduce SCR by hedging capital market risk factors. Put options on equity index (exchange-traded for domestic; foreign exchange for overseas). OTC for long-dated liabilities with embedded guarantees. OTC price reflects market price of risk + bank cost of capital + profit margin. Exchange-traded may lack sufficient term. Also usable to increase risk exposure (alternative to direct investment). Reduces SCR market risk module under Solvency II. Static hedge commonly used for liability guarantee hedging.
Asset-Liability Matching Review ALM position to allow smaller reserves and/or capital requirements. Reduces cost of options and guarantees. Reduces interest rate risk. May require restructuring the asset portfolio. Cost of restructuring (transaction costs, bid-offer spreads). Closely linked to Solvency II matching adjustment and volatility adjustment eligibility. See Ch.18.
Securitisation & Financial Reinsurance — Regime Comparison
Dimension Pre-Solvency II / Non-profit-crediting Regime Solvency II / Profit-Crediting Regime
Future profits in balance sheet Not reserved — not recognised until earned Recognised via BEL (best estimate liabilities reflect future profits within contract boundary)
Securitisation effectiveness High — selling future profits raises cash without corresponding liability increase Low — future profits already in balance sheet; no additional capital benefit from securitisation
Financial reinsurance effectiveness Effective for crystallising future profit value in the balance sheet Much reduced (or eliminated) — same reason as securitisation
Exception / workaround N/A Possible to construct arrangements crediting profits beyond the contract boundary (e.g. renewals beyond Solvency II contract boundary)
Traditional reinsurance Reduces insurance risk capital in any regime Reduces SCR insurance risk component — partially offset by increased counterparty risk. Regulatory arbitrage possible.
