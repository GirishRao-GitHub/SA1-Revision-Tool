CI — Product Overview
SP1 Chapter 2 · Definition, nature, characteristics
💡
Exam Lens: CI questions test your ability to link product features to their rationale, compare CI with IP and PMI, and apply knowledge to novel scenarios (new conditions, new markets, recession). Always state why, not just what.
❤️ Definition & Nature
  • Pays a lump sum on diagnosis of one of a defined list of serious conditions, or on undergoing a specified surgical procedure
  • Pure protection product — no surrender value, no maturity benefit; policy ends on first claim or at term end
  • NOT indemnity — sum insured chosen by policyholder independently; not designed to return policyholder to pre-condition position
  • Covers both morbidity and (sometimes) mortality risk
  • Also known as: dread disease insurance, serious illness insurance, crisis cash, living assurance
  • Historically grew from recognition that surviving a heart attack or cancer creates large financial need even if not permanently disabled
⚖️ CI vs IP vs PMI
  • CI vs IP: CI pays on diagnosis regardless of ability to work. IP requires inability to work. CI is lump sum; IP is income replacement
  • CI vs PMI: PMI reimburses actual treatment cost (indemnity). CI pays fixed sum on diagnosis regardless of treatment cost
  • CI premiums usually level throughout the term (unlike IP which may escalate)
  • CI is usually long-term (terms >5 years); PMI is short-term annually renewable
  • CI benefit: policyholder decides how to use it — mortgage, treatment, income replacement
💳 How Policyholders Use the CI Benefit
  • Meet immediate and ongoing medical costs (treatment, surgery, hospital stays)
  • Repay mortgage or other loan on diagnosis
  • Fund other insurance premiums / continuing retirement savings when unable to work
  • Support a dependant requiring care as result of the illness
  • Fund day-to-day living costs during recovery (rent, food, utilities)
  • Fund adaptations to home environment (wheelchair access), domestic help, recuperation
  • Ensure policyholder not reliant on state benefits or family financial support
📋 Key Characteristics
  • Exhaustive list of covered conditions — only named conditions are insured
  • Can be sold standalone OR as rider to life insurance (accelerated or additional basis)
  • Products sold on: with-profits, unit-linked, or without-profits basis
  • Increasing use of tiered/partial payments for less severe conditions (e.g. early-stage cancer)
  • Survival period: claimant must survive minimum period (typically 14-28 days) after diagnosis before benefit paid
  • Benefit payment can also be in instalments (reduces fraud risk) or used to purchase impaired-life annuity
Three Criteria for Including a Condition
📌
These three criteria are examinable directly. A fourth criterion sometimes added is: ability to avoid anti-selection.
CriterionExplanationExample / Implication
1. Perceived serious & occurs frequently Public perception drives coverage — condition must be seen as "lifestyle-threatening" even if not life-threatening. Must not be so rare as to be negligible. Cancer, heart attack, stroke — universally perceived as serious. Rare conditions: insufficient claims data; premium too small relative to admin cost
2. Clearly defined Avoid ambiguity at claim time. Use precise medical terminology. Prevents windfall payments for minor conditions and disputes. Heart attack: must show typical symptoms + EKG changes + specific enzyme/Troponin levels. Excludes angina, other coronary syndromes.
3. Sufficient pricing data Must predict future incidence accurately. Difficult due to medical advances (vaccines, new treatments). New conditions take time for claims data to build. New/emerging diseases: initially impossible to price. Conditions added to CI product after initial launch carry model risk.
Consumer Value & Regulatory Scrutiny (IFoA Apr 2025)
📉 Poor Value Indicators
  • Low CI claims payout ratio (e.g. 55-60%) → overly restrictive definitions or harsh claims management
  • Rising claim settlement times → policyholders need benefit quickly; delays generate complaints
  • Expected lifetime premiums > maximum benefit payable → fails fair value test
  • Rising advisor commission without corresponding policyholder value → churning, mis-selling
  • Adding conditions with very low incidence → appears comprehensive but delivers no real value
✅ Consumer Responsibility (Consumer Responsibility Regulation)
  • Define "good outcomes" and "fair value" for own CI products; Board reporting required
  • Target market review: changing customer needs (longevity, earlier diagnosis, lifestyle changes)
  • Product design review: conditions, benefit levels, severity-based partial payments
  • Clear product literature; conditions should be widely understood
  • Appropriate distribution strategy and commission governance
  • Fair pricing benchmarked against competitors; benefit levels reviewed for real-terms adequacy

CI Conditions Covered
Core, additional, terminal illness, TPD, children's benefit
Core vs Additional Conditions
CategoryConditionsNotes
Core (~90% of claims) Cancer, Heart Attack, Stroke, Coronary Artery Bypass Surgery, Kidney Failure, Major Organ Transplant, Multiple Sclerosis ABI Model Wording (UK) standardised these — reduces disputes, faster settlement, better industry data. India: no uniform standard yet.
Additional (variable by market) Alzheimer's, AIDS/HIV (via transfusion/occupation), Aorta graft surgery, Benign brain tumour, Blindness, Coma, Deafness, Heart valve replacement/repair, Loss of limbs, Loss of speech, Motor neurone disease, Paralysis/paraplegia, Parkinson's, Severe burns, Severe head injury Incidence much lower than core. ⚠️
Pricing trap — up to 40 separate condition distributions to estimate. Medical advances affect new vs existing business differently — conditions can be removed from new policies but not from guaranteed in-force ones. Separate disease-based and treatment-based trend modelling is advisable.
Terminal Illness Benefit
📋 Definition
  • Illness expected to cause death within a specified period (typically 12 months) in opinion of hospital consultant
  • Triggered by severity and effect on life expectancy — NOT by diagnosis of a specific CI condition
  • Fills gaps where severe illness significantly reduces life expectancy but doesn't qualify as listed CI condition
  • Allows insured to use funds while alive — for care, family provision, or other needs
  • Often ceases 12-24 months before policy end (to avoid claims near term end)
💰 Cost Implications
  • Accelerated CI: Main effect is to bring forward the death benefit. Additional cost is very small — approximately 12 months' investment return on sum insured
  • Standalone CI: TI is a genuine extra cost — no death benefit to accelerate; incidence by cause of terminal illness must be priced and reserved separately
  • TI benefit ≠ CI benefit — CI pays on diagnosis of specific conditions; TI pays when ANY condition is expected to cause death imminently
Total and Permanent Disability (TPD)
📋 Definition Nuances
  • "Permanent" = beyond hope of recovery in policyholder's lifetime
  • "Total" = major and substantial part of role/function failed (not every single element)
  • Permanency distinguishes TPD from IP (which also covers temporary disability)
  • Waiting period often used before claim accepted as permanent; IBNS maintained while outcome unclear
  • Benefit paid immediately if diagnosis is unequivocal
  • Many CI conditions (paralysis, dementia) also qualify as TPD — significant overlap; TPD can be added to extensive CI for relatively little additional cost
⚖️ TPD Definition Types
  • Own occupation: Most generous; highest cost. Unsuitable for high-risk manual work
  • Suited occupation: Unable to work in occupation suited by education/training/experience. Middle ground; dispute risk at claims stage
  • Any occupation: Most restrictive; lowest cost. Standard for manual/high-risk workers
  • ADL-based: Inability to perform specified number of Activities of Daily Living (Feeding, Dressing, Washing, Toileting, Mobility, Transferring). Applicable at any age, including post-retirement. Less subjective. Called "loss of independent existence" when used throughout life
Children's Benefit
Children's CI Benefit — Design & Cost ›
  • Cover for each child of policyholder, typically until age 18
  • Child claims do NOT terminate the policy — cover continues for subsequent child claims AND the policyholder's own claim; only the policyholder's own claim terminates cover
  • Reduced lump sum typically paid (e.g. 25% of adult sum insured)
  • Conditions for children may differ from adult conditions — certain childhood illnesses (e.g. Type 1 diabetes) may be included
  • Often included at no extra premium — actual cost is minimal: childhood CI incidence is low (e.g. 1 in 10,000 children diagnosed with cancer per year in Britain)
  • However, perceived value to parents is very high → strong marketing and sales feature
  • Early product designs excluded conditions that could be caused intentionally (e.g. third-degree burns) due to moral hazard concern — these fears proved unfounded in practice
  • Current exclusions: typically only pre-existing congenital defects

Return of Premium (ROP) Options (IFoA Apr 2023)
ROP — Triggers, Pricing, IFA Considerations ›
Possible ROP Triggers
  • Surrender/lapse: premiums returned if policy cancelled
  • End of term / age expiry: if no CI claim made during term
  • Death (outside a CI claim): premiums returned to estate
  • Conversion to another policy
  • Claim decline
  • Claim within moratorium/survival period
  • Partial claim: proportion of premiums returned
Pricing Considerations
  • Probability and timing of each trigger event
  • Expected policyholder behaviour (e.g. selective lapse by healthier lives for surrender ROP)
  • Investment return on pre-funded reserves
  • Amount of premium returned (full vs partial, net of charges)
  • Fairness between policyholders with and without the option
IFA Considerations
  • Client need and affordability — ROP increases premium, reducing affordability
  • Value as quasi-savings vehicle or tax-deferral tool
  • Commission implications (higher premium = higher commission if linked) — TCF risk
  • Regulatory risk if added complexity leads to policyholder misunderstanding
  • ROP may widen the protection gap if it makes standard CI unaffordable for lower-income policyholders
Mental Health Conditions in CI (IFoA Sep 2024)
Mental Health — Suitability, Challenges, Catch-All Definitions ›
Suitable conditions (criteria)
  • Severe and debilitating impact comparable to traditional CIs
  • Occurs with sufficient frequency
  • Clearly and precisely defined to avoid claim ambiguity
  • Sufficient data to support pricing
  • Objective measurable triggers (e.g. inability to work 12 months; hospitalisation for specified period)
Unsuitable conditions
  • Temporary or curable — payment would be a windfall not protection
  • Conditions without objective measurable triggers
  • Adding only to new business → dissatisfaction among existing policyholders who requested inclusion
UW changes needed
  • New UW questions: clear, relevant, proportionate, sensitive; comply with anti-discrimination law
  • Specialist medical advice needed for definitions AND underwriting criteria
  • Reinsurers: key resource for definitions, UW criteria, data from other markets, reinsurance availability and cost
⚠️
Catch-all definition (e.g. "serious infectious diseases") risks: pricing uncertainty; past data unusable; higher reserves and capital; legal/dispute risk around what qualifies. If leads to materially higher-than-expected sales: capital strain. Over-prudent pricing: unaffordable. Under-prudent: insolvency risk. Pricing tension is structurally unresolvable without a specific condition list.

CI — Pricing
Incidence rate formulas, premium build-up, medical advances, reviewable premium review process
CI Pricing Formulas
Accelerated CI — Claim Incidence Rate
λ_CI_accelerated = ix + (1 − kx) × qx
ix = CI incidence rate (probability of CI diagnosis, surviving survival period, not yet dead)  |  qx = mortality rate  |  kx = proportion of deaths caused by CI condition
⚠️ Double-counting kx overstates the rate → understates claim cost → inadequate premiums and reserves
Standalone CI — Claim Incidence Rate
λ_CI_standalone = ix × P(survive survival period)
No death benefit interaction. Must separately estimate incidence of each covered condition.
Overall CI incidence = Σ incidence rates by cause (heart disease + stroke + cancer + other). Avoid double-counting overlapping conditions.
📊 Data Requirements
  • Up to 40 separate condition distributions to estimate
  • Incidence rates by: age, gender, smoking status, occupation, benefit type
  • CI data historically limited — reinsurers are key data source
  • CI not similar to normal insured population (insured lives are selected — healthier initially)
  • Separate first and subsequent incidence rates (for tiered CI)
  • Medical advances affect new vs existing business differently — conditions can be removed from new policies but NOT from guaranteed in-force ones
📈 Medical Advances — Pricing Challenge
  • Screening programmes → earlier diagnosis → shifts timing of claims; may trigger claims that might otherwise have lapsed
  • New treatments → conditions that previously led to death now survivable → more CI claims (earlier) but fewer deaths
  • Better survival rates → CI incidence increases; death incidence decreases
  • New diseases emerge — not on existing CI list; new diseases initially impossible to price
  • Definition of conditions may need updating as medical understanding evolves
  • Separate disease-based and treatment-based trend modelling advisable
🏗 Premium Build-up
  • Risk premium (expected claims cost) as base
  • + Expense loading (initial + renewal; amortised over expected policy years)
  • + Profit margin / risk discount rate
  • + Contingency loading (model risk; data uncertainty)
  • + Reinsurance cost (net of recoveries)
  • = Office premium
  • Profit test: verify required ROC at projected volumes
  • For tiered CI: higher contingency loading given uncertainty in multiple-claim distributions
🔄 Reviewable Premium Review Process (IFoA Sep 2020 Q3)
  • Split existing policies into homogeneous groups by product type, pricing series, premium rates
  • Experience investigation for each group: incidence rates by age, gender, occupation, region, duration, cause of claim
  • Compare actual to assumed; calculate A/E ratio; investigate causes of deviation
  • Medical/lifestyle trend analysis; reinsurer experience comparison
  • Assess future trends: medical advances, lifestyle changes, demographic shifts
  • Profit test new rates; check against regulatory requirements
  • Communicate changes clearly to policyholders; reasons for increase must be explained in plain language
  • Good practice: cannot increase premiums purely to recoup earlier losses
Inflation Impact on CI (IFoA Sep 2024)
High Inflation Environment — CI-specific impacts ›
  • Long-term guaranteed-premium CI is most exposed to inflation risk — premiums locked while claims costs and expenses inflate
  • Short-term and group products can reprice annually — lower inflation risk; strategy should shift sales toward these in high-inflation environment
  • Higher inflation → higher living costs → policyholders more likely to lapse, particularly healthy lives who feel less exposed → adverse selection
  • Guaranteed premium rates become more attractive to policyholders in uncertain inflationary environment → demand increases
  • Expenses inflate → expense loading in existing guaranteed premiums becomes inadequate
  • Investment returns may be eroded; matching assets harder to access (inflation-linked bonds become expensive)
  • Commission payments linked to premiums: if premiums increase, commission increases → cost pressure
  • Higher socio-economic groups less price-sensitive → premiums for CI/IP can be increased without reducing sales volumes in this segment; expenses should be loaded to reflect this differential

Underwriting & Claims
UW approach, declined claims, anti-selection, reinsurance, front-end UW
CI Underwriting
📋 UW Approach for CI
  • CI is typically fully medically underwritten at outset (unlike health cash plans which use community rating)
  • Long-term nature justifies cost of full UW — premium locked for many years
  • UW aims to select lives as assumed in pricing basis
  • Substandard lives: rated premium (loading), exclusion of specific conditions, decline
  • Anti-selection risk: asymmetry of information (e.g. occupational exposure to cancer not picked up by standard UW questions)
  • Waiting periods (e.g. 6 months for cancer-related claims) mitigate anti-selection in absence of full UW
🔍 Front-End vs Back-End UW (IAI June 2019)
  • Back-end UW (India traditional): limited initial UW; anti-selection risk managed via waiting periods and claims assessment at claim time
  • Front-end UW: detailed medical assessment at proposal; explicit exclusions; faster claims processing; peace of mind for policyholder (no waiting periods)
  • Challenges of FMU: difficult to design tests covering all conditions; expensive; healthy applicants opting in but unhealthy avoiding → may worsen selection; lengthy process; may increase declines and reputational risk
  • Recommendation: FMU as option for younger applicants (<40); pass benefit of lower claims to customer via lower premiums
Declined Claims — Causes & Implications (IAI July 2022 Q1)
Reasons for Increase in Declined Claims ›
  • Volume effect: Simply more claims made (maturing book; adverse mix change) → absolute number of declines rises even if decline rate is unchanged
  • Improved claims control: More experienced staff, stricter process → more claims properly declined that were previously paid in error
  • Policyholder misunderstanding: Precise cover not explained clearly at sale; new disease emerged not covered; policyholder tries to claim for something not covered
  • Fraud: Especially likely in economic downturn; policyholders in financial stress attempt fraudulent claims
  • Non-disclosure / misrepresentation: Pre-existing condition not declared at application (poor selling practices; insurer not asking right questions; insured forgetting)
  • Other policy breaches: Premium non-payment making cover void; policy expiry
  • Definition disputes: Condition diagnosed does not meet precise definition (e.g. cancer that does not meet malignancy criteria; heart attack without Troponin threshold)
Implications of Increased Declined Claims ›
  • If due to improved claims control: good news short-term — lower claims costs; but if customers expected to be paid, adverse customer experience and complaints
  • Reputation damage — perception that CI policies "don't pay out"; reduces demand long-term
  • Brokers upset by disputed claims → less willing to sell the insurer's CI products
  • Ombudsman/court may subsequently overturn declines → more claims paid than expected; legal costs
  • If due to worsening claim incidence: profitability and solvency implications
  • If due to mis-selling: higher-than-expected lapses; further claim disputes in future; regulatory action
  • Admin burden processing declined claims and subsequent complaints
Reinsurance for CI (IAI May 2023 Q1)
Why Reinsure CI? ›
  • Protect actual solvency — especially given uncertainty about future CI incidence trends (screening effects, medical advances)
  • Increase capacity to accept risk — write more business with same capital; accept very large risks and wider range of substandard lives
  • Iron out experience fluctuations — protect against "blips" in surplus emergence
  • Reduce new business strain via financing arrangements (reinsurance commissions)
  • Reduce capital requirements (though less critical for established insurers)
  • Reinsurer provides UW and claims control expertise and advice — particularly valuable for impaired lives
  • Reinsurer may provide software for pricing, UW, claims administration
  • Reinsurance terms serve as a check on premium adequacy
  • May be available at favourable price (reinsurer accepting at a loss to build market share)

Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)
📋 Standard ADL List
  • Feeding (self)
  • Dressing
  • Washing
  • Toileting
  • Mobility
  • Transferring (moving from bed/chair)
  • Trigger: inability to perform (unaided) a specified number — typically 3 or 4 out of 6
⚖️ ADL vs Occupational
  • ADL advantages: Applicable to non-workers (housepersons, unemployed, retired); usable beyond retirement age; single definition throughout policy term; less subjective than occupational
  • ADL limitation: May be harder to satisfy than occupational definition; less sensitive to partial disability
  • TPD on ADL basis throughout life = "loss of independent existence"
  • LTCI: typically 50% benefit on failure of 2/6 ADLs; 100% on failure of 3+/6 (or cognitive impairment)
Alternative Definitions: FATs, ADWs, PCAs
DefinitionHow It WorksKey Advantage
Activities of Daily Working (ADWs) Skills-based tests relating to workplace activities — dexterity, mobility, communication More objective than occupational; can be linked to occupational class; applicable to non-earners
Functional Assessment Tests (FATs) Physical tests of capability — e.g. getting up from chair, standing 10 mins, walking 200m, lifting 2kg, climbing 12 stairs, bending, using hands. Fail specified number (e.g. 3 of 7). Mental incapacity: overriding test alongside FAT. Applicable at any age; easily understood; precisely defined; easier to apply in practice; no occupation-specific expertise needed
Personal Capability Assessments (PCAs) Hybrid — fails if cannot perform specified number of personal capabilities OR suffers one of list of serious illnesses (blindness, terminal illness, complete dependency). Trigger: fail 3+ personal capabilities OR meet serious illness criterion. Combines objective functional tests with defined serious illness triggers; broadly applicable across all age groups and employment statuses

CI Risks & Risk Management
Lapse risk in recession, anti-selection, medical advances, new product launch risks
Core CI Risks
📉 Morbidity Risk
  • Higher-than-expected CI claim incidence
  • Medical advances: screening → earlier diagnosis → more claims; new treatments → more survivors → more CI claims
  • New conditions emerge after pricing (e.g. pandemic disease)
  • Definition disputes lead to claims being paid that were not priced for
  • Claim definitions become outdated (e.g. heart attack Troponin thresholds as diagnostics improve)
  • Up to 40 separate condition distributions — model risk on each
🔄 Lapse & Anti-Selection Risk
  • Selective lapsing: healthy lives lapse (feel less exposed); sicker lives stay → deteriorating risk pool
  • On reviewable premium review: if premiums increase, healthy lives more likely to lapse → worsening experience → further increases → spiral
  • Lapse before expenses recovered → loss on policy
  • Fixed costs spread over fewer policies → profitability pressure
  • Asset disinvestment at unfavourable prices if large-scale lapsing occurs
  • Reinsurance premiums become relatively more expensive per remaining policy
🏗 New Product Launch Risks (IAI Apr 2016)
  • Product substitution: new product outsells existing profitable product
  • Over-selling: too much capital-intensive business destabilises portfolio
  • Sales failure: development costs not recovered; small portfolio has poor morbidity experience
  • Expense overrun: high cost of sales reduces available capital
  • Product design failure: product doesn't meet target market needs; poor positioning vs competitors
  • Pricing error: under/over-priced due to lack of data, modelling errors, inadequate UW
📋 Operational / Definition Risks
  • Complex medical terminology → dispute risk → costly litigation
  • Non-standardised definitions across market → customer confusion; adversarial claims
  • Moral hazard (lower than IP due to lump-sum nature) — but monitoring still important
  • TPD: permanence difficult to establish early — IBNS reserves needed
  • Fraudulent claims — especially in economic downturns
  • Mis-selling risk on tiered CI — complex benefit structure; higher potential for misunderstanding
Recession & Lapse Management (IFoA Apr 2022)
Minimising Lapses — Actions on Existing and New CI Policies ›
Existing Policies
  • Customer retention strategy; train retention team
  • Write to all policyholders outlining the value their policy provides
  • Offer incentives to most profitable subgroups
  • Provide flexible options: reduce sum assured; convert escalating to level premiums; pay-up options
  • Introduce premium holiday (no catch-up) or premium deferral (catch-up later)
  • Target shortest-duration policies (where unrecovered expenses greatest)
  • Approach distributors to discourage churning
New Policies
  • Add benefits to attract retention-conscious buyers: children's benefit, tiered payments
  • Simplify underwriting to reduce barriers to entry
  • Offer more flexible premium structures (reviewable; monthly; step-down)
  • Ensure product value proposition is clear and meaningful
  • Commission structure: include clawback to discourage distributor churning
⚖️
Premium Holiday (Option A) vs Deferral (Option B): Option A retains the customer but permanently reduces premium income and profitability. Option B recovers missed income but relies on policyholder's ability and willingness to pay later. Both are superior to lapse for the insurer if they prevent selective lapsing by healthier lives.
Group CI — Recession-Specific Issues (IFoA Apr 2022)
Group CI Premium Adjustment & Renewal in Recession ›
Mid-Year Premium Return (redundancies reducing exposure)
  • Standard method: assume employees join/leave at uniform rate → calculate updated exposure
  • In recession with mass redundancies: actual exit rate not uniform; redundancies may be concentrated at higher-risk roles/ages
  • Insurer must consider: mix of those made redundant vs those retained; adverse selection if higher-risk lives kept on (firm retains younger fitter workers temporarily)
  • Employer considers: whether they will receive a return or face an increase; timing of redundancy announcement relative to year-end
Renewal Premium Negotiation
  • Insurer considers: change in risk profile of employees retained; claims experience during year; cost trends; reinsurance terms; competitive pressures; employer's financial position (credit risk)
  • Employer considers: affordability; benchmarking vs market; value of scheme to employee retention; whether to amend benefit levels

i) How CI meets policyholder needs
  • Meets medical costs (treatment, surgery, hospital stays)
  • Repays mortgage or loan on diagnosis
  • Provides income from lump sum (via annuity) when cannot work
  • Funds other insurance / retirement savings continuation
  • Supports dependants requiring care
  • Funds day-to-day costs during recovery; home adaptations; domestic help; recuperation
  • Ensures policyholder not reliant on state benefits or family
ii) Regulator's concerns — from the table
  • Payout ratio 55-59%: Very low — policyholders claiming but only ~57% being paid. Could mean overly restrictive definitions, harsh claims management, or mis-selling (policies not covering what policyholders think). Rising conditions (10→100) barely moved payout ratio → new conditions are low-incidence, low-relevance → perceived but not real value
  • Claims settlement time 14→72 days: Significant increase. Policyholders need benefit urgently (to fund treatment); 72-day delay causes financial hardship and dissatisfaction. Likely reflects: more complex assessments; resource cuts; more disputed claims
  • Premium/benefit ratio 0.8→1.5: Now expected lifetime premiums EXCEED maximum benefit. Fails fair value test. Policyholders now paying more in total premiums than they could ever receive
  • Commission $240→$2,400: 10x increase — rising distributor commission without corresponding policyholder value. Incentivises churning and mis-selling. Likely driver of increasing conditions list and premium growth
  • Conditions 10→100: Vast majority of additional conditions are low-incidence/low-relevance — adding apparent comprehensiveness without delivering real protection value. Increases product complexity and claim disputes
iii) Ensuring good consumer outcomes and fair value (Company A)
  • Review and tighten CI condition definitions to reduce dispute rates; simplify where possible
  • Remove or reclassify conditions that rarely result in claims; focus on meaningful coverage
  • Introduce severity-based partial payments to better match benefit to degree of illness
  • Benchmark premiums against competitors; ensure premium/benefit ratio does not exceed 1.0 for typical policyholders
  • Reduce claims settlement time — invest in claims team capacity; streamline process; set internal SLA targets
  • Review and reduce commission levels; introduce clawback to discourage churning
  • Clear product literature explaining exactly what is and is not covered; plain language condition definitions
  • Review benefit levels regularly to ensure they keep pace with inflation in real terms
  • Target market review: ensure CI product meets changing needs (longevity, earlier diagnosis, lifestyle shifts)
iv) Monitoring for Consumer Responsibility compliance
  • Monitor CI claims payout ratio regularly (target: materially above 60%); investigate falls
  • Track claims settlement time: monthly; set internal target (e.g. 30 days); root cause analysis for delays
  • Track premium/maximum benefit ratio for new business: should remain <1.0 for typical policyholders
  • Monitor commission levels vs competitor benchmarks; ensure not driving mis-selling incentives
  • Policyholder satisfaction surveys; complaint volumes and themes; ombudsman referrals
  • Lapse rates by duration: high early lapses may indicate mis-selling or poor product fit
  • Board reporting: annual Consumer Responsibility report with all metrics above
IFoA Apr 2022 — Q148 marks
CI lapse risk in recession — adverse lapse consequences, minimising lapses, premium holiday vs deferral, stakeholder interests, group CI premium adjustment
▾
Country A: recession causing mass CI lapse requests. Individual CI: policyholders want to stop premiums due to income uncertainty. Group CI: recession causing redundancies, reducing group exposure during year.
i) How increased lapses adversely affect insurer
  • Anti-selection: healthy lives lapse → sicker risk pool remains → higher expected future claims; potentially higher premiums → further lapses → spiral
  • Lapse before expenses recovered → loss on those policies; initial expenses and commission unrecovered
  • Fixed costs spread over fewer policies → profitability pressure; economies of scale lost
  • Forced asset disinvestment at unfavourable recession prices (if surrender values must be paid)
  • Reinsurance premiums become relatively more expensive per remaining policy
  • Regulatory scrutiny if large-scale lapsing processed slowly; operational strain on systems
  • Reputational damage if distributors churn — increased commission costs if replaced at re-entry
ii) Actions to minimise lapses
  • Existing policies: Retention team; proactive communication of policy value; flexible premium options (reduce SI, pay-up, premium holiday/deferral); target shorter-duration policies; approach distributors to discourage churning; incentives for most profitable subgroups
  • New policies: Add value-adding benefits (children's benefit, tiered payments); simplify UW; more flexible premium structures; commission clawback to discourage churning on re-entry
iii) Premium holiday (Option A) vs deferral (Option B) vs lapse
  • Option A (premium holiday): vs lapse — retains customer relationship; avoids selective lapse effects; maintains in-force volume (fixed cost spread). Vs lapse — permanently reduces premium income; profitability reduced; potential customer re-assessment at end of holiday
  • Option B (premium deferral): vs lapse — same retention benefits; deferred premiums eventually recovered. Vs lapse — relies on policyholder ability and willingness to pay catch-up premiums; operational complexity; potential for dispute at catch-up date
  • Both Options A and B: better than lapse if they prevent selective lapsing of healthy lives
iv) Stakeholder interests
  • Shareholders: Concerned about profitability impact (reduced premium income, potential increased costs of options); whether selective lapsing or options preserves long-term value; dividend impact
  • Individual policyholders: Want to maintain cover at reduced/deferred cost; concerned about whether CI benefit will be available when needed; fairness of option terms
  • Government: Concerned about maintaining private insurance sector's ability to pay claims; reducing reliance on state benefits if CI cover lapses widely; social policy implications
  • Distributors: Impact on commission income (premium holiday = no commission); risk of client going to competitor; potential for churning to generate new business commission
  • Staff: Job security if business volume falls; increased workload managing lapse requests and premium options; potential redundancies

CI guaranteed vs reviewable premiums — advantages/disadvantages; good practice statement; premium review process; actions when profitability below target
▾
CI insurer offering both guaranteed and reviewable premium CI. No industry statements of good practice on reviewable premiums. Recent review shows reviewable business not meeting profit criteria.
i) Reviewable premiums — advantages and disadvantages for insurer
  • Adv: Better risk management — can re-price if experience deteriorates. Lower initial premiums → market appeal. Lower reserves and capital (option to re-price reduces long-term liability). Easier to obtain reinsurance. Less reliance on pricing data credibility at outset. Less product differentiation risk from competitors with guaranteed products
  • Dis: Review process is onerous and time-consuming. Systems cost (run dual system for guaranteed and reviewable).   • Demonstrate reviews are calculated fairly; disclose basis on which review is carried out
  • Review assumptions regularly; specify the reasons and circumstances under which assumptions can be changed
  • Cannot increase premiums purely to recoup earlier losses
  • Changes to CI conditions list: customers to be on "no worse" terms going forward
  • Communicate results to policyholders clearly; explain reasons for increases in plain language
  • If premiums increase, give policyholders option to amend policy (e.g. reduce SI instead of premium increase)
  • Allow sufficient time for policyholders to make decisions; complaints process must be available

  • Split business into homogeneous groups (by product type, pricing series, premium rates)

  • Medical/lifestyle trend analysis; compare with reinsurer experience
  • Assess future trends: medical advances, screening effects, lifestyle changes
  • Profit test revised rates; compliance check with regulatory requirements
  • Communicate outcome to policyholders with reasons; allow options if premiums increasing
iv) Actions when profitability below target
  • Increase premiums on reviewable business to restore profitability (but balance selective lapse risk)
  • Reduce benefits or tighten condition definitions on new business (not possible on guaranteed in-force)
  • Tighten underwriting on new business to improve future risk selection
  • Improve claims management and control (reduce improper payments; speed up assessment)
  • Review expense base — reduce overheads where possible
  • Consider reinsurance treaty renegotiation or new reinsurance arrangements
  • Consider whether to cease writing new business if product is not viable
  • Communicate transparently with regulator if solvency could be affected

  • Suitable IF: severe debilitating impact comparable to traditional CIs; occurs with sufficient frequency; can be clearly defined (objective measurable triggers); sufficient pricing data available

  • Overall: partially meets needs; existing policyholders not served; some new policyholders excluded by UW
iii) Factors in determining which mental health conditions to cover
  • Must be severe and persistent (not temporary/curable)
  • Must be objectively measurable (e.g. hospitalisation for specified period; inability to work for 12 months)
  • Must have sufficient frequency and data to support pricing
  • Must comply with anti-discrimination law in UW questions
  • Reinsurer input on definitions, UW criteria, pricing data from other markets, reinsurance availability
  • Specialist medical advice on definitions and UW criteria
  • Consider fraud risk (mental health harder to verify objectively than cancer or heart attack)

  • Positive: first-mover advantage if no competitors cover mental health; attracts new segment; PR benefit

  • New mental health questions: clear, relevant, proportionate, sensitive; comply with anti-discrimination law
  • Specialist medical advice needed to develop questions and underwriting criteria
  • Reinsurer guidance on UW approach for mental health conditions
  • Staff training for underwriters on mental health assessment
  • Increased monitoring of claims experience post-launch to validate UW assumptions

Definition open to challenge; if a new pandemic occurs: could face mass claims; capital strain. Over-prudent pricing → unaffordable. Under-prudent → insolvency. Pricing tension structurally unresolvable without specific condition list
  • Adv (policyholder): Greater certainty of coverage; simpler to understand; no risk of coverage gap from new disease
  • Dis (policyholder): Premium likely higher due to uncertainty loading; potential for insurer insolvency risk harming them; disputes at claim stage about what qualifies
IFoA Apr 2023 — Q233 marks
Standalone CI with Return of Premium (ROP) options — triggers, pricing, IFA considerations, policy types, first-mover risk of ceasing ROP
▾
Country Z: industry sells only SACI. ~70% of sales include at least one ROP option. All sold via IFAs. Regulator concerned ROP making SACI unaffordable and widening protection gap.
i) Four possible ROP triggers
  • Surrender/lapse: premiums returned if policy cancelled before term end
  • End of term / age expiry: premiums returned if no CI claim made during term
  • Death (outside a CI claim): premiums returned to estate
  • Claim decline: premiums returned if claim is denied
  • (Also: conversion to another policy; claim within survival/moratorium period; partial claim)
ii) Pricing factors for ROP options
  • Probability and timing of each trigger event
  • Expected selective lapse by healthier lives for surrender ROP (adverse selection on trigger)
  • Investment return assumed on pre-funded reserves
  • Amount of premium returned (full vs partial, net of charges)
  • Fairness between policyholders with and without the option
  • Anti-selection implications: which lives are more likely to select each trigger type?
iii) IFA considerations before recommending ROP
  • Client need and affordability — ROP increases premium significantly; may reduce sum assured to stay affordable
  • Value as quasi-savings vehicle: does client have alternative savings? Is ROP the best mechanism?
  • Tax implications of premium return (varies by jurisdiction)
  • Commission implications — higher premium = higher commission; must demonstrate this is in client's interest (TCF)
  • Regulatory risk if added complexity leads to client misunderstanding
vi) Consequences for first insurer to cease ROP
  • Competitive disadvantage — other insurers still offering ROP; policyholders and IFAs prefer ROP option
  • Potential reduction in sales volumes and market share
  • IFAs may recommend competitors; insurer-distributor relationship damaged
  • If new lower premium attracts different (younger/healthier) target market → could be beneficial to risk pool long-term
  • First-mover may capture positive PR for acting in consumer interest — regulatory goodwill
  • Market may not follow immediately — competitive disadvantage period before industry moves
