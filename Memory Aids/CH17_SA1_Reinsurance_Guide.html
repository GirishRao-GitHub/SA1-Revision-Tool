💡 Why Reinsure? — The Full Framework
The seven reasons are always the starting point. But in the exam you must frame each reason in the specific scenario given. A reason unsupported by context earns ½ mark at most.

1. Control of Risk — Limit Exposure & Smooth Profits ▼
  • Despite statistical analysis and experienced underwriters, future claims outgo cannot be predicted with certainty — this uncertainty is the essence of insurance.
  • Factors governing reinsurance appetite: size of insurer; marketplace experience; available free assets; portfolio size (credibility factor); predictability of business outcomes.
  • Larger free assets and larger portfolio reduce the need for RI — but a large insurer writing unusual/large risks may still need substantial RI.
  • For large individual risks (CI, IP, specialist LTCI for younger policyholders with large funds): XL or surplus treaty to stabilise emerging profits.
  • For group schemes: more prone to accumulation risk — catastrophe XL for simultaneous events (epidemic, legal risk, group screening programme).
  • For portfolio concentration (by product, geography, industry): reinsurer can organise retrocession to diversify across its own portfolio.
  • For new products / territories: quota share with low initial retention; reduces as insurer's confidence grows.
  • Reinsurance reduces variance of experience relative to mean — directly improves year-on-year account stability, especially for immature portfolios.
IAI 0523 Examiner Answer: "It can protect the actual solvency of the company. This may be especially welcome given uncertainty about future trends in the incidence of claims and duration of illness, e.g. the impact of screening. Can iron out experience fluctuations — even for an established insurer, it protects against unwanted 'blips' in the emergence of surplus."
2. Capital & Solvency Relief ▼
  • Solvency capital requirements are normally reduced in proportion to the amount ceded — but subject to an upper limit.
  • Upper limit reason: cedant remains legally liable to policyholders even if 100% reinsured. A minimum capital level must always be held regardless of RI cover.
  • RI also reduces the Solvency II SCR: morbidity risk decreases (though counterparty default risk increases), which reduces the risk margin and reduces volatility of the realistic balance sheet.
  • Deposit back arrangements mitigate (but do not eliminate) reinsurer default risk.
⚠ Reinsurer Default Risk: Cedant remains liable to policyholders even if fully reinsured. Consequences of reinsurer failure: cedant liable for reinsurer's share of all claims; solvency position worsens; must seek replacement cover (likely more expensive); premium rate increases likely.
3. Financial Assistance — New Business Strain, Solvency, Takeovers ▼
  • (i) New business strain financing: Quota share commission "factors" future surplus streams — effectively an advance loan repaid via higher future RI premiums. Reinsurer takes lapse risk. Commission clawback if policy lapses.
  • (ii) Free asset improvement: Reinsurer pays initial commission in exchange for a share of future surpluses. Can be off-balance-sheet if contingent (but this benefit largely eliminated under Solvency II / realistic reporting regimes).
  • (iii) Takeover / M&A financing: Reinsurer advances funds against anticipated future profits of the acquired portfolio. Same structure as NB strain but applied to acquisitions.
  • (iv) Securitisation: Insurance cash flows converted into tradeable financial instruments; effectiveness also reduced under Solvency II.
  • Effectiveness of financial RI under Solvency II is much reduced — any funding provided is offset by the liability to repay it in future; net effect on available capital ≈ zero.
IAI 0322 — FinRe Suitability for SAHI (7 marks): "Suitability depends on what drives the low solvency. VIF monetisation works for large blocks of profitable long-term business — which is less applicable to a medium-size health insurer. Commission financing helps reduce NB strain — again, works for long-term products with high initial acquisition costs. Overall, given the nature (SAHI), FinRe solutions are less likely to help here."
4. Technical Assistance — The Most Underestimated Reason ▼
Product Development & Data
  • Reinsurer data aggregated from many cedants — particularly valuable for new product launches where the insurer has no own experience
  • Data adjustments needed before use: mix of business, UW level, product terms, claim conditions, geography, credibility, time period, inflation, currency etc.
  • Reinsurers drove CI insurance development in the 1980s using data from other markets weighted by local population data
  • Access to national and international databases — reinsurers typically will not release data on individual cedants but can provide aggregate "no-names" results
Risk Management Support
  • Underwriting manual: rules on acceptance; evidence requirements; exclusions; unusual risk loadings; referral procedures; medical section
  • Increasingly, reinsurers will audit the cedant's underwriting practice rather than accepting underwriting risk directly
  • Claims control: training, initial assessment, monitoring experience — critical because health claims can recur, are hard to verify, and long-term policies are at fixed rates
  • Policy wording & product design: identify defects in wording leading to unintended claims
  • IT software: automates proposal processing (up to 80% of cases); assists claims acceptance; supports reserving
  • Overseas operations: local data, contacts, legal/regulatory guidance, translation of forms
Key exam point: When the insurer enters a new market / launches a new product, technical assistance is the primary reason for RI — not risk transfer. The reinsurer aligns its incentives by taking a risk share rather than charging a fee (so both parties want the product to succeed). In some markets, fees are rare.
5. Tax Arbitrage & Capital (Regulatory) Arbitrage ▼
Tax Arbitrage
  • Reinsurance should theoretically be self-balancing — what insurer pays out = what reinsurer receives
  • If basis of taxation differs for reinsurer vs cedant, it may be possible to improve the after-tax bottom line for both parties via a treaty
  • Example: if insurer is taxed on investment returns but reinsurer is taxed only on profits → passing income through the reinsurer reduces overall tax bill
  • Savings shared between both parties → insurer can price more competitively
  • Risk: rules may change in the future, destroying the arbitrage
Capital Arbitrage
  • Reinsurer may be required to hold less capital per unit of risk than the insurer under their respective regulatory regimes
  • Lower cost of capital in reinsurer's hands → savings can be shared with insurer via cheaper reinsurance premiums or higher commission
  • Makes both parties better off — win-win arrangement
  • Quota share is popular for both arbitrage types (though it also cedes profit, so the arbitrage must outweigh the profit ceded)
6. Treaty as Regulatory Check / Premium Validation ▼
  • The terms on which the reinsurer accepts business provide a check on the insurer's premium rates
  • Reinsurer co-approves premium tables under quota share; retains the right to vary commission if insurer changes premium rates
  • For a new company, the presence of a reinsurance treaty may be a regulatory requirement
  • Regulator can require additional reinsurance to limit exposure to adverse experience — protecting policyholders from insolvency risk; however, availability on acceptable terms cannot be guaranteed
⚡ Difference of Interests (often examined)
INSURER prefers:
  • Quantity of business
  • Quick claims settlement (bears small retention)
  • Volume growth
REINSURER prefers:
  • Quality of business
  • Careful claims consideration (bears large share)
  • Underwriting discipline
Why does insurer handle large XL claims prudently even if 100% reinsured above excess? (1) Long-term reinsurer relationship; (2) profit commission at stake; (3) future premiums are experience-rated.

Proportional Reinsurance
Quota Share
  • Fixed R% of every risk in treaty ceded
  • Always treaty basis; short-term health = original terms only
  • Reinsurer receives R% premiums, pays R% claims
  • Reinsurance commission paid to cedant (covers initial expenses)
  • Deposits back: reinsurer deposits its reserve share with cedant — cedant retains full investment income; mitigates default risk
  • Profit commission: reinsurer repays share of profits if portfolio performs well
  • Advantages: simple; reduces solvency capital; financing; reciprocal business; parameter risk reduction
  • Disadvantage: inflexible — same % regardless of risk size; cedes profit on all policies including small risks
Surplus Reinsurance
  • Cedant sets a monetary retention per risk
  • Cedes only the excess above retention → proportion varies by policy
  • Long-term only; fixed sum insured required at outset
  • Not applicable: PMI (indemnity basis, claim unknown at outset); short-term health
  • Example (retention £75k):
  • Policy A SI=£150k → 50% ceded
  • Policy B SI=£100k → 25% ceded
  • Policy C SI=£50k → 0% ceded
  • Advantage: efficiently targets large risks; doesn't cede profit on small risks
  • Disadvantage: less effective for parameter risk; unsuitable for financing

Non-Proportional (Excess of Loss) Reinsurance
Risk XL
  • Operates on individual claims
  • Reinsurer pays excess of each claim above retention up to upper limit
  • Layers must be contiguous — no gaps
  • Main use: PMI portfolios (large individual treatment costs)
  • Proportional co-participation within layer (e.g. 90%) to deter moral hazard
  • No RI commission; profit commission possible on lower layers
Aggregate XL / Stop Loss
  • Covers total claims for the whole account over 12 months above excess point
  • Limits expressed as % of earned premiums — prevents insurer expanding volume to trigger fixed monetary limits
  • In H&C: aggregate XL and stop loss are interchangeable (only one peril: morbidity)
  • Reinsurers reluctant: limited control over underwriting/claims; historically loss-making
  • Rarely used for individual long-term insurance
Catastrophe XL
  • Triggered by single common event (epidemic, workplace explosion) exceeding retention on aggregate basis
  • Pandemic risk generally not available at affordable premiums — extreme uncertainty
  • If treaty defines pandemic as single occurrence → could attach; but definitions matter critically
  • Government backstop may be the only realistic solution for pandemic
Stability clause: Indexes XL retention and upper limit to inflation — preserves real value of cover over time. Cedant pays additional premium if retention not indexed. Important for PMI where medical cost inflation can be significant.
Key Rule: Non-proportional reinsurance is generally only appropriate for short-term contracts (PMI) and possibly group long-term contracts. Not appropriate for individual long-term contracts with fixed sums insured (CI, IP) — because claim size is already capped at the sum insured, so XL is unlikely to attach.
Financial Reinsurance (Fin Re)
Structure 1: Risk Premium RI with Financing
  • RI commission acts as an upfront loan
  • Repaid via inflated future RI premiums over remaining policy term
  • Lapse risk factored into repayment schedule
Structure 2: Contingent Loan (Surplus Relief)
  • Reinsurer advances loan repayable only from future VIF profits
  • If VIF does not emerge, reinsurer has made a capital gift
  • May be off-balance-sheet under some regimes (not under Solvency II)
  • Contingent repayments > non-contingent (priced for default risk)
Impact on EV and Solvency II
  • Realistic EV broadly unchanged: loan received ≈ PV of future repayments (which reduce future profit) → net effect ≈ zero
  • However, risk is reduced: uncertain VIF converted into certain cash
  • Under Solvency II: effectiveness much reduced — any funding offset by liability to repay in future
  • Only effective under regimes where: future profits not recognised as assets AND realistic liability not required for loan repayments
  • Assets restructuring: reduces inadmissible assets to improve solvency ratio

Stop-Loss — Calculation SP1 Revision Notes
EXAMPLE: Stop Loss with Co-participation
Earned premiums: £292m
Incurred claims: £333m
Stop loss: Lower limit 105% | Upper limit 125% | Reinsurer covers 80%
Claims ratio = £333m / £292m = 114% ← within layer (105%–125%)
Lower limit £ = 105% × £292m = £306.6m
Upper limit £ = 125% × £292m = £365m
Claims within layer = £333m − £306.6m = £26.4m
Reinsurance recovery = 80% × £26.4m = £21.12m
Cedant co-participation = 20% × £26.4m = £5.28m
Why % of earned premiums? Fixed monetary limits allow insurer to expand volume to trigger the cover. Expressing as % of earned premiums keeps the attachment proportional to business written — reinsurer cannot be exploited by volume growth.
IAI 1222 — Stop-Loss Premium Ranking Logic 18 marks
Products: Individual IP (mean=85cr, SD=25cr) | Group CI (mean=75cr, SD=30cr) | LTCI (mean=90cr, SD=5cr) | PMI (mean=75cr, SD=30cr)
Treaty StructureKey LogicRanking (highest → lowest premium)
XS 70cr LTCI: 70cr is well below mean of 90cr (50%+ chance of recovery). IP/CI: 70cr ≈ 0.2 SD below mean. PMI: 0.33 SD below mean. But for large recoveries, high SD products (IP/CI/PMI) win over stable LTCI. LTCI highest premium (highest probability of any recovery); then IP ≈ CI ≈ PMI for large recoveries
XS 100cr PMI: 100cr is 1.67 SD above mean → greatest recovery probability. CI: 0.83 SD above. IP: 0.6 SD above. LTCI: 2 SD above (very unlikely). PMI > CI > IP > LTCI
XS 90cr capped 25cr PMI most likely to breach upper limit (high SD relative to layer). Same order as XS 100cr. PMI > CI > IP > LTCI
Burning cost limitation (IAI 1222 examiners): Burning cost of CI always appears larger than IP (consistent with large individual claims). However, burning cost for IP and LTCI at high attachment points = zero (no historical exceedances) — implying zero premium, which is inappropriate since there is still some probability of future recovery. Neither burning cost alone nor analytical approach alone is sufficient.
Practice Q&A — Mechanics
▶Why would an insurer reinsure only the sum at risk rather than the full sum insured on a long-term contract?
  • At any point, the insurer already holds assets = the statutory reserve for the policy
  • If a claim occurs, the insurer only needs additional funds for the excess of sum insured over the reserve — the sum at risk
  • Reinsuring the full sum insured means paying premiums on the reserve-backed portion for no useful risk protection
  • Since reinsurance carries a net cost (RI premiums > expected recoveries), reinsuring the reserve portion wastes profit
▶Why are stop loss limits expressed as % of earned premiums rather than absolute monetary amounts?
  • If limits were fixed in £, the insurer could write substantially more business to increase the likelihood of triggering the stop loss without the reinsurer having been paid an appropriate additional premium
  • Expressing as % ensures both the attachment point and upper limit rise proportionally with business volume
  • Keeps the cedant's co-participation proportionate and maintains appropriate reinsurance pricing
⚖️ Determining the Retention Level
Two Approaches to Setting Retention
1. Ruin Probability Method
  • Use stochastic model to simulate portfolio claims across many scenarios
  • Set retention at the level that keeps probability of insolvency below a specified threshold (e.g. 1 in 200)
  • For long-term fixed-benefit (IP, CI): model claim incidence stochastically
  • For short-term indemnity (PMI): model both incidence AND claim amounts stochastically
2. Cost Minimisation Method
  • As retention rises → cost of RI falls but insurer must hold a larger internal fluctuation reserve
  • As retention falls → RI cost rises (more external cover) but fluctuation reserve shrinks
  • Set retention at level that minimises total cost = cost of fluctuation reserve + cost of RI premiums
  • Practical tool for balancing internal capital vs external risk transfer
Factors Driving Lower Retention (More RI)
INCREASES REINSURANCE (lower retention):
  • Greater uncertainty about future claims experience
  • Lower acceptable insolvency probability (more conservative risk tolerance)
  • Higher variance in claim amounts (indemnity products)
  • Lower free assets (less internal capital buffer)
  • Newer insurer / new product / new territory
  • Higher writing capacity sought (want to write more volume)
  • Regulatory requirement (new company)
DECREASES REINSURANCE (higher retention):
  • Large, well-capitalised insurer with substantial free assets
  • Large diversified portfolio (more credible claims experience)
  • Long, established track record — confident in pricing assumptions
  • RI becomes expensive / poor value (rates cycle up)
  • Insurer wants to retain more profit (concern about ceding profit)
  • Fixed-benefit products (CI/IP) — large insurers may dispense with XL entirely
IAI 0619 — CFO Proposes Reducing RI Substantially 8 marks
CFO concern: Net claim ratio rising faster than gross → reinsurer appears to be profiting at insurer's expense. CFO wants to recapture this profit by reducing RI.
Reply points (examiner):
  • ✓ RI premium is paid annually in advance; recoveries received over next year — timing mismatch creates apparent imbalance
  • ✓ If RI is making profit, it is on expected value basis in the long run — in any individual year, results will vary
  • ✓ Major reason for RI is to reduce variance and protect company from short-term fluctuations — not purely to make a profit on RI account
  • ✓ Lower retention → lower variance → lower capital needs; higher retention → need more capital buffer
  • ✓ Conduct a study to find optimal RI level aligned with company's risk appetite and capital position
  • ✓ Being a new company, may be technically dependent on RI; existing treaty contracts may restrict ability to reduce
IAI 0916 — Recapture Decision 4+5 marks
  • Recapturing business from reinsurer → insurer retains its share of RI profits going forward
  • Reinsurer will charge a recapture fee — must be validated against value of future RI profits being released
  • Validate fee: compare recapture fee to actuarial estimate of PV of future reinsurance profits (VIF of RI book); if fee > VIF of RI profits, recapture is not value-accretive
  • Also consider: impact on capital requirements post-recapture (RI was providing solvency relief); available free assets must be sufficient to cover higher net retention
  • Treaty may have restrictions on recapture timing and conditions
📈 Financial Impact of Reinsurance
Reinsurance Credit in Valuation IAI 0523 — 12+3+5 marks
SINGLE CI POLICY — Expected Pattern Over Term
  • Gross reserve: Increases in first half (initial expenses + prudence margin; premium < cost of cover in later years). Reduces gradually to zero at expiry — same U-shaped pattern as a level premium endowment.
  • Reinsurance credit: = discounted PV of future expected excess of RI recovery over RI premium for remaining term. Starts highest in Year 1 (longest remaining term); reduces monotonically to zero at policy expiry.
  • Net reserve (= Gross − RI credit): follows same broad shape as gross reserve but lower throughout.
AGGREGATE CI BOOK — Year-on-Year Stability (0523 Q1iiib)
  • If similar volume written for 12+ years → mix of policy durations is broadly stable
  • Aggregate gross reserve, RI credit and net reserve all expected to be similar to prior year
  • The "steady state" effect — new business entering the book replaces matured/lapsed policies
IMPACT OF RI ARRANGEMENT ON RESERVES / EV / INVESTMENT INCOME (0523 Q1ivb)
  • Reserves: reduced — prudence margin on 50% of benefit is lost (if RI premium = BE mortality assumption)
  • EV (traditional method): improves (reduced reserves + reduced capital requirement)
  • EV (market-consistent): no material impact (RI priced at BE → no economic gain)
  • Investment income: slightly reduced — RI premiums paid in advance, recoveries received in arrear → cash-flow timing disadvantage
Overall Financial Impact — Pricing and Profitability
  • Net cost: RI premiums generally exceed expected RI benefits — reinsurer must cover own expenses and profit. Net cost must be included in pricing model.
  • Exception — arbitrage: where tax or capital arbitrage exists, pricing net of RI can improve competitiveness (net premium lower than gross).
  • Office premium build-up includes: risk premium + expense loading + profit margin + cost of capital + cost of reserves + reinsurance cost/recoveries + margins + investment return credit + persistency adjustment + tax
  • Liquidity: RI can reduce liquidity requirement; but gross claims may need to be paid before RI recoveries are received — creates tension with matching strategy
  • New business strain: RI commission (quota share, original terms) offsets initial NB strain — allows higher volumes to be written with the same capital
IAI 1120 — XL → QS switch assessment (5 marks): "QS is preferable when company is looking for: solvency capital relief; technical support from reinsurer; expanding to new geographies/risks; writing more business without increasing capital base. A high level of QS would imply the company playing more of a fronting role. There may be regulatory limits on % reinsured. XL is preferable when: company is more experienced and confident taking risk; concerned about passing too much profit to reinsurer under QS."

IP / CI — Reinsurance Logic
  • Long-term; fixed sums insured → proportional RI preferred
  • Surplus: cedes only excess above retention → efficiently targets large sums insured (key person IP, large CI)
  • Quota share: for new products/territories; parameter risk reduction; NB strain financing
  • XL not appropriate for individual long-term CI/IP — claim size capped at sum insured (XL unlikely to attach)
  • Group IP: quota share (original terms) simplest; proportion may reduce over time; catastrophe XL if concerned about simultaneous claims from same employer
IAI 0524: "Insurer likely to arrange quota-share RI, probably on original terms, in order to build up experience in the first few years without taking too large a risk."
PMI / OP Cover — Reinsurance Logic
  • Short-term indemnity; claim amount unknown at outset → non-proportional RI applicable
  • Risk XL: caps individual large treatment costs (e.g. cancer, complex surgery)
  • Stability clause critical — medical cost inflation can erode real value of XL limits
  • Stop loss: rarely used but applicable for group/aggregate protection
  • OP cover (IAI 0722): high frequency / low severity → catastrophe XL less relevant. Quota share more appropriate — provides capacity, technical assistance, solvency relief. XL less relevant as claims are smaller but more numerous.
  • Proportional co-participation (e.g. 90% reinsured) in XL layers to maintain cedant claims management incentive
IAI 0921 — Assess 5 RI Types for Pandemic Risk 15 marks (3 each)
RI TypeSuitability for Catastrophic Pandemic RiskVerdict
Quota Share Cedes x% of all claims — insurer still exposed to (1-x)% of the catastrophe. Helps with solvency capital and technical support. Does not address the fundamental concern. Not appropriate
Individual Surplus Manages large individual fixed-sum claims. Pandemic risk materialises as high frequency, not high individual face amounts. Still exposed to frequency risk. Not appropriate
Per-person XL Cumulates claims on same person per year above attachment point. Pandemic = high frequency of moderate claims, not large per-person costs. Low attachment = quasi quota share (reinsurer reluctant). Partially appropriate
Per-occurrence XL Depends critically on treaty wording. If pandemic = single occurrence → appropriate. If occurrence = one claim episode → does not address frequency risk. Generally lower cost than aggregate XL. Conditionally appropriate
Aggregate XL Covers total claims above threshold over 12 months — directly addresses the frequency/accumulation risk of a pandemic. More expensive; availability limited; cedant co-participation likely required. Most appropriate
🌐 Contextual Q&As — Key Exam Scenarios
New Market / New Product Entry
▶[IAI 0524 pattern] An insurer is launching a new product (e.g. Impaired Life Annuity / Group IP). Discuss reinsurance considerations.~4–5 marks per product
  • RI will protect against claim volatility, which will be uncertain for a new product with small initial volumes
  • Technical assistance: data for pricing, underwriting criteria, product design input — critical when insurer lacks own experience
  • Reduces risk → reduces capital requirements (solvency capital relief)
  • Insurer likely to arrange quota share, probably on original terms, to build experience without taking too large a risk
  • The proportion ceded might reduce over time as experience develops and confidence grows
  • For group IP: may consider catastrophe XL if concerned about multiple simultaneous claims from same employer
  • Check availability of RI for the target product in the new territory — not always guaranteed
▶What data adjustments are needed before using reinsurer data for pricing?
  • Mix of business (product type, customer profile)
  • Level of underwriting applied
  • Product terms and conditions (options, features)
  • Claim conditions and control processes
  • Geography / jurisdiction
  • Credibility and time period of the data
  • Effects of medical advances and technology changes
  • Policy sizes and target market
  • Sales channel / distribution processes
  • Reinsurance arrangements of the contributing cedants
  • Competitiveness of the market from which data is drawn
  • Effects of inflation (on sums insured and claim amounts)
  • Trends in mortality / morbidity
  • Currency differences
Solvency & Capital Problems
▶[IAI 0322] Discuss the suitability of financial reinsurance as a solution to a SAHI's borderline solvency problem.7 marks
  • Suitability depends on the nature of the solvency problem — what specifically is driving the low solvency
  • Also depends on: whether the solution is acceptable to the regulator; cost vs benefit of the arrangement
  • Different FinRe structures:
  • → Lump sum fund + profit participation: creates assets immediately; works when insurer has predictable future surpluses from RI business
  • → VIF monetisation: requires large blocks of profitable long-term insurance business — less applicable to a SAHI with mainly short-term PMI
  • → Commission financing: reduces NB strain — works for long-term products with large initial acquisition costs
  • → Asset restructuring: reduces inadmissible assets to improve regulatory solvency ratio
  • Overall: for a medium-sized SAHI primarily writing short-term business, FinRe solutions are less likely to help materially
  • Effectiveness also much reduced under Solvency II / realistic reporting regimes
Underwriting Innovation & RI Implications
▶[IFoA 0422] What are the reinsurance-specific risks of photo-based underwriting?~2 marks
  • Reinsurer reluctance to accept business underwritten using photo-based tools — may require higher margins to price the additional uncertainty
  • Algorithmic bias risks may also concern reinsurers from a regulatory / reputational standpoint
  • Vendor dependency risk (if photo AI company ceases trading) could undermine underwriting integrity, which reinsurer relies on
▶[IFoA 0924] How could reinsurers assist when adding mental health conditions to a CI product?~3 marks
  • Assist with defining eligible mental health conditions (condition definitions)
  • Provide underwriting criteria and exclusions for mental health history
  • Supply pricing data from other markets where mental health CI is already offered
  • Confirm availability and cost of reinsurance for the mental health-inclusive product (availability not guaranteed)
Deteriorating Claims Experience — RI Actions
▶What reinsurance actions can an insurer take when IP / PMI claims experience deteriorates?
  • Seek reinsurer advice on claims management improvements and underwriting tightening
  • Review and potentially change reinsurance programme — e.g. add aggregate XL / stop loss if not already in place
  • Consider reducing retention level to transfer more risk to reinsurer
  • Engage reinsurer as technical support for product redesign (stricter claim definitions, deferred periods, benefit caps)
  • Check whether reinsurance recoverability is being maximised for in-payment claims
  • Review reinsurance treaty terms — ensure RI commission thresholds trigger appropriately on unfavourable experience
  • Regulator may require additional RI as condition of continued operation (IFoA 0922)
High Inflation Context & Reinsurance IFoA 0924 / IAI 0924
▶How does a high-inflation environment affect reinsurance considerations?
  • Counterparty risk: reinsurers may be downgraded as their own claim costs inflate — cedant should monitor reinsurer financial strength more actively
  • XL limits: need to review stability clauses — if limits are not indexed, real value of cover erodes and XL is less likely to attach for the same severity of loss
  • RI premium levels: reinsurers will reprice at renewal to reflect higher claims costs — RI becomes more expensive, and cost must be reflected in office premium build-up
  • Outsourced services: third-party administrators may also face cost pressures, affecting quality — this has indirect RI implications if it affects claims data quality
  • Office premium build-up: multiple components shift simultaneously — including RI cost — so full review needed before repricing
Overseas PMI — RI Data Sources & Mitigants IAI 0523
  • Data sources: national health statistics; local regulator publications; hospital expense data; competitor pricing data; reinsurer multi-national databases; own domestic morbidity data (adjusted for local conditions)
  • Risk mitigants: compulsory scheme membership (reduces anti-selection); reinsurance / coinsurance (risk transfer + technical assistance); strong PED exclusions; full individual underwriting for high-risk classes; clear terms and conditions; strong claims controls
  • Solvency impact: entering new overseas market → short-term deterioration (start-up costs, NB strain, capital requirements); long-term improvement if profitable. Capital raising or quota-share RI can manage the transition.
🏷 Badging — Alternative to Reinsurance
What is Badging?
  • Arrangement where insurer provides policy under its own brand name, but a specialist health insurer actually issues the policy and undertakes all administration, claims processing and payments
  • Key distinction from reinsurance: under badging, all risk is taken on by the specialist insurer from the outset; under reinsurance, the issuing company takes on the risk first and then (partly or totally) passes it on to the reinsurer
Advantages of Badging
  • Non-specialist insurer ("Popular Insurance") gains access to health expertise without taking insurance risk
  • Specialist insurer ("Healthwise") gains access to large customer base / brand distribution
  • Both parties benefit — profit-sharing arrangement on retrospective basis
  • Reduces insurance risk similarly to reinsurance → lower capital requirement for the branding insurer
  • No need to build internal health and care infrastructure
Risks of Badging
  • Reputational risk: poor service by specialist insurer could damage the branded insurer's reputation
  • Legal permissibility: pure badging may not be allowed — local regulation may require the product provider's name to be clearly disclosed in product literature
  • Loss of control over customer experience and claims management
  • Dependency on the specialist — loss of the specialist partner is disruptive
  • Profit-sharing terms must be carefully negotiated and regularly reviewed
▶Compare badging and reinsurance as methods of accessing health and care expertise without taking full risk.
  • Badging: Selling insurer takes NO insurance risk — all risk borne by specialist from outset. Not technically an alternative to RI in the strict sense; the selling insurer never assumed the risk. Better described as a distribution arrangement.
  • Reinsurance: Issuing company takes risk first, then cedes part or all to reinsurer. Issuing company remains liable to policyholders if reinsurer defaults.
  • Badging gives simpler risk elimination but at cost of less control and potential regulatory/reputational risk.
  • Reinsurance preserves issuing company's regulatory relationship with policyholders but requires minimum capital to be held regardless.
📋 Inwards Reinsurance Reserving IAI May 2025 — NEW
This topic appeared for the first time in the IAI May 2025 paper (Q2, 13 marks). It covers the perspective of the reinsurance company itself — calculating IBNR reserves for inwards reinsurance business. High probability of re-examination.
Grouping Data for Inwards RI Reserving 2 marks
  • Type of contract: Facultative vs Treaty
  • Type of cover: Quota Share / Surplus / Per-risk XL / Aggregate XL
  • Basis of cover: Loss occurring / Risk attaching / Claims made
  • Line of business (CI / PMI / IP etc.)
  • Data characteristics: must be homogeneous, credible, relevant, reliable, complete and accurate
Additional Data Issues vs Direct Insurance Reserving 2 marks
  • Longer claims reporting delays — insurers submit bordereaux after end of quarter → reinsurer's data is more lagged than a direct insurer's
  • Greater heterogeneity — more contract types, differing terms and conditions, differing case reserving philosophies → development patterns are less stable
  • Industry benchmarks less applicable — heterogeneity means standard industry development factors may not transfer well
  • Less detail on losses — information provided on aggregated basis, not individual claim level → harder to assess tail development
  • IT complexity — complexity of reinsurance contracts makes it hard to capture all terms and conditions in IT systems accurately
Accident Year vs Underwriting Year Chain Ladder 2 marks
  • Accident year: chain ladder estimates ultimate claims for claims occurring in that accident year only — includes only expired risk (past events). No future IBNR for unexpired exposure in that cohort.
  • Underwriting year: chain ladder estimates ultimate claims for all claims arising from policies sold in that underwriting year — includes both expired and unexpired risk portions. IBNR includes claims from policies that have not yet expired.
  • For IBNR purposes at the valuation date: accident year is appropriate (IBNR relates to claims already incurred on expired exposure).
Earned vs Written Premium for IBNR 2 marks
  • IBNR reserves relate to claims that have already occurred — consistent with earned premium (the portion of premium covering exposure already provided)
  • As at valuation date, a portion of written premium is unearned — this covers claim events occurring after the valuation date
  • Using written premium overstates the base → overstates the implied IBNR for incurred claims
IBNR Calculation Example 5 marks
IAI 0525 Q2e — CI Reinsurance IBNR (3 Accident Years)
AY2023: Earned premium = ₹1,200L (100% earned) | ULR = 93%
Ultimate claims = 93% × ₹1,200L = ₹1,111L
Paid/notified to date = (₹1,111L − ₹166.76L) → IBNR = ₹166.76L
AY2024: Earned premium = ₹1,250L × 66.67% = ₹833.33L | ULR = 89%
Ultimate claims = 89% × ₹1,250L = ₹1,112.5L (on full UY)
Earned share = 66.67% × ₹1,112.5L → IBNR = ₹27.69L
AY2025: Earned premium = ₹1,100L × 33.33% | ULR = 76%
IBNR = ₹83.33L
Total IBNR = ₹166.76L + ₹27.69L + ₹83.33L = ₹277.79L
Finalising the IBNR — Issues to Consider 2 marks
  • If CI policies have term significantly different from 3 years → distorts earned premium calculation and hence IBNR
  • Development percentages derived from domestic Indian CI data may not apply to inwards CI reinsurance from other territories
  • IBNR calculated is gross of retrocession — retrocession recoveries must be calculated separately to arrive at net IBNR
  • Level of reserving margin to book — depends on company reserving policy and materiality
  • Whether changes in claims development patterns invalidate the assumption that past development is a good guide to the future
✍️ Exam Technique — Reinsurance Questions
⚡ The 5 Rules for Reinsurance in the Exam
  • Rule 1 — Always anchor to the scenario. "Reduces variance" earns ½ mark. "Reduces variance of IP claims which are particularly volatile given the impaired lives book and new market entry" earns 1 mark.
  • Rule 2 — State the RI type, not just the concept. Don't say "reinsurance can help". Say "quota share on original terms would provide..." or "XL reinsurance is appropriate here because..."
  • Rule 3 — Cover both sides. For "assess" questions, always give advantages AND disadvantages / suitability AND limitations. Each XL type in the 0921 question was worth 3 marks — typically 1.5 marks each side.
  • Rule 4 — Match RI type to product type. Never suggest XL for individual long-term CI/IP. Never suggest surplus for PMI. This is an automatic mark loss.
  • Rule 5 — Address cost vs benefit explicitly. For any RI question, always note that: (a) RI premiums exceed expected benefits → net cost to insurer; (b) this cost must be recovered in office premiums; (c) exception: where arbitrage (tax/capital) makes RI value-accretive.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • ✗ Suggesting XL reinsurance for individual CI or IP — claim is fixed at sum insured, XL won't attach
  • ✗ Suggesting surplus reinsurance for PMI — no fixed sum insured; not applicable
  • ✗ Saying financial RI "eliminates" solvency problem — its effectiveness is much reduced under Solvency II
  • ✗ Confusing "badging" with reinsurance — under badging, the branding insurer never takes the risk in the first place
  • ✗ Ignoring the timing issue: RI credit DECLINES over the policy term to zero at expiry (not increases)
  • ✗ Forgetting cedant remains liable to policyholders even if 100% reinsured — minimum capital always required
  • ✗ Writing "reinsurance commission paid by insurer to reinsurer" — it's the opposite (reinsurer pays commission to cedant under original terms)
Quick Recall — Non-Negotiable Facts
  • Short-term health: only quota share on original terms (no surplus, no risk premium basis)
  • Surplus: only long-term, only fixed sum insured at outset
  • XL commission: none (except profit commission on lower layers)
  • Stop loss limits: % of earned premiums, not absolute £
  • RI credit at policy expiry: zero
  • Reinsurer default: cedant remains liable to policyholders
  • Fac/Ob: cedant has discretion; reinsurer must accept
  • Ob/Ob: most common treaty basis
  • Deposits back: cedant retains investment income on reinsurer's reserve share
  • Badging: selling insurer takes no insurance risk
  • FinRe EV impact: broadly neutral (loan ≈ PV of repayments)
  • Pandemic XL: generally not available at affordable premiums
