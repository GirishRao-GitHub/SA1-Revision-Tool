Contract Boundary (IFRS 17.34)
The contract boundary is 1 year. Since the insurer has the practical ability to reprice or reject at renewal, the boundary ends at the anniversary date. All cash flows beyond this boundary are excluded from the measurement of the group. This is critical for annual health policies — unlike multi-year products, no future renewals are included.

IFRS 17 Measurement Model (GMM)
Building blocks of the insurance contract liability
Under the General Measurement Model (GMM), the insurance contract liability = FCF + CSM. For annual health policies, the Premium Allocation Approach (PAA) is often permitted as a simplification if it approximates GMM. We walk through GMM here for completeness.
Three Building Blocks
Building Block 1
Present Value of FCF
Best estimate of all future cash flows (premiums, claims, expenses) discounted at a current risk-free rate + illiquidity premium
Building Block 2
Risk Adjustment (RA)
Compensation for non-financial uncertainty. Typically set using a confidence interval (e.g. 75th percentile). Disclosed as equivalent confidence level.
Building Block 3
CSM
Unearned profit for future coverage. Released over coverage units as services are provided. Never negative (floor at zero — surplus = Loss Component).

PAA Simplification — Eligibility for Health Policies
Most individual annual health policies qualify for PAA because: (a) coverage period ≤ 1 year, or (b) the PAA liability approximates GMM. Under PAA, the LRC is simply the unearned premium reserve — a straight-line or pattern-based amortisation. LIC is still measured on a full FCF basis. Indian insurers widely use PAA for annual health products.

GST Treatment
Health insurance attracts 18% GST in India. GST collected (₹15,000 × 18% = ₹2,700) is not included in IFRS 17 premium — it is a pass-through to the government. The ₹15,000 in this walkthrough is the net premium (ex-GST). Input Tax Credit on business expenses follows GST law separately.

Claims Management & Reserving
Incurred claims, IBNR, and LIC measurement
A claim occurs on 10 August. The insured undergoes hospitalisation. Total hospital bill: ₹1,20,000. The insurer receives the claim intimation on 15 August. After investigation and processing, ₹1,15,000 is approved (after deductible/co-pay); ₹5,000 is rejected. Payment is made on 5 September.
Claims Life Cycle
10 Aug — Loss event (hospitalisation begins)
Claim is "incurred". IBNR reserve covers this until intimation.
15 Aug — Claim intimation received
Claim is registered. IBNER (Incurred But Not Enough Reserved) tracking begins. TPA forwards documents.
20–28 Aug — Investigation & adjudication
TPA scrutinises bills, verifies policy terms, exclusions, sub-limits. Cashless not applicable (reimbursement policy).
30 Aug — Claim sanctioned
₹1,15,000 approved. Case reserve established. ₹5,000 repudiated (non-medical expense).
5 Sep — Payment made
Claim settled. LIC reduced. Any residual reserve released.

IRDAI Reserving Requirements (Ind AS / IRDAI basis alongside IFRS 17)
Indian insurers must maintain reserves per IRDAI (Assets, Liabilities and Solvency Margin of Health Insurers) Regulations 2016 alongside IFRS 17. The IRDAI basis (Ind AS 104 / IRDAI circulars) uses Unearned Premium Reserve (UPR) + Unexpired Risk Reserve (URR) + IBNR. Solvency is computed on the statutory basis, not IFRS 17.

Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) Method
BF blends the Chain Ladder with an a priori expected loss ratio. For new / thin cohorts, BF is more stable. Formula: IBNR_BF = Expected Ultimate × % Unreported. If expected ultimate for AY 2024-25 is ₹70L and % unreported = (1 − 1/1.393) = 28.2%, then IBNR_BF = ₹70L × 28.2% = ₹19.7L.
IFRS 17 requirement: The Appointed Actuary certifies the IBNR. Under IFRS 17.B36, the estimate must be unbiased, probability-weighted, and include a Risk Adjustment for non-financial risk. The RA on LIC is typically 5–10% of the IBNR for health.
Reserve Adequacy Test (IRDAI regulation)
IRDAI mandates that the Unexpired Risk Reserve (URR) must be the higher of 50% of net premium or the expected claims under outstanding policies. If the UPR is insufficient, a top-up known as the Additional Unexpired Risk Reserve (AURR) is maintained. This is the statutory equivalent of IFRS 17's Loss Component.

Coverage Units
For medical reimbursement: 1 coverage unit = 1 policy month of coverage. CSM released per month = CSM ÷ remaining months. If the policy lapses mid-year, coverage units cease and remaining CSM is released. If the policy is cancelled, any unearned CSM is derecognised and the refund of premium is processed.

OCI — Disaggregation Election
If the insurer elects OCI disaggregation: the difference between current-year discount rates and locked-in rates on the LIC is routed through OCI (not P&L). This reduces P&L volatility from interest rate changes. For short-duration health policies, this difference is typically small.
Risk Adjustment Release
The RA is released as claims are settled or the uncertainty is resolved. The RA release is shown separately in Insurance Service Expenses (negative expense = income). If actual claims were better than expected, RA releases faster — a favourable experience variance.

IRDAI Solvency Framework
Indian statutory capital & solvency requirements
IRDAI mandates a minimum Solvency Ratio of 1.5x (150%) at all times. The Solvency Ratio = Available Solvency Margin (ASM) ÷ Required Solvency Margin (RSM). This is computed on the IRDAI statutory basis — not IFRS 17. Solvency is reported quarterly.

Reinsurance Accounting
Reinsurance contracts held — IFRS 17.60–70A
Assume the insurer cedes 20% of risk to a reinsurer under a proportional quota share treaty. Ceded premium = ₹3,000. Reinsurance commission (RI commission) received = ₹450 (15% of ceded premium).
Under IFRS 17, reinsurance contracts held are measured separately from the underlying contracts — they are assets (not netted against liabilities). The RI asset follows the same building blocks (FCF of RI, RA of RI, CSM of RI — here called the Loss Recovery Component or Net Cost).

IRDAI Reinsurance Rules
IRDAI mandates that health insurers must first offer reinsurance to GIC Re (Indian market). For medical reimbursement products, IRDAI's reinsurance regulations require: (a) mandatory cession to GIC Re; (b) programme filed annually; (c) reinsurance with Indian reinsurers preferred before offshore placements. Offshore RI requires IRDAI approval above certain thresholds.

Appointed Actuary — Dual Certification
The Appointed Actuary (AA) must certify: (a) IRDAI basis — adequacy of UPR, URR, IBNR, and solvency; (b) IFRS 17 basis — FCF estimates, RA methodology, coverage unit definition, CSM adequacy, onerous contract identification. The AA's report forms part of the Board submission and is reviewed by IRDAI.

Day 1 — Inception
Contract boundary set. Groups formed. FCF estimated. CSM = ₹2,070. LRC = ₹15,000. Solvency RSM charge updated.
Monthly — Coverage period
Insurance Revenue recognised (₹1,250/month). CSM unwound (₹190/month). Acq. cost amortised. IBNR updated by actuary each quarter.
Aug — Claim event

Quarter-end
Solvency ratio computed (IRDAI basis). IBNR refreshed. RA reassessed. FCF re-estimated. Onerous contract test run.
31 March — Policy expiry
LRC = 0. CSM = 0. Any residual IBNR (LIC) retained. RI settlements finalised. Annual statutory & IFRS 17 accounts filed. AA certifies.
Renewal — New contract
New contract. New group (if different cohort year). Fresh FCF estimate, possibly with experience adjustments. Pricing reviewed by AA for adequacy.
Upcoming change: IRDAI is working on aligning its regulatory framework with IFRS 17 over the medium term. Insurers should expect convergence in solvency computation and liability measurement, potentially transitioning to an economic-value-based solvency regime (similar to Solvency II) in the coming years.
