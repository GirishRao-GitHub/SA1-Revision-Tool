PMI — Product Overview
SP1 Chapter 4 · Core Reading fundamentals
💡
Exam Lens: PMI questions frequently test your ability to apply core product features to novel scenarios (new markets, new regulations, new product variants). Always link features to their rationale, not just list them.
🏥 Definition & Nature
  • Indemnity-based — reimburses actual cost of private medical treatment (contrast with benefit-basis fixed sums)
  • Covers acute (curable) conditions only — NOT chronic (incurable) conditions which are State's responsibility
  • Short-term, annually renewable — insurer can change premium and terms at each renewal; no re-underwriting at renewal
  • Individual premium does NOT normally depend on individual claims experience (though NCD schemes exist)
  • Insurer pays treatment provider directly (cashless settlement in India; reimbursement in UK)
  • Policy may be cancelled but entire product lines are rarely withdrawn; individual policies rarely cancelled mid-term
✅ Typical Inclusions
  • Acute condition treatment (inpatient and day-care)
  • In-patient tests and surgery
  • Hospital accommodation and nursing
  • Specialist consultations (OP)
  • Day-care procedures (no overnight stay)
  • Domiciliary care (treatment at home)
  • Overseas cover (for travel/expatriate)
  • AYUSH treatments (India: Ayurveda, Yoga, Unani, Siddha, Homeopathy — mandated at par since IRDAI 2024)
❌ Typical Exclusions
  • Pre-existing conditions — at inception (FMU) or moratorium period applies
  • GP services (primary care)
  • Chronic conditions — ongoing, incurable, State responsibility
  • A&E treatment — State provides; prior auth impractical in emergency
  • Cosmetic surgery (no medical necessity)
  • Self-inflicted injuries
  • Experimental drugs / treatments
  • War risks, hazardous pursuits
  • Pregnancy / maternity (often excluded; may be covered under health cash plans)
🎯 Why Customers Buy PMI
  • Access speedy diagnosis and treatment (avoid NHS/State waiting lists)
  • Choice of specialist and hospital
  • Better hospital environment and accommodation
  • Convenience — appointment times to suit
  • Access to treatments/drugs unavailable on State system
  • Peace of mind / financial security
  • Employer benefit (group PMI)

⚠️
Exam Trap — Why A&E is excluded: (1) State provides A&E free; (2) Private hospitals lack emergency infrastructure; (3) Pre-auth impractical in emergency; (4) Patient may be incapacitated — can't phone insurer; (5) Patient less concerned about comfort/choice in emergencies.
🇮🇳
India (IRDAI 2024) — Key Regulatory Points: Max PED waiting period 36 months continuous cover; Mental illness at par with physical; 100% cashless target; 1-hr pre-auth TAT; Claim denial only with CRC approval; No claim repudiated without citing specific policy terms; ABHA / electronic health records improving UW.

PMI — Product Design
Design features, variations, India-specific context
Key Product Design Features
📐 Benefit Structure
  • Indemnity basis — reimburses actual cost (life insurers in India: benefit basis only; GI/SAHI: both)
  • Sum Insured (SI) — max reimbursement per year; must be adequate for serious illness
  • SI Reinstatement — restores SI for new illnesses (not pre-existing); annual or per-claim; critical design choice affecting benefit adequacy
  • Coverage scope: IP / OP / Day-care / Domiciliary / AYUSH — each must be explicitly defined
  • OP cover: high frequency, low severity — major operational readiness needed before OP launch
💳 Premium Structure
  • Annual reviewable — rates/terms can change each year; essential given medical inflation
  • No guaranteed premium unless multi-year product (unusual)
  • Age-rated — premium typically increases with age at renewal
  • Age at entry basis — some products keep premium level at entry age (cross-subsidy within product)
  • Community rating (Ireland model) — all policyholders same premium; requires risk equalisation mechanism
🔄 No Claim Discount (NCD)
  • Two forms: (1) cumulative bonus — increased SI without premium increase; (2) renewal premium discount
  • Policyholder chooses form at renewal
  • Self-selection by healthy lives — these are better risks → pricing must allow for selection benefit
  • NCD scales tend to be smaller in PMI than other short-term products (claim frequency is high)
  • Risk: encourages policyholders to forgo legitimate claims; ethical concern
📊 Rating Factors
  • Age (strong driver — morbidity rises sharply with age)
  • Gender (where legislatively permitted; EU gender-neutral ruling 2012)
  • Smoker status (affects morbidity significantly)
  • Geographical location / hospital band (cost of care varies by region)
  • Occupation (risk of some occupational diseases; access to care)
  • UW method (moratorium vs FMU — different selection effects)
  • Sum insured / plan tier
  • NCD status

PMI — Pricing
Risk premium, burning cost, medical inflation, rating
Pricing Methodology
PMI Pricing Formula
Risk Premium = Σk [ ik × ACk ]
Where ik = incidence rate for benefit/procedure class k  |  ACk = average claim cost for class k  |  Split by: age, gender, location, hospital band, smoker status, occupation
📊 Burning Cost Approach
  • Burning cost = frequency × severity
  • Frequency: incidence rate per 1,000 covered lives per year, by age/gender/plan
  • Severity: average claim cost per claim, by diagnosis, procedure, network
  • Split IP from OP — very different profiles
  • Dental/optical/maternity priced separately (distinct frequency/severity)
  • Adjust for excesses, co-payments, sub-limits — both incidence and average cost
📈 Medical Inflation
  • Key risk for PMI — historically ~12-15% p.a. in India (varies by treatment)
  • Super-inflation trend: new treatments, advanced diagnostics, high-cost drugs
  • Failure to trend forward leads to rapid deterioration in loss ratio
  • Separate from general CPI/RPI — often far exceeds it
  • Must project claims forward to mid-point of policy year (at minimum)
  • Trend assumptions require regular review; reinsurer help often needed
🏗 Premium Build-up
  • Risk premium (expected claims cost) as base
  • + Expense loading (initial + renewal; spread by expected policy years)
  • + Profit margin (required return on capital)
  • + Contingency / catastrophe loading
  • + Reinsurance cost (net of recoveries)
  • = Office premium (gross premium)
  • Profit test: verify required return on capital at projected volumes
⚡ Adjustments Needed
  • UW loading for substandard lives (disease loading by condition)
  • Gender-neutral weighting: weight gender-specific rates by expected male:female mix (anti-selection risk if one gender is cheaper)
  • NCD adjustment: model take-up, selection benefit, behavioural response
  • Age-banding or individual age rating
  • Lapse/persistency adjustments (expense recovery assumptions)
  • Selective lapsing adjustment (healthy lives more likely to lapse when repriced)
Data Sources for a New Insurer
IAI Sep 2018 Q2 — Pricing steps & challenges for new insurer ›
Data Sources
  • IRDAI industry data (claim frequencies by age/gender/plan); GIC pooled data
  • Reinsurer data on Indian morbidity experience and international benchmarks adjusted for India
  • International data (WHO, industry studies) — adjusted for Indian conditions
  • Data from distribution partners (if any — e.g. bancassurance, TPA)
  • Public health statistics (NSSO, NFHS, hospital discharge data)
Key Challenges
  • No own-experience data → credibility concerns; must rely heavily on industry/reinsurer data
  • Industry data may not be granular enough for specific product design / target market
  • Cannot quantify anti-selection without historical experience of the UW approach chosen
  • Medical inflation trend assumption uncertain — highly sensitive to output
  • Expense and volume uncertainty — fixed costs may not be covered in early years
  • Selection effect in early years (good UW experience) wears off over time — need durational adjustment
  • If entering new territory: lifestyle/morbidity differences; demographic idiosyncracies; behavioral differences; currency risk
Repricing Risks & When to Reprice ›
  • Annual repricing is essential for PMI given medical inflation and short-term nature
  • Risk of selective lapsing if competitor pricing lags — healthy lives leave; sick lives stay (anti-selection spiral)
  • If emerging risk (e.g. climate/flood, new disease) — consider: will claims deteriorate immediately or gradually? PMI's annual renewability reduces urgency vs long-term products
  • Weigh profitability risk of not repricing vs competitive/lapse risk of repricing ahead of market
  • Assess solvency position — can insurer absorb short-term losses while waiting for market to catch up?
  • Competitor response analysis critical — first-mover repricing disadvantage vs profitability protection

Underwriting & Claims
UW approaches, claims management, capitation, cost control
Underwriting Approaches
ApproachHow It WorksProsCons
Full Medical Underwriting (FMU) Detailed medical questionnaire; explicit exclusions applied to pre-existing conditions Clearest policy position; fewer post-claim disputes; reduces adverse selection; pricing more accurate High admin cost; delays acceptance; discourages applications; may deter older/sicker lives (adverse selection into other products)
Moratorium No upfront questions; blanket exclusion for any conditions present in X years pre-inception; exclusion lifted after Y clean years Simple; fast acceptance; lower admin cost; attractive to applicants Claim disputes at point of claim (was condition pre-existing?); non-contestable after 60 months (except fraud) under IRDAI; higher uncertainty in claims
CPME (Continued Personal Medical Exclusion) Exclusions carry over from previous insurer to new insurer on switching Facilitates portability without loss of protection; prevents anti-selection on switch Requires data transfer between insurers; complexity; trust in previous insurer's records
Community Rating (no UW) No medical questions; all accepted Maximum inclusivity; simple sales; mass market appeal Maximum adverse selection; requires waiting periods and risk equalisation to be viable
Claims Management — Key Features
🏥 Cashless Settlement (India)
  • IRDAI 2024: 100% cashless target for all network hospitals
  • Pre-authorisation mandatory; 1-hour TAT for routine; 3-hour for emergency
  • No claim denied without CRC (Claim Review Committee) approval
  • Claim denial must cite specific policy terms
  • TPA (Third Party Administrator) often manages cashless interface
📋 Claims Characteristics
  • High complaint volumes are characteristic of PMI (complex policy terms, disputed exclusions)
  • OP claims: high-frequency, low-value; different reporting lag; fraud/waste higher
  • IP claims: lower frequency; easier to verify (hospitalisation evidence clear)
  • Chronic condition claims — boundary disputes with insurer; may require PMC review
  • Large claims (cancer, organ transplants, medical evacuation) — catastrophic tail risk
Capitation vs Fee-for-Service vs Bundled
📌
Tested in IAI July 2022 Q2vi and IAI Sep 2017 Q1. Core question: who bears utilisation risk?
ModelMechanismInsurer RiskProvider RiskBest For
Capitation Fixed per-member-per-year to provider; provider bears all utilisation risk Low (cost certainty) High (bears utilisation risk) OP/dental where costs predictable; mass enrolment
Fee-for-Service Insurer pays agreed tariff per procedure/consultation High (bears utilisation risk) Low IP; specialist procedures; where volumes uncertain
Bundled Payment Single payment for entire episode of care (e.g. hip replacement all-in) Medium Medium Defined surgical episodes; controls complications costs
Capitation — Merits & Challenges (IAI July 2022 Q2vi) ›
Merits
  • Provider receives fixed PMPY — cost certainty for insurer
  • OP is prone to waste/abuse/fraud — capitation emphasises efficiency of care (provider profits decrease with higher utilisation)
  • Particularly useful for new risks where insurer lacks own OP data
  • Predictable income for provider; enables long-term planning
  • Reduces admin (no per-claim billing)
Challenges
  • Hard to negotiate blanket capitation — provider needs to estimate expected utilisation
  • Policyholders may be unhappy if provider compromises on quality of care
  • Provider may convert OP to IP to manage their cost — adverse for insurer
  • Members may need to attend different providers for OP vs IP — inconvenient
  • Regulations may prohibit arrangement
  • Counterparty risk if provider financially distressed
  • Provider reputation damage reflects on insurer
  • Could cost more than fee-for-service if provider over-estimates capitation fee
Experience Analysis — Poor PMI Performance
Causes of PMI Claims Deterioration ›
  • Business mix shifts (older or higher-risk lives entering pool; new distribution channels with different risk profile)
  • Selective lapsing — healthy lives lapse on repricing, leaving sicker pool behind
  • Higher-than-expected medical inflation (super-inflation effect)
  • New / more expensive treatment protocols (new drugs, diagnostics)
  • Large individual claims (cancer, medical evacuation, multi-organ failure)
  • Lax claims management / increased fraud
  • Provider price increases (tariff renegotiation, market power)
  • Reduction in State healthcare quality → demand shifts to private
  • Moral hazard (overclaiming; induced demand by providers under FFS)
  • Random fluctuation (especially for small portfolios)
  • Introduction of new coverage (mental illness, OP) — insufficient pricing data
Actions to Reduce A/E Deterioration ›
  • Add pricing margins for anti-selection; review and tighten UW standards
  • Tighten claims management (GP referral requirements; pre-auth; second opinion)
  • Review benefit utilisation; introduce or increase deductibles/co-payments
  • Diversify business mix toward healthier segments (digital/younger lives)
  • Incentivise healthy behaviours (wellness programs, NCD)
  • Negotiate fixed-fee schedules with providers; preferred network expansion
  • Introduce managed care for chronic/high-risk patients
  • Review policy wording to limit unexpected claims; clarify chronic/acute boundary
  • Consider reinsurance (XL for catastrophic IP; QS for OP volume)
  • Run-off poor segments; withdraw products if unviable

Health Cash Plans — Deep Dive
💊 Product Features
  • Defined cash benefits for: dental, optical, physiotherapy, maternity, hospitalisation, recuperation, hearing aids, consultations
  • Very low premiums (~£2/week or equivalent)
  • Benefits may be % of medical bill (e.g. 50%) — a form of co-insurance
  • Benefits bought in 'units' — higher unit = proportionally higher premium + benefit
  • Typically community-rated (no individual UW)
  • Waiting period often applies (6 months); no waiting on accidents
  • Moratorium may exclude pre-existing conditions
📈 Why Community Rating?
  • Full UW cost would be disproportionate to small premiums
  • UW delays are unacceptable — simplicity and speed are key selling points
  • Waiting period substitutes for UW to reduce anti-selection
  • Community rating creates cross-subsidy: older/sicker pay less than risk, subsidised by younger/healthier
  • Better risks may switch to individually-rated insurers → adverse selection spiral risk
📊 Profitability Drivers
  • Long-term policyholder loyalty — initial expenses spread over many years
  • Cost-efficient claims administration (high volume/low value — automation critical)
  • Large volumes in force to spread fixed costs
  • 'Inertia' factor — people don't claim for small entitlements; adjust pricing if inertia not already in data
  • High renewal rates (persistency) essential for expense recovery

Personal Accident Insurance
📋 Key Features
  • Short-term, annually renewable
  • NOT indemnity — fixed benefit for specified injuries (loss of limb, sight, hearing, PTD, accidental death); financial value of physical loss cannot be objectively quantified
  • Benefits can cover policyholder's family; rider benefits for children common at lower level
  • Exposure measure: person-year (or member/employee-year)
⚖️ Rating & Claims
  • Rating factors: primarily occupation (demolition vs clerical); age/gender generally NOT used
  • Industry serves as proxy for occupation in group schemes
  • Claims usually reported and settled quickly — incidence clear; benefits predetermined
  • Exception: PTD (Permanent Total Disability) may take months/years to confirm
  • Claims can be very large; frequency tends to be stable

PMI — Risks & Risk Management
Key risks, new market entry, government schemes, market dynamics
Core PMI Risks
📉 Insurance/Morbidity Risk
  • Higher-than-expected claim frequency or severity
  • Medical inflation exceeds pricing trend assumption
  • New treatments / expensive drugs emerge after pricing
  • Adverse business mix (age, channel, UW method)
  • Deterioration of State healthcare quality → demand shift
  • Catastrophic individual claims (cancer, organ transplants)
  • Epidemic / pandemic risks (sudden surge in claims)
🔄 Anti-Selection Risk
  • Lapse of healthy lives on repricing → sick lives stay → deteriorating pool
  • Community-rated products particularly vulnerable
  • New distribution channel (digital) attracts different risk profile
  • Gender-neutral pricing → adverse selection if one gender is cheaper
  • Portability (India) — waiting periods don't re-apply to transferred policies
  • Moratorium UW → dispute at claim time if condition was pre-existing
⚙️ Operational / Model Risk
  • High complaint volumes — regulatory scrutiny
  • OP launch operational readiness — high-volume low-value systems
  • Fraud: provider overbilling; policyholder fabrication; especially OP
  • Provider (capitation) counterparty risk
  • TPA dependency / data quality
  • Data privacy risk (wearables, ABHA, DPDP 2023)
  • Cyber risk (digital health records)
🏛 Regulatory / Political Risk
  • Government introduces free public healthcare → mass lapse risk
  • Government subsidy to healthcare providers → demand for low-tier PMI falls
  • IRDAI regulatory changes (waiting period caps, AYUSH parity, mental illness)
  • Gender-neutral pricing mandate → need to rebalance rates
  • PMJAY expansion → boundary with private PMI blurs
  • Community rating mandate (as in Ireland) → structural market change
New Market Entry Risks (IAI May 2023)
Entering a New Overseas PMI Market — Key Risks & Data Sources ›
Risks
  • Lifestyle and morbidity prevalence differences from home market
  • Idiosyncratic demographics (e.g. aging population driving higher healthcare demand)
  • Medical innovation/technology costs — different from home market
  • Policyholder and healthcare provider behaviour differences
  • Claims control and fraud effectiveness — unfamiliar territory
  • Macro-economic factors (currency fluctuation, local medical inflation)
  • Regulatory uncertainty — different approval process, solvency requirements
  • Distribution channel risk — no established relationships
  • Expense uncertainty — no economies of scale initially
Possible Data Sources
  • Local regulator published statistics (equivalent of IRDAI data)
  • Global reinsurer databases (experience from similar markets)
  • WHO, World Bank health statistics
  • Local TPA / hospital discharge data
  • Academic/public health research on disease prevalence
  • Partnership with local insurer (joint venture) to access portfolio data
  • Own market experience adjusted by relativity indices
Government Subsidy Schemes — Impact on PMI Market
Government IP-only Healthcare Subsidy (IFoA Apr 2023) ›
Why IP-only subsidy?
  • Target highest-cost needs (hospitalisation)
  • Limit fiscal exposure (OP volume is vast)
  • Contract with manageable number of hospital providers
  • Standardise fees for set procedures
  • Rely on readily available hospital cost data
  • OP subsidies harder to administer; more prone to fraud; broader cost base
Impact on Insurers
  • Reduces demand for low-tier PMI / health cash plans (subsidised segment)
  • Selective lapsing among subsidised policyholders
  • Adverse volume and expense impact for insurers targeting low-income market
  • Potential opportunity: demand for top-up / supplementary cover grows
  • Means-tested: only affects certain income bands → partial market disruption
Digital Distribution Risks
IFA Distribution / Online Sales — Specific Risks (IFoA Sep 2022) ›
  • Online distribution attracts younger, healthier lives — different risk profile vs traditional broker/agent
  • AI-based UW: regulatory concerns; IRDAI compliance required; transparency/fairness challenges
  • IFA/financial advisor churning risk: advisors may move policyholders to competing insurers to earn fresh commission → selective lapses, reduced persistency
  • Commission clawback terms are the primary mitigant for churning
  • IFA-distributed business competes on both price AND policy terms — not price alone
  • New entrant could disrupt by offering more comprehensive terms at similar premium
  • If government introduces free public healthcare → market for short-term products may contract sharply; mitigation = product diversification and monitoring of policy developments

📌
IFoA questions are scenario-based. The key skill is adapting PMI principles to the fictional country/context. Always look for the specific factors in the scenario before applying generic knowledge.
IFoA Apr 2021 — Q1 43 marks
PMI market — current healthcare assessment, community rating, rating restrictions, reserving for not-for-profit insurer
▾
Country A: PMI-based healthcare (limited State provision); aging population; rising treatment costs; new government wants to improve affordability and coverage via 3 options: (1) mandatory low-cost Health Cash Plan alternative; (2) restrict rating factors (no gender, limited age banding, no UW); (3) government not-for-profit PMI company with minimal UW.
Option 1 — Mandatory HCP alternative: Advantages / Disadvantages
  • Citizens (Adv): Lower cost entry-level cover; wider access; defined certainty on benefit amounts; appeals to lower income; encourages healthcare engagement
  • Citizens (Dis): Fixed cash benefit may not cover actual costs (inflation risk on policyholder); less comprehensive than PMI; may create two-tier system
  • Insurers (Adv): New market segment; volume growth; simpler product to administer
  • Insurers (Dis): Mandatory requirement may force cross-subsidy; may cannibalise PMI sales; adverse selection if mainly sick lives choose HCP; regulatory burden of compliance
Option 2 — Rating Factor Restrictions (no gender; age cap 3:1; no medical UW)
  • Citizens (impact): Younger lives pay more (subsidise older); women may pay more or less depending on current rating; policyholders with PECs benefit (no UW); overall affordability improves for older/sicker lives; affordability worsens for young/healthy
  • Insurer concerns: Inability to rate by gender removes a key differentiator; restricted age banding creates cross-subsidy risk; without UW, anti-selection is acute; need open enrolment mandate + risk equalisation to survive
  • Government actions: Mandate open enrolment (prevent cherry-picking); implement risk equalisation (redistribute premiums from low-risk to high-risk insurers); lifetime community rating to incentivise early joining
Option 3 — Government not-for-profit PMI Reserving Considerations
  • No historical data → must use industry data, reinsurer data, international benchmarks
  • Minimal UW → higher adverse selection → heavier morbidity assumptions needed
  • Not-for-profit → no profit margin; but still need adequate reserves + capital
  • Government backing → implicit guarantee? Does this reduce capital requirement?
  • Aging population → future trend assumption critical; IBNR pattern uncertain without data
  • Medical inflation assumption → highly uncertain; must stress test
  • Lapse assumption → minimal UW products attract older/sicker who tend to persist → lower lapse rate than market average
IFoA Sep 2022 — Q1 34 marks
PMI vs MME — preference, distribution risk profiles, risk management; Company A (online) vs Company B (IFA/high-wealth)
▾
Country X: young/healthy population; private-only healthcare; main products PMI and MME (fixed sum on major surgery). Company A = online/social media (no advice). Company B = high-wealth credit card + IFA distribution.
Why prefer MME over PMI?
  • Lower premiums (MME is cheaper — limited scope of cover)
  • For young/healthy: PMI may cover more than needed; prefer self-funding minor costs
  • Simplicity — fixed benefit, simpler underwriting, faster acceptance
  • Fewer exclusions — medical UW for MME often less stringent
  • Belief that they won't need comprehensive cover
Company A (Online) Risk Profile vs Company B (IFA/HNW)
  • Company A: Younger ages; less financially sophisticated; less wealthy; MME and simplified PMI with moratorium UW; higher lapse risk; lower sum assured; ACI (Accelerated CI) for mortgage-linked younger lives
  • Company B: Older/wealthier; PMI more common (can afford); higher sums assured; IFA advice → better matched products; LTC possible given HNW profile; better health (wealthier occupations); IFA churning risk
Key Risk Differences

Community Rating — Definition & Advantages/Disadvantages
  • Definition: All policyholders (or a defined subset) charged same premium regardless of individual risk characteristics (age, gender, health status)
  • Adv: Equitable — sicker/older pay affordable premiums; encourages broad participation; avoids underwriting cost/complexity; simpler product design; reduces stigma of illness
  • Dis: Cross-subsidy → young/healthy subsidise old/sick → young may choose not to buy → adverse selection spiral; must mandate open enrolment or provide incentives to join young; requires risk equalisation to prevent insurer cherry-picking
Risk Equalisation
  • Mechanism: transfers funds from insurers with lighter-than-average risk pools to those with heavier-than-average pools
  • Prevents adverse selection at insurer level (cherry-picking healthier lives)
  • Allows community rating to function sustainably
