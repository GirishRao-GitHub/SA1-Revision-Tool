High-cost Top-up
1. Define benefit design & data scope
  • Specify IP vs OP vs day-care boundary explicitly
  • Decide deductibles, co-pays, sub-limits, waiting periods, and exclusions — each directly affects the risk premium
  • For OP add-on: dental, optical, and maternity costs require separate estimation streams
  • Reinstatement or floater features (e.g. sum insured auto-reinstatement) must be scoped before pricing begins
Sep18-Q2i, Jul22-Q2iii, Mar17-Q1
  • Define the CI condition list and whether severity tiers apply (full pay / partial pay by stage)
  • Specify survival period and linked claims period before next claim can begin
  • Clarify whether first and subsequent occurrences of the same condition are both covered — this drives incidence rate estimation
  • Identify interlinkages between conditions (e.g. cardiac event preceding stroke) to avoid double-counting
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Decide the range of deferred periods offered (e.g. 1, 3, 6, 12 months) — each is a separate risk class
  • Define incapacity: own-occupation vs any-occupation; partial vs total disability benefit
  • Set salary definition (basic vs total compensation) and the pre-disability averaging period
  • Establish benefit limitation (% of pre-disability income minus all other income sources) and linked claims period
  • Determine whether cover is compulsory for all employees or voluntary
Mar21-Q1ii, Dec22-Q2ii
  • Decide whether cover is comprehensive (all treatments) or a defined procedure list
  • Specify per-visit limits, annual sub-limits, co-insurance, waiting period, NCB, and pre-existing dental exclusions
  • Define cashless-only or reimbursement-also approach — this affects provider network design and pricing
  • Confirm whether regulatory constraints apply (IRDAI health rules for OPD products)
Mar18-Q1i
  • Select procedures suitable for packaging: high-frequency, low complication rate, low mortality, small cost variability, short length of stay, sufficient supply of providers
  • Use ICD/CPT codes to define scope precisely; liaise with clinical coding specialists
  • Identify common procedure combinations that should be grouped into one package
  • Confirm scope of package (pre-op diagnostics, surgery, anaesthesia, medicines, post-op follow-up, complications) — package must be all-inclusive with zero additional billing
May25-Q1b/c
  • Define the AML (Annual Maximum Limit) threshold above which the top-up triggers
  • Establish whether membership is compulsory or voluntary — directly affects anti-selection assumption
  • Specify any waiting periods, exclusions, or time-bound restrictions built into the design — these reduce claim frequency
  • Decide whether this is a group add-on or individual policy and what product combinations it can sit alongside
Sep21-Q3i/ii
2. Data collection & sources
  • Industry aggregate data from IRDAI / GIC pooled statistics
  • Reinsurer morbidity data on Indian experience — often the most granular source available
  • International benchmarks adjusted for Indian demographics, healthcare costs, and product design
  • For overseas PMI: national statistics from the target country; data from local hospitals; competitor pricing via market surveys; multinational reinsurer data
  • Own portfolio data where available; external data must be adjusted for differences in target market, underwriting, and network
Sep18-Q2i/ii, May23-Q2iib
  • Industry morbidity data on CI incidence rates by condition and age/gender
  • Reinsurer CI pricing tables as a benchmark and cross-check
  • Medical literature on disease progression and survival rates, particularly for severity-based triggers
  • Own portfolio data (if selling standard CI already) to understand base rates before launching severity-based variant
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Industry absence and disability incidence data (limited in India; reinsurer data is primary source)
  • Employer's own absence records for the specific group being quoted
  • Sector-specific data: mining, construction, and transport carry different injury and absence profiles than white-collar roles
  • In a new market with no prior IP experience: use fewer rating factors; rely more heavily on reinsurer guidance and apply conservative margins
Mar21-Q1ii, Dec22-Q2v
  • Industry claims data or reinsurer dental loss data adjusted for local conditions
  • Market research to estimate demand, take-up rates, and consumer willingness to pay
  • Study comparable product launches by other insurers in India or internationally
  • Check what proportion of dental cost is already covered by government schemes or employers, to avoid over-pricing
Mar18-Q1i
  • Historical hospital claims data (ICD/CPT coded) from the hospital group — primary and most relevant source
  • Adjust data for COVID-19 pandemic years (2020–21) where utilisation was atypical
  • Data to cover: salary of staff, medical supplies, consumables, medicine, specialist fees, pathology, radiology, room and nursing charges
  • No comparable competitor pricing data likely to be available — novel product type
May25-Q1c
  • Own portfolio has no claims above the current AML by definition — own data is therefore insufficient for pricing
  • Industry experience of high-cost medical events (major surgeries, critical illness, accidents) provides frequency insight
  • Reinsurer data and expertise on extreme cost events is primary pricing input
  • Pandemic or epidemic context data may be needed if the top-up is triggered by a correlated event
Sep21-Q3ii
3. Data analysis & experience investigation
  • Derive claim incidence rates by age, gender, and benefit type; calculate average claim amount by diagnosis and procedure
  • Analyse medical cost inflation trends — Indian medical inflation has run significantly above CPI
  • For experience analysis: subdivide morbidity by underwriting method, duration from entry, NCD level, group size, and industry (for group business)
  • Focus on whether new procedures, drugs, or equipment are changing treatment patterns and costs — this affects trend assumptions materially
  • Investigate expense, lapse, investment return, and new business mix separately
Sep16-Q1i, Sep18-Q2i, Jul22-Q2iii
  • Estimate incidence rates separately for each severity tier and for first vs subsequent occurrences of the same condition
  • Identify statistical interlinkages between conditions — e.g. a cardiac event increasing the likelihood of a subsequent stroke claim
  • Claim frequency will be higher than for standard CI but average severity lower — model both dimensions
  • Analyse post-illness mortality rates — these affect both claim duration and future premium income
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Investigate incidence rates and termination (recovery / death / retirement) rates by age, gender, occupation, location, duration from entry, underwriting status
  • Analyse cause of claim (accident vs sickness; type of injury/illness) — critical for experience rating
  • Actual-to-expected analysis: compare experience against pricing assumptions by issue year (underwriting effect) and calendar year (general trend effect)
  • Separate expense, lapse, and investment experience investigations — each affects premium review differently
Dec22-Q2vii
  • Analyse claim frequency and average cost by procedure type, age band, gender, and network vs non-network
  • Identify high-claiming segments (e.g. rural male age 20–40 showed favourable experience in exam case — warranting targeted repricing rather than blanket increases)
  • Monitor take-up of cashless vs reimbursement — affects cost and administration
Mar18-Q1iii
  • Severity analysis only — utilisation rate does not need to be estimated since the procedure is the trigger
  • Apply clinical entry criteria (age limits, length of stay, level of care) as rules to filter data
  • Run scenario: with and without outlier exclusions — use the difference to quantify complication and outlier risk
  • Identify whether common procedure combinations can be bundled into single packages at a lower aggregate cost
May25-Q1c
  • Stochastic modelling and fitting of a distribution to the tail of claims above the AML
  • Frequency estimation: uses experience from high-cost CI, surgical, and accident claims as a proxy for events that would breach AML
  • Severity estimation: distribution of excess cost above AML threshold, allowing for catastrophic scenarios including pandemic backdrop
  • Reinsurer benchmark rates to be compared against internal modelled cost
Sep21-Q3ii
4. Assumption setting
  • Claim inception rates by age, gender, and plan design
  • Average claim amounts by diagnosis and procedure type; medical inflation rate
  • Persistency / lapse rates; initial and renewal expense rates; claims handling expenses
  • Investment return (less material for short-tail PMI but still required)
  • All assumptions to be documented and signed off; sensitivity analysis required on key assumptions
  • Selection effect assumption: underwriting creates lighter early-year experience that wears off over time — must not be over-estimated
Sep18-Q2i/ii
  • CI incidence rates by condition and severity tier (full-pay and partial-pay thresholds)
  • Post-illness survival period and mortality rates after claim event
  • Expected volume of new business (market research required — demand uncertain for novel severity-based product)
  • Risk discount rate: should be set higher than for standard CI to reflect uncertainty in best-estimate incidence assumptions
  • Profit margin: higher than standard CI given uncertainty, novel design, and higher capital requirement
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Morbidity incidence rates and termination rates (recovery and death) as primary assumptions
  • Rating factors in a mature market: age, gender, occupation, location, income level, disability definition, deferred period, replacement ratio
  • In a new market: fewer rating factors — data limitations, need for simplicity, and regulatory constraints (e.g. no gender discrimination) all reduce the number of viable factors
  • Salary definition and pre-disability averaging period as policy-level inputs to each claim calculation
Dec22-Q2v, Mar21-Q1ii
  • Claim incidence rate by procedure type and policyholder segment
  • Average cost per procedure (network and non-network); medical cost inflation
  • System development and mobile app maintenance costs to be estimated separately
  • Volume assumption is critical — expense per policy is high relative to premium; break-even volume must be assessed
Mar18-Q1i
  • Healthcare cost per procedure = hospital cost + medication + specialist fee + anaesthetist fee + pathology fee + radiology fee
  • Medical inflation assumption: price must cover all future costs; no cross-subsidisation mechanism is available
  • Complication rate assumption: low complication rate is a selection criterion for procedures, but residual complication cost must be priced in
  • Provider negotiation outcome (fee rates agreed with network) is a direct input to the cost assumption
May25-Q1c
  • Frequency of high-cost events exceeding the AML threshold
  • Severity: expected cost of claims above AML, including distribution of excess amounts
  • Anti-selection loading: if voluntary, adverse selection is likely; if compulsory, this loading can be significantly reduced
  • Reinsurer pricing rates benchmarked and adjusted for local cost-of-care differentials
Sep21-Q3ii
5. Risk premium / burning cost calculation
  • Burning cost = frequency × severity, estimated for each combination of key risk factors (age, gender, network, occupation, location)
  • Align burning cost to product design: apply deductible, co-pay, and sub-limit adjustments to reduce the gross cost to the insured cost
  • Underwriting loading: apply additional loading for sub-standard lives above the free cover limit; use claims and underwriting experience to calibrate loadings by risk category
  • For OP cover: estimate dental, optical, and maternity components separately before aggregating into total OP burning cost
  • Iterative process: profit testing and market testing repeated until a viable balance is reached
Sep18-Q2i, Jul22-Q2iii
  • Estimate expected claims cost per policy year by multiplying incidence rate (per severity tier) by sum assured payable at each tier
  • Claim assessment cost is higher than for standard CI due to multiple possible claim events per policy — must be factored explicitly into cost calculation
  • Allow for the impact of the survival period in reducing spurious claims — this reduces the effective claim incidence rate used in pricing
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Risk premium = incidence rate × expected benefit per claim (benefit as % of salary × expected claim duration)
  • Apply separate calculations for each deferred period offering — shorter deferred periods produce materially higher premiums
  • Adjust for benefit limitation (offset of state benefits and other income sources) to arrive at the net benefit payable by the insurer
  • Partial disability benefit loading added if included in the product design
Mar21-Q1ii, Dec22
  • Risk premium = expected claims frequency × average claims cost per procedure
  • Apply annual sub-limit and per-visit limit adjustments to cap the gross cost
  • Differentiate between network (lower cost, cashless) and non-network (higher cost, co-pay) cost streams
Mar18-Q1i
  • Price per procedure = sum of all component costs (hospital + medication + specialist + anaesthetist + pathology + radiology) under the agreed protocol
  • No frequency or incidence rate estimation required — the patient is purchasing cover for a specific known upcoming procedure
  • Include an explicit margin for complication scenarios derived from the outlier scenario test in data analysis
  • Set up negotiated fee schedules with network providers in advance — these rates are direct pricing inputs, not assumptions
May25-Q1c
  • Expected cost of top-up = E[max(claim − AML, 0)] = frequency of breaching AML × expected excess above AML
  • Stochastic simulation used to model the tail distribution — simple deterministic burning cost is insufficient given the extreme tail nature of the risk
  • If the top-up is triggered during a pandemic backdrop, correlations between individual claims become material and aggregate risk increases significantly
Sep21-Q3ii
6. Loadings (expenses, profit, contingency, capital)
  • Add to risk premium: initial expenses (acquisition, system, launch), renewal expenses, claims handling expenses
  • Profit margin: target return on capital (assessed against WACC or risk discount rate)
  • Contingency loading for adverse deviation, especially where experience data is limited
  • Reinsurance cost (net of expected recoveries) added as a separate loading
  • Initial product development and system setup costs must be spread over the expected in-force volume — creates pricing pressure in early years when volumes are low
Sep18-Q2i, Jul22-Q2iii
  • Higher profit margin than standard CI to reflect pricing uncertainty and novel design
  • Capital requirement is higher than standard CI — capital cost loading is therefore greater; availability of sufficient capital must be confirmed before product launch
  • Advertising and training costs likely to be higher for a more complex product concept — these launch costs need to be recouped through loading
  • Risk discount rate used to discount future profit cashflows may need to be uplifted to reflect uncertainty in best-estimate incidence rates
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Expense loading: initial commission, renewal commission, claims management and rehabilitation costs
  • Cost of capital loading for long-tail liability: IP reserves are long-duration and capital is locked up for extended periods
  • For guaranteed premium structure: higher contingency loading required since there is no future repricing option — this is a structural difference from reviewable structures
Dec22-Q2ii, Mar21
  • Commission or remuneration for distribution channel (if not direct-to-consumer)
  • System development, testing, and mobile app maintenance costs are significant for a digital product and must be loaded into the premium
  • Claims handling costs: dental has high frequency and wide provider network — per-claim administrative cost likely higher than IP or CI
Mar18-Q1i
  • Administration charges (hospital admin, digital booking platform, payment processing)
  • Margin for adverse deviation: explicit reserve for complication outcomes not fully captured in base price
  • Profit margin for the hospital group or the actuarial/insurance entity
  • GST: added to the final price as a regulatory requirement
May25-Q1c
  • Profit margin, expense allowance, and cost of capital are all factored through the reinsurance-benchmarked cost
  • Capital loading is significant: this is a high-severity, low-frequency risk with potentially correlated claims across the portfolio
  • If reinsurance is purchased, the reinsurance premium is a direct additional loading on top of the retention cost
Sep21-Q3ii
7. Profit testing & sensitivity / scenario analysis
  • Profit test to verify product meets required return on capital at projected new business volumes; if volumes are lower, per-policy fixed costs rise and profitability deteriorates
  • Sensitivity analysis on all key assumptions — identify which assumption drives the greatest profit sensitivity
  • Maximum probable loss (MPL) scenarios: assess whether capital is sufficient for a worst-case year
  • Test competitiveness: ensure rates are attractive vs market while remaining profitable
  • Iterative loop: profit test → rate adjustment → re-test until required profit and competitiveness are simultaneously achieved
Sep18-Q2i, Nov20-Q2ii, Jul22-Q2iii
  • Profit test must cover three criteria simultaneously: profitability, competitiveness, and resilience to adverse scenarios
  • Run sensitivity on incidence rate assumptions — these are the highest-uncertainty inputs for a severity-based product
  • Test capital adequacy under stressed incidence rates given that capital requirements are higher than standard CI
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Project cashflows from issue to end of policy term; discount at risk discount rate to produce embedded value or profit measure
  • Sensitivity test on morbidity incidence, termination rates, and lapse assumptions — all three are highly material for long-tail IP
  • At premium review: project cashflows from the review date using updated assumptions; test whether rate increase is needed to maintain original profit target
  • Assess risk of selective withdrawal if rates are increased: higher-risk lives may retain cover while healthy lives lapse — adverse selection feedback loop
Dec22-Q2vii
  • Project volume and profitability under multiple scenarios (optimistic, base, pessimistic) to assess range of outcomes
  • Assess solvency impact: despite lower capital requirement than IP/CI, ensure solvency ratios remain acceptable under a high-claim scenario
Mar18-Q1i
  • Outlier scenario test: compare results including vs excluding extreme-cost data points to quantify complication and outlier risk
  • Stress-test medical inflation assumptions — if inflation rises above the priced-in level, the fixed package price becomes loss-making
  • No standard profit test benchmarks available since this is a novel product — internal hurdle rate must be set by the hospital or entity pricing the product
May25-Q1c
  • Stochastic scenarios used rather than simple deterministic stress tests, given the tail nature of the risk
  • Benchmark internal modelled cost against reinsurer quoted rates — material divergence signals a modelling error or a data problem
  • Assess correlation impact: if multiple policyholders claim simultaneously (pandemic scenario), aggregate loss could be a multiple of expected
Sep21-Q3ii
8. Reinsurance considerations
  • Quota share, surplus, or stop-loss reinsurance — type depends on the specific risk management objective
  • For a new insurer with limited capital: quota share reduces net exposure and capital strain; reinsurer also provides pricing data and technical support
  • For overseas PMI: reinsurance is practically necessary to access the reinsurer's data on the overseas market — purchasing reinsurance may be the price of data access
  • Net reinsurance cost (premium less expected recoveries) is a direct input to the gross premium loading
Mar18-Q1i, May23-Q2iib, Sep18-Q2i
  • Reinsurance is essential for large sum insured given the lump-sum nature of the benefit
  • For multi-pay / severity-based design: reinsurer must understand and price the multiple-claim structure — not all reinsurers may be willing to offer terms
  • Reinsurance terms may be difficult to obtain at launch given limited market experience with severity-based CI designs
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • Reinsurance is essential given the long-tail, high-severity nature of IP liabilities
  • XL (excess of loss) or quota share depending on capital position and risk appetite
  • For guaranteed premium structures: some reinsurers may decline to offer guaranteed terms, creating a constraint on product design choice
Dec22-Q2ii
  • Reinsurance can reduce capital requirements and smooth claims volatility, particularly for large employer groups
  • Stop-loss reinsurance is most relevant: dental claims are frequent and low-severity individually but could accumulate on a single large group scheme
Mar18-Q1i
  • Risk is primarily borne by the hospital / provider rather than an insurer — the package price mechanism transfers overrun risk to the provider
  • If an insurer is involved in the structure, reinsurance is less critical but stop-loss protection for systemic cost increases (e.g. medical inflation spike) is worth considering
May25-Q1c
  • Reinsurance is highly likely given the high-severity, low-frequency nature — insurer's own capital cannot efficiently absorb large losses above AML
  • Reinsurer's expertise is a pricing input: their view of the cost of catastrophic health events must be obtained and benchmarked against the internal model
  • Reinsurance pricing feeds directly into the premium loading: if the reinsurer's rate is above the internal model, this signals inadequate internal pricing
Sep21-Q3ii
9. Premium reviewability approach
  • Standard market practice is yearly reviewable premiums for individual PMI
  • Reviewable rates allow the insurer to correct mispricing — particularly important when launching a new product variant (e.g. wellness-linked) with limited experience
  • Premium increase at renewal must be carefully managed: no increase was justified at first renewal in the exam case until a full claims analysis was completed and early-year distortions were understood
Mar17-Q1iv_a, Jun19-Q1iv
  • Reviewable premiums are recommended for a new severity-based product given the absence of credible own experience at launch
  • Reviewable structure allows lower initial premium (no cost-of-guarantee loading), which improves marketability
  • The basis for review must be clearly set out in product literature; customers must understand under what circumstances premiums may change
  • Comply with IRDAI regulations governing premium reviewability; reputational risk of a large increase must be managed by setting assumptions conservatively at outset
Apr16-Q2iii_c
  • Guaranteed vs reviewable: guaranteed rates increase sales certainty and avoid reputational risk of increases, but require heavier reserves and capital and remove the ability to reprice
  • In a new market with no local IP experience (Country B scenario): launch with premiums guaranteed for an initial period (e.g. 5 years), then reviewable — balances marketability with the insurer's need to reprice as data accumulates
  • Lapse risk: selective lapsation on reviewable structures (high-risk lives retain cover; healthy lives leave) must be modelled in the profit test
Dec22-Q2ii/vi
  • Decide at outset whether premium is fixed for a defined number of years or annually reviewable
  • For a digital product with aggressive growth targets: annually reviewable rates give more flexibility to correct experience early
Mar18-Q1i
  • No ongoing premium or reviewable rate concept — the product is a one-off upfront payment per procedure
  • Package prices may be updated periodically (e.g. annually) by the hospital as medical costs change, but this is a price list update rather than a premium review
May25-Q1c
  • Given very limited data at launch, reviewable premium structure is appropriate to allow correction as experience develops
  • If embedded within a group scheme, the AML enhancer premium can be reviewed at the scheme anniversary alongside the base cover renewal
Sep21-Q3ii
10. Regulatory filing & competitive benchmarking
  • Ensure premiums comply with IRDAI regulations, including any minimum/maximum premium requirements
  • File product and rates under Use & File or File & Use procedure as applicable under IRDAI (Insurance Products) Regulations 2024
  • Benchmark rates against existing market products to assess competitiveness; avoid being richer than the market (adverse selection risk) or more expensive (volume risk)
  • For overseas PMI: competitor pricing may be obtained via market surveys or by requesting quotes
Sep18-Q2i, May23-Q2iib
  • IRDAI standardised CI definitions apply to common conditions — product must comply with or clearly deviate from the standard
  • Full product filing required; severity-based variant may require additional regulatory engagement given novelty
  • Benchmark CI condition list and severity thresholds against competitors; risk of being the first mover is that competitors may replicate quickly
Apr16-Q2iii_a
  • IRDAI oversight applies; product must be filed; benefit-not-to-exceed-salary rule (over-insurance prevention) must be satisfied
  • Regulatory may prohibit certain rating factors (e.g. gender discrimination rule tested in Nov23) — this must be checked before finalising the rating structure
  • Limited IP market in India means competitive benchmarking is against self-funded employer sick pay schemes rather than other insurers
Dec22-Q2v, Nov23-Q2i
  • IRDAI health product regulations apply to dental/OPD add-ons; digital channel regulatory compliance for mobile app–based issuance
  • Benchmark per-procedure limits vs market; digital-first mobile app is a competitive differentiator if network and technology are well-executed
Mar18-Q1i
  • Regulatory classification is uncertain — the product sits at the boundary between healthcare and insurance; IRDAI jurisdiction question must be resolved before launch
  • If classified as insurance: full product filing with IRDAI required; GST treatment must be confirmed
  • No direct competitor pricing available — benchmark instead against out-of-pocket surgical costs in the market to set affordability-anchored pricing
May25-Q1c
  • Standard IRDAI product filing applies if the add-on is insurance-classified
  • No direct market comparator for pricing benchmark — internal benchmarking against reinsurer rates is the primary check
Sep21-Q3ii
11. Post-launch monitoring & premium review process
  • Monitor actual vs expected (A vs E) for all key assumptions: morbidity incidence, average claim cost, medical inflation, lapses, expenses, and new business mix
  • Morbidity analysis: subdivide by age, gender, duration, NCD level, underwriting method, network, and provider — focus on whether new procedures or drugs are shifting costs
  • At renewal: complete a full detailed claims analysis before deciding on any premium rate change; early-year experience distortions (underwriting selection effect) must be understood before acting
  • For overseas PMI: corrective actions available include re-pricing, product redesign (tighter benefit limits, referral protocols), managed care renegotiation, underwriting tightening, and reinsurance adjustment
Sep16-Q1i, Jun19-Q1i/iv, Nov24-Q2e
  • Monitor incidence rates by condition and severity tier; compare against pricing assumptions
  • Track claim assessment cost trend — multiple-claim products have higher adjudication cost than standard CI and this should be monitored separately
  • If experience deteriorates: reviewable premiums allow repricing; communicate basis of review clearly to policyholders and regulators
Apr16-Q2iii_c
  • Full experience investigation: collect morbidity data since last pricing/review date; analyse incidence and termination rates for all homogeneous risk groups; calculate A vs E
  • Expense investigation: actual vs expected split by initial, renewal, and claims expenses, adjusted for inflation
  • Lapse investigation: analyse by duration, given long-term nature of IP; selective lapsation on a rate increase must be modelled
  • Revise best-estimate assumptions; re-run profit test from the review date; decide on rate change and communicate to policyholders with sufficient notice
  • Consider impact of rate changes on future volume and overhead coverage before finalising the review decision
Dec22-Q2vii, Nov19-Q1iii
  • Experience analysis showed unfavourable overall claims — triggered a portfolio-wide premium increase
  • Sub-segment analysis identified that rural males aged 20–40 had favourable experience — marketing director proposed targeted premium reduction for this segment rather than blanket increase
  • Key monitoring metric: overall loss ratio vs target, and segment-level loss ratios to guide differential repricing decisions
Mar18-Q1iii
  • Monitor actual procedure costs against the package price for each procedure code; track complication rate vs the priced-in assumption
  • Review provider fee schedules periodically and renegotiate if cost trends diverge from the medical inflation assumption used in pricing
  • Update the package price list annually or as required; communicate updated prices clearly to patients before they commit
May25-Q1c
  • Monitor high-cost claim frequency above AML; compare actual tail-event rate vs modelled frequency
  • Update stochastic model parameters as own data accumulates; reinsurer benchmark to be refreshed at each renewal
  • Given very limited own data at outset, experience credibility builds slowly — external data sources remain important for several years post-launch
Sep21-Q3ii
