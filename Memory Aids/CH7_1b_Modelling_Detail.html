① Uses of Models
4 Core Uses
  • Pricing — profit testing, premium rate setting
  • Reserving — statutory & management accounting reserves
  • Options & Guarantees — costing and reserving
  • Model Office — NB projections, EV, solvency, takeovers
Extended Uses (exam list)
  • Product design
  • Profitability of new & existing business
  • Analysis of surplus
  • Embedded value calculations
  • Determining capital requirements
  • Return on capital assessment
  • Reinsurance needs
  • Investment strategy (ALM)
  • Expense budgeting
  • M&A / merger valuation
4 Model Types by Scope
  • Single policy profit test — one policy, date of issue → uses: pricing, product design
  • New business model — future NB only → uses: capital for NB, RoC, goodwill / appraisal value
  • Existing business model — current inforce → uses: EV, solvency testing, surplus analysis
  • Full model office — NB + existing → uses: management decisions, NB projections, EV, solvency, takeovers

All models project the balance sheet and revenue account (P&L). The four scope types differ on which policies are included and the focus of their output.

② Objectives & Basic Features of a Health Insurance Model
Prime Objective & Model Points
Prime objective: enable actuary to advise company so it is run in a sound financial way — day-to-day management + checks & controls.
  • Model point = data record representing a policy or group of policies, containing key characteristics
  • Number of model points influenced by: computer power · variability/complexity of contracts · age of company · stochastic vs det. · purpose & importance · time available · sensitivity to choice of model points
Operational Requirements (Generic)
  • Adequately documented
  • Workings easy to appreciate & communicate; results clearly displayed; sensible joint behaviour
  • Outputs capable of independent verification for reasonableness
  • Not overly complex (results must remain interpretable; cost & run time must be reasonable)
  • Capable of development & refinement
  • Range of implementation methods available for testing & parameterisation
H&C-Specific Model Features
  • All cashflows arising from contracts — including options & guarantees (policyholder-choice benefits, e.g. option to extend/increase cover without further evidence)
  • Supervisory reserve & solvency cashflows — establishment (notional outgo) and release (notional income); solvency capital above reserves also modelled
  • State-based cashflow projection — cashflows differ by state (healthy, sick-not-claiming, claiming, different claim levels for LTCI); each state projected separately
  • Dynamic asset–liability links — model must reflect management responses (e.g. switch to matching assets if solvency worsens); vital for stochastic models where parameters change automatically each simulation
  • Stochastic capability where appropriate — e.g. to assess cost of guaranteed inflation-linked benefits; must incorporate correlated financial & demographic variables (sickness ↔ economic conditions)
③ Deterministic vs Stochastic Models
③ Deterministic vs Stochastic — Head-to-Head
Deterministic "One question, one answer"
  • Parameters: each has a fixed value
  • Output: point estimate
  • Sensitivity testing: re-run with varied parameters to explore range
  • Advantage: simple, fast, transparent, explainable
  • Limitation: cannot capture distribution of outcomes; dynamic responses must be manually tweaked for each scenario
Stochastic "Distribution-in, distribution-out"
  • Parameters: some vary with own probability distributions
  • Output: probability distribution of outcomes (run many times using random samples)
  • Advantage: captures volatility, year-on-year uncertainty, worst-case tail scenarios, guarantee costs
  • Disadvantages: complex & time-consuming · results hard to communicate · dynamic links are essential (automated) · risk of spurious accuracy
When Stochastic is Invaluable
  • Assessing impact of options & guarantees (e.g. guaranteed inflation-linked LTCI benefits)
  • Variable has a reasonably stable, predictable distribution (e.g. investment returns in stable economy)
  • Indicating effect of year-on-year volatility (random fluctuations) on risk
  • Identifying high-risk scenarios by tracing sequence of events leading to worst simulated outcomes
H&C more important than pure life — claim incidence, benefit amount (PMI medical inflation, IP policy inflation, LTCI care costs) far harder to predict
Stochastic Calibration
  • Risk-neutral (market-consistent): used for valuation (esp. options & guarantees). Parameters chosen so average PV of simulated cashflows = known market price of financial instruments. Focus: replicate observable prices. Both bonds & equities priced at 100 → both average 100 in simulations.
  • Real-world: used for projections (e.g. capital adequacy at given confidence level). Parameters reflect realistic long-term expectations & observable real-world probabilities. In simulations, equities expected to outperform bonds on average (as in reality).
④ Sensitivity Analysis
Two Sources of Sensitivity
  • Model-point error: sensitivity to which model points are chosen. Normally enough points to eliminate significant error; but when using reduced model points (e.g. to run many sensitivity tests quickly), this error must be assessed.
  • Parameter mis-estimation: actual future experience ≠ assumed. Sensitivity analysis varies each parameter in turn (one-at-a-time). Scenario testing changes multiple correlated parameters simultaneously (e.g. lower yield + lower expense inflation).
Key Parameters to Test
  • Claim inception / recovery / transfer-between-states rates
  • Mortality rates
  • Lapse & non-renewal rates
  • Average claim size (PMI)
  • Future expense levels & expense inflation
  • Benefit & claims inflation
  • Investment yield
  • Volumes & mix of new business
  • Taxation basis
Uses of Sensitivity Analysis
  • Pricing: determine margins to incorporate in parameter values; if too sensitive → redesign product (e.g. revise commission if sensitive to lapses; revise reinsurance if sensitive to morbidity)
  • Return on capital / profitability: quantify effect of departures from chosen parameters when presenting results to management
  • Model validation: sanity check that dependencies work correctly (e.g. ↑mortality should ↑profits on most H&C products unless death benefit exists)
  • Capital & reserves: determine variance of profit / RoC analytically or via Monte Carlo; supports additional risk margins, global reserves, capital requirements
Stochastic modelling may be obligatory for supervisory reserve calculations
⑤ Formula / Equation of Value Approach
Mechanism
PV(income) = PV(outgo): solve for the unknown premium.
Numerator = PV(claims + expenses) − PV(investment income)
Denominator = PV(1 unit of premium)
Premium = Numerator ÷ Denominator
  • Claims: discounted future claims outgo (inc. claims handling expenses; inflation; separate benefit escalation options)
  • Premiums: one unit projected & discounted (inc. escalation, persistency, premium waiver; commission directly related to premium size included here)
  • Expenses: expenses not linked to premiums/claims projected & discounted with appropriate inflation
  • Investment income: allows for timing between income and annual outgo (rest invested to year-end); discount factors adjusted for incidence during year (e.g. claims paid at ¾ of year → multiply by v0.75)
  • Tax: adjusted via discount rate or explicit allowance
  • Profit: explicit margin subtracted from denominator (e.g. 50% of annual premium target → subtract 0.5 from denominator)
Drawbacks (8 key limitations)
  • ❌ Proper timing of events — only average timing assumed
  • ❌ Accumulation of reserves — reserves completely ignored
  • ❌ Capital needs — shareholder required return cannot be incorporated
  • ❌ Net negative cashflows — implicitly assumes capital can be borrowed at discount rate
  • ❌ Separate inspection of cashflows — cannot track premiums/claims/expenses separately by year
  • ❌ Varying assumptions over time — one fixed discount rate used throughout
  • ❌ Changes in future experience / sensitivity measurement
  • ❌ Complex product structures — e.g. unit-linked not suited
Best suited to: short-term contracts (e.g. 1-year PMI) — most drawbacks only matter for long-term contracts. Result ≈ same as 1-year cashflow projection anyway.
⑥ Cashflow Approach
Pricing Process
  • Step 1: select model points reflecting expected NB (or existing business profile for existing product; marketing input for new product)
  • Step 2: project all cashflows per model point, including reserves and solvency capital, using base parameter values
  • Step 3: discount net cashflows at risk discount rate (= required return + loading for statistical risk of cashflows) → gives NPV of future profits
  • Step 4: set premium/charges to satisfy profit criterion — either per model point OR in aggregate (accepting cross-subsidy; but then profitability exposed to mix changes)
  • Step 5: sensitivity test; iterate; extend to full premium table by interpolation
Used for long-term contracts · Not necessary for 1-year PMI (formula approach sufficient)
Cashflow Items — Conventional Business
  • Premiums (income)
  • Expenses: initial / renewal / claim handling (outgo)
  • Commission (outgo)
  • Claims (outgo)
  • Contribution to reserves (outgo / notional)
  • Contribution to capital requirements (outgo / notional)
  • Interest on cashflows and reserves (income)
  • Tax (outgo)
Additional Items — Unit-Linked
  • Cost of unit allocations · Bid-offer spread · Claims cost in excess of unit fund · Risk charges (morbidity/mortality) · Unit/policy charges · Fund management charges · Unit fund growth · Unit fund payouts on claim/surrender
Assessing Profitability (Existing Business)
  • Cashflow model on existing inforce → NPV of future profits = embedded value component
  • EV = net assets + PVIF (present value of in-force profits)
  • Uses: compare profitability by product / distribution channel · identify need to reprice · embedded value for statutory / management reporting
Return on Capital
  • Net cashflows from model points grossed up for expected NB → assess total capital requirement (regulatory or economic basis)
  • Add one-off development costs (not yet amortised)
  • Compare expected profits vs total capital → expected RoC
  • Uses a new business model
Determining Capital Requirements
  • Use full model office — NB cashflows scaled up and incorporated into whole-company model
  • Observe amount and timing of capital needed to write the product
  • May lead to product redesign if capital requirement is a constraint
  • Must include supervisory solvency capital requirements
⑦ Multi-State Modelling
Overview & States
Used when: policyholders exist in multiple states, each with different cashflows. Most relevant to IP and LTCI (claim does not terminate the policy).
  • IP states (simplified practical model):
    Healthy ↔ Sick → Dead / Lapsed
    Deferred period handled by explicit adjustment (not a separate state)
  • Transition intensity μijx = annual rate of moving from state i to state j at exact age x (i ≠ j)
  • Applied each period (monthly) to project numbers in each sub-cohort for the next period
NotationMeaning
ₜpxHHProb healthy life aged x is healthy in t years (may have been sick in between)
₅p30HDProb healthy life aged 30 is dead in 5 years
₅p30DS= 0 (dead cannot become sick)
ₜpxDD= 1 (dead stays dead)
₃p50SSProb sick life aged 50 stays continuously sick until age 53
Pricing with Multi-State Models
  • Project sub-cohort counts: starting from initial population (e.g. 10,000 healthy), apply transition probabilities each year → Hx+1, Sx+1 etc.
  • EPV of claims = average sick lives in period × benefit amount × discount factor
  • EPV of premiums = healthy lives each year × 1 unit premium × discount factor
  • Equate EPV(claims) = EPV(premiums) → solve for P
In practice: subdivide further by gender, sickness duration, claim history → many sub-cohorts but risk spurious accuracy. Data availability is the binding constraint. Combine cohorts, use fewer but more reliable transition intensities.
  • Data availability drives choice: use multi-state for individual IP (sufficient data); use inception/annuity approach for group IP (insufficient data for full multi-state)
  • Same framework for reserving: identical structure but assumptions adjusted for reserving purpose (e.g. statutory vs sale-of-company assumptions)
  • Sensitivity testing easier with multi-state than inception/annuity approach — value of sensitivity testing should never be underestimated
⑧ Model Office — Main Components (Appendix)
Policy Liability Model
  • Inputs: model points (inforce + NB) + parameter values (claim rates, withdrawal rates etc.)
  • Function 1: project forward inforce portfolio year-by-year — function of: existing inforce · new business · state transitions · mortality/lapse · policy expiry
  • Inforce projection needed to calculate supervisory reserves and solvency capital at each year-end
  • Function 2: project total policy cashflows each year → enables calculation of projected assets and annual profit
Expense Model
  • Full model office: all company expenses — modelling fixed overheads is straightforward here
  • Profit test / NB model: only policy-related expenses; fixed overheads are difficult to allocate and are often excluded or estimated
  • Expenses covered in full in Chapter 29
Asset Model
  • Simple (profit test): expected total annual investment return only
  • Complex (full model office for investment strategy): stochastic, by asset class, separating income and gains, reflecting asset mix over time
  • Must be consistent with liability model — especially inflation (benefit levels + investment returns must respond consistently)
Consolidation & Verification
  • Add outputs of liability + expense + asset models
  • Manual adjustments for: un-modelled business · tax · contingency reserves
  • Verification (3 checks):
    (1) Compare with supervisory valuation
    (2) Compare with previous model results
    (3) Compare with highly simplified model
Diagram flow: Data package + Sales projection → Model points → Policy liability model + Asset model + Expense model → Consolidation + Manual adjustments → Draft results → Verification → Presentable results
The 4 model types differ in which policies are included and the focus of output (e.g. profit test: annual profit emergence; full office: projected supervisory balance sheet & profit distributions)
