Principles · Process · Purpose Comparison
8 Core Principles When Setting Any Assumption

P2

P4

4-Step Assumption-Setting Process
  • Step 1 — Historical: Investigate past experience; best estimates of parameters from data
  • Step 2 — Future Conditions: Assess commercial & economic environment that will apply
  • Step 3 — Best Estimates: Derive best estimates for the future period
  • Step 4 — Margins: Adjust for prudence; size depends on purpose & degree of risk of each parameter
Consistency — Correlated Pairs to Watch
  • Morbidity ↔ Lapse rates (selective lapsing → worsening pool)
  • Morbidity ↔ Claims expenses (claim volume drives cost)
  • Morbidity ↔ Investment return (economic climate links both)
  • NB volumes ↔ Fixed expenses (per-policy loading)
  • NB volumes ↔ Lapse/non-renewal rates
  • Lapse rates ↔ Investment return (economic climate)
  • Investment return ↔ Expense inflation (long-run real return must be consistent)
  • Also: consistency across related products (e.g. stand-alone CI vs accelerated CI as starting point)

CH14 — Assumptions (2): Demographic Assumptions
Morbidity · Recovery · Mortality · Lapses
General Derivation Process (All Demographic Assumptions)
STEP 1 — COLLECT
Choose appropriate database · ~3–4 years of data (volume vs heterogeneity tension) · Own + reinsurer + market + population sources · Standard tables where own data insufficient
STEP 2 — GROUP
Divide into homogeneous risk cells · Balance credibility (large groups) vs heterogeneity (meaningful cells) · Trial & error combinations to find best groupings for pricing
STEP 3 — CALCULATE
Adjust standard table rates: i_x = a · i_x(S) + b · Exposed-to-risk techniques where no standard table · CI: project each illness separately then combine (watch double-counting)
STEP 4 — ADJUST
Allow for trends (improving & worsening) · Changes in target market, distribution channel, UW basis · Adjust for future social/economic conditions · Note: future lives almost always different from historical data cohort
Claim Incidence — by Product Type
  • IP: Inception rates (healthy→sick) + recovery rates (sick→healthy) — both crucial
  • CI: Per illness type: cancer · heart attack · stroke · CABS · TPD · others proportionately · Project each separately (different trends) then combine (adjust for double-counting)
  • LTCI: Inception rates (able→claiming) + transitions to higher benefit levels · Recovery generally ignored (unlikely given age & claim conditions)
  • PMI: By benefit category separately · Annual renewal → projections shorter horizon · Claim amounts also required (see Ch15)
  • Sophisticated IP/PMI: may load for higher re-incidence among past claimants
Social/Economic influences: State welfare generosity · unemployment / economic conditions (↑IP incidence; PMI utilisation) · inflation (↑PMI renewal lapse risk; selective lapsing)
Rating Factors (Data Sub-Divisions)
  • Age
  • Gender (may be prohibited in EU; monitored internally)
  • Smoker status
  • Occupation / occupational class
  • Benefit size / sum insured
  • Product class (stand-alone vs rider; length of deferred period)
  • Individual vs group
  • Distribution channel / source
  • Region / territory
  • Duration since policy entry
  • Duration since start of current benefit (LTCI transitions)
  • Underwriting status (standard / special terms)
  • PMI: age (band), maybe not gender; smoker not yet widespread as rating factor
Recovery Rates — Income Protection Focus
  • Crucial for IP (benefit is income annuity until recovery / death / expiry)
  • LTCI: generally ignored in pricing (recovery unlikely given age & conditions)
  • Key recovery drivers:
  • Keenness to return to work — economic availability of work
  • Policy income ratio: total post-disability income must be < pre-disability earnings (insurer controls via policy conditions)
  • Insurer involvement in rehabilitation — significant impact
  • Cause of claim — very influential
  • Medical advances: aid recovery but also extend life in disability → net effect on claim cost is unclear
  • Large benefit amounts → claims continue significantly longer than small amounts
Mortality — Significance by Context
  • Non-claiming policyholders: Standard insured lives table + future improvement adjustment; least research needed
  • IP & LTCI claimants in payment: More effort required — mortality of sick lives far less predictable; has large financial impact (claim termination)
  • CI survivorship period: Probability of dying within e.g. 28 days post-diagnosis → adjust CI incidence rate (illness-specific; post-diagnosis survival differs markedly by illness)
  • Policies without death benefit: Do NOT over-estimate mortality — reduces exposure → under-prices the risk
  • 10% fall in healthy-life mortality → <0.5% increase in CI exposure. But 10% fall in claiming-life mortality → very large increase in IP/LTCI claim cost.

CH15 — Assumptions (3): Financial Assumptions
Benefits · Expenses · Commission · Investment · Taxes
Benefit Amount & Benefit Inflation
Benefit Amount
Long-term (IP/CI/LTCI): Benefits pre-determined; premium expressed as rate per unit of benefit (e.g. £3 per £100 p.a. benefit). Large cases only: adjust for stricter UW + higher socio-economic group (better experience). Per-policy expenses → priced at average policy size.
PMI: Must forecast average claim amount per benefit category. Starting point: own experience (or similar insurer). Adjust for: differences in policy conditions · hospital deal arrangements (predictability + cost control) · future provider capacity & charges · treatment price lists as alternative source.
Benefit Inflation
  • IP: Earnings index (benefit replaces lost income)
  • CI: Purpose-dependent — care costs / debt repayment / earnings / price inflation; sum insured usually fixed in practice
  • LTCI: Index of nursing & residential home care costs; ceiling common to contain insurer exposure
  • PMI: Medical inflation + changes in treatment protocols + hospital charging structure + future age profile of portfolio + provider capacity trends
  • Fixed % avoids unknown index — explicitly modelled like level-premium contract (initial funding)
  • CPI often used but understates medical costs (salary inflation in medical staff; modern technology)
Expenses — Types, Loading & Comparison with Life
9 Expense Types (by Function)
  • Initial acquisition (commission + marketing + sales)
  • Initial medical underwriting
  • Initial administration
  • Renewal administration (PMI: highest — full re-pricing of each policy)
  • Renewal commission / channel reward
  • Investment expenses (PMI: not important — small reserves)
  • Withdrawal / cancellation (PMI: swamped by renewal costs)
  • Claims administration (validity check; ongoing monitoring; rehabilitation)
  • Termination
Direct (class-specific) vs Indirect (overheads). Fixed vs Variable. Per-policy vs per-premium vs per-benefit.
Loading Per-Policy Expenses into Premiums
Problem: premium rate expressed per unit of benefit → per-policy expense loading varies by policy size → large policies subsidise small ones.
Method 1 — Individual calculation: Calc premium for each policy explicitly. Only practical for large cases (small policies: implausibly large % expense loading).
Method 2 — Policy fee: Most common. Separate flat fee covers per-policy costs; premium rate covers proportionate costs. Fee may be reduced for competitiveness → must compensate via higher % loading; risk if actual mix skews small.
Method 3 — Sum insured differential: Different premium rates per benefit band. Requires volume projection per band. Often combined with a policy fee.
Higher than life insurance because: Lower non-medical limits → more UW effort · Product must be sold (not demand-led) · Claims validation more time-intensive · Ongoing claims management (IP, LTCI) · Rehabilitation effort
Expense Inflation
  • Always needed for long-term products; also for reviewable contracts
  • Mix of price inflation (minority) + earnings inflation (majority — staff costs dominate)
  • Analyse company's own recent expense inflation; if absent, use industry
  • Consider: current price & earnings rates · expected future rates · ILG vs nominal govt bond yield differential (market-implied inflation) · company/industry recent actual experience
  • Apply to: renewal admin · claims admin · termination expense loadings
  • Two horizons: inflation during policy term and inflation between now and when new policies are issued
Commission & Clawback
  • Based on rates actually paid (market levels + legislation)
  • Higher than life: extra product explanation + extra UW information collection
  • Initial commission usually higher than renewal commission (reflects sales effort)
  • Weighted average if multiple distribution channels have different commission structures
  • Clawback credit risk: If early lapse AND intermediary defaults → commission not recovered. Assume partial non-recovery in pricing basis.
  • Analysis: early lapses vs policies exposed at each duration subject to clawback; split by product type and channel
  • Example: 10% lapse rate · 50% clawback · 5% non-recovery rate → expected clawback = 10% × 50% × 95% = 4.75% of initial commission
Investment Return — Significance by Product
Pre-funded single premium LTCI
CRITICAL
Very large reserves for very long period. Possible to match with assets → lock in yield at outset. Reinvestment risk from uncertainty of claim timing still remains.
Regular premium pre-funded LTCI
IMPORTANT
Large reserves; cannot match most liabilities until late in term → high reinvestment risk throughout policy.
Regular premium CI / IP
LIMITED
Relatively small reserves; profits insensitive to investment return changes. Limited reinvestment risk.
PMI
NEGLIGIBLE
One-year policies; claims settled within the year or shortly after; reserves tiny. Investment expense also not important.
Asset Mix Considerations
  • Fixed monetary benefits (IP/CI): Fixed interest bonds / gilts to match
  • Real / inflation-linked benefits: Index-linked government bonds
  • Care cost inflation (LTCI): Some equity — mirrors real increases in care costs; long investment period supports equity risk
  • Unit-linked products: managed fund return (mix of equity + bonds) for unit fund; separate return for non-unit reserves
  • Market-consistent (risk-neutral): set return = risk-free rate regardless of actual assets held; investment return volatility/correlation still depends on actual assets
  • Must be consistent with inflation assumption (implied real return must be realistic)
Taxes — Three Approaches
  • Profits tax: Insurance company taxed on contract profits. Multiply future profits by (1 − tax rate). Tax authorities typically restrict prudence in reserves allowed for tax purposes.
  • I–E basis: Tax on investment income less allowable expenses. Model all flows net of tax rate. Staggered expense relief in some territories (spread over 3–7 years).
  • Premium tax: Insurer neutral financially (collects on behalf of state). BUT: increases consumer cost → reduce NB volume + increase lapse/non-renewal rates → must feed into those assumptions (consistency).
  • Best approach: use current rates, adjusted for any known future changes

CH16 — Assumptions (4): Other Assumptions
New Business · Risk Discount Rate · Market Consistency
New Business Volume & Mix
New Business Volume
  • Needed primarily for pricing (for reserving/EV: actual volume known)
  • Purpose 1: Allocation of fixed expenses per policy in pricing basis
  • Purpose 2: Assess capital adequacy to support NB strain at launch
  • Based on: own past experience + market data + competitive position of product
  • Circularity risk: volume depends on price; price depends on expense loading; loading depends on volume — resolve by assuming volume insensitive across a band of prices
New Business Mix — 6 Dimensions
  • Average policy size: Linked to socio-economic profile of target market; inflation and consumer demand trends
  • By product: Allocate indirect expenses accurately to each product line
  • By distribution channel: Commission rates may differ; volumes needed if uniform pricing across channels
  • By territory / region: Risk varies substantially; needed if region not a rating factor
  • By gender: Required for unisex pricing (EU legislation); morbidity significantly different
  • By occupation: Material impact on claim propensity; needed where broad groupings used
  • Anti-selection risk: uniform premiums → higher-risk lives attracted → monitor mix continuously; competitors with finer subdivisions win the lighter risks
Risk Discount Rate
Risk Discount Rate — CAPM + Product-Specific Risk
Component 1 — Shareholders' Required Return (Company-level)
Investors compare returns across companies relative to risk. They require: risk-free rate (e.g. short-term stable-government bonds) + risk premium for investing in an insurance company.
Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):
E(i) = r + β · [E(m) − r]

E(i) = expected return on company's shares
r = risk-free rate
E(m) = expected market return (e.g. equity index)
β = beta = covariance(r_i, r_m) / variance(r_m)

β > 1: more volatile than market · β < 1: less volatile
CAPM captures systematic (market) risk only — diversifiable risk eliminated.
Market is the final judge of required return — actuary makes assumptions but cannot dictate what shareholders demand.
Component 2 — Product-Specific Statistical Risk
E(i) is the company-wide required return. Individual products are priced with a project-specific RDR that adjusts for relative riskiness vs company average:
6 FACTORS INCREASING PRODUCT RISK → ↑ RDR
Lack of historical data
High guarantees
Policyholder options
Overhead costs
Complexity of design
Untested market
4 METHODS TO ASSESS STATISTICAL RISK
  • Analytically — via variances of individual parameter distributions
  • Sensitivity analysis — deterministic range of plausible parameter changes
  • Stochastic simulation — vary parameters by assumed distributions; 1,000+ scenarios → distribution of return on capital
  • Market data comparison
Three key RDR conclusions: (1) RDR must always exceed risk-free rate — rises when market rates rise. (2) Margin above risk-free reflects ALL product risk sources; different products priced at different RDRs reflecting relative risk. (3) Overall return on company capital across all products must meet shareholders' required return (cost of capital). Margins in individual parameters and margins via RDR are alternative but equivalent ways of expressing the same risk.
Market Consistency (Alternative / Validation Approach)
Risk-Neutral Valuation
  • Discount all cashflows at risk-free interest rates (term-dependent)
  • Risk-free curve sourced from swap rates or government bond yield curves
  • Investments assumed to earn risk-free rate regardless of actual assets held
Risk Margin in Parameters
  • Because RDR = risk-free, risk must instead be reflected in parameter margins (morbidity, expenses)
  • Risk margin compensates the "market" for taking on uncertain non-investment liability cashflows
  • Cost of capital approach (also used in Solvency II risk margin — see Ch22)
Use as Reasonableness Check
  • Even where traditional CAPM / RDR approach is primary, market-consistent valuation provides a useful cross-check
  • Ensures traditional approach produces defensible margins
  • Increasingly common in European / Solvency II environments
