CH17 — Pricing (1): Individual Business
IP · CI · LTCI · PMI · A&S · Cash Plans
Income Protection — Two Pricing Approaches
Approach 1: Inception / Disabled Life Annuity
  • What it ignores: recoveries (or treats death = recovery); no subsequent re-sickness modelling
  • Claim inception rate (i_x^d): P(claim commences in year [x, x+1]) — i.e. after surviving deferred period. NOT the same as sickness inception rate.
  • Sickness vs claim inception: equal only when deferred period = zero. Claim inception = sickness inception × P(staying sick through deferred period).
  • Disabled life annuity: EPV at date of claim inception of all future benefit payments, allowing for death, recovery, benefit escalation and interest up to policy expiry.
EPV of claims incepting in year t
EPV = (1)·(2)·(3)·(4)·(5)
(1) Survival prob (eligible to claim) · (2) Claim inception rate · (3) Disabled life annuity · (4) Discount factor · (5) Annual benefit amount
Approach 2: Multi-State Modelling
  • More general: tracks policyholder through all states simultaneously
  • States tracked: Healthy premium payer → Sick (in deferred period) → Claimant → Recovered → Dead
  • Each sub-cohort has own transition probabilities (sickness inception, lapse, mortality, recovery, expiry)
  • Rates can vary by duration within state and number of previous sick spells
  • Practical constraint: data volume may not justify full sophistication — sub-cohorts combined in practice
Inception/annuity is a simplification of multi-state. Both methods are used; multi-state is theoretically superior but more data-intensive.
Critical Illness — Formulae
Stand-Alone CI
  • Overall CI incidence: i_x = i_x^hd + i_x^s + i_x^c + i_x^o
  • Overlaps (same person has two CI conditions): allow for explicitly if using population medical records; automatically handled if from claims data (each claim attributed to one cause)
  • Claim incidence = i_x × P(surviving the survival period)
  • Over-counting overlaps → over-estimates claim rates → uncompetitive pricing (but less dangerous than under-estimating)
Accelerated CI (earlier of death or CI)
Accelerated CI claim rate at age x
i_x = i_x(CI) + (1 − k_x) · q_x
k_x = proportion of deaths in [x, x+1] caused by CI already in i_x(CI). Subtracts CI deaths from q_x to avoid double-counting.
  • Risk of over-counting k_x: understates claim cost → inadequate premiums / reserves (more dangerous than stand-alone)
  • Cost = claim incidence × CI sum insured
LTCI, PMI, A&S and Cash Plans
Long-Term Care Insurance
  • Uses same two frameworks as IP: inception/annuity or multi-state
  • Multiple benefit levels (eg home care / residential / nursing): value incremental benefit at each transition to higher level
  • Multi-state diagram: Healthy → Level 1 → Level 2 → Level 3 → Dead (no reverse transitions assumed in basic model)
  • Transition rates may vary by duration in current state and cause of incapacity
  • Disability annuities: whole-life (payments cease only on death); include benefit inflation allowance
  • Three annuity tables needed: one per benefit level, by age at inception
Private Medical Insurance
PMI Risk Premium (age, gender)
RP = Σ_k (i_k × AC_k)
i_k = incidence rate for benefit/procedure class k · AC_k = average claim cost for class k. Sum over all k.
  • Adjust for: excesses (reduce incidence + adjust avg cost); NCD structures (model long-term effect)
  • Unisex pricing: weight gender rates by expected gender mix → anti-selection risk if mix differs from assumed
  • Multi-state modelling needed if premiums guaranteed for >1 year
  • Rating factors: age, gender (if permitted), location, hospital band, smoker, occupation
Accident & Sickness Insurance
  • Short-term (typically <5 year benefit); income protection of loan repayments
  • Uses inception/annuity approach
  • Average rate per scheme: same premium per £ loan regardless of individual age/gender — pooled rate agreed with lender; brings extra pricing complexity
  • Large schemes → group pricing techniques (Chapter 18)
Health Cash Plans
  • Premium = Σ (inception rate × average benefit) for each benefit category
  • Allow for excesses and coinsurance factors in average benefit calculation
  • Inertia adjustment: reduce expected claims for under-claiming (forgotten cover + low benefit amounts + customer apathy)
  • Flat-rate premiums across broad age bands; if inertia already in own experience data, no further adjustment needed

CH18 — Pricing (2): Group Business
Book Rate · Experience Rating · Credibility · Burning Cost
Special Characteristics of Group Risks
Free Cover
  • Cover up to the free cover limit without full underwriting
  • Basic proposal / health declaration (eg continuously at work for 3 months) only
  • Terms of free cover = product design decision
Broker / Intermediary Control
  • Broker channels information and revenues — data accuracy risk
  • Insurer may receive same (or less) detail as competitors quoting on same scheme
  • Broker may resist "unpopular" requests (detailed membership data, risk assessments) to preserve client relationship
Limited Insured Information
  • Exact membership details often unknown at policy start → deposit and adjustment premiums
  • Deposit estimated; adjusted up/down at year end when details known
  • Becoming less common — full info often supplied at quotation stage now; but retrospective adjustments still needed for staff changes
Newcomers & Leavers — Risk Profile Changes
  • Leavers: fitter lives more likely to change jobs (career progression) → remaining group higher risk on average
  • But: if most leavers are ill-health retirees → remaining workforce lower risk ("healthy worker" effect)
  • Reduction in size: down-sizing → may prompt claims before leaving
  • Increase in size: newcomers usually younger & fitter — generally favourable; but check claims history if via merger/takeover
Flexible Benefits
  • Employees choose their own cover mix (insurance + pension + holiday etc.)
  • Anti-selection risk: sicker employees choose higher healthcare cover; healthier employees opt out
  • Mitigation: benefit limits + statements of good health + restricted switching windows
Book Rate Calculation
Why Group Rates Are Lower Than Individual Rates
  • Less anti-selection: compulsory membership means insurer doesn't face self-selection risk; lives can't opt in only when sick
  • Lower expenses: less underwriting effort needed; economies of scale in admin
  • Book rates are consistent with individual rates — same risk-factor relationships; but absolute level may differ
  • Individual-level rating factors: age, gender, smoker, occupation (same as individual business)
  • Group-level rating factors: industry type, location, size of group, employer attitude to employee health
  • Larger scheme → experience rating carries more weight; individual risk factors carry less
Experience Rating & Credibility
Experience Rating Formula
Credibility-weighted risk premium
RP = Z · A + (1 − Z) · E
Z = credibility factor (0 to 1) · A = group's own experience rate · E = book rate (insurer's standard rate)
  • Z = 0: pure book rate (new or tiny scheme)
  • Z = 1: 100% experience-rated (large, data-rich scheme)
  • In practice, PMI given full credibility with <100 man-years (commercial pressure); IP requires more data for full credibility
  • Individual business: priced on community rate (book rate only — no experience element)
Burning Cost
  • Definition: accumulation of past claims, adjusted for: changes in numbers insured · changes in cover · medical inflation
  • Must include: IBNR (incurred but not reported) + RBNS (reported but not settled)
  • For IP: must capitalise (expected present value of future payments from past inceptions), because claims are paid over years not as lump sums
  • Most applicable to group PMI and CI; used as adequacy check — compare burning cost vs risk premiums actually charged
  • Purpose: earliest possible signal on whether current premium rates are adequate

Profit Criteria · Marketability · Competition · SCR · Reinsurance · Regulator · Reviews
Profit Criteria — Comparison
Criterion
Net Present Value (NPV)
Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
Discounted Payback Period
Definition
Profit signature discounted at RDR → single £ value
Rate at which discounted cashflows = 0
Duration at which cumulative discounted profit = 0 (capital recovered)
Strengths
Theoretically optimal (economic theory); expressible as % of PV premiums or % initial commission; always exists; single unambiguous value
Easily understood concept; intuitive to non-actuaries
Useful for capital-constrained firms; clear capital management tool
Weaknesses
Says nothing about competition directly; subject to law of diminishing returns
Not unique if profit signature has >1 sign change; may not exist if profits from outset; cannot be related to market share or premiums; IRR can conflict with NPV
Ignores all cashflows after payback date → not a complete profitability measure; rarely used alone
Best Use
Primary criterion; compare across products using % of initial commission or % PV premiums
Secondary or communication tool; do not use when NPV and IRR conflict
Secondary to NPV; use for product design comparison; favoured where capital is scarce
NPV positive (or zero) ⟹ product's rate of return meets (or exceeds) RDR target. NPV as % of PV premiums: ties profitability to market share — insurer can grow proportionally. Recommended approach: NPV primary, DPP secondary.
Marketability & Competitiveness
Marketability Levers
  • If premiums uncompetitive: revisit product design (remove costly features; add differentiating features)
  • Change distribution channel (may allow revised assumptions or support higher premium)
  • Reduce profit requirement
  • Abandon product if no viable combination exists
  • Always re-examine: acquisition costs, marginal admin costs, fixed overhead contribution
Anti-Selection from Competition
  • Competitor with finer rating factors: cherry-picks lighter risks by charging them less
  • Broader pricer's assumptions are undermined: actual mix skews to heavier risks → average premiums too low
  • Market pressure pushes all insurers toward similar rating factor sets to avoid worst anti-selection
  • Monitor: (1) level of overall premiums vs market · (2) number and accuracy of rating factors
Loss Leaders & Cross-Subsidy
  • Price below cost to gain market share ("loss leader") — margin subsidised from other profitable lines
  • Risk: adverse mix — loss leader over-sold; subsidising product under-sold → aggregate profitability fails
  • Mitigation: reviewable premiums on loss leader; hold adequate free assets as buffer
  • Over time: each product brought back to own contribution level; profits then immune from mix fluctuations
  • Fixed expenses → volume matters → economies of scale → incentive for mergers & acquisitions
Reserves & SCR · Reinsurance · Regulator
Reserving & Solvency Capital in Pricing
  • Locked-in capital has an opportunity cost: (IRR − asset yield) × SCR amount per annum
  • Reserving assumptions in pricing model = assumptions insurer realistically expects to use in future (prudent if regulation demands prudence; best estimate if regulation demands best estimate)
  • SCR must also be allowed for in the model — can be substantial under best-estimate reserving
  • Reinsurance reduces SCR: pure reinsurers hold less capital → total industry capital reduced → lower premiums possible
  • Some well-capitalised insurers omit SCR cost to compete on price — risk: misjudging relative profitability of product designs
Reinsurance Impact on Pricing
  • 7 roles of reinsurance: technical expertise · risk sharing · smoothing profits · NB strain financing · tax arbitrage · capital arbitrage · enabling larger risks
  • If reinsurance cost > benefit: net cost included as pricing item (EPV reinsurance premiums paid minus EPV reinsurance benefits received)
  • If arbitrage available (tax or capital): price net of reinsurance for competitive advantage
  • Quota share: reinsurer often co-approves premium tables and retains right to vary commission if rates change
Regulatory Impact
  • Pricing freedom restricted: community rating requirement · prescribed basis · prior approval needed before marketing
  • Underwriting restrictions: ban on genetic test use; gender ban (EU) → anti-selection risk absorbed in pricing
  • Asset investment rules: "local asset" requirement → lower investment return assumed in basis
  • Premium tax: expense loading in basis; neutral to insurer but reduces consumer demand
  • Regulatory costs affect all market participants equally → no competitive distortion within the market
Premium Rate Reviews — 3 Types
1 — New Business Basis Monitoring
  • Keep all assumption components under continuous review
  • When any assumption is no longer valid: test impact on existing tariff → decide if price change needed
  • Not every assumption change forces a premium change — significance test first
  • Core part of the actuarial control cycle
2 — Reviewable Premium Adequacy
  • Test whether current reviewable premiums are still adequate given emerging experience
  • Must allow for worsening morbidity in existing book (anti-selective lapsing)
  • Sensitivity testing throughout: ensure adverse future scenarios don't cause financial distress
3 — Loss-Leader Reserve Adequacy
  • Monitor that reserves set aside to cover losses from deliberately under-priced products remain adequate
  • Adjust as business mix emerges — if subsidising product volumes are below plan, increase loss-leader reserves
  • Over time each line brought back to its own profit contribution

CH20 — Pricing (4): Options & Guarantees
Health Options · Valuation · Assumptions · Stochastic · Guarantees
Mortality / Morbidity Options — Nature & Cost
Three Types of Health Options
  • Option 1 — Purchase additional cover without further medical evidence at normal premium rates for that age at exercise date
  • Option 2 — Renew the policy at end of original term (eg a CI policy) without further evidence of health
  • Option 3 — Buy-back option reinstate life (mortality) cover after an accelerated CI policy has paid out on a specified disease event
  • Health options differ from life options: cannot be exercised while currently claiming (or in deferred/linked-claim period) — someone recovering may be eligible again
  • Terms restricting exercise timing (eg every 5 years, or on qualifying life event) reduce anti-selection risk
Option Cost — Key Dynamics
  • Cost = P(exercising) × average health impairment of exercisers above standard
  • If only high-risk lives exercise: small number but very high average impairment
  • If all lives exercise: larger number but average impairment diluted by healthy lives → lower cost per exercising policyholder
  • Select lives who exercise incur little extra cost to insurer (would have passed UW anyway)
  • Constant monitoring essential — options can come "into the money" unexpectedly (eg widespread onset of a disease)
  • Remedies if option becomes onerous: remove from new business · increase standard premiums · stop issuing underlying policy type · reinsure · reduce reminders to exercise
Valuation of Health Options — Two Methods
Aspect
Conventional Method (100% take-up)
North American Method (realistic take-up)
Take-up assumption
All eligible policyholders exercise at every possible date; maximum benefit always chosen
Realistic take-up rates varying by exercise date and option alternative; ideally from past data
Morbidity of exercisers
Ultimate mortality/morbidity (consistent with 100% take-up — healthy and unhealthy mix)
Heavier than base — eg 150% of base table, or age-rated +5 years; linked to take-up rate assumption
Morbidity of non-exercisers
Not applicable (all exercise)
Set so that weighted average of exercisers + non-exercisers = base expected level (lighter than base)
Advantage
Simple; conservative; no data needed
More realistic; better for pricing and capital management
Disadvantage
Over-states cost; may lead to uncompetitive option pricing
Data on take-up rates historically only available in North America; harder to implement
Additional Assumptions Required for Option Valuation
5 Extra Inputs
P(option exercised) at each possible exercise date Additional benefit level chosen (if at policyholder discretion) Expected morbidity of lives who exercise (heavier than select) Expected morbidity of lives who do NOT exercise (set to maintain average = base) Additional admin expenses (reminders + processing)
Cashflow Valuation Mechanics
  • Extra benefits payable under option (to exercisers at assumed morbidity)
  • Extra premiums received: at standard select rates at the exercise date (same as a new policyholder who just passed UW)
  • Additional expenses (notification, administration)
  • Additional reserves: pre-exercise (option creates future liability now) and post-exercise (anti-selection not observable — reserve without knowing who exercised for health reasons)
  • Higher reserves required than equivalent policy without option, due to increased morbidity risk and additional uncertainty
Stochastic Modelling of Options
  • Subdivide policyholders into risk sub-groups; model proportions in each and claim experience as stochastic variables
  • Run 1,000+ simulations → distribution of option cost → choose premium at required statistical confidence level
  • For extreme events (eg sudden increase in disease prevalence): run deterministic "what-if" parameter shifts, re-run model — equivalent to sensitivity testing in stochastic context
  • Cannot reliably model tail scenarios with standard probability distributions — stress scenarios used instead
Guarantees — Resilience, Reserves & Regulatory Approval
Resilience Testing
  • Test whether guaranteed premium covers all outgoings under adverse (resilience) scenarios — essentially sensitivity testing applied to guaranteed contracts
  • Requires building reserves in early years (when risk is lower and more predictable) to cushion later adverse experience
  • Investment guarantees rarely onerous in most H&C (small reserves vs benefit); exception: pre-funded LTCI where reserves are large
Adequacy of Reserves in Pricing
  • Include a loading for the cost of holding guarantee reserves (as well as the expected claim cost of the guarantee)
  • If true cost is transparent in the premium, policyholders may prefer cheaper reviewable contracts
  • Pricing model must fully incorporate: supervisory reserves + solvency capital requirement + cost of holding that capital
Regulatory Approval
  • Regulator typically requires evidence of techniques and outputs used to justify adequacy of premiums and reserves
  • Especially important for long-term guaranteed contracts where adverse experience cannot be corrected by premium review
  • Submission of basis and calculations may be required before product can be marketed
