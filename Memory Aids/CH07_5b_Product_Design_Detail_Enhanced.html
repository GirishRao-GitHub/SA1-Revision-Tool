The 5 Customer Design Factors
Overview — what makes a product attractive to customers
① Meet Specific Needs
  • Repay loans (mortgage / other)
  • Replace income during disability
  • Finance medical treatment bills
  • Product must evolve with changing needs over time (1890s vs 1990s IP example)
② Provide Cash
  • Cash → policyholder chooses level & use
  • Risk of poor decision when sick (less able to decide)
  • Indemnity → insurer negotiates cheaper provider prices (PMI)
  • Insurer may be unable to guarantee future indemnity (LTCI)
③ Peace of Mind
  • Positive psychological effect of protection
  • Customers often unaware of true risk size
  • State benefits perceived as more generous than reality (IP)
  • CI sells well vs IP in some markets — severity over probability
  • Information asymmetry in healthcare (unknown costs) → certainty of procedure cost is a powerful peace-of-mind driver IAI May-25
④ Simplicity & Clarity
  • Health events more variable in severity than life events
  • Definition problems throughout (incapacity, critical illness)
  • Exclusions / conditions / limits add confusion
  • Complexity must be justified by significant marketing advantage
  • Mature CI markets: ~50–60% claims paid rate = clarity failure at scale — definitions too restrictive vs layperson understanding IFoA Apr-25
  • Back-end UW (moratorium): exclusions only known at claim stage → major source of PMI complaint and clarity failure in practice IAI Jun-19
⑤ Guaranteed vs Reviewable
  • Customers strongly prefer guaranteed premiums
  • H&C uncertainty → guaranteed expensive / often unavailable
  • Where reviewable, customer must be clearly informed of premium change potential
  • Selective lapsing: better risks leave on renewal → worsens pool
  • Good practice (reviewable CI): demonstrate reviews calculated fairly; communicate changes clearly and in advance — TCF obligation IFoA Sep-20

Cash vs Indemnity & Benefit Form — By Product
ProductCash / IndemnityLump Sum / IncomeKey Design Features
IP Cash Income stream (regular payments) Commutable to lump sum — anti-selection caution · Proportional/rehabilitation benefit for part-time return → incentivises recovery · Benefit stops on recovery (key contrast with CI)
CI Cash Lump sum (single payment) Cannot be withdrawn once paid · Not linked to actual loss → windfall risk → anti-selection (angioplasty example) · Lump sum can fund mortgage repayment, lifestyle change, annuity · Tiered benefits attempt to match severity — but add complexity
LTCI Usually cash (not indemnity) — few insurers offer full indemnity Income at onset of incapacity (pre-funded) OR immediate needs (lump sum → income) Full indemnity too uncertain: longevity risk + benefit inflation + unknown future care costs/supply/demand · Insurer takes annuity risk + may index-link benefits · Some insurers offer care management service to help policyholder navigate
PMI Indemnity (standard) — insurer settles with provider directly N/A (indemnity reimburses cost) Insurer negotiates cheaper provider prices (volume advantage) · Cash exceptions: (1) health cash plans (contribution to cost — reduces anti-selection) (2) daily hospital benefit for State treatment (3) major medical expenses lump sum (4) personal accident lump sum on specified injury · May have cap on procedure fees → policyholder covers excess
OP Cover IAI Jul-22 Cash co-payment/deductible per OP consultation controls moral hazard in high-frequency, low-severity business. Annual aggregate cap on benefit. GP gatekeeper referral before specialist. Dental/optical/maternity priced and capped separately. Co-pay per item (e.g. per lab test, per prescription) to limit abuse.
Microinsurance IFoA Sep-22 Fixed daily hospital benefit (cash, not indemnity) preferred: avoids anti-selection risk vs indemnity and greatly simplifies administration at scale. Deductible/co-insurance also useful to deter moral hazard even at low benefit levels — though administration of small deductible may be costly.
Overseas top-up IAI Nov-20 Cap on annual overseas treatment benefit; regional geographic options (cost varies significantly); cover travel and companion costs partially; restrict to specific CI or conditions where local treatment exhausted — keeps cost lower and encourages take-up. Fixed capped benefit easier to price without reliable overseas cost data.

Simplicity & Clarity — Issues by Product
IP — 5 Complexity Issues
  • 1
    Benefit limits — usually 60% of pre-disability income; also capped by State benefits received. Necessary to preserve return-to-work incentive but complicates product.
  • 2
    Salary linkage — individual IP often expressed as cash amount, not % salary → needs annual review. More of a policyholder/adviser issue.
  • 3
    Complex underwriting — occupation difficulty, past medical history, family medical history. Some individuals may struggle to get accepted.
  • 4
    Exclusions — alcohol/drug abuse · self-inflicted injury · war/civil commotion · failure to follow medical advice · non-approved residence.
  • 5
    Objective incapacity test — own occupation vs any job definition; ADLs, ADWs (activities of daily working), FATs (functional assessment tests), PCAs (personal capability assessments).
CI — Simple AND Complex
Why CI is Attractive — The Simple Case
  • Lump sum cannot subsequently be withdrawn by insurer → strong pull for wary customers
  • Diagnosis/procedure trigger is easy to explain
  • Covers a risk consumers can readily recognise (cancer, heart attack, stroke)
Sources of Confusion / Complexity
  • Exclusions — not all CI events covered
  • Point-of-claim underwriting — checks at claim stage
  • Non-standard definitions — heart attack definition differs by insurer; terms more restrictive than layperson's understanding
  • Tiered benefits: harder to compare products + higher claims dispute risk
  • Mature market evidence: ~50–60% claims-paid rate — regulator responded with mandatory definition review and consumer protection rules IFoA Apr-25
  • Adding mental health conditions: hard to define objectively; high prevalence → adverse selection risk; definitions even more likely to differ from layperson understanding IFoA Sep-24
LTCI — Complexity Issues
  • Concept straightforward — but cash ≠ full indemnity (significant confusion risk)
  • Limited benefit periods or maximum amounts (need may last a lifetime)
  • Trigger = ADLs (bathing, dressing, eating) or cognitive impairment — not doctor's referral; must be explained
  • Multiple benefit levels add complication for less expert purchasers
  • Purchaser may believe care is being bought; may be disappointed
PMI — Complexity Issues
  • UW & acceptance process — moratorium/MHD confusing to applicants
  • Excesses and upper limits (procedure/annual aggregate)
  • Exclusions: chronic/non-acute · pre-existing conditions · cosmetic · lifestyle
  • Cost-sharing: coinsurance (% of claim) + excess/deductible
  • Pre-authorisation — insurer approval before treatment (but reduces disputed claims)
  • Hospital bands/networks — restricted provider choice; gatekeeper doctor referral
  • Back-end UW (moratorium) in India: exclusions only applied at claim stage — customer unaware at purchase → disputes and complaint volumes. Proposal to move to front-end UW: better clarity but slower sales process IAI Jun-19

IAI Nov-23Gender-neutral marketing (when gender rating prohibited): target gender indirectly — advertise in gender-targeted media; use worksite marketing targeting occupations with gender bias; offer discounts for gender-biased services; highlight specific illnesses covered. Allows risk management without breaching regulatory constraint.
IAI Mar-18Online distribution complexity constraint: product sold without advice must have minimal underwriting complexity — complex products mis-sold without adviser guidance; staff at point of sale require targeted training even for simple products.

Guaranteed vs Reviewable Premiums & Benefits
ProductTypical StructureWhy / Design RationaleKey Risks & Considerations
IP Reviewable predominates Claims experience subject to many external influences (unemployment, economic trends, social factors) outside insurer's control · Guarantees require large reserves → expensive premium Selective lapsing on review — healthier lives leave, limiting premium increase effectiveness · Customer must be clearly informed of reviewability · Guaranteed premiums available but priced significantly higher
CI Reviewable more suitable; but competitive pressure has led to guaranteed being sold Medical advances change rapidly — new treatments, changing definitions, new CI events · Rate of change makes future experience very hard to guarantee Guaranteed CI may be mispriced if medical practice changes significantly · Reviewable allows insurer to amend cover (expand or restrict) · New disease expectations must be managed carefully
LTCI Immediate needs = guaranteed / Pre-funded = reviewable Immediate needs: lump sum purchased at point of need → premium determined then → no long-term guarantee issue · Pre-funded: uncertainties over longevity + care cost inflation → reviewable Pre-funded: insurer must be robust to changes in state provision and care costs · Reviewability must be disclosed clearly to purchaser
PMI Annually renewable — both premiums and conditions reviewed Short-term nature removes long-term guarantee problem entirely · Renewal provides annual opportunity to reprice and revise policy terms Good experience → insurer can expand cover (reward loyalty/persistency) · Poor experience → premium increase or benefit reduction → selective non-renewal risk · Monitor competitor pricing to control anti-selection
IP — Guaranteed detail IAI Dec-22 Guaranteed IP advantages: increases sales (customers prefer certainty); no reputational risk from rate increases; less selective lapsation (no renewal trigger to prompt switching); simpler admin. Disadvantages: onerous capital requirements for full guarantees; inflexible if experience deteriorates — insurer absorbs all variance. Hybrid structure (e.g. 5-year initial guarantee then reviewable): balances marketability vs capital risk — initial guarantee builds customer confidence; review after 5 years reflects actual experience. Favoured when lack of data makes full guarantee very expensive.
CI — Reviewable good practice IFoA Sep-20 Good practice statements for reviewable CI: (1) demonstrate reviews calculated fairly; (2) review assumptions at frequency stated in policy literature; (3) communicate changes clearly and in advance; (4) TCF obligation — policyholder research (focus groups) to understand impact on switching behaviour. Undertake research to understand if customers would consider switching to guaranteed premium product.
New product — reviewable rationale IAI Apr-16 Reviewable justified when launching new product with no credible own experience: confidence intervals on pricing basis are wide; cost of guarantee very high with limited data; allows lower initial premium to penetrate market. Must assess marketability impact vs competitors offering guaranteed — important commercial trade-off.

Insurer: The 8 Iterative Design Targets
Key Principle Product design and pricing are iterative — each is adjusted in turn until ALL targets are simultaneously met. The actuary must involve sales, marketing, systems, legal and reinsurance teams throughout the process.
  • ① Customer
    Customer acceptability — product must be clear about benefits & premiums; meet customer needs; provide customer gain (perceived value > premium cost); marketable; not overly complex for the intended distribution channel
  • ② Regulator
    Regulator requirements — product approval before launch; premiums may need to be filed (compulsory/concentrated markets); comply with sales, disclosure and conduct-of-business rules
  • ③ Distributors
    Needs of distributors — consult sales/marketing teams early; appropriate channels (not too complex for internet sales); competitive premiums; commission & clawback structures; actuary involved in sales training
  • ④ Profitability
    Adequate profitability / ROC — premiums must cover benefits + expenses + profit margin; adequate return on capital; balance competitive pricing vs required margin; each contract contributes its required profit share in medium term
  • ⑤ Culture
    Company culture in product style & price — consistency with existing products (systems, training, admin, printing efficiencies); major design change → systems development time; avoid dissatisfying existing policyholders with more favourable new terms (→ surrender wave)
  • ⑥ Systems
    Systems & internal constraints — IT must: record policy inception; align claims data; monitor profitability; group by risk characteristic; add external data; model & project cashflows including capital. New products may require systems reorganisation — allow development & testing time
  • ⑦ UW
    Underwriting methodology — medical UW is fundamental to product design; decision to exclude pre-existing conditions affects design, pricing, systems; claims UW must be consistent with initial UW criteria and pricing data assumptions; competitors' claims practices must be matched (avoid anti-selection)
  • ⑧ Risk Mgmt
    Risk management — anti-selection/moral hazard control (clear policy rules); data for pricing; reinsurance (method, amount, provider — may assist with contract design); minimise financing strain; cross-subsidies monitored; guarantee costs; premium review triggers; level of risk acceptable depends on ability to absorb or reinsure
Strategies for launching in a new/uncertain risk environment (e.g. first CI in a market)
Offer with reviewable premium Reinsure large part of risk Incorporate large margins Offer as rider on term insurance (accelerated death claims)
★ New Product Launch — Exam-Tested Additional Targets & Risks
IAI Apr-16New product launch risk list (frequently examined): (1) Product substitution — new product outsells existing profitable product, eroding margin. (2) Too-much-success — capital-intensive product consumes capital faster than planned. (3) Sales failure — development costs not recovered; expense overruns per policy. (4) Regulatory non-compliance risk. (5) Reputational risk if product fails to meet customer expectations.
IAI Mar-18Pre-launch checklist: understand customer requirements; market research and demand assessment; study competitor/market success stories; check if government or employer already covers part of the benefit (dental example); assess reinsurer appetite and technical support; review regulatory product filing requirements and timelines.
IAI Mar-21Online/virtual distribution — design requirements: compulsory participation (all dependants covered) minimises UW requirements; set low annual limits to reduce need for individual UW; plain language 'what is not covered' prominently disclosed at sign-up; fixed co-pays/deductibles; simplified claims process; fast digital claim notification.
IFoA Apr-24Automated UW (AI/ML) impact on design targets: potential for better risk classification but loss of human judgment; change in business mix/volume if more applicants declined; claims experience shifts — existing data no longer representative; upfront development cost must be allocated over new business; insurer becomes dependent on third-party system (contingency planning required); must still handle IP-specific factors (back pain, stress) not relevant to other H&C products.
IAI May-24Cross-product launch points: allow for cost of product disclosure and sales training required by sales regulations; risk of too-successful launch consuming capital from elsewhere in business; more sales than expected may lead to resourcing problems and compromised service standards; new H&C business line adds uncertainty — allow for this in capital and pricing margins.

Insurer: Product-Specific Risk Control
IP — Control Measures
  • UW by occupation, area of residence, age, gender (if permitted)
  • At claim: check benefit level vs current salary (maintain return-to-work incentive)
  • Claims counsellor: (a) advise claimant on coping (b) establish likely return-to-work date
  • Early notification of sickness → enables early rehabilitation services
  • Max benefit cap (60%) — remove over-insurance risk
  • Linked-claims period — removes penalty of relapsing; encourages short return-to-work attempts
  • Proportionate benefit for part-time return
  • After 2 years: revert to 'any job' definition + assist with retraining
  • Periodic monitoring of ongoing claims by specialist nurses
CI — Control Measures
  • Strong UW and claims processes to guard vs anti-selection and non-disclosure
  • Stand-alone: survival period (28–30 days) to avoid inadvertent death claims
  • IBNR reserves — late notified claims can arise up to 10 years post-diagnosis; some impose notification period (e.g. 2 years)
  • Manage policyholder and distributor expectations re: new diseases at little or no cost — all must be properly costed
  • Monitor market moves to extend/restrict cover (guaranteed insurability options)
  • Tiered CI specific: data scarce → large margins + reinsurer essential; careful policy wording for each tier; UW more complex; higher dispute risk
  • Regulatory response to low claims-paid rate: review definitions; target improvement in claims-paid ratio; implement MI framework tracking rejection rates and complaint volumes IFoA Apr-25
LTCI — Control Measures
  • Policy design robust to changes in state provision (benefits must remain relevant)
  • Robust to regulatory interpretation of benefit promises (title 'long-term care' sets strong expectations)
  • Manage longevity risk (annuity risk retained by insurer)
  • Manage benefit inflation risk (care costs, salaries of care workers)
  • Some insurers offer care management service (advice, care navigation) — adds value at low cost
PMI — Control Measures
  • Provider price negotiation — volume leverage to reduce claim costs
  • Managed care / case management — active claims management to minimise duration and cost
  • Pre-authorisation — confirm cover before treatment; reduces disputes; controls hospital quality
  • Hospital bands/networks — preferred provider arrangements
  • IBNR is material — must be reserved for
  • Group PMI: experience rating; employer incentivised to control claims (premium feedback)
  • Gatekeeper doctors — refer to consultants only when necessary
  • Back-end UW control risk: move to front-end UW improves clarity but slows sales process — key design trade-off between control quality and distribution speed IAI Jun-19
★ Additional Product-Specific Control Measures (Exam-Tested)
IAI Jul-22Outpatient (OP) cover: co-payment per consultation (moral hazard deterrent for high-frequency, low-severity business); annual aggregate cap; GP gatekeeper before specialist referral; pre-authorisation for high-cost items; separate pricing and caps for dental/optical/maternity; co-pay per item (lab test, prescription).
IAI Sep-21Top-up / excess layer anti-selection controls: 6-month waiting period before eligible to claim; higher co-pay in first year (skin in the game); restrict cover to specific event types only (e.g. accidents + named CI conditions); individual UW for groups below minimum size; volume caps to limit capital strain.
IFoA Sep-22Microinsurance controls: reviewable premiums and benefit terms essential (allows reaction to unfavourable experience); co-insurance/deductible to limit moral hazard even at low benefit levels; pilot in limited geography before national rollout; volume limits to control capital strain.
IAI Mar-17Wearable/wellness product specific risks: mispricing risk (limited credible data for discount parameters; wearable tech rapidly evolving); reputational risk (product fails to meet premium-reduction expectations; device failures); data breach risk (large data volumes stored); counterparty risk if device manufacturer fails — contractual protections and multiple manufacturers essential.

Regulator as Stakeholder & Design Conflicts
Regulator Priorities & Tools
Two Core Priorities
  • Insurer solvency — adequate and accurate regulatory reporting
  • Consumer protection — minimise disadvantages in sales, admin and claims
Consumer Protection Tools
  • Clear and unambiguous policy wording
  • Range of benefits matching what product title implies (CI / LTCI danger zones)
  • Key features document — written explanation of policy to prospective buyer
  • Value for money relative to market comparables
  • Sales process regulated: insurer discovers customer's needs; recommends accordingly
  • CI: mandatory claims-paid ratio monitoring and definition review following low claims payout rates IFoA Apr-25
  • IRDAI: policyholder-centric governance; grievance redressal emphasis; prevent mis-selling at point of sale IAI Jun-19
Sales Monitoring Requirements
  • Appropriate product sold at right level for customer needs and ability to pay
  • No undue pressure to purchase; cooling-off periods available
  • Promises made consistent with actual policy conditions
  • Product sold to intended target market; if wider, adequacy must be evaluated
  • Premium increases require regulatory approval in compulsory/concentrated markets
  • Guaranteed renewability: regulations prevent unilateral worsening of existing policy conditions — regulatory approval required; policyholder reasonable expectations must be respected IAI Mar-18
  • Free product (promotional): regulator concerned about financial soundness (no premium), adequacy of reserves, enrolment process transparency, and whether minimum spend excludes genuine-need customers IFoA Apr-24
