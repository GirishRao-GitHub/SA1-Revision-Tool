📋 What is IFRS 17?
  • IFRS 17 Insurance Contracts is the IFRS standard governing accounting for insurance contracts. It replaced IFRS 4 from 1 January 2023.
  • It is the first comprehensive international accounting standard applicable to all types of health and care insurance contracts.
  • It is much more prescriptive than IFRS 4 and its adoption has led to substantial changes to financial disclosures and the systems and processes that produce them.
  • IFRS 17 aims to make financial statements more relevant, comparable, and transparent.
  • EU regulations require all listed insurance companies to use EU-adopted IFRS for their consolidated financial statements. In the UK, all listed companies must use IFRS.
  • Key related IFRS standards for health and care insurers: IFRS 9 (Financial Instruments) and IFRS 7 (Financial Instruments: Disclosures).
⚠️ Why Replace IFRS 4? (Main Criticisms)
  • Sensitivity to assumptions: Reported profits were highly sensitive to reserving assumptions.
  • Lack of comparability: Companies could use local accounting standards or different methodologies, making cross-insurer comparison difficult.
  • Early profit recognition: Premiums and profits for long-term contracts could be recognised too early.
✅ What IFRS 17 Achieves
  • Defers profit on long-term contracts through the CSM — profit recognised over the service period.
  • Standardises methodology — all insurers follow the same GMM framework.
  • Separates insurance profit from investment returns — revenue and expenses presented separately.
  • Immediate loss recognition — loss-making contracts cannot be offset against profitable ones.
📊 IFRS Principles (Background)
  • Fair representation: Financial statements must present fairly financial position, performance, and cashflows.
  • Going concern: Statements prepared assuming the entity will continue in operation.
  • Accruals based: Revenue when earned, expenses when incurred — regardless of actual cashflows.
  • Comparability: Presentation must be consistent period to period unless justified by changed circumstances.
  • Compliance: Entity must make an explicit statement of compliance with all IFRS requirements.
  • Reporting period: Statements prepared at least annually; period changes must be disclosed.
  • Understandability: Information should be readily understandable to knowledgeable users.
  • Offsetting: Assets and liabilities, and income and expenses, may generally not be offset.
📄 Disclosure Requirements
  • Insurance revenue earned — amount earned from providing insurance coverage.
  • Insurance service expenses incurred — claims, benefits, and other expenses of providing insurance.
  • Insurance finance income and expenses — the unwinding of discount on insurance liabilities.
Implementation note: The implementation of IFRS 17 took much longer than planned and has been complex and expensive for many health and care insurers — requiring significant changes to systems, processes, and data.

BLOCKS 1 + 2 · Best Estimate Liability (BEL)

Block 1 — Fulfilment Cashflows (FCF): Probability-weighted estimates of all future cashflows arising from the contract — premiums, claims, benefits, and expenses.

Block 2 — Time Value of Money: FCFs are discounted using a rate derived from observable market data to get the present value.

The BEL is analogous to the Solvency II Best Estimate. Methodology: derive best estimate assumptions → derive a discount rate from observable market data → discount projected cashflows → allow for the cost of any guarantees.

BEL = PV(Future claims + expenses − future premiums)

BLOCK 3 · Risk Adjustment (RA)

The RA represents the value of non-market risks not captured in the BEL — analogous to the Solvency II Risk Margin.

Calculation methods: VaR (Value at Risk), CTE (Conditional Tail Expectation), or Cost of Capital (CoC). IFRS 17 does NOT specify the confidence level — this is chosen by the insurer.

This flexibility means comparability of RA across insurers is limited unless they disclose confidence levels.

RA = Compensation for bearing non-market risk uncertainty

BLOCK 4 · Contractual Service Margin (CSM)

The CSM is a new concept under IFRS 17. It represents future (unearned) profit on the contract. At inception:

CSM = (Initial premium − Initial expenses) − (BEL + RA)

It is set so that zero profit arises at the point of sale. If BEL + RA exceeds the initial net cashflow (i.e. CSM would be negative), a loss is recognised immediately — the CSM cannot be negative.

The CSM is then released over the policy term in line with a chosen coverage unit measure (e.g. number of policies in-force), smoothing profit emergence.

The CSM also absorbs changes in BEL and RA due to non-investment assumption changes — so reported profit is driven by actual experience, not assumption volatility.

📐 Contract Grouping Rules
  • Profitable and unprofitable contracts cannot be offset.
  • Each policy is categorised as: loss-making at inception; profitable with no significant risk of loss; or any other profitable contract.
  • Annual cohort rule: Policies written more than one year apart cannot be grouped — contracts must remain in annual cohorts.
  • Losses recognised immediately; profits emerge over the contract term via CSM release.

INCEPTION · SETTING THE CSM

At policy inception, the CSM is set to ensure zero profit is recognised at point of sale.

CSM₀ = (Initial premium − Initial expenses) − (BEL₀ + RA₀)

For a profitable contract, the present value of future profit loadings in all future premiums exceeds current expenses — so CSM₀ is positive.

Key insight: Even if initial expenses exceed the first premium (common in regular premium business), the BEL will be negative (PV of future premiums net of benefits is positive), making the CSM positive as long as the RA is smaller than the total profit loadings.

EACH REPORTING PERIOD · RELEASING THE CSM

The CSM is released over the contract term according to a chosen coverage unit measure — for example, number of policies in-force, sum at risk, or expected claims.

CSM released each period = CSM balance × (coverage units in period ÷ total remaining coverage units)

This creates a smooth, systematic emergence of profit over the policy term — rather than a large profit at inception and losses in later years.

The CSM balance also unwinds at the discount rate each period (interest accretes on the CSM).

ASSUMPTION CHANGES · CSM AS BUFFER

If non-investment assumptions change (e.g. revised morbidity or lapse assumptions), the resulting change to BEL + RA is absorbed by the CSM rather than flowing through to profit/loss.

This is a key design feature: reported profit is driven more by actual experience than by assumption changes, reducing volatility.

→ If BEL + RA increases by £X: CSM decreases by £X (P&L unaffected)

→ If BEL + RA decreases by £X: CSM increases by £X (profit deferred)

Exception (GMM only): Changes in discount rate cannot be absorbed by the CSM under the GMM — these flow directly to profit/loss. Under the VFA, discount rate changes are absorbed by the CSM.

CSM INSUFFICIENT · IMMEDIATE LOSS

If the required increase in BEL + RA exceeds the remaining CSM balance, the CSM is exhausted and the shortfall is recognised immediately as a loss in P&L.

Loss recognised = Increase in (BEL + RA) − Remaining CSM

After this point, the contract becomes an onerous contract. Any subsequent favourable experience or assumption changes first restore the onerous contract balance before the CSM can become positive again.

LOSS-MAKING CONTRACT · NO CSM

The CSM cannot be negative. If (BEL + RA) exceeds initial net cashflows at inception, the entire day-one loss is recognised immediately in P&L.

If (BEL₀ + RA₀) > (Initial premium − Initial expenses): CSM = 0, loss = full shortfall

Loss-making and profitable contracts must be kept in separate groups — a profitable contract cannot subsidise a loss-making one within the liability measurement.

📝 Coverage Unit

The measure used to determine how the CSM is released each period. Examples: number of policies in-force, sum at risk, expected claims. Must be chosen consistently and applied on an annual cohort basis — policies written more than one year apart cannot be grouped together.

💡 Key Design Goal

The CSM ensures profit is recognised as insurance services are provided — not at inception. This addresses the IFRS 4 criticism of early profit recognition, and prevents insurers from "front-loading" profit by changing assumptions.

IFRS 17 · Valuation Methods
Three Measurement Models
GMM / BBA
General Measurement Model
Also: Building Block Approach
  • Default methodology — applies to any contract not eligible for VFA or PAA.
  • Appropriate for traditional long-term life business e.g. term assurance, without-profit annuities.
  • Discount rate based on market observable data.
  • Discount rate changes flow through directly to P&L — CSM not unlocked for rate changes.
VFA
Variable Fee Approach
Mandatory for direct participation features
  • Used where a significant portion of cashflows vary with the value of a defined pool of assets.
  • Applicable to unit-linked, with-profits, or similar contracts.
  • Discount rate calculated with reference to the underlying asset pool.
  • CSM is "unlocked" — absorbs changes in BEL and RA due to discount rate movements.
PAA
Premium Allocation Approach
Simplified model
  • Simplified method — similar to the unearned premium method used by general insurers.
  • May be used for short-term health and care business (typically annual contracts).
  • Permitted where it would produce a liability not materially different from the full GMM.
  • Significantly reduces computational complexity vs GMM.
⚖️ GMM vs VFA — Key Differences
Dimension GMM VFA
Applicable to Traditional long-term contracts (term assurance, annuities) Unit-linked, with-profits, and direct participation contracts
Discount rate Market observable data — not linked to a specific asset pool Calculated with reference to the pool of assets backing the contract
Discount rate changes → P&L? Yes — flows directly to P&L (CSM not unlocked) No — absorbed by the CSM (CSM unlocked)
Non-investment assumption changes Absorbed by CSM (up to available balance) Absorbed by CSM (up to available balance)
CSM "unlocking" Not unlocked for financial assumptions Unlocked to absorb financial assumption changes
P&L volatility from market moves Higher — market movements pass through directly Lower — market movements absorbed in CSM, released over time
Memory hook: Under GMM the CSM is a fixed dam that absorbs non-financial shocks but lets market waves through to P&L. Under VFA, the CSM is a flexible reservoir that absorbs both financial and non-financial shocks.
IFRS 17 · Effective date: 1 January 2023
Transitional Requirements
Companies transitioning to IFRS 17 were required to calculate a transitional CSM for all in-force business at the transition date. Three approaches are available, applied in order of preference.
PREFERRED
Full Retrospective Approach (FRA)
Determines the position at transition as if IFRS 17 had always applied throughout the full policy lifetime. Requires complete historical data.
Must be used if practicable.
IF FRA NOT PRACTICABLE
Modified Retrospective Approach (MRA)
A simplified version of the FRA, using reasonably available information. Aims to produce a result as close as possible to FRA.
Used when FRA is not practicable; requires reasonably available information.
LAST RESORT
Fair Value Approach (FVA)
Transitional CSM = fair value of policies less fulfilment cashflows at the transition date. Requires only current-date data.
Used when MRA is also not practicable.
FRA → MRA → FVA (in order of preference)
🔎 FVA — Transitional CSM Formula
Transitional CSM (FVA) = Fair value of in-force policies at transition date
                           − Fulfilment cashflows at transition date
Practical challenge: IFRS 17 implementation took much longer than planned and proved complex and expensive. Systems needed to hold historical data for FRA, build new actuarial models for the GMM building blocks, and produce new disclosure formats — all simultaneously.
📋 Other Key Points
  • Ind AS 117 is the Indian equivalent of IFRS 17 — relevant for Indian health insurer reporting.
  • IFRS 9 (Financial Instruments) governs the asset side: default measurement is Fair Value through Profit and Loss (FVPL).
  • FVPL means changes in asset fair value come through as profit/loss — consistent with a market-consistent approach to both sides of the balance sheet.
  • FVOCI (Fair Value through Other Comprehensive Income) allows some unrealised gains/losses to bypass the P&L where restrictive criteria are met.

Exam-Style Questions & Answers
These Q&As are drawn directly from the SA1 revision notes (Ch14). Click a question to reveal the model answer.
1
Why would the CSM be expected to be positive for a profitable regular premium contract, despite initial expenses likely exceeding the initial premium?
▶
Model Answer
For a regular premium contract, the BEL would be negative because the present value of future premiums (net of future benefits and expenses) exceeds current obligations — the profit loadings in future premiums are expected to recover initial expenses and provide a profit margin. As long as the RA is smaller than the discounted value of all profit loadings, the CSM = (Initial premium − Initial expenses) − (BEL + RA) will be positive.
2
What are the main criticisms of IFRS 4 that IFRS 17 addresses?
▶
Model Answer
IFRS 4 was criticised for three main reasons:

(1) Sensitivity of reported profits to reserving assumptions — small changes in assumptions could dramatically alter reported results.

(2) Lack of comparability between different health and care insurance companies — companies could use local standards or different methodologies, making cross-insurer comparison difficult.

(3) Early recognition of premiums and profits for long-term contracts — profits could be front-loaded at inception rather than spread over the service period.
3
How does the CSM smooth the emergence of profit under IFRS 17?
▶
Model Answer
The CSM smooths profit in two ways:

(1) Systematic release: The CSM is set at inception to equal the present value of all future profit loadings. It is then released over the contract term in line with a coverage unit measure (e.g. policies in-force), causing profit to emerge steadily rather than at inception.

(2) Absorption of assumption changes: Changes in the BEL and RA due to non-investment assumption changes are absorbed by the CSM (rather than flowing directly to P&L). So reported profit is driven primarily by actual experience rather than by changes in assumptions — further reducing volatility.
4
What is the key difference between the GMM and VFA with regard to the discount rate and CSM?
▶
Model Answer
GMM: The discount rate is based on market observable data and is not linked to a specific asset pool. Changes in the discount rate flow through directly to the profit and loss statement, and the CSM is not unlocked — financial assumption changes are not absorbed by the CSM.

VFA: The discount rate is calculated with reference to the pool of assets backing the contract. The CSM is unlocked at each future period to absorb changes in BEL and RA arising from discount rate changes — so these changes are deferred into future profit releases rather than being recognised immediately in P&L.

Both models absorb non-investment assumption changes in the CSM in the same way.
5
What happens if the CSM is insufficient to absorb an increase in BEL + RA?
▶
Model Answer (derived from notes)
If the CSM is insufficient to absorb the required increase in BEL + RA:

(1) The CSM is reduced to zero — exhausting its available balance.

(2) The shortfall is recognised immediately as a loss in P&L — the contract becomes onerous.

The CSM cannot go negative. Once the contract is onerous, any subsequent favourable experience changes first restore the onerous contract balance before the CSM can rebuild.
6
Describe the three approaches available for transitioning in-force business to IFRS 17, and in what order they should be applied.
▶
Model Answer
Three approaches are available, applied in order of preference:

(1) Full Retrospective Approach (FRA) — preferred: Must be used if practicable. Determines the IFRS 17 position as if the standard had always applied throughout the full policy lifetime.

(2) Modified Retrospective Approach (MRA): A simplified version of the FRA, using reasonably available information to produce a result as close as possible to FRA. Used when FRA is not practicable.

(3) Fair Value Approach (FVA) — last resort: Used when MRA is also not practicable. Transitional CSM = fair value of policies less fulfilment cashflows at the transition date.
Key Terms · SA1 Chapter 14
IFRS 17 Glossary
IFRS 17
Insurance Contracts — the IFRS standard specifically governing accounting for insurance contracts, replacing IFRS 4 from 1 January 2023.
GMM / BBA
General Measurement Model / Building Block Approach — the default IFRS 17 valuation methodology for insurance contracts. Defines four building blocks: fulfilment cashflows, time value of money, risk adjustment, and CSM.
VFA
Variable Fee Approach — an IFRS 17 measurement model used where a significant proportion of cashflows vary with the value of a defined pool of assets (e.g. unit-linked, with-profits). Mandatory for direct participation contracts.
PAA
Premium Allocation Approach — a simplified IFRS 17 model similar to the unearned premium method used by general insurers. May be used for short-term health and care business.
BEL
Best Estimate Liability — the probability-weighted estimate of the present value of future net cashflows arising from fulfilment of policyholder obligations. Calculated using best estimate assumptions and a market-observable discount rate.
Risk Adjustment (RA)
The IFRS 17 component representing the value of non-market risks not captured in the BEL (analogous to the Solvency II risk margin). Calculated using VaR, CTE, or cost of capital; the confidence level is chosen by the insurer.
CSM
Contractual Service Margin — an IFRS 17 building block representing the unearned (future) profit on a contract. Set at inception so no profit is recognised at sale; released over the contract term as insurance services are provided. Cannot be negative.
Coverage Unit
The measure used to determine how the CSM is released over the contract term (e.g. number of policies in-force). Contracts must remain in annual cohorts — policies written more than one year apart cannot be grouped.
FRA
Full Retrospective Approach — the preferred IFRS 17 transitional approach. Determines the position at transition as if IFRS 17 had always applied throughout the full policy lifetime.
MRA
Modified Retrospective Approach — a simplified IFRS 17 transitional approach used when FRA is not practicable; uses reasonably available information to produce a result as close as possible to FRA.
FVA
Fair Value Approach — an IFRS 17 transitional approach used when MRA is also not practicable; transitional CSM = fair value of policies less fulfilment cashflows at the transition date.
FVPL
Fair Value through Profit and Loss — the default IFRS 9 measurement basis for financial assets. Changes in fair value are recognised directly in profit and loss as they arise.
FVOCI
Fair Value through Other Comprehensive Income — an IFRS 9 classification where changes in fair value are recognised in other comprehensive income rather than directly in P&L.
IASB
International Accounting Standards Board — the body that produces IFRS standards.
IAS 1
Presentation of Financial Statements — sets out the overall requirements for general purpose financial statements prepared under IFRS.
Ind AS 117
The Indian equivalent of IFRS 17 — applicable to Indian insurers reporting under Indian Accounting Standards.
