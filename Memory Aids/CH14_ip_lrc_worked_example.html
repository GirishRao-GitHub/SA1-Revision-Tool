The four actuarial assumptions that drive the LRC
Incidence rateProbability Priya becomes disabled in any given year1.8% p.a.
Recovery rateProbability an active claimant returns to work each year35% p.a.
MortalityProbability of death while claiming (terminates the benefit)0.4% p.a.
Discount rateRisk-free rate used to present-value future cashflows under Ind AS 1176.5% p.a.
What the LRC must capture: for every future year of remaining coverage, the LRC is the probability-weighted present value of benefits that would be paid if Priya becomes (or stays) disabled — discounted back to today. It is not just "incidence × benefit". It must also account for how long a claim would last (recovery rate), whether she dies on claim, and the time value of money across a 25-year horizon.
For each future year, we calculate the expected benefit outgo — then discount it. The LRC is the sum of all these discounted cashflows.
Expected benefit in year t =
P(disabled in yr t) × E[benefit months | disabled] × ₹30,000

P(disabled in yr t) = P(in-force at t) × incidence rate
E[benefit months | disabled] = 1 / (recovery rate + mortality rate)
= 1 / (0.35 + 0.004) ≈ 2.83 years = 34 months

Discounted value = Expected benefit / (1 + 0.065)^t
First 10 years of the 25-year projection. Highlighted = year with highest undiscounted expected outgo.
The deferred period effect: Priya's 13-week deferred period means not all new claims translate immediately into outgo. A claim in November might not start paying until February. We model this as a 0.25-year lag in the benefit start — reducing the effective benefit months for late-year inceptions. This is a real economic difference from a 4-week deferred period.
The LRC at inception is the sum of all 25 discounted expected benefit cashflows, plus the discounted future expenses. The RA and CSM sit on top — they are separate components of the ICL, not part of the LRC itself.

Three things happening simultaneously: (1) The LRC falls as coverage is earned and future expected cashflows shorten. (2) The RA releases to the insurance service result each year as uncertainty resolves. (3) The CSM releases based on coverage units — for IP, that means probability-weighted expected benefit-months remaining, which front-loads the release. By year 15 the CSM is nearly exhausted even though 10 years of cover remain.
