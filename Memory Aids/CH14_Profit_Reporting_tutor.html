Purpose and users of financial reporting

Foundation▼

Health and care insurers report regularly on their financial position for a range of users.

  • Regulators — assess solvency position and available capital
  • Shareholders — assess profit and value creation (statutory accounts)
  • Creditors, employees, tax inspectors, general public

Two key purposes: demonstrate assets cover liabilities and show size and source of profits. Financial statements include balance sheet, revenue/profit statement, and disclosure notes.

Key design decisions for accounting standards

Concepts▼
  • Rules-based vs principles-based — IFRS and US GAAP are both principles-based; management can exercise discretion, but this risks manipulation and reduces comparability
  • Going-concern vs wind-up basis — profit measures assume going concern (will keep selling business)
  • Profit recognition timing — accruals basis (when earned/incurred), not when cash received/paid
  • Consolidation rules — netting of reinsurance, derivatives, subsidiaries affects balance sheet size
  • Calculation complexity — stochastic models take time; timeliness vs accuracy trade-off
  • Reporting basis — best estimate vs prudent assumptions. Newer standards use best estimate + explicit risk margin
  • Consistency — statutory standards require cross-industry comparability (e.g. FTSE investors compare insurers with miners)

IFRS 17 vs IFRS 4 — why IFRS 17?

Key exam area▼

IFRS 17 took effect 1 January 2023. It replaced IFRS 4 which had three main criticisms ("ACE"):

IFRS 4 problem

  • Profits sensitive to reserving assumptions (small changes → big P&L swings)
  • No comparability — each company could use different local standards
  • Premiums & profits recognised too early for long-term contracts

IFRS 17 solution

  • CSM absorbs non-economic assumption changes → profit driven by actual experience
  • Single global principle-based standard with extensive disclosure requirements
  • CSM defers profit and releases it over the coverage period

US GAAP vs IFRS — key differences

Comparison▼
  • Insurance contract definition — US GAAP splits H&C products into 3 categories: traditional non-linked, unit-linked/with-profits/investment/limited pay, non-unitised with-profits
  • Compliance statement — IFRS requires explicit unreserved compliance statement; US GAAP does not
  • Historical cost — US GAAP values assets at acquisition cost; IFRS uses fair value (via IFRS 9)
  • Matching principle — US GAAP uses DAC (Deferred Acquisition Cost) to match expenses with revenues; also uses DPL (Deferred Profit Liability)
  • Offsetting derivatives — US GAAP: offsetting optional; IFRS: required when conditions are met
  • Asset classification — US GAAP: trading / available-for-sale / held-to-maturity; IFRS 9: FVPL / FVOCI / Amortised Cost

Supplementary value-based reporting (EV)

Section 4▼

Some H&C companies report embedded values (EV) to inform shareholders of the true economic value of their interest and changes over time.

EV = PV of expected future transfers to shareholders from existing business + net assets attributable to shareholders

Components: Free surplus + Required capital − cost of holding capital + PVIF (present value of in-force business)

Standards developed to improve consistency: EEV (European EV principles) and MCEV (Market Consistent EV). Note: EEV and MCEV are outside the SA1 syllabus.

Under Solvency II, many EU companies no longer publish EV because the 'information gap' EV filled is now much smaller — accounting and solvency reporting are both moving towards market-consistent valuations.

Three measurement models — GVP

Core▼
  • GMM (General Measurement Model) — also called BBA (Building Block Approach). Default for all contracts. Appropriate for traditional long-term H&C: term assurance, income protection, critical illness, without-profit annuities
  • VFA (Variable Fee Approach) — mandatory for direct participation features (unit-linked, with-profits / DPF). CSM is "unlocked" when discount rate changes, absorbing the gain/loss rather than hitting P&L
  • PAA (Premium Allocation Approach) — simplified model for short-term contracts (similar to unearned premium reserve). Not covered further in SA1 subject

GMM building blocks — BEL, RA, CSM

Core▼
Total liability = BEL + RA + CSM
  • BEL (Best Estimate Liability) — PV of probability-weighted future net cashflows, discounted at market-observable rates. Calculated like Solvency II BEL. Can be negative for profitable regular-premium contracts
  • RA (Risk Adjustment) — compensation for non-financial, non-market risks. Calculated via VaR, CTE, or CoC. Confidence level chosen by insurer (not specified by IFRS 17). Calculated allowing for diversification but attributed to individual contracts
  • CSM (Contractual Service Margin) — "future profit" or "unearned profit". Set at inception so total liability = initial net cashflow (zero day-one profit). Released over the coverage period using coverage units (e.g. policies in-force). Annual cohort grouping applies
CSM at inception = (Initial premium − Initial expenses) − (BEL + RA)

CSM can never be negative. If BEL + RA > initial net cashflow, the shortfall is recognised immediately as a loss.

CSM — four key properties (NORA)

High-yield▼
  • Never negative — floors at zero. Losses recognised immediately
  • Offsets non-economic assumption changes — changes to morbidity, mortality, expense assumptions adjust CSM first, not P&L. Only if CSM insufficient does the shortfall hit P&L. Economic changes (discount rates) go straight to P&L under GMM
  • Released over coverage period — written down in line with coverage units. Contracts grouped in annual cohorts (policies more than 1 year apart cannot be grouped)
  • At-inception = day-one profit — ensures zero profit on day of sale; all loadings are deferred into the CSM

GMM vs VFA — critical comparison

Exam favourite▼

GMM

  • Discount rate from market-observable data
  • When rate rises: BEL & RA fall → gain goes straight to P&L
  • CSM not unlocked for discount rate changes
  • Higher P&L volatility from interest rate movements
  • Used for: term assurance, IP, CI, without-profit annuities

VFA

  • Discount rate references the underlying asset pool
  • When rate rises: BEL & RA fall → CSM is unlocked (increased)
  • Profit absorbed by CSM, released gradually as CSM unwinds
  • Lower P&L volatility — rate movements buffered by CSM
  • Used for: unit-linked, with-profits (DPF)

Three transition approaches — FMF

Transition▼
  • FRA (Full Retrospective Approach) — preferred. Use if practicable. Apply IFRS 17 as if it always existed — full historical look-back to calculate the transitional CSM for all in-force business
  • MRA (Modified Retrospective Approach) — if FRA not practicable. Simplified using available reasonable information. Achieves closest result to FRA
  • FVA (Fair Value Approach) — last resort. Transitional CSM = fair value of policies minus fulfilment cashflows at transition date. No historical data required

The transitional CSM was a major challenge for insurers at the 1 Jan 2023 implementation date — required looking back through historical data to reconstruct what the initial CSM would have been for each policy.

Contract profitability categories

IFRS 17▼

Under IFRS 17, profitable and unprofitable contracts must not be offset. Each policy categorised as:

  • Loss-making at inception — loss recognised immediately (CSM cannot absorb it)
  • Profitable with no significant risk of making a loss — CSM positive, released over term
  • Any other profitable contracts — profitable at inception but may become loss-making over time; requires significant systems/process changes to monitor

This deliberate asymmetry (losses immediate; profits deferred) is a key feature of IFRS 17.
