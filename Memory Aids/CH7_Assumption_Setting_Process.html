A · Morbidity (Inception & Termination Rates)
Morbidity – Inception Rates
  • Collect morbidity experience data since last pricing or premium review date
  • Subdivide by homogeneous group: age, gender, occupation, region, duration from entry, underwriting method
  • For CI, further split by condition severity if reduced benefits apply to lower-severity cases (e.g. early-stage cancer)
  • For LTCI, split by care setting (home vs. residential vs. nursing)
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Carry out data checks on all extracts; reconcile policy data against policy admin system control reports
  • Investigate early claims and claims by notification date to detect anti-selective behaviour
  • Check for outlier/abnormal claims that may distort incidence rates
  • Ensure claims are correctly included in the investigation period
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Only lives insured within the investigation period should be included in exposed-to-risk figures
  • Adjust exposed-to-risk on a life-by-life basis where a life's exposure covers only part of the period (using dates of entry and exit)
  • Ensure claims and lapses in the period are correctly reflected so that crude incidence rates are not distorted
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Monitor actual claims experience against expected level for each homogeneous group
  • Consider sufficiency and credibility of own data; if data for certain groups are sparse, supplement with reinsurer, market or population data (adjusted for relevance)
  • For a small market (e.g. LTCI), credibility of own data may be low; more weight placed on external benchmarks
Sep 2020 Q3(iii,iv) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Revise best estimate incidence assumptions based on experience investigation results
  • Make allowance for future trends in incidence: diagnostic trends, changes in treatment protocols, introduction of new conditions, changes in claims eligibility criteria (e.g. ADL definitions for LTCI)
  • Consider whether the cause mix of claims is shifting (e.g. rise in mental health/stress for IP) and adjust inception rate projection accordingly
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Given uncertainty (e.g. new disease, limited LTCI data), add appropriate margins above best estimate for pricing/reserving
  • For a new or uncertain product, margins will be higher; reduce over time as credibility builds
  • Reinsurer or independent review can validate the assumptions; document all judgements
  • Assumptions should be approved by senior colleagues (e.g. Chief Actuary) and the process clearly documented
Apr 2019 Q1(v) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii) · Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
Morbidity – Termination / Recovery Rates
  • Collect claim duration data and cause-of-claim data to understand termination patterns
  • For IP: distinguish recovery (return to work), mortality in-claim, and withdrawal from claim; analyse by cause of claim and deferred period
  • For LTCI: recovery rates likely immaterial; focus on mortality in-claim as principal termination decrement
Sep 2024 Q3(i,iii)
  • Validate claim termination dates; check for duplicate or incomplete claim records
  • Reconcile claims in payment at start and end of period against reserves
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Compare actual termination/recovery rates against expected; investigate if cause mix has shifted (e.g. more mental health claims with lower recovery rates)
  • Assess whether claims management or rehabilitation interventions have materially affected observed recovery rates
Sep 2024 Q3(i,iii)
  • Adjust recovery rate assumptions for projected changes in cause mix and effectiveness of claims management
  • For LTCI: project impaired mortality trends; improving general mortality extends claim durations
  • Allowance for partial, proportional or rehabilitation benefits affects the net termination rate assumption
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(ii)
  • If cause-mix shift is uncertain (e.g. mental health trends), margins in termination rates should reflect that uncertainty
  • Document rationale for any departure from previous assumption; sign off at appropriate level
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii)
B · Mortality
Mortality (pre-claim & in-claim)
  • Investigate separately: mortality pre-claim (affects exposure), impaired mortality in-claim (affects claim termination)
  • For CI accelerated cover, death is a claim trigger; for standalone CI, death releases reserve — both require data collection
  • For LTCI, impaired mortality is the primary in-claim decrement
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Reconcile death claims against insured lives data; check for missing death notifications
  • Ensure deaths within the deferred period are correctly excluded from IP claim inception counts
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Compare actual deaths to expected under current basis; assess whether improving mortality trend is continuing
  • For LTCI: improving general mortality means more lives reaching care age AND longer durations in claim — both directions adverse
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Estimate future mortality improvement trends; in a well-developed country, continued improvement is likely
  • For LTCI, project both pre-claim mortality (more lives reaching care age) and in-claim impaired mortality (longer claim durations) separately
Apr 2021 Q3(ii,iii)
  • Mortality improvement uncertainty requires margins, particularly for long-term products (LTCI, IP) where small changes compound over many years
  • Guaranteed return-of-premium on death (LTCI) may be more costly than expected; review and add margin if experience has been adverse
Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
C · Lapse & Persistency
Lapse Rates
  • Collect lapse data by: duration in force, premium payment method, premium size, distribution channel, individual vs. group, product type
  • For CI: also analyse by whether premiums are guaranteed or reviewable, as this affects lapse incentive
  • For PMI: track non-renewal rates at annual renewal as the equivalent of lapse
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Check for duplicate lapse records; ensure lapses and surrenders are correctly distinguished
  • Verify lapse dates align with premium payment records
Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
  • Compare number of lapses in each duration band to the exposed-to-risk in that band to produce crude lapse rates by duration
  • A key focus for long-term business is duration from entry: determines the degree to which acquisition costs have been recouped prior to lapse
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Compare actual lapse rates against expected; investigate whether selective lapsing is occurring (healthy lives exiting, impaired lives remaining)
  • Assess whether distributor churning or adverse economic conditions are driving observed lapses above expected
  • Consider sufficiency: if portfolio is small, lapse data may lack credibility for fine sub-divisions
Sep 2020 Q3(iii,iv) · Apr 2022 Q1(i) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Set best estimate lapse rates allowing for expected economic conditions, competitive environment and product features (e.g. surrender penalties, payment holidays)
  • Allow for the lapse-rate–anti-selection interaction: higher lapse assumptions imply worse remaining pool, which feeds back into morbidity assumptions
  • Regular experience analysis on lapses helps ensure the loading is adequate
Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Apr 2022 Q1(ii)
  • In an economic downturn or high-inflation environment, add margins to lapse assumptions to reflect greater uncertainty
  • Commission clawback and surrender penalties reduce lapse risk; their presence should be reflected in the governance framework for the assumption
Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
D · Expenses
Expense Rates & Inflation
  • Divide expenses into: acquisition (initial), policy administration (renewal), claim assessment, claims management expenses
  • Collect actual expense data by product line and split fixed vs. variable components
  • Identify and separately record any one-off or non-recurring expenses to exclude from the ongoing basis
  • Commission is collected separately and excluded from the expense investigation proper
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2021 Q3(ii)
  • Carry out spot checks on expense data; reconcile total expenses to management accounts or audited figures
  • Ensure all data extracts used are subject to systematic checks (e.g. number of data items, total premiums per product line)
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Monitor actual expenses incurred against expected level, allowing for inflation since last analysis
  • Assess whether fixed costs are being adequately recovered given current policy volumes; smaller book = higher per-policy expense
  • Check whether expense loading method (by premium, by sum insured, fixed per policy, or combination) remains appropriate for the business mix
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2022 Q1(iii,iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(v)
  • Update expense assumptions for observed inflation; in a high-inflation environment, both fixed (rent, IT) and variable (staff salaries) components increase
  • Project future expense levels allowing for expected new business volumes: if volumes are lower than expected, per-policy loading must increase
  • Expense loading by sum insured may best reflect the risk profile for CI and IP in a higher socio-economic market segment
Sep 2024 Q1(i,v) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii)
  • Ensure expense loadings are sufficient to cover anticipated costs; higher margins needed if volumes are uncertain or inflation is elevated
  • Regular experience analysis on expenses helps ensure the loading remains adequate given lapse experience
  • For a new product or market, there is greater need to incorporate margins to reflect approximations and unknowns
Apr 2019 Q1(v) · Sep 2022 Q1(iv)
E · Claims Costs & Medical Inflation
Average Claim Amount & Claims Inflation
  • Collect average claim amounts by condition, treatment type, provider and region
  • For LTCI (indemnity basis): collect cost of care data separately by care setting; distinguish care cost inflation from general inflation
  • For PMI: track claim amounts at the level of individual procedures/treatments to detect protocol or tariff changes
Apr 2021 Q3(ii) · Apr 2022 Q2(i)
  • Identify large individual claims that may be outliers; assess whether they distort the average
  • Reconcile total claims paid to management accounts; check for double-counting or missing claims
Apr 2022 Q2(i) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Compare actual average claim amounts and total claims costs against expected; decompose into frequency and average cost movements
  • Investigate whether medical inflation, changes in treatment protocols, new procedures or changes in provider tariffs explain the deviation
  • Assess whether provider fee schedules have been renegotiated at higher rates
Apr 2022 Q2(i) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Set a claims inflation assumption that reflects the specific drivers of medical cost inflation (not just general CPI), including staff salaries, treatment protocols, new procedures and provider pricing power
  • Where a fixed fee schedule exists with providers, the inflation assumption may be lower and more predictable
  • For long-term products (IP, LTCI) with salary-linked benefits, allow separately for salary inflation as a driver of claim amount
Apr 2022 Q2(i,ii) · Apr 2021 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • In a high-inflation environment, add margins on the claims inflation assumption, particularly for products with guaranteed premiums where the insurer cannot reprice
  • Reviewable premiums reduce the need for embedded inflation margins; guaranteed premiums require larger ones
  • Document the rationale for the inflation assumption relative to market data and provider tariff evidence
Sep 2024 Q1(i,vi) · Apr 2021 Q2(ii)
F · Investment Return
Investment Return
  • Collect actual investment income received (after allowing for capital movements) split by investment class: dividends, interest, capital gains (realised and unrealised)
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Reconcile investment income to audited accounts; check market values of assets against independently sourced data (e.g. Bloomberg)
  • Check credit ratings and currency of assets against independent sources
Apr 2019 Q2(iii) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Monitor actual investment income against expected; note that for short-tail products (PMI) the investment income assumption is less material than for long-tail products (LTCI, IP)
  • Assess impact of changes in interest rates on the value of claims-in-payment reserves
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2024 Q3(i)
  • Investment return assumption must be consistent with the risk discount rate and expense inflation assumption
  • The risk-free rate should be consistent with current economic conditions; the risk discount rate is based on the insurer's required profit margin
  • In a high-inflation environment, consider whether Asset Liability Matching (inflation-linked bonds, swaps) is in place; if not, the investment return assumption carries more uncertainty
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Sep 2022 Q1(iv) · Sep 2024 Q1(i)
  • Best estimate investment return forms the base; the risk margin (Solvency II) uses a 6% cost-of-capital rate applied to projected non-hedgeable risks
  • If investment strategy has changed (e.g. in response to inflation), re-examine margins; document any departure from prior basis
Apr 2019 Q2(i,iii) · Sep 2019 Q1(viii)
G · Reinsurance Costs
Reinsurance Costs & Recoveries
  • Collect actual reinsurance premiums paid and recoveries received by product line and treaty
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Regular (e.g. quarterly or at each premium due date) review of reinsurance premiums and reinsured sum at risk
  • Check reinsurance recoverables and ensure any expected counterparty default has been allowed for
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) 		Not covered in provided material
  • Note: reinsurance is not part of the contractual arrangement between insurer and policyholder, so profit/loss on reinsurance is excluded from the premium review exercise itself
  • However, for reserving purposes, reinsurance recoverables must be assessed separately from the gross BEL
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
  • Reinsurers may provide advice on rates and market data to inform assumption setting, particularly for products with limited own-data credibility (e.g. LTCI)
  • Seek independent advice from reinsurers on whether they are also considering changes to reinsurance premium rates, as this affects the overall cost base
  • As own data credibility grows over time, the level of reinsurance may be reduced
Apr 2021 Q3(ii) · Sep 2020 Q3(iv) · Apr 2023 Q1(iv)
  • Counterparty default risk on reinsurance must be assessed and reflected in capital/reserves
  • Reinsurer may require higher margins for business accepted using a new underwriting tool until experience is built up
  • Document any reinsurance treaty changes and their impact on the net assumption basis
Apr 2022 Q3(ii) · Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
H · Overall Assumption-Setting Process & Governance
Homogeneous Groups & Model Points
  • Split existing policies into homogeneous groups by product type (e.g. standalone vs. accelerated CI; group vs. individual), pricing series and premium rate band
  • Within each group, select a set of model points to represent the existing policy profile, or assess on a policy-by-policy basis if data permits
Sep 2020 Q3(iii)
  • Check that business has been segmented appropriately at the homogeneous risk group level (Solvency II: by prescribed Lines of Business and further by currency)
  • Check that all parameters and assumptions have been entered correctly into models
Apr 2019 Q2(iii) · Sep 2019 Q2(iv)
  • Experience investigation should be carried out for each homogeneous group
  • If data for certain sub-groups are sparse, consider whether further subdivision is credible or whether groups should be merged
Sep 2020 Q3(iii,iv)
  • Results of experience investigations form the basis for revising all best estimate assumptions
  • Assess whether any deviation from expected arises from a single controllable item or from broad multi-assumption trends
  • Consider whether the experience exercise itself is sufficiently robust to rely upon before acting on results
Sep 2020 Q3(iv) · Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
  • All assumptions should be best estimate and consistent with the results of the experience investigations
  • Investment return, expense inflation and risk discount rate must be mutually consistent
  • Profit test each policy/model point by projecting cashflows forward from the review date (premiums, expenses, commission, claims, reserve contributions, capital requirements, interest, tax)
Sep 2020 Q3(iii) · Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
  • Governance: assumptions must be reviewed and approved at an appropriate level of seniority (e.g. Finance Director, Chief Actuary)
  • Experience investigation team makes recommendations; senior review follows; the process must be clearly documented
  • Review compliance with relevant professional and regulatory standards (e.g. TASs in the UK)
  • Consider commissioning an external independent review to demonstrate accuracy and transparency
  • Review issues and errors log; ensure all material issues are addressed with agreed follow-up actions
Sep 2019 Q2(iv) · Apr 2019 Q2(iii)
Profit Testing & Office Premium Build-up Not covered in provided material Not covered in provided material Not covered in provided material
  • Compare the calculated office premium against market pricing and competitor rates; assess whether the required profit margin can be achieved at a marketable price
  • Assess price elasticity by product line and business mix segment to understand the realistic range for the final premium
Sep 2024 Q1(vi)
  • Calculated premium = underlying risk premium + loading for: expenses, profit margin, commission, cost of capital, cost of reserves, reinsurance, margins, investment return offset, persistency adjustment, tax
  • Final office premium = calculated premium adjusted for market conditions, position in underwriting cycle, loss leaders/cross-subsidies, distribution channel, regulatory min/max levels
Sep 2024 Q1(vi)
  • If calculated premium cannot achieve acceptable profit for a product line, options are: sell as a loss leader; cease selling; substitute with more profitable product lines
  • Consider regulatory guidance on reviewability, materiality of changes and Treating Customers Fairly before implementing rate changes arising from the review
  • Consider precedent, policyholder reactions, press coverage and distributor reactions when determining the final action
