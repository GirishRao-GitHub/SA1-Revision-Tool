SA1 · CH17

Uses & Benefits of Reinsurance

Syllabus ref 4.3  ·  Types · Risk Control · Financial · Technical · Impact · Badging · Quiz · Mnemonics

Types of Reinsurance

Ch17 §1
Reinsurance is cession of risk from a direct insurer (cedant) to a reinsurer. The arrangement can be facultative (per risk) or treaty (bulk). The ceded proportion may be proportional or non-proportional.
ARRANGEMENT Facultative vs Treaty
  • Facultative — each risk individually assessed and reinsured separately. Used for unusual, large, or complex risks outside treaty terms (e.g. very high IP sum insured for a key employee).
  • Treaty — standing contract; many risks automatically covered under one arrangement. Cheaper, simpler, avoids adverse selection by reinsurer.
PROPORTIONAL Quota Share & Surplus
An agreed proportion of each risk is ceded — premium and claims shared in that ratio.
  • Quota share — same proportion of all risks ceded. Most common in health RI (e.g. new product entry, financial strain relief).
  • Surplus — retention fixed per risk; ceded proportion varies. Not applicable to short-term health insurance (lacks large/varying sums at risk).
Basis: Original terms (coinsurance) — reinsurer takes proportion of office premium · or · Risk premium — specific RI premium charged for the risk reinsured.
NON-PROPORTIONAL XL & Stop Loss
Claims above a defined retention are paid by the reinsurer. Normal for short-term / group health.
  • Excess of Loss (XL) — per risk (e.g. large PMI procedure), aggregate, or catastrophe basis.
  • Stop Loss — covers loss ratios between defined limits for a whole account over a period. Smooths overall results; can be expensive / unavailable.
FINANCIAL RI Financial Reinsurance
Used primarily to improve the cedant's financial / regulatory position rather than to transfer underwriting risk.
  • Crystallises future expected profits as current assets.
  • Much reduced under Solvency II (funding offset by liability to repay).
  • May still be effective in non-SII jurisdictions.

Key Terms

RETROCESSION
Reinsurance of reinsurance — the reinsurer cedes part of its own risk to another reinsurer.
CEDANT
The direct insurer that transfers (cedes) risk to the reinsurer under the reinsurance arrangement.
COMMISSION CLAWBACK
Treaty provision requiring the cedant to repay RI commission if a policy lapses before the reinsurer recoups its advance.
CONTRACT BOUNDARY (SII)
Point beyond which the insurer has no obligation to continue the policy; profits beyond this cannot feature on the SII balance sheet.
LAPSE RISK (RI)
Risk that policyholders cancel before the reinsurer recoups its initial financial assistance — taken wholly or partly by the reinsurer under financial RI.
FREE COVER LIMIT
Group scheme threshold below which individual members are accepted without underwriting evidence.

Control of Risks

Ch17 §2
Examiner angle: Risk-control questions often ask you to distinguish why a particular reinsurance structure is used. Link each scenario to the specific form of reinsurance.
2.1 Limitation of Exposure to Risk
▼
  • Uncertainty is the essence of insurance — claims from any period cannot be predicted accurately, regardless of statistical skill or experienced underwriters.
  • An insurer's appetite for reinsurance depends on: size, marketplace experience, free assets, portfolio size (credibility), and confidence in predictability of outcomes.
  • Reinsurance is considered where a loss (single event, related events, peril, geographic concentration) could materially affect results within a business line.
  • Reinsurance reduces the need for large margins in assumptions → allows more competitive premium rates.
2.2 Large Risks & Smoothing of Profit
▼
  • Long-term: CI and IP can produce large individual sums at risk — reinsured facultatively or via treaty. Very high IP sum insured (e.g. key employee) may fall outside standard treaty conditions → facultative.
  • LTCI: Pre-funded products build large funds — younger policyholders accumulate significant sums at risk.
  • Short-term (PMI): Expensive procedures (e.g. cancer treatment) → non-proportional (XL) reinsurance.
  • Capital insufficiency: If insufficient capital, surplus treaty reduces solvency requirements in line with the ceded proportion (subject to an upper limit), enabling the insurer to accept business it otherwise could not.
2.4 Group Schemes
▼
  • A large portfolio of group schemes is manageable provided reinsurance covers large individual sums at risk — on a surplus basis.
  • Group schemes are prone to accumulation of risk — requiring aggregate or catastrophe XL.
  • Triggering events: outbreak of infectious disease; workplace explosion (many simultaneous IP/CI claims); legal risk; group-wide screening programme.
  • For group medical insurance: proportional RI useful to improve capacity.
2.5 Concentration of Risks
▼
  • Must reinsure where there are a small number of large group schemes or a scheme with high concentration by occupation or geography.
  • Such schemes may be reinsured on a catastrophe basis or by quota share.
  • A small number of group schemes lacks diversification and is prone to concentration risk.
2.6 Control of Portfolio Risk
▼
  • Diversification: accumulation of health risks → unwelcome concentration → reinsurance spreads risk across the portfolio.
  • Account management: stop loss smooths year-on-year development of an immature portfolio (though may not be affordable).
  • New products: quota share with low initial retention, decreasing as insurer expertise grows.
  • Avoiding product concentration: reinsurer reduces cedant's exposure to a particular risk; may organise retrocession to further spread risk; may assist with launching new products.
RETROCESSION
The reinsurer cedes its own assumed risk onward — RI of RI.
STOP LOSS
Limits the cedant's loss ratio for a whole account between defined limits over a period.

Exam Q&A — Risk Control

What factors determine an insurer's appetite for reinsurance?▼
Size of the insurer; experience in the marketplace; available free assets; size of portfolio (credibility factor); and the degree to which the business outcome is felt to be predictable within bounds.
Why might group schemes require aggregate or catastrophe XL reinsurance?▼
Group schemes are prone to accumulation of risk — e.g. an infectious disease outbreak or workplace explosion could generate many simultaneous IP or CI claims; a legal risk or group-wide screening programme could trigger multiple CI claims. Standard per-risk XL would not protect against such correlated events.
How can reinsurance assist an insurer with insufficient capital?▼
Reinsurance (e.g. surplus treaty) reduces solvency requirements for a line of business in line with the ceded proportion (subject to an upper limit), enabling the insurer to write business it could not otherwise accept.
What is retrocession and why might a reinsurer use it?▼
Retrocession is reinsurance of reinsurance — the reinsurer cedes its own assumed risk to another reinsurer. It is used to diversify the spread of risks across the reinsurer's portfolio, especially when helping a cedant avoid product-concentration risk.

Financial Reinsurance

Ch17 §3
Key exam point: Financial RI is much reduced under Solvency II because any reinsurer funding is offset by the liability to repay it — net effect on available capital is approximately zero.
3.1 Financing New Business Strain
▼
  • Under proportional RI, solvency requirements are normally reduced in line with the proportion ceded (subject to an upper limit). Quota share is the normal method.
  • Where cash outflow substantially exceeds premium income in early stages, RI commission can 'factor' predicted future surplus streams — lending now against future premiums net of expenses and claims.
  • The reinsurer takes the lapse risk wholly or in part. Many reinsurers are unwilling to accept this.
  • If a policy lapses, the insurer loses the chance to recoup early expenditure and cannot repay the reinsurer → commission clawback arrangements handle this in the treaty.
3.2 Solvency — Solvency II Impact
▼
  • Financial RI historically crystallised future expected profits on the regulatory balance sheet. Under SII this is much reduced — any reinsurer funding is offset by the liability to repay, leaving the net position approximately unchanged.
  • It may be possible to arrange credit for profits beyond the contract boundary — profits beyond this boundary are excluded from the SII balance sheet. Selling these to the reinsurer increases assets without changing regulatory liabilities.
  • Still effective in non-SII jurisdictions.
  • Traditional RI reduces the SCR: morbidity risk is reduced (counterparty default risk increases); both SCR and risk margin decrease; volatility of the balance sheet reduces.
3.3 Financing Takeovers
▼
  • Financial assistance from reinsurers can also facilitate acquisition of an insurance company.
  • Reinsurer advances funds in anticipation of future profit from the acquired portfolio; pays an initial commission; in return takes a proportion of future surpluses for the duration of the arrangement. Reinsurer again takes the lapse risk.
  • Essentially the same mechanism as financing new business strain — applied to a takeover context.
3.4 Securitisation
▼
  • Insurance risk is converted into financial instruments accessible to the capital markets — moving one step towards the investment market.
  • Potential investors are likely to have specialist knowledge.
  • Effectiveness also much reduced under SII (sells future profits).

Exam Q&A — Financial RI

How can reinsurance assist with new business strain?▼
Under proportional RI, solvency requirements are reduced in line with the proportion ceded. Additionally, reinsurance commission can 'factor' predicted future surplus streams — effectively lending against future premiums net of expenses and claims — funding initial new business strain. The reinsurer takes the lapse risk in whole or in part.
Why is financial RI much reduced under Solvency II?▼
Under SII the realistic balance sheet is little changed by financial RI because any funding provided by the reinsurer must be offset by the liability to repay it. The net effect on available capital is approximately zero.
How can traditional RI reduce a company's Solvency II SCR?▼
As risk is transferred to the reinsurer, morbidity risk in the insurer's portfolio decreases (though counterparty default risk increases). This reduces both the SCR and the risk margin, and also reduces the volatility of the realistic balance sheet.

Technical Assistance

Ch17 §4
Technical assistance is most valuable when entering new products, new territories, or unfamiliar risk classes. The reinsurer's alignment of interest (via risk share) motivates quality advice.
4.1 Product Development & Data Provision
▼
  • New products, markets and techniques: reinsurer has established expertise; invaluable in boosting competence and creating a sound platform.
  • Data access: reinsurer pools data across many cedants and territories; high-level aggregate results available on a 'no-names basis'. Adjustments needed for: mix of business, UW level, product terms, claims conditions, geography, credibility, time period, medical inflation, trends, currency, etc.
  • Sharing of risk: aligns objectives between cedant and reinsurer → ensures quality of advice provided.
  • Training: more important in health than life because health experience emerges quickly. Reinsurers offer training in credibility pricing, IBNR, expense bases.
  • Assessment of future trends: reinsurer can form views on future cost/incidence trends via research and data aggregation — enables underwriting of risks that might otherwise be unacceptable.
4.2 Risk Management — Underwriting & Claims
▼
  • Underwriting manual covers: acceptance at standard rates; sums insured limits requiring further evidence (questionnaires, bloods, medicals); exclusions; min/max premiums & benefits; loadings for unusual risks; referral procedures; detailed medical section.
  • Increasingly reinsurers audit cedant UW practice rather than accept UW risk — whether they honour claims after a cedant mistake depends on relationship, frequency, and treaty wording.
  • Claims control more critical in health than life/GI because: policyholders may make false declarations; multiple claims possible during contract; some claims dubious; policy conditions complex; long-term products tie the insurer at fixed premium rates.
  • Policy wording review: shortfalls/defects can result in out-of-scope claims; reinsurers assist in reviewing policy documents, application forms and marketing literature.
UNDERWRITING MANUAL
Rules and guidelines from the reinsurer covering acceptance, evidence requirements, exclusions, benefit limits, unusual risk loadings and a medical reference section.
CLAIMS CONTROL
Initial assessment, approval, monitoring and management of claims. Critical in health RI because claims can recur and moral hazard is higher than in life insurance.
4.3 Overseas Developments
▼
  • All domestic technical assistance is even more important in overseas markets — understanding the interplay between state provision and private provision is crucial.
  • Reinsurer provides: copies of local policy documents, UW & claims forms (with translations); key local contacts (distributors, TPAs, hospitals, consultants); local risk statistics on claims incidence and amounts; experience analysis results.
  • Legal/regulatory advice on local custom and practice — reinsurer provides a guide; professional local legal expertise also necessary.
4.4 IT Software
▼
  • IT systems allow automated proposal processing by junior staff — up to 80% of cases accepted consistently (can be 50% or less for complex health products).
  • Claims acceptance software: checks original proposal information against claim document for inconsistencies.
  • Reserve-setting systems: record standard amounts to PMI case estimates updated as more data arrives.
  • Health pricing is more complex than life — multi-state models required.
  • Internet-hosted systems: insurer accesses the latest version online; reinsurer assists remotely. Risk: confidentiality of data moving between sites.
  • Products change (legislation, regulation, technology) → software must be easily modifiable; internet hosting ensures all users instantly access the latest version.

Exam Q&A — Technical Assistance

Why is claims control more important in health insurance than in life or general insurance?▼
In life insurance the claim event (death) is one-off and easily verified. In GI, short-term policies allow premium adjustment at renewal. In health: policyholders may make false declarations; multiple claims are possible during the contract; some claims have dubious validity; policy conditions are complex; and for long-term products the insurer is bound at fixed premium rates for many years.
What would an underwriting manual provided by a reinsurer contain?▼
Rules on acceptance at standard rates without further medical evidence; sums insured limits requiring further evidence (supplementary questionnaires, blood tests, medical examinations); information required at each underwriting stage; exclusions; minimum and maximum premiums and benefits; acceptance of unusual risks (loadings for occupations, pastimes, medical conditions); referrals to senior underwriting staff and medical practitioners; and a detailed medical section.
What adjustments might be needed when using reinsurer data?▼
Mix of business (product type, customer mix); level of underwriting; product terms and conditions; claims conditions and control; geography; credibility and time period; effects of medical advances; policy sizes; target market; sales channels; reinsurance arrangements; competitiveness; timing of payments; inflation effects on sums insured; trends in mortality and morbidity; currency.

Impact of Reinsurance & Badging

Ch17 §5–6
§5.1 Tax Arbitrage
Reinsurance is theoretically self-balancing (net financial gain = zero). In practice, differing tax treatment of insurer and reinsurer can improve the combined after-tax position — especially via quota share in tax-efficient territories. Risk: tax rules may change.
§5.2 Regulatory (Capital) Arbitrage
Reinsurer may hold less capital for the same unit of risk under its regulatory regime. Lower cost of capital is shared with the cedant via cheaper RI premiums or higher RI commission. Also called capital arbitrage.
§5.3 New Markets
Both parties benefit: insurer gains in-force experience in a market it could not enter alone; reinsurer gains a quota share of a potentially profitable tranche, diversification, and enhanced credibility as a reinsurer in that class.
§5.4 Consultancy Fees vs Risk Share
Reinsurers earn via (1) risk share of profitable business or (2) consultancy fees. For new products, risk share is preferred — aligns incentives when volume and profitability are uncertain. Fees more justified where reinsurer work is excessive relative to risk share taken. In some markets fees are rare.
§5.5 Predictability of Results
Health results are less predictable than life results. Reinsurance smooths outcomes. The 'peace of mind' benefit to a smaller insurer facing volatile health contracts justifies the cost — even if the pure financial gain favours the reinsurer.
§5.6 Difference of Interests
Under a high quota share: insurer (bears little of each claim) wants to pay quickly; reinsurer (bears most) wants careful consideration. Insurer → quantity of business; Reinsurer → quality of business. Relationship is ongoing — conflicts are not in either party's interest.

Badging — An Alternative to Reinsurance

Ch17 §6
Badging is not true reinsurance. Under badging, the selling insurer assumes no insurance risk. Under reinsurance, the issuing company takes on risk first and then cedes part.
DEFINITION
An arrangement where the insurer provides a policy under its own brand, but a specialist health insurer issues the policy and undertakes all processing, claims administration, and payments. All insurance risk rests with the specialist insurer.
EXAMPLE
Popular Insurance (large brand, no health expertise) badges with Healthwise (small specialist). Popular provides market presence; Healthwise provides health expertise. Product carries Popular's brand name. Payment via profit-sharing arrangement.
RISKS OF BADGING
Poor service by the specialist damages the branded insurer's reputation. Regulatory risk — regulations may require the actual product provider's name to appear in literature, reducing the branding benefit.

Exam Q&A — Impact & Badging

What is capital arbitrage in the context of reinsurance?▼
Capital arbitrage occurs when the reinsurer is required to hold less capital than the insurer for a given unit of risk under their respective regulatory regimes. The lower cost of capital in the reinsurer's hands is shared with the insurer via cheaper reinsurance premiums or higher commission rates.
Describe the difference of interests between a direct insurer and its reinsurer where a high quota share has been ceded.▼
The direct insurer, bearing relatively little of each claim, wants to pay claims quickly with minimum fuss. The reinsurer, bearing the majority of each claim, requires very careful consideration of each claim. More broadly, the insurer prioritises quantity of business; the reinsurer prioritises quality of business.
What is badging and how does it differ from reinsurance?▼
Badging is an arrangement where the selling insurer provides a policy to the customer under its own brand, but a specialist insurer issues the policy and handles all administration, claims and payments. Unlike reinsurance, the selling insurer takes on no insurance risk — all risk is borne by the specialist from the outset. Under reinsurance, the issuing company takes on the risk first and then partly or totally cedes it to the reinsurer.
In what circumstances would a reinsurer charge consultancy fees rather than take a risk share?▼
Consultancy fees are more justified where the work undertaken by the reinsurer is excessive relative to the risk share taken. For new products, a risk share is preferred (aligning incentives; neither party knows future volumes or profitability). In some markets, fees to the reinsurer are rare.
