Phase 1 · Run the investigation (DANCE)
D Data collection & cleaning Gather relevant records and check quality. Fix duplicates, missing values, late-reported events (IBNR/IBNER). Choose investigation period — recent enough to be relevant, long enough for credibility. Mortality: death registrations, policy termination records Morbidity: claim inception records, medical evidence files Claim termination: recovery notices, claim closure dates, cause of termination Lapses: policy cancellation records, premium cessation dates
A Analyse into homogeneous cells Subdivide data so like is compared with like. Each cell should contain policyholders whose expected rate is genuinely similar. Mortality: age, gender, policy duration, smoker status, care level (INLTC) Morbidity: cause of claim, age, occupation class, deferred period, benefit period Claim termination: cause of claim, duration on claim, age at claim, benefit type Lapses: policy year, premium size, distribution channel, product type, payment method
N Normalise — calculate exposed-to-risk Count only the time each life was actually exposed to the decrement in question. Adjust for new entrants, partial years, exits for other reasons. Correct contaminated cells. Mortality: lives exposed to death risk; INLTC L1: exclude time spent at L2 care level Morbidity: healthy in-force lives exposed to claim inception; exclude those already on claim Claim termination: open claims exposed to recovery or death; split recovery ETR from death ETR Lapses: in-force policies exposed to voluntary lapse; exclude deaths, maturities, surrenders
C Compare actual vs expected (A/E) Choose a benchmark — standard industry table or original pricing basis. Compute A/E ratio per cell. Identify cells where experience deviates materially. Consider credibility of thin cells. Mortality: CMI INLTC IL series, CMI assured lives tables, or pricing basis qx Morbidity: CMIR inception rates, industry PHI tables, or pricing incidence assumption Claim termination: CMIR recovery rates / IPTS tables; separate recovery from death termination Lapses: pricing lapse rates; industry surveys if available; compare by policy year shape
E Extrapolate trends Look at own data over a longer window and cross-reference industry/population sources. Identify persistent trends expected to continue into the future. Mortality: mortality improvements, care pathway changes (faster discharge), ageing population Morbidity: rising mental health claims, changed policy definitions, better diagnostics Claim termination: improved rehabilitation, stricter claims management, medical advances Lapses: economic downturns, competitor pricing, changes in distribution or customer profile
Phase 2 · Set the assumption (CPM)
C Calibrate the basis Derive best-estimate rates from A/E analysis. Smooth or graduate sparse cells using credibility methods or graduation. For novel products, use analogous data with explicit adjustments. Mortality: INLTC L1 contamination fix — use early durations only or track care address changes Morbidity: if new product, borrow from similar benefit/occupation class with adjustment factor Claim termination: set separate recovery and mortality rates within claim; use claim duration shape Lapses: set rates by policy year; check select vs ultimate pattern; adjust for new channels
P Project future rates Apply improvement or deterioration factors identified in E. Allow for anticipated changes in underwriting, products, or external environment. Check consistency with wider industry projections. Mortality: CMI improvement model; allow for future care setting shifts Morbidity: project mental health trend; allow for any definition tightening planned Claim termination: project impact of new rehabilitation programmes on recovery rates Lapses: project economic scenario impact; allow for upcoming product redesign
M Margin for uncertainty + Monitor Add prudent margin — direction depends on which way the risk bites. State and document the margin. Then commit to monitoring emerging experience and updating the basis. Mortality (annuity): assume lighter mortality → longer payments → higher reserve Morbidity: assume higher inception rates and slower recovery → more / longer claims Claim termination: assume slower recovery → claims stay open longer → higher reserve Lapses: direction depends — high lapses hurt if profitable, low lapses hurt if unprofitable
🔄 Monitor & update — always earns marks
For any experience type, the answer is incomplete without mentioning that emerging experience will be monitored closely and the basis updated as it develops — especially for novel or uncertain products.
