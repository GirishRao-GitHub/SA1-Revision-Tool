Background & Scope
What is Solvency II?
📋 Background
  • Solvency II is a risk-based, harmonised EU-wide regulatory framework for insurance firms, focused on adequate protection of policyholders and beneficiaries.
  • Operative from 1 January 2016, replacing the patchwork of national Solvency I regimes across EU member states. Greater harmonisation was intended to increase competition.
  • Applies to all EU insurance and reinsurance companies with gross premium income > €5m or gross technical provisions > €25m.
  • EIOPA (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority) provides technical advice to the European Commission for Solvency II development.
  • Transitional arrangements are available for some aspects (e.g. technical provisions, risk-free rates) for up to 16 years.
  • Although an EU framework, Solvency II has influenced regulation in many non-EU countries — including the Indian market's move toward Solvency II-style RBC.
  • In 2021, the UK PRA began consulting on potential changes to how Solvency II is applied in the UK (the 'Solvency UK' project), particularly the matching adjustment.
Three-Pillar Framework
P1
Quantitative Requirements
Pillar 1 — Capital & Valuation
  • Sets minimum capital requirements (SCR and MCR)
  • Specifies market-consistent valuation methodologies for assets and liabilities
  • Two capital thresholds: SCR (first intervention) and MCR (ultimate intervention)
  • SCR: standard formula or approved internal model
  • Supervisors may impose a capital add-on
P2
Qualitative & Supervisory
Pillar 2 — Governance & Risk
  • Systems of governance and risk management
  • Four key functions: risk management, actuarial, compliance, internal audit
  • Each insurer must conduct an ORSA (Own Risk and Solvency Assessment)
  • Supervisory review process
  • Capital add-on (added to SCR) in exceptional cases
P3
Reporting & Disclosure
Pillar 3 — Transparency
  • Private supervisory reporting: RSR + QRT (annual; MCR/SCR subset quarterly)
  • Public disclosure: annual SFCR (Solvency and Financial Condition Report)
  • Market discipline from public disclosures adds an extra layer of confidence
  • ORSA is both a Pillar 2 process and a Pillar 3 report to the regulator
Exam tip: Pillars 2 and 3 are sometimes referred to together as "Pillar 5" due to their close synergies — the ORSA from Pillar 2 is also one of the defined Pillar 3 reports submitted to the regulator. [SA1 Ch11 — IFoA Apr 2020 Q1(i)]

🌍 Group Supervision
  • Insurance groups supervised by a group supervisor in the home country, co-operating with other national supervisors — avoiding double counting of capital.
  • Double use of capital: e.g. regulated entities making subordinated loans to each other. Double leverage: parent raises debt to fund subsidiary, inflating that subsidiary's capital position. Solvency II group supervision addresses both.
  • Each insurance group must cover its overall group SCR (with diversification) AND its group solvency floor (= sum of all subsidiaries' MCRs).
  • Equivalence for third countries: Full (indefinite) — Bermuda, Switzerland; Provisional (10 years) — USA, Australia, Japan, Brazil, Canada, Mexico.
Pillar 1 · Technical Provisions
Valuation of Assets & Technical Provisions
Technical provisions (TPs) should represent the amount an insurer would pay to transfer its obligations immediately to another insurer — a market-consistent, transfer-value concept. TPs = BEL + Risk Margin.
📦 Asset Valuation — Three Components
INVESTMENTS
  • Valued at market value using quoted market prices in active markets
  • If no active market → mark-to-model (market-consistent)
  • Significant change from Solvency I where book values were often used
RI RECOVERIES
  • Shown as an asset (not a reduction in gross liabilities)
  • Adjusted for best estimate of expected losses due to reinsurer default
  • Change from previous regimes where netting was common
PARTICIPATIONS
  • Companies where insurer owns ≥20% voting rights
  • Valued using market-consistent approach
  • Mainly arise in groups or where outsourcing subsidiaries exist
Prudent person principle: Assets must be appropriate for the nature and term of liabilities; chosen in best interests of policyholders; derivatives only for risk reduction or efficient portfolio management; assets properly diversified by class, issuer, geography.

🔵 Best Estimate Liability (BEL)
BEL = PV [ Future cashflows (benefits + expenses − premiums) ] using risk-free yield curve
  • No prudential margins — all assumptions must be best estimate.
  • Ideally calculated policy-by-policy; grouped model points permitted with validation.
  • BEL can be negative (no floor of zero) — e.g. where PV future premiums > PV future outgo.
  • Contract boundary: Future premiums only included up to the point the insurer can unilaterally terminate, refuse premiums, or reprice to fully reflect risks. For long-term health = maturity date; for annually renewable PMI = end of policy year.
  • Allowance for all expected decrements and policyholder actions including lapses — and how policyholder behaviour varies with circumstances (e.g. option in-the-money, financial position of company).
  • Expenses: both overheads and directly attributable expenses included; no closure reserve required.
  • For guarantees and options: stochastic / market-consistent simulation may be required.
📉 Risk-Free Discount Rates
  • Published monthly by EIOPA for each currency — insurers cannot set their own discount rate.
  • Based on swap rates (where sufficiently deep/liquid) or government bond rates, adjusted downward for counterparty default credit risk.
  • Extrapolated for longer maturities using the Smith-Wilson method to a long-term equilibrium rate.
  • EIOPA publishes curves for 53 countries — including India, China, Australia, USA.
MATCHING ADJUSTMENT (MA)
  • For long-term predictable liabilities where insurer holds matching assets to maturity
  • MA = Asset spread − Fundamental spread (FS = allowance for retained credit risk, published by EIOPA)
  • Assets must be ring-fenced, bond-like; liability risks relate to longevity and expenses only (no surrender risk)
  • Requires regulator approval; losing eligibility removes right to use MA for 24 months (unless compliance restored within 2 months)
  • Typically used for annuity business
VOLATILITY ADJUSTMENT (VA)
  • For liabilities not eligible for the matching adjustment
  • Purpose: reduce risk of forced asset sales in extreme bond spread movements
  • Based on spreads on a representative portfolio for each currency/national insurance market
  • Published by EIOPA alongside risk-free curves
  • Subject to certain risk management requirements (e.g. liquidity plan, sensitivity analysis) and prior supervisory approval in some member states
🟢 Risk Margin (RM)
The Risk Margin is the addition to the BEL that brings TPs to the transfer value — the amount another insurer would require to take on the BEL. It compensates for the risk that future experience is worse than best estimate, and for the cost of holding regulatory capital against this.
  • Calculated using the cost of capital method.
  • Non-hedgeable risks covered: all insurance risk, reinsurance credit risk, operational risk, and residual market risk (where liability duration > available risk-free asset duration).
  • Cost of capital rate: 6% per annum (fixed under Solvency II).
RM = Σ [ (SCR_non-hedgeable at time t) × 6% ] discounted at risk-free rates
Three steps:
  • Step 1: Project future non-hedgeable SCR at each future year-end during run-off
  • Step 2: Multiply each projected SCR by 6% p.a.
  • Step 3: Discount the resulting amounts at risk-free rates to give the RM
Simplified approaches:
  • Select a driver with approximately linear relationship to required capital (e.g. policy count for expense risk; sum at risk for mortality/morbidity; total reserves for operational risk)
  • Project capital as same percentage of projected driver as at t=0
  • RM can be reduced for diversification between lines up to legal entity level
Worked example: SCR = £10m, Sum at Risk = £100m → SCR = 10% of SAR
SCR at t=1 (SAR=£90m) = £9m; t=2 (SAR=£85m) = £8.5m; t=3 = £8.2m; t=4 = £8m; t=5 = £0
[IAI Nov 2023 Q1v]
Pillar 1 · Capital Requirements
SCR, MCR & Supervisory Intervention
🎯 SCR — Solvency Capital Requirement
  • A Value at Risk measure based on a 99.5% confidence interval of the variation over one year of basic own funds (assets − TPs).
  • Covers: non-life underwriting risk; life underwriting risk; health underwriting risk; market risk; counterparty default risk; operational risk.
  • Can be calculated using the standard formula (prescribed stress tests + correlation matrices) or a company-specific internal model (approved by regulator).
  • A partial internal model can combine both approaches for different risk modules.
  • Undertaking Specific Parameters (USPs) can replace prescribed parameters in underwriting risk modules (with regulatory approval) if they better reflect the company's risk profile.
  • Firms using standard formula must justify it is appropriate; regulator can compel use of an internal model if it is not.
  • Regulator can impose a capital add-on to the SCR in exceptional circumstances.
Important: The SCR net asset value is calculated as assets − BEL (not assets − TPs) to avoid circularity — the RM calculation itself requires the SCR, so only BEL is used. [SA1 Ch12 Exam QA]

MARKET RISK MODULE
  • Sub-modules: equity, property, interest rate, credit spread, currency, concentration
  • Interest rate risk: stress on risk-free yield curve up and down
  • Spread risk: credit spreads widening on corporate bonds and other assets
  • Concentration risk: excessive exposure to a single counterparty or issuer
  • Dynamic hedging is not permitted in standard formula (may be allowed in internal model)
COUNTERPARTY DEFAULT RISK MODULE
  • Type 1 exposures: May not be diversified; counterparty likely to be rated — e.g. derivatives, reinsurance, cash deposits at banks
  • Type 2 exposures: Usually diversified; counterparty unlikely to be rated — e.g. receivables from intermediaries and policyholders
  • Risk mitigation techniques recognised in SCR provided basis risk is immaterial
HEALTH INSURANCE RISK — THREE COMPONENTS
SLT Health
  • Disability/morbidity: 35% increase in disability rates for Year 1 + permanent 25% increase thereafter
  • 20% permanent decrease in disability recovery rates
  • Also: mortality, longevity, lapse, expense, revision risks
Non-SLT Health
  • Volume measure × standard deviation × adjustment factor
  • Premium risk + reserve risk + lapse risk
  • Applies to PMI and similar short-term products
CAT Risk
  • Extreme events: epidemics, pandemics, climate change, anti-microbial resistance
  • Standardised scenario-based approach

LIFE UNDERWRITING RISK
  • Applies to life insurance obligations (not health SLT/Non-SLT)
  • Sub-risks: mortality, longevity, disability/morbidity, lapse, expense, revision, CAT
  • For SA1: accelerated CI (attached to life policy) likely classified as a life insurance obligation here
  • Correlation with health module: 25%
NON-LIFE UNDERWRITING RISK
  • Does not overlap with health Non-SLT (correlation between health and non-life = 0%)
  • Premium risk, reserve risk, lapse risk, CAT risk (natural catastrophe scenarios)
OPERATIONAL RISK MODULE
  • Relatively simple formula based on percentages of earned premiums and technical provisions
  • Added to BSCR with no diversification credit — no correlation matrix reduction
  • Plus two further adjustments to BSCR: loss absorbing capacity of TPs and deferred taxes (Adj)
SCR = BSCR + SCR_op + Adj
Where BSCR combines market, default, life, health, non-life modules via correlation matrix

🔬 Internal Model — Six Approval Tests
#TestKey requirements
01Use TestModel widely used throughout business; significant role in governance, risk management, decision-making, capital allocation. Most challenging to demonstrate — requires evidence of embeddedness and risk culture.
02Statistical QualityMinimum quality standards for assumptions and data (accurate, complete, appropriate, updated annually); probability distribution forecasting; expert judgment; materiality; aggregation methods.
03CalibrationSCR derived must be equivalent to VaR at 99.5% confidence over one year. Alternative confidence levels/time periods permitted if equivalent policyholder protection is demonstrated.
04P&L AttributionDemonstrate how the risk categorisation in the model will be used to explain causes and sources of actual profits and losses.
05ValidationFully validated by the company; regular control cycle review including testing against emerging experience, sensitivity testing, independent reviews.
06DocumentationDesign and operational aspects clearly and thoroughly documented — including theory, assumptions, and circumstances under which the model does not work effectively.
Key challenges: Limited historical data for calibrating extreme morbidity events; setting dependency structures / correlations under extreme conditions; non-linearity and non-separability of risks (e.g. options that bite at a threshold interest rate; risks correlated under stress but not normally). Once approved, cannot revert to standard formula without regulatory approval.
🔴 MCR — Minimum Capital Requirement
  • A simple factor-based linear formula targeted at VaR with 85% confidence over one year.
  • For SLT health/life: based on technical provisions and capital at risk (on death or disability), multiplied by specified factors that vary by product type (with-profits, unit-linked, without-profits).
  • For Non-SLT health/non-life: based on technical provisions and written premiums, with factors varying by product type.
  • Calculated and reported quarterly.
Floor: MCR ≥ 25% of SCR
Cap: MCR ≤ 45% of SCR
Absolute minimum: €3.7m (long-term) / €2.5m (short-term)
The MCR floor/cap (25–45% of SCR) may bite for a significant number of health and care insurance companies — particularly smaller ones where the formula-based MCR would otherwise fall below 25% of the SCR.

✅
COMPLIANT
Capital ≥ SCR
  • Normal operations; no supervisory action required
  • ORSA may still prompt internal capital management actions
⚠️
FIRST INTERVENTION
Capital falls below SCR
  • Immediate notification to regulator (also if breach likely within 3 months)
  • Submit a recovery plan to regulator within 2 months
  • Rectify breach within 6 months (extendable by 3 months; further extension in exceptional market falls)
  • Remedies: increase eligible own funds OR reduce risk profile → lower SCR
🔴
ULTIMATE INTERVENTION
Capital falls below MCR
  • Immediate notification to regulator (also if breach likely within 3 months)
  • Submit a short-term finance scheme within 1 month
  • Rectify breach within 3 months
  • Regulator may restrict or prohibit disposal of assets
  • If scheme fails: authorisation withdrawn — no more business

Pillar 1 · Quality of Capital
Own Funds & Tiering
💰 Own Funds — Definition and Split
  • Own funds = assets in excess of technical provisions + any subordinated liabilities.
  • Basic own funds: Capital that already exists within the insurer — broadly assets minus TPs plus subordinated liabilities.
  • Ancillary own funds: Capital that may be called upon in adverse circumstances but does not currently exist within the insurer (e.g. unpaid share capital). Requires prior supervisory approval before it can be recognised.
🏆 Tiering Framework
Capital is tiered based on two properties: loss absorbency (ability to absorb losses as a going concern) and permanence (length of time capital is available). Tier 1 is highest quality on both dimensions.
Tier
Characteristics
Examples
TIER 1
Highest quality. Immediately available to absorb losses; most permanent; ranks behind all policyholders and creditors in wind-up; no obligation to pay dividends/interest
Paid-up ordinary share capital; retained earnings
TIER 2
Intermediate quality. Some limitations on permanence or immediate loss absorbency; subordinated but with some restrictions (e.g. fixed coupon payments)
Subordinated liabilities with longer maturities; some preference shares
TIER 3
Lowest quality. Least permanent; limited loss absorbency; e.g. shorter-dated subordinated debt; incentives to redeem
Subordinated debt with shorter maturity; some deferred tax assets
Tiering Criteria (relates to loss absorbency then permanence):
Loss absorbency criteria:
  • Whether paid up or not
  • Whether immediately available to absorb losses
  • Ranking in a wind-up (subordination)
  • Extent of any obligation to pay dividends/interest
Permanence criteria:
  • Its outstanding term (maturity)
  • Existence of any incentives to redeem the capital
📏 Capital Quality Restrictions
SCR COVERAGE REQUIREMENTS
  • At least 50% Tier 1
  • Less than 15% Tier 3
  • Tier 2 fills the remainder
MCR COVERAGE REQUIREMENTS
  • At least 80% Tier 1
  • No Tier 3 allowed
  • Tier 2 fills the remainder (up to 20%)
Exam memory hook: MCR needs more quality capital — 80% T1 vs 50% T1 for SCR; and absolutely no T3 for MCR. The MCR is the point of ultimate intervention so the capital quality requirements are stricter.
📊 Data Quality Standards
  • EIOPA guidance requires data used for technical provisions (and across Pillar 1) to be accurate, complete and appropriate, with data sets updated at least annually.
  • Good data quality enables: wider range of methodologies; more reliable validation; better comparisons over time and against market data.
  • Data quality requirements must be applied consistently across Pillar 1 — for both TPs (using standard formula or internal model) and the SCR calculation.
Governance, Risk Management & Reporting
Pillars 2 & 3
🏛️ Four Key Functions (Pillar 2)
RISK MANAGEMENT FUNCTION
  • Manages the risk management framework
  • If an internal model is used: responsible for detailed implementation of the model (not the actuarial function)
ACTUARIAL FUNCTION
  • Coordinate calculation of technical provisions
  • Ensure appropriateness of methodologies and assumptions
  • Assess sufficiency and quality of data for TPs
  • Compare best estimates against experience
  • Express opinion on overall underwriting policy
  • Express opinion on adequacy of reinsurance arrangements
  • Contribute to effective risk management system
COMPLIANCE FUNCTION
  • Monitor compliance with laws, regulations and internal policies
  • Must be segregated from internal audit function
INTERNAL AUDIT FUNCTION
  • Independent review of all functions and processes
  • Must not assume responsibility for any other key function — strict segregation
Note: The Board retains overall responsibility for ongoing compliance with Solvency II. Persons in the internal audit function cannot assume responsibility for other functions.
🔍 ORSA — Own Risk and Solvency Assessment
Defined by EIOPA as "the entirety of processes and procedures employed to identify, assess, monitor, manage and report the short- and long-term risks an insurance undertaking faces or may face, and to determine the own funds necessary to ensure overall solvency needs are met at all times."
STEP 01
Risk Identification
Identify all risks including qualitative risks not assessed under Pillar 1 (e.g. reputational risk)
STEP 02
Solvency Assessment
Quantify ability to continue meeting MCR and SCR over business planning horizon (3–5 years), including new business
STEP 03
Embeddedness
Evidence that ORSA is genuinely used by senior management in strategic decisions — not a regulatory tickbox
STEP 04
Capital Add-On
ORSA is one factor the supervisor considers when deciding whether to impose a capital add-on to the SCR
  • Does not have to be at a prescribed confidence level — the company sets a level appropriate to its risk appetite or target credit rating.
  • Relatively little prescriptive guidance allows companies to build a process that genuinely fits their business and integrates with strategic planning.
  • Capital add-ons added to the SCR; used only in exceptional circumstances.
📄 Pillar 3 Reporting Requirements
PRIVATE · SUPERVISORY
RSR + QRT
  • RSR (Regular Supervisory Report) — qualitative + quantitative (QRTs); pre-defined sections including ORSA
  • QRT (Quantitative Reporting Templates) — balance sheet; premiums/claims/expenses by LoB; own funds; SCR/MCR; assets; TPs; reinsurance; group data
  • Full QRT: annually
  • MCR/SCR subset of QRT: quarterly
  • Summary (material changes) RSR acceptable under certain conditions
PUBLIC · MARKET DISCIPLINE
SFCR
  • SFCR (Solvency and Financial Condition Report) — public annual disclosure
  • Contains extracts from QRT and qualitative information from RSR
  • Certain items may be omitted if demonstrably confidential
  • Must be published and submitted to regulator within 3–4 months of year-end
  • Market discipline: enables capital markets, rating agencies, analysts to form informed views on capital and risk management
Why public disclosure? The discipline of public disclosures adds an additional layer of confidence in the regime. Other stakeholders (capital markets, rating agencies) can form an informed view — and a company's desire to maintain its share price or credit rating reinforces supervisory oversight. [SA1 Ch11 Exam QA]
💼 Impact on Business Culture and Strategy
  • Solvency II is not just a reporting framework — it is a risk management framework with implications for capital allocation, risk mitigation and performance management.
  • May impact optimal product mix and product design — some products become relatively more capital-efficient.
  • Impacts optimal asset mix — some asset classes more attractive due to lower capital charges.
  • Risk diversification benefits may affect corporate structures and trigger M&A activity.
  • Management information changed to align Solvency II metrics with business decision-making.
Indian Regulatory Context
India Solvency Regime & Solvency II Transition
India currently operates a factor-based solvency regime (ASM/RSM). This differs fundamentally from Solvency II's risk-based approach. The market is moving toward Solvency II-style RBC — making this a live exam topic.
CURRENT INDIA REGIME · IRDAI
ASM / RSM Framework
  • ASM = Available Solvency Margin = excess of admissible assets over liabilities
  • RSM = Required Solvency Margin = factor-based minimum capital requirement
  • Solvency Ratio = ASM ÷ RSM
  • Regulatory minimum: ASM ≥ 150% of RSM (i.e. solvency ratio ≥ 150%)
SR = ASM / RSM

δ(SR) = δ(ASM)/RSM − δ(RSM)×(ASM/RSM²)
= δ(ASM)/RSM − SR × δ(RSM)/RSM
Change in SR = ASM change as % of RSM − SR × RSM change as % of RSM [IAI Jul 2022 Q1iv]

SOLVENCY II-STYLE TRANSITION · IAI May 2024 Q2vii
Key differences when moving to Solvency II-style regulations
CURRENT INDIAN REGIME
  • Factor-based (not risk-based)
  • Prudent reserves in statutory accounts
  • ASM ≥ 150% RSM threshold
  • No formal BEL + Risk Margin split
  • No formal matching/volatility adjustment
SOLVENCY II-STYLE (PROPOSED)
  • Risk-based capital — capital tied to actual risk profile
  • TPs = BEL + Risk Margin (market-consistent)
  • SCR at prescribed confidence level
  • Standard formula OR internal model (with 6 tests)
  • Indian equivalent: Ind AS 117 for IFRS 17
INVESTMENT STRATEGY FOR ASM · IAI Jul 2022 Q1vi–vii
ALM for Shareholder Fund & ULIP equity exposure
  • Portion of ASM supporting regulatory minimum: Should mirror the policyholder fund's asset allocation, because RSM is a proxy for the risk of the liability book and moves similarly to policy liabilities.
  • Excess ASM (well above minimum): May be invested for higher expected returns — equity, bonds, property — as the buffer provides tolerance for market movements.
  • ULIP business creates an important ALM issue: ULIP unit funds and reserves move with equity markets → RSM also moves with equity markets → need corresponding equity in ASM to avoid the RSM increasing without a matching ASM increase (which would erode the solvency ratio).
BORDERLINE SOLVENCY · IAI Mar 2022 Q2
Implications, investigation and recovery plan
IMPLICATIONS OF BORDERLINE SOLVENCY
  • Regulatory intervention — closure to new business, capital injection required
  • Bad publicity → lower sales; broker withdrawal
  • Share price drop (if listed)
  • Credit rating downgrade
  • Staff morale/turnover impact → customer service quality
  • Increased lapses → further capital deterioration
  • Company expansion plans stalled
RECOVERY PLAN CONTENTS
  • Analysis of causes — systemic vs temporary
  • Business plan and projected solvency over 3+ years (certified by Appointed Actuary)
  • List of actions, rationale, impact on solvency, timelines
  • PRE implications and how policyholder/investor confidence will be maintained
  • Regular monitoring and reporting framework
  • Board approval for the plan
  • Whether plan will be made public and why no regulatory action is necessary
Why expenses matter for solvency (IAI Mar 2022 Q2v): The CFO who says expense management is unrelated to solvency is wrong. Expense overrun eats directly into available capital; risk-based capital frameworks include expense risk as a driver of required capital; expense management drives profitability and hence long-term capital generation.

Exam Q&A — Solvency II
Questions drawn from IAI (Sep 2021, Nov 2023, Mar 2022, Jul 2022, May 2024) and IFoA (Apr 2019, Apr 2020, Sep 2021, Sep 2022) past papers.
1
Describe how assets and technical provisions (BEL and risk margin) are valued under Solvency II. [25m]
IAI Sep 2021 Q2ii
▶
Model Answer
Assets — three components: Investments valued at market value (quoted prices in active markets); if unavailable, mark-to-model (must be market-consistent). Reinsurance recoveries shown as an asset (not netted off); adjusted for expected reinsurer default. Participations (≥20% voting rights) valued on market-consistent basis. Prudent person principle applies — assets appropriate for nature/term of liabilities; chosen in best interests of policyholders; properly diversified.

TPs — Best Estimate Liability: Market-consistent approach; represents transfer value to another insurer. BEL = PV of expected future cashflows (benefits + expenses − premiums) discounted at risk-free yield curve. Best estimate assumptions; no prudential margins. Policy-by-policy preferred; model points permitted. BEL can be negative. Contract boundary — premiums only to point of unilateral repricing. Matching adjustment or volatility adjustment can be added to discount rate where applicable.

TPs — Risk Margin: Addition to BEL bringing TPs to transfer value. Represents compensation for non-hedgeable risks and cost of holding regulatory capital. Cost of capital method: project future non-hedgeable SCR during run-off; multiply by 6% p.a.; discount at risk-free rates. Simplified driver approach permitted. Can be reduced for diversification.
2
Outline each pillar of the three-pillars approach under Solvency II. [4m]
IFoA Apr 2020 Q1(i)
▶
Model Answer
Pillar 1 (Quantitative): Sets minimum capital requirements (SCR and MCR) and specifies market-consistent valuation methodologies for assets and liabilities. Two capital requirements: SCR (calculated via standard formula or internal model) and MCR. Supervisors may impose a capital add-on. SCR and MCR must be held in addition to technical provisions.

Pillar 2 (Qualitative): Governance, systems of governance and risk management. Requires four key functions (risk management, actuarial, compliance, internal audit). Each insurer must conduct an ORSA. Supervisory review process.

Pillar 3 (Reporting/Disclosure): Defined reports to regulators (RSR + QRT, annually; MCR/SCR subset quarterly) and public (SFCR, annually within 3–4 months of year-end). Market discipline from public disclosures reinforces supervisory oversight.
3
Describe the risk margin calculation under Solvency II, including the cost of capital method and simplified approaches. [12m]
IAI Nov 2023 Q1v
▶
Model Answer
Purpose: The risk margin brings TPs to the transfer value — the theoretical compensation for non-hedgeable risks and the cost of holding regulatory capital against them.

Non-hedgeable risks covered: All insurance risk, reinsurance credit risk, operational risk, and residual market risk (where liability duration > available risk-free asset duration).

Three-step cost of capital method:
(1) Project future capital requirement (non-hedgeable SCR subset) at each future year-end during run-off of existing business.
(2) Multiply each year's projected capital by the cost of capital rate — 6% p.a. (fixed under Solvency II).
(3) Discount the resulting amounts at risk-free rates to give the overall risk margin.

Simplified approaches: Select a driver approximately linearly related to the required capital (e.g. policy count for expense risk; sum at risk for morbidity; total reserves for operational risk). Express initial capital as % of driver; project capital as same % of projected driver at each future date. More sophisticated methods using multiple drivers and correlations may be needed in practice.

Diversification: RM can be reduced for diversification between lines of business up to legal entity level, even though it must be disclosed separately by line.
4
Discuss whether the company should use the standard formula or an internal model to calculate the SCR. [12m]
IAI Nov 2023 Q1vi
▶
Model Answer
In favour of standard formula: If the standard formula is a good fit to the company's risk profile, it saves significant resources. Lower cost — no model development, documentation, or regulatory approval required. Suitable for smaller companies without specialist expertise. Easier for senior management to understand. Simpler to maintain and audit.

In favour of internal model: If the risk profile differs materially from SF assumptions — e.g. specialist products, unusual risks — the internal model provides a more accurate capital picture. May reveal lower capital requirements if company has better-than-assumed diversification. Competitive advantage: better risk management, better business decisions. If the company already has risk models for other purposes, extending them to Solvency II may be less resource-intensive. Demonstrates sophistication to investors.

Challenges of internal model: Must pass 6 tests — particularly the use test (most challenging). High cost: staff, consultant, IT, documentation. Limited historical data for extreme morbidity events. Complex dependency structures under stress. Once approved, cannot revert to standard formula without regulator approval.

Partial internal model may be a good compromise — use internal model for specialist risk modules where SF is a poor fit, and standard formula elsewhere.

A PMI insurer has commissioned an independent review of its Solvency II TPs and SCR. What areas should the review cover and what information is needed? [17m]
IFoA Apr 2019 Q2(i) & Q2(ii)

Areas for review (i): Review of methodology, approach and assumptions for TPs against Solvency II requirements. Appropriateness of standard formula (iv): Assessment of whether the insurer's risk profile materially deviates from SF assumptions, assessed per risk module separately. Check insurer has expertise and funds for an internal model if SF is not appropriate.

Why is the SCR net asset value taken as assets minus BEL (not assets minus TPs)? Calculate SCR_health given SCR_SLT=£614.3m, SCR_Non-SLT=£250m, SCR_CAT=£100m.
SA1 Ch12 Exam QA
▶
Model Answer
Why BEL only: TPs = BEL + Risk Margin. The Risk Margin calculation itself requires the SCR to be calculated first (to project the future non-hedgeable capital). Using TPs (which include the RM) in the SCR calculation would introduce a circularity — the SCR depends on the RM and the RM depends on the SCR. Using only the BEL avoids this circularity and makes the calculation practical.

SCR_health calculation:
SCR_health = √(SCR_SLT² + SCR_Non-SLT² + SCR_CAT² + 2×ρ_SN × SCR_SLT × SCR_Non-SLT + 2×ρ_SC × SCR_SLT × SCR_CAT + 2×ρ_NC × SCR_Non-SLT × SCR_CAT)

= √(614.3² + 250² + 100² + 2×0.50×614.3×250 + 2×0.25×614.3×100 + 2×0.25×250×100)
= √(377,484.5 + 62,500 + 10,000 + 153,575 + 30,715 + 12,500)
= √646,774.5 ≈ £804.2m

What should a company do when its solvency ratio falls to a borderline level, and what actions can it take to restore solvency? [14m combined]
IAI Mar 2022 Q2ii–Q2iv
▶
Model Answer
Implications (Q2ii): Regulatory intervention — possible restriction or closure to new business, requirement for immediate capital injection. Bad publicity leading to lower sales; broker withdrawal; share price drop; credit rating downgrade. Staff morale and turnover impact. Increased lapses worsening financials. Company expansion plans stalled.

Chief Actuary investigation (Q2iii): Investigate drivers from both ASM (assets) and RSM (liabilities/capital requirement) sides. Outline the tests an internal model must pass to be used to calculate the SCR under Solvency II-style regulations. [7m]
IAI May 2024 Q2vii
▶
Model Answer
(1) Use test: Model must be widely used throughout all relevant areas of business. Must play a significant role in internal governance, risk management, decision-making, economic/solvency capital assessments, and capital allocation. Most challenging test to demonstrate — requires evidence of embeddedness and risk culture (senior management sign-off, ORSA integration).

(2) Statistical quality standards: Minimum quality standards for assumptions and data (accurate, complete, appropriate, updated annually); probability distribution forecasting; use of expert judgement; materiality considerations; methods of aggregation.

(3) Calibration standards: SCR must be calibrated equivalent to VaR at 99.5% confidence over one year. Alternative confidence levels/time horizons permitted if equivalent policyholder protection is demonstrated.

(4) Profit and loss attribution: Demonstrate how the risk categorisation in the model will be used to explain the causes and sources of actual profits and losses.

(5) Validation standards: Fully validated by the company; regular control cycle review including testing against emerging experience; independent reviews; comparison against external data.

(6) Documentation standards: Design and operational aspects clearly and thoroughly documented — including theory, assumptions, and circumstances under which the model does not work effectively.
Key Terms
Solvency II Glossary
Solvency II
A risk-based, harmonised EU-wide regulatory framework for insurance firms. Operative from 1 January 2016. Main objective: adequate protection of policyholders and beneficiaries. Three pillars: Quantitative (P1), Governance (P2), Disclosure (P3).
EIOPA
European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority — provides technical advice and support to the European Commission for Solvency II. Publishes risk-free yield curves monthly for 53 countries.
SCR
Solvency Capital Requirement — VaR at 99.5% confidence over one year of basic own funds. Can use standard formula or internal model. First point of supervisory intervention if breached; recovery plan required within 2 months; rectify within 6 months.
MCR
Minimum Capital Requirement — factor-based linear formula targeted at VaR 85% over one year. Floor 25%, cap 45% of SCR. Absolute minimum €3.7m/€2.5m. Reported quarterly. Ultimate point of supervisory intervention — breach leads to authorisation withdrawal.
Technical Provisions
Market-consistent transfer value of insurance obligations = BEL + Risk Margin. Segmented by homogeneous product type. Classified as SLT health (IP, SACI, LTC) or Non-SLT health (PMI).
BEL
Best Estimate Liability — PV of expected future cashflows (benefits + expenses − premiums) discounted at risk-free yield curve. No prudential margins. Can be negative. Ideally policy-by-policy; model points permitted.
Contract Boundary
The point at which the company can unilaterally terminate a contract, refuse premiums, or reprice to fully reflect risks. Future premiums only included within this boundary. Long-term health = maturity date; annually renewable PMI = end of policy year.
Risk Margin
Addition to BEL bringing TPs to transfer value. Calculated by cost of capital method: project future non-hedgeable SCR; multiply by 6% p.a.; discount at risk-free rates. Can be reduced for diversification.
Matching Adjustment
Addition to risk-free discount rate for long-term predictable liabilities backed by matching assets held to maturity. MA = asset spread − fundamental spread. Requires regulator approval; strict eligibility requirements.
Volatility Adjustment
Addition to risk-free discount rate for liabilities not eligible for the matching adjustment. Reduces risk of forced asset sales in extreme bond spread movements. Based on representative portfolio spreads per currency. Published by EIOPA.
Prudent Person Principle
Principles-based test for non-linked asset investments under Solvency II. Replaces prescriptive admissibility limits. Requires assets appropriate for nature/term of liabilities; chosen in best interests of policyholders; properly diversified.
Standard Formula
Prescribed approach for calculating SCR using standard stress tests and factors for each risk module, aggregated using prescribed correlation matrices. Firms must justify it is appropriate for their risk profile.
BSCR
Basic SCR — combination of module SCRs (market, counterparty default, life, health, non-life) using a specified correlation matrix. SCR = BSCR + SCR_op + Adj (loss-absorbing capacity adjustment).
SLT Health
Similar to Life Techniques — health contracts written on similar technical basis to life insurance (long-term). Covers IP, SACI and LTC. Health SLT module includes: disability/morbidity, mortality, longevity, lapse, expense, revision risks.
Non-SLT Health
Health contracts similar to non-life insurance (short-term). Covers PMI. Module uses volume measure × standard deviation × adjustment factor.
CAT Risk (Health)
Health catastrophe risk sub-module — extreme events including epidemics, pandemics, climate change, anti-microbial resistance. Standardised scenario-based approach.
ORSA
Own Risk and Solvency Assessment — entirety of processes to identify, assess, monitor, manage and report risks and determine own funds needed. Part of Pillar 2 governance and also a Pillar 3 report to supervisor. Must be genuinely embedded in senior management decision-making.
Capital Add-On
Additional capital requirement added to the SCR by the supervisor in exceptional circumstances — e.g. where the risk profile materially differs from SF assumptions and no internal model has been developed.
Own Funds
Assets in excess of TPs plus subordinated liabilities. Split: Basic own funds (existing capital) and Ancillary own funds (callable capital). Tiered into Tier 1 (highest), 2, 3 (lowest) based on loss absorbency and permanence.
Tiering — SCR/MCR
SCR: ≥50% Tier 1; <15% Tier 3. MCR: ≥80% Tier 1; no Tier 3. Higher quality capital required for MCR given it is the ultimate intervention threshold.
RSR
Regular Supervisory Report — private annual report to supervisor containing qualitative information and QRTs. Pre-defined sections including ORSA. Summary report acceptable under certain conditions.
SFCR
Solvency and Financial Condition Report — annual public disclosure. Must be published and submitted to regulator within 3–4 months of year-end.
QRT
Quantitative Reporting Templates — quantitative part of private regulatory reporting. Annual full submission; quarterly MCR/SCR subset.
Use Test
Internal model approval test — model must be widely used throughout all relevant areas of business; significant role in governance, risk management, capital allocation decisions. Most challenging test to evidence.
Equivalence
Determination that a third country's regulatory regime is broadly Solvency II compliant. Full (indefinite): Bermuda, Switzerland. Provisional (10 years): USA, Australia, Japan, Brazil, Canada, Mexico.
Group Solvency Floor
Sum of MCRs (or local equivalent) for each entity in the group. An insurance group must cover both its overall group SCR and this group solvency floor.
ASM / RSM (India)
Available Solvency Margin / Required Solvency Margin — India's current factor-based regime. Regulatory minimum: ASM ≥ 150% of RSM. RSM is factor-based (not risk-based). India is transitioning toward Solvency II-style RBC.
