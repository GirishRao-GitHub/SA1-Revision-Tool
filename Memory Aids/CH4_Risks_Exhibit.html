01 Policy & Data Insurer database; accuracy; availability
  • Renewal data must capture claims history, benefit utilisation, and demographic shifts — incomplete data prevents credible experience rating at renewal.
  • Network provider data (charges, procedure codes) essential for claims pricing; data latency in cashless settlement systems creates IBNR exposure.
  • Condition code accuracy at claim stage is critical — misclassified CI conditions undermine experience monitoring and morbidity assumption updates.
  • Policy data must record underwriting exclusions applied; omission leads to contested claims and regulatory complaints at claim stage.
  • Salary data must be refreshed periodically to avoid replacement ratio exceeding the contractual cap — data quality failure is a source of over-insurance.
  • Occupation class recorded at inception is a primary pricing factor; occupation changes during the policy term may not be reported, creating mis-rating.
  • Long policy duration (30–40 years) makes policyholder contact data especially prone to becoming stale — critical for claim notification and ADL assessment scheduling.
  • ADL assessment results must be recorded with precision; subjective assessments create data inconsistency across a claims portfolio.
  • Group schemes require member-level data feeds from the employer's HR/payroll system — feed failures cause membership mis-counts and premium miscalculation.
  • Joiner/leaver notifications must be timely; delayed notifications create cover-without-premium or premium-without-cover gaps.
02 Claims — Rates Model · Parameter · Random fluctuation
  • Model risk: frequency–severity model may not capture the non-linearity of high-cost, low-frequency PMI claims (e.g. oncology, cardiac surgery).
  • Parameter risk: utilisation rates by treatment category, age-band, and network tier are highly sensitive assumptions; small errors compound over a large book.
  • Random fluctuation: even if the model and parameters are correct, actual claims in a small PMI portfolio can deviate materially from expected in any single year. HIGH
  • Model risk: multi-state model must distinguish between no-claim, CI claim, and post-CI mortality states — failure to model survival-period lapse distorts claim rates.
  • Parameter risk: condition-specific incidence rates (especially cancer by stage) are sensitive to population health trends and medical advances; reinsurer data may lag. HIGH
  • Random fluctuation: for small CI books, a cluster of high-severity claims in a single condition can dominate the annual result.
  • Model risk: two-state (sick/well) inception-annuity models may fail to capture partial recovery and rehabilitation, understating expected claim duration.
  • Parameter risk: both incidence and recovery rates are uncertain; a 10% worsening in recovery rates compounds claim duration significantly. HIGH
  • Random fluctuation: economic cycle correlation with incidence and recovery creates systemic year-on-year volatility beyond random.
  • All three claim rate risks are acutely present over the very long policy duration; longevity changes alter both the timing and severity of LTCI claims. HIGH
  • Morbidity improvement trends are poorly understood — compression of morbidity (people staying healthy longer) vs expansion (longer disability periods) creates fundamental model uncertainty.
  • Group claim rates depend on the employer's industry, workforce age profile, and absence culture; parameter risk is partly mitigated by experience rating.
  • Random fluctuation is lower for large groups (law of large numbers) but concentration risk from a single large employer can dominate.
03 Claims — Amounts Severity; medical inflation; limits
  • Medical inflation — driven by new treatments, technology, and hospital cost structures — is the dominant severity risk in indemnity PMI; reviewable premiums are the primary mitigation. HIGH
  • Unbounded indemnity exposure for catastrophic claims (e.g. complex cancer protocols) is mitigated only by per-claim or aggregate reinsurance.
  • CI pays a fixed lump sum — claim amount risk is eliminated for the insurer. The risk instead relates to frequency and the accuracy of the sum assured assessed at underwriting.
  • Severity risk re-enters via severity-tiered structures where the payment percentage depends on clinical stage — classification errors affect the pay-out amount. MEDIUM
  • Claim amount is a function of the replacement ratio × pre-disability salary × benefit duration; all three components have uncertainty.
  • Wage inflation during the claim period increases claim cost if benefit escalates with earnings — an underappreciated severity risk on long-duration claims. MEDIUM
  • Care cost inflation (labour-intensive, exceeds CPI) on indemnity LTCI is the dominant severity risk; the risk is compounded over a potential 10–20 year claim duration. HIGH
  • Residential vs nursing home vs home care have materially different cost levels; care setting mix may differ from the pricing assumption.
  • Group PMI claim amounts face same medical inflation risk as individual; group IP claim amounts depend on the salary distribution of the covered workforce.
  • High earners dominate aggregate claim cost in group IP and CI — one high-salary long-term disability can dwarf many routine claims.
04 Discontinuance Anti-selective lapsing; selective withdrawal
  • Under community rating, healthy lives lapse to cheaper risk-rated alternatives, leaving a progressively adverse pool — the death spiral risk.
  • No-claims discounts and loyalty incentives are standard tools to retain good risks before they lapse.
  • Selective lapsing: policyholders in good health are more likely to lapse when prices rise; those who know or suspect a claim is coming tend to persist. HIGH
  • Unit-linked CI: a large charge review triggers lapse spikes among healthy lives, worsening the morbidity of the remaining book.
  • IP lapse experience is relatively low; however policyholders in good health lapse when premiums rise (e.g. reviewable products), leaving a worse risk pool. MEDIUM
  • Economic downturns drive involuntary lapse as policyholders cannot afford premiums — correlated with the period when IP claims are already rising.
  • Pre-funded LTCI: lapses by healthier lives who reassess affordability worsen the remaining pool; older lapsers with large surrender values create cash-flow risk.
  • Premium increase at repricing can trigger a lapse spike specifically among better risks, creating a feedback loop of worsening pool and further increases. HIGH
  • Group lapse is driven at the employer level — loss of one large corporate can be equivalent to thousands of individual lapses in one event.
  • Group IP: employer insolvency or M&A can cause abrupt large-scale discontinuance; the insurer must manage run-off of in-force claims from ceased groups.
05 Expense Risk Unit costs; inflation; volume mis-estimation
  • Claims handling costs per episode are high in PMI due to pre-authorisation, network management, and cashless settlement administration — cost overruns erode margins.
  • Expense over-run on distribution (panel broker fees, bancassurance commissions) is amplified if new business volumes fall below the budgeted level.
  • CI expense risk is moderate — claims are discrete lump sums with lower per-claim administration cost than PMI; however contested CI claims can generate significant legal and assessment expense. MEDIUM
  • Expense inflation on medical evidence gathering (GP reports, specialist assessments) adds to per-claim cost.
  • Claims management expenses are high for long-duration IP claims — regular medical reviews, rehabilitation co-ordination, and periodic benefit recalculation all add cost.
  • Unit expenses must be estimated for the full in-force management period (potentially decades); expense inflation assumptions compound significantly over long durations. MEDIUM
  • ADL assessment costs (requiring qualified assessors) are a substantial per-claim overhead on LTCI — more so than other H&C lines.
  • Expense inflation over a 20–30 year claim period is very difficult to forecast; wage inflation in care professions exceeds general CPI. HIGH
  • Group schemes benefit from per-member expense economies of scale; however, employer HR integration and customised reporting add fixed costs that may exceed projections for small groups.
  • Tender process costs (actuarial, legal, systems) are a significant pre-inception sunk cost that must be recovered from the scheme's margin.
06 Investment / ALM Return risk; liability matching; liquidity
  • PMI liabilities are short-tail — most claims are settled within 12 months — so investment risk is moderate; short-duration assets are appropriate and mismatching is limited. LOW–MED
  • However, IBNR reserves require some medium-term investment; interest rate falls reduce discount rate and increase reserve values.
  • CI liabilities are also relatively short-tail (lump sum on diagnosis); investment risk is limited versus long-tail products. LOW
  • Unit-linked CI: the investment component is policyholder-borne; the insurer retains only morbidity charge risk, not fund performance risk.
  • Long-duration IP claims create long-tail liabilities requiring investment in longer-dated assets — mismatching creates interest rate sensitivity on the reserve. MEDIUM
  • Benefits linked to wage inflation require inflation-hedging assets (index-linked gilts, infrastructure); illiquid assets limit the insurer's ability to pay benefit if asset sales are needed.
  • LTCI has the most acute ALM challenge across all H&C products: very long-duration liabilities (30–40 years) must be matched to similarly long-duration assets. HIGH
  • Low interest rate environments squeeze the margin between asset return and care cost inflation — a key systemic risk for the LTCI market.
  • Illiquid asset investment (infrastructure, private credit) can improve yield but creates liquidity mismatch if claims accelerate unexpectedly.
  • Group PMI/CI: investment risk profile mirrors individual (short-tail). Group IP: same long-tail ALM considerations as individual IP, potentially at larger scale.
  • Self-funded group schemes managed by the insurer create an additional ALM risk: if the employer's own funds are insufficient, the insurer may face credit risk on deferred claim settlement.
07 Reserving IBNR; adequacy; basis risk
  • IBNR estimation is significant for PMI — healthcare claims are often notified weeks after treatment; development patterns must be estimated from historical reporting lags.
  • Basis change risk: if medical inflation accelerates after the reserving date, IBNR held at old cost levels will be deficient. MEDIUM
  • CI reserves cover claims incurred but not yet reported and claims in the process of assessment; the survival period (typically 28 days) creates a reservoir of near-claim cases that must be estimated.
  • If claim assessment is protracted (contested conditions, medical dispute), reserves held for in-assessment claims may be held for longer than assumed. LOW–MED
  • IP claim reserves are complex: the insurer must estimate the expected future benefit stream for each open claim, requiring assumptions on recovery rates, future salary inflation, and mortality. HIGH
  • Deterioration in recovery rates post-reserving date (e.g. due to economic downturn) makes reserves deficient — a source of reserve strengthening.
  • LTCI in-claim reserves require projecting care costs and claim duration over decades — longevity improvement and care cost inflation can make initial claim reserves materially deficient. HIGH
  • Changes in regulatory reserving standards (e.g. IFRS 17 adoption) can trigger significant reserve re-measurement that affects capital position.
  • Group PMI IBNR: timing of employer premium payment and claim notification creates data cut-off mismatches that complicate IBNR estimation.
  • Group IP claim reserves face the same long-tail challenge as individual IP; large individual claims within a group dominate the portfolio reserve.
08 Reinsurance Counter-party; basis; availability
  • Counter-party risk: if the reinsurer becomes insolvent, the cedant retains the full gross risk — reinsurer credit ratings must be monitored continuously.
  • Basis risk: if the reinsurance treaty definition of covered conditions differs from the cedant's policy wording, some claims may fall in the gap between retained and reinsured risk. MEDIUM
  • Reinsurance is essential for CI given the large sum assureds and limited own data on condition-specific incidence — counter-party risk is therefore more acute. HIGH
  • New condition extensions may not be covered under existing reinsurance treaties; new specific agreements may be required, at cost and possibly with restrictive terms.
  • XL reinsurance on IP is essential for very long-duration claims; reinsurer appetite for long-tail disability risk may reduce at renewal in adverse market conditions. MEDIUM
  • Quota share arrangements improve capital position but transfer underwriting profit — the balance between capital relief and profit retention is a strategic choice.
  • Reinsurance availability is structurally limited for LTCI — few reinsurers offer long-tail LTC cover at commercial rates; cedants may have no choice but to retain risk. HIGH
  • Any available reinsurance is likely to be at significant cost with restrictive terms, limiting its effectiveness as a risk mitigation tool.
  • Group schemes frequently use stop-loss reinsurance capping aggregate claims in a year above a threshold — basis risk arises if the aggregate definition excludes some claim types.
  • Per-risk XL limits the insurer's exposure to a single large claim (e.g. a very high-earning long-term IP claimant); these treaties are essential for large salary multiples in group CI/IP.
09 Moral Hazard & Fraud Policyholder behaviour; over-utilisation
  • Over-utilisation: insured individuals tend to use more healthcare than uninsured individuals — insurer must use excesses, co-payments, and pre-authorisation to manage demand. HIGH
  • Provider-induced demand: networks with fee-for-service structures incentivise providers to recommend additional tests and procedures not clinically necessary.
  • Fraud: falsified bills, duplicate claims, and non-existent treatments in cashless networks are a material source of claim inflation in markets with weak provider governance.
  • Moral hazard is limited post-claim (the event either happened or it didn't); however, manufactured diagnosis (paying a doctor to certify a CI condition prematurely) is a fraud risk. LOW–MED
  • Non-disclosure at inception (hiding a pre-existing condition related to a CI) is a more common fraud vector and is addressed through claim-stage underwriting.
  • Malingering: policyholders may extend their disability period beyond genuine incapacity — this risk increases when the replacement ratio is close to or at 100% of net income. HIGH
  • Moral hazard at inception: individuals with known health concerns may deliberately purchase IP cover knowing a claim is likely — addressed by medical underwriting and deferred periods.
  • ADL manipulation: care providers or family members may assist or encourage claimants to fail ADL assessments that they could manage independently — difficult to detect and prove. MEDIUM
  • Benefit-to-care-provider payment creates a moral hazard if the provider has an incentive to maintain the claimant in a more dependent state to preserve their income.
  • Group moral hazard is lower than individual because the employer has a reputational and commercial incentive to manage absence within the group scheme.
  • However, claims culture within a specific employer (industry norms, union influence on absence management) can create elevated claim rates for that group.
10 Regulatory & Legal Capital; TCF; product governance; litigation
  • Community rating mandates (where imposed by regulation) constrain the insurer's ability to price for individual risk — a regulatory risk that limits profit potential and creates sustainability pressure.
  • IRDAI product filing requirements in India mandate prior approval of rates and benefits; delays in approval create time-to-market risk and may leave the insurer with an uncompetitive product. MEDIUM
  • Consumer Duty / TCF obligations require CI product governance to ensure the target market is appropriate and the product delivers fair value — regulatory risk of enforcement action if CI is mis-sold. MEDIUM
  • Definition standardisation imposed by regulators (e.g. mandatory minimum conditions list) reduces product differentiation and may make existing products non-compliant.
  • State benefit change risk: changes in government sick pay or disability benefit levels alter the replacement ratio arithmetic and may reduce demand for IP or change which deferred period is most popular. MEDIUM
  • Employment law changes affecting the definition of disability or reasonable adjustments may influence claim eligibility in ways the policy wording did not anticipate.
  • Means-testing changes in state care funding (e.g. raising the asset threshold at which the state pays) reduce the incentive to purchase private LTCI — a demand-side regulatory risk. HIGH
  • Care quality regulation raising minimum standards in care homes increases care costs, amplifying the severity risk on indemnity LTCI policies.
  • Employment legislation (e.g. obligations on employers regarding long-term absence, return-to-work adjustments) creates a changing context that affects how group IP benefits interact with employer obligations.
  • Data protection regulation (GDPR/equivalent) constrains the insurer's use of employee health data in group scheme administration and claims management. MEDIUM
11 External / Systemic Economic; pandemic; medical advances; demographic
  • Pandemic / catastrophe: a systemic health event (e.g. pandemic) generates correlated large-scale PMI claims across the entire portfolio simultaneously — standard diversification offers no protection. HIGH
  • Medical advance risk: new treatments that are highly effective but very expensive (e.g. gene therapies, targeted oncology) create claim cost inflation the insurer did not price for.
  • Medical advance risk: earlier detection of CI conditions (e.g. liquid biopsy for cancer) increases claim rates as more conditions are detected within the policy term without worsening actual morbidity. HIGH
  • Longer survival post-diagnosis (medical improvement) increases the proportion of policyholders who survive 28 days and therefore claim — increases incidence without being a worsening health event.
  • Economic cycle: recessions increase IP incidence (more people become disabled or claim) and lengthen recovery rates (policyholders defer return to a weak labour market). HIGH
  • Demographic change: ageing workforce and increasing prevalence of chronic conditions (diabetes, musculoskeletal) shifts the risk profile of the in-force book without premium adjustment.
  • Longevity improvement: people living longer means more individuals survive to require care — a core systemic risk for LTCI. HIGH
  • Demographic cliff: a wave of Baby Boomers entering care age creates a surge in LTCI claims over a compressed time window, testing reserves and capital simultaneously.
  • Medical advance: treatments that prevent or delay dementia onset would significantly reduce LTCI claims — a positive but uncertain scenario that makes it difficult to price conservatively.
  • Pandemic creates simultaneous elevated absence across the entire group portfolio — correlated claims that overwhelm both individual group stop-loss and aggregate XL reinsurance. HIGH
  • Industry concentration risk: a downturn in a specific sector (e.g. financial services, manufacturing) affects multiple group schemes in that industry simultaneously.
12 Product-Specific Unit-linked CI; reviewable premium mechanics
  • Reviewable premium risk: inability to increase premiums sufficiently (due to competitive or regulatory pressure) means the insurer absorbs medical inflation in its margin.
  • Network break-down risk: if key hospital network providers withdraw from the cashless network, PMI benefit value is impaired and lapses may increase.
  • Unit-linked CI specific risk: reviewable morbidity charge must be re-priced periodically; if the charge review triggers policyholder lapses (selective against insurer), the in-force book deteriorates. HIGH
  • Charge communication risk: increases in the morbidity charge must be communicated clearly; failure to communicate clearly leads to regulatory scrutiny and mis-selling complaints.
  • Investment performance (policyholder-borne) affects the policy's remaining fund value — if low fund value leads to policy lapse, the insurer loses future morbidity charge income. [inferred: logical extension of unit-linked mechanics]
  • Reviewable premium IP: same anti-selective lapse risk as CI on premium review; additionally, occupation class changes that the policyholder has not reported create mis-rating risk on the in-force book. MEDIUM
  • Immediate-needs annuity specific: the insurer must assess whether the claimant genuinely requires care and at what level — adverse selection at the point of purchase is high as only those already in care buy.
  • Pre-funded LTCI: premium holiday provisions (where policyholders stop paying in difficult financial periods) create cash-flow strain and potentially increase lapse rates.
  • Free cover limit breaches: if a group's average benefit exceeds the FCL (due to salary inflation) without individual UW being updated, the insurer takes on unintended risk above the FCL. MEDIUM
  • Self-funded scheme administration risk: errors in claims payments or benefit calculations by the insurer acting as TPA create legal liability to the employer.
01

Policy & Other Data Risk

DATABASE INTEGRITY · ACCURACY · AVAILABILITY · LATENCY
▼

The insurer's database is the foundation of every actuarial process — pricing, reserving, experience monitoring, and claims management all depend on accurate, complete, and timely policy and claims data. Data risk is systemic: it propagates errors into every downstream process.

Three dimensions of data risk: (1) Accuracy — individual policy records contain errors at inception or introduction (e.g. wrong age, wrong occupation class, exclusions not recorded). (2) Completeness — missing fields (e.g. no benefit in-force at claim date) prevent claims assessment. (3) Timeliness — late data updates mean the insurer's view of its in-force book lags reality, particularly critical for group scheme joiner/leaver lists.

Sub-categories of data risk
  • Inception data errors: wrong age, occupation class, sum assured recorded — mis-pricing, incorrect reserves, contested claims.
  • Claims data integrity: incorrect condition codes, IBNR mis-estimation if claims reporting dates are wrong.
  • Exclusion recording failures: UW exclusions not attached to policy record — claims paid that should have been declined.
  • Group data feeds: HR/payroll system feed failures — members covered without being charged or charged without being covered.
  • Experience monitoring accuracy: if data used for A vs E investigation is different from pricing data, the comparison is invalid.

Claims Risk — Rates

MODEL RISK · PARAMETER RISK · RANDOM FLUCTUATION RISK
▼

Three distinct risks attach to claims rates (incidence / inception / frequency):

Model risk: the mathematical model used to project claim rates is structurally wrong (e.g. using a two-state model when a multi-state model is more appropriate for IP).

Parameter risk: the model is correctly specified but the input assumptions (e.g. incidence rates by age and condition) are estimated incorrectly — the most common source of pricing inadequacy.

Random fluctuation risk: even with the perfect model and parameters, actual claims in any given year deviate from expected due to statistical randomness — especially in small portfolios. This is the irreducible risk of insurance.

The three claim rate risks — exam point
  • Model risk: wrong functional form for how age, duration, and occupation interact in an IP pricing model; use of static tables when trends are non-stationary.
  • Parameter risk: morbidity/incidence tables derived from inadequate or unrepresentative data; reliance on reinsurer tables that don't reflect the insurer's target market.
  • Random fluctuation: in a small book (e.g. fewer than 1,000 policyholders), a single catastrophic year can be two standard deviations from the mean — standard reinsurance does not help unless aggregate stop-loss is in place.
  • Correlation: in a pandemic or economic downturn, all three risks manifest simultaneously — the economic event both increases true incidence (parameter shift) and reveals model inadequacy.

Claims Risk — Amounts

SEVERITY · MEDICAL INFLATION · CARE COST · SALARY INFLATION
▼

Distinct from claims rate risk, claims amount risk concerns the cost per claim once it arises. This is most acute in indemnity products (PMI, indemnity LTCI) where the insurer has no fixed cap on the per-claim outflow.

Fixed-benefit products (CI, defined IP benefit) transfer amount risk to the policyholder — the insurer's exposure per claim is contractually capped. However, severity risk re-enters in severity-tiered CI structures and salary-linked IP.

Key dimensions of claim amount risk
  • Medical inflation (PMI): structural driver above general CPI; new treatments, hospital cost structures, clinician fees.
  • Care cost inflation (LTCI): labour-intensive sector; care worker wages, regulatory quality standards, property costs of residential homes.
  • Salary inflation (IP): if benefit is earnings-linked, claim cost grows with wage inflation over a long claim duration.
  • Severity-tier classification errors (CI): wrong clinical stage assigned at claim → wrong benefit percentage → reserving error.
  • Treatment technology change: a new, highly effective but expensive treatment (gene therapy, precision oncology) creates a step-change in claim cost that pricing did not anticipate.
