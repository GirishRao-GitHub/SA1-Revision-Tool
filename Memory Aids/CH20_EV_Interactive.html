What is Embedded Value?

Embedded Value (EV) is the consolidated measure of shareholders' interests in an in-force life and long-term health insurance business. It is the answer to: "If no new business were written from today, what would the existing book be worth to shareholders?"

The Core Relationship
Embedded Value = Adjusted Net Worth + Value of In-Force Business
Component 1

Adjusted Net Worth (ANW)

The market value of assets in excess of the supervisory reserves, plus any required solvency capital released. It represents the capital already in hand — what shareholders own today if the business runs off.

Component 2

Value of In-Force (VIF)

The present value of future shareholder profits from all in-force policies, discounted at the Risk Discount Rate (RDR). It's the economic value locked up in the existing policy portfolio.

Key Rate

Risk Discount Rate (RDR)

The hurdle rate used to discount future profits. It equals the risk-free rate + a risk premium that reflects the riskiness of the projected profits. Higher risk → higher RDR → lower VIF.

Shareholder Profit

What Flows to Shareholders?

In each period: Premiums + Investment income − Claims − Expenses − Increase in reserves. The increase in reserves is key — setting up reserves "locks up" profit until later years when reserves are released.

From your SA1 material (Reporting Framework, P5)

EV is used for: M&A valuations, management reporting, appraisal value calculations, and communicating the value of the existing book to shareholders and analysts. It is most relevant for long-term health business (IP, CI, LTCI). For short-term renewable business (PMI, HCP) it is much less meaningful because there is no long-term contractual right to future premiums.

Key distinction: Supervisory Reserving vs EV vs Pricing

Your material explicitly notes: "Pricing (realistic + profit margin) vs. Supervisory (prudent/margins) vs. Embedded Value (market consistent/broadly realistic)." The EV basis is closest to best estimate — risk is captured in the discount rate, not by loading assumptions.

Why is Year 1 often negative for long-term products?

Initial acquisition expenses and commissions are high, and the reserve build-up is large in the early years (as the insurer sets aside money for future claims). This is a key difference between long-term health (IP, CI, LTCI) and short-term products (PMI). The VIF captures all these future profit years discounted back to today.

Reserve Dynamics and EV
Long-Term Products (IP, CI, LTCI)

Reserves build, then run off

For a level-premium protection product, reserves build in early years as the increasing cost of cover exceeds the level premium, then run off as the remaining term shortens. The VIF is therefore meaningful and significant.

Short-Term Products (PMI, HCP)

No long-term reserve build

Annual renewable contracts. Reserves are mainly UPR + OSLR + IBNR. No multi-year profit signature from a single policy. EV is less meaningful — future premiums from renewals are not contractually guaranteed.

How to read this

ANW moves with asset values and prudence of reserves. VIF is highly sensitive to the RDR — raising the RDR by 1% materially reduces VIF. Lapse rates reduce VIF because future premium income is lost. This mirrors the SA1 syllabus principle: "EV assumptions are broadly realistic, with risk captured via the risk discount rate."

Short-term business (PMI, HCP) — why excluded from VIF?

PMI and HCP are annually renewable. There is no contractual right to future premiums — the policyholder can lapse or the insurer can reprice. Therefore no multi-year profit stream can be valued. Only the unexpired period reserves (UPR) matter. This is a crucial distinction for your exam.

Indian Context

LTCI in India

Long-term care insurance is nascent in India. EV for LTCI is highly sensitive to mortality assumptions (as policyholders must survive to make claims), ADL transition rates, and care costs. Market-consistent assumptions are hard to obtain given limited data.

Indian Context

PMI and PMJAY

PMI is the dominant short-term product. No VIF applies. The ANW reflects the net assets backing UPR + OSLR + IBNR. Medical inflation (a key IRDAI concern) doesn't directly affect VIF but hits the claims reserves and next year's pricing heavily.

From your material — on consistency of assumptions

"Assumptions must be considered in their totality, not in isolation." Key correlated pairs for an Indian health insurer: morbidity & lapse rates (selective withdrawal); morbidity & investment return (economic climate affects incidence); new business volume & fixed expenses (important for LTCI where Indian volumes are low); lapse/non-renewal rates & investment return.

Why EV assumptions differ from supervisory reserving

Supervisory reserves under the current Indian regime use prudent assumptions with MAD (Margin for Adverse Deviation). This creates an implicit surplus which is not recognised in the supervisory balance sheet but is recognised in EV. The difference between the supervisory reserve and the best estimate reserve is released as profit over time — this is captured in the VIF. As the IRDAI moves towards RBC (Risk-Based Capital), this interplay will become increasingly important.

EV Through the Six-Pillar Lens

Embedded Value is not just a P5 topic — it touches all six strategic pillars of the SA1 framework. Here's how each pillar feeds into or is affected by EV.

P1

Product Design & Distribution

Product features (guaranteed premiums, options, GIOs) affect the shape and riskiness of the VIF. Options with significant take-up rates reduce EV.

↳ EV impact: Options & guarantees reduce VIF; reviewable products may have higher VIF stability
P2

Pricing & Underwriting

Underpriced products reduce VIF. Anti-selection (moratorium underwriting for PMI) erodes ANW over time. EV basis ≠ pricing basis — see Assumptions tab.

↳ EV impact: Pricing adequacy directly drives VIF; RDR must exceed pricing profit margin
P3

Claims Management

Actual claim experience vs assumed affects VIF each period. Experience variances cause "analysis of surplus". Good rehabilitation (IP) keeps morbidity down → higher VIF.

↳ EV impact: Claims experience differences drive EV surplus / deficit each year
P4

Risk Management & Reinsurance

Reinsurance can increase EV by releasing capital tied up in prudent reserves (embedded value financing). Also affects VIF through ceded profit sharing.

↳ EV impact: RI reduces gross reserves, changes net VIF; EV financing is a specific RI structure
P5

Valuation & Data ← Core

This is home territory. EV = ANW + VIF. Model office, assumptions, RDR, reserve basis — all live here. Analysis of surplus explains year-on-year EV movements.

↳ EV is a P5 deliverable — calculated by the Appointed Actuary under P5 framework
P6

Regulation & Ethics

IRDAI does not mandate EV disclosure currently, but M&A and capital raising use it. As RBC evolves, EV concepts become central to regulatory capital reporting.

↳ EV impact: Not regulatory (India) but used in appraisal value for M&A; ethical disclosure required
Analysis of EV Surplus — Year-on-Year Movement

EV doesn't just sit still. Each year it changes due to several sources — this is the "analysis of surplus" or "analysis of change in EV".

Movement 1

Expected Return on EV (unwinding of discount)

Even if nothing changes, EV grows each year as future profits move one year closer and the discounting unwinds at the RDR. This is the "natural" growth — if actual EV grows less than this, value has been destroyed.

Movement 2

New Business Value

New policies written during the year add VIF. However, in year 1, acquisition costs often exceed the VIF added — so new business can reduce EV initially before adding value in later years.

Movement 3

Experience Variances

Actual experience (claims, lapses, expenses, investment) vs assumed. E.g. if morbidity is worse than best estimate, VIF falls. This feeds back into the monitoring and assumption-setting process (P5 control cycle).

Movement 4

Assumption Changes

If the actuarial team revises best estimate assumptions (e.g. upward revision to medical inflation in India), VIF changes accordingly. A worsening of assumed future morbidity reduces VIF immediately.

Movement 5

Other Items

Capital injections, dividends paid, reinsurance restructuring (e.g. EV financing), changes in investment strategy, currency movements (for overseas business).

Test Yourself

SA1-style questions on Embedded Value. Answer each one, then check the explanation.

1. An Indian health insurer sells both IP (Income Protection) and PMI. When calculating Embedded Value, which of the following is correct?
Correct answer: B. EV and VIF are concepts most relevant to long-term health business. For IP (a long-term product), future premiums are contractually locked in, so a multi-year profit signature can be valued and discounted. PMI is annually renewable — there is no contractual right to future premiums, so no VIF can be calculated for it. PMI does feature in the ANW (assets backing UPR, OSLR and IBNR), but not in the VIF.
2. The Risk Discount Rate (RDR) used in an EV calculation is increased from 12% to 14%. All else equal, what happens to the Embedded Value?
Correct answer: B. The VIF is the present value of future shareholder profits discounted at the RDR. A higher RDR reduces the present value of all future profits — exactly like discounting any cashflow at a higher rate gives a lower NPV. The ANW is unaffected by the RDR (it's based on current asset values vs reserves). So EV = ANW + VIF, and since VIF falls while ANW stays the same, EV falls overall.
3. A health insurer's Appointed Actuary is preparing the EV for the in-force LTCI (Long-Term Care Insurance) book. Which of the following describes the most appropriate approach to morbidity (ADL) assumptions?
Correct answer: C. The EV basis is "broadly realistic" — using best estimate assumptions. Risk is not loaded into assumptions (as in supervisory reserving) but is instead captured through a higher Risk Discount Rate. For LTCI specifically, the uncertainty around ADL transitions, longevity, and care costs is significant, so the RDR premium should be higher than for, say, a standard IP product. This is a direct application of: "Pricing vs Reserving vs EV assumptions — EV/profitability bases are broadly realistic, with risk allowed for via a risk discount rate."
4. A life reinsurer proposes an "EV financing" arrangement to a direct Indian health insurer. What does this involve?
Correct answer: B. Your SA1 material (Chapter 12) explicitly states: "Embedded value financing involves the reinsurer making a loan to the insurer which is repaid from future surpluses." The reinsurer is essentially monetising the VIF — advancing cash against future profits locked up in the in-force book. This improves the insurer's liquidity and solvency position today, at the cost of sharing future profits with the reinsurer.
5. An Indian health insurer's EV has fallen year-on-year despite profitable new business. Which combination of factors could explain this?
Correct answer: B. This question tests knowledge of "EV surplus analysis." Two key negative drivers: (1) Adverse morbidity experience — actual claims worse than best estimate → experience variance reduces VIF; (2) Upward revision to assumed medical inflation — worsens future expected claim amounts → assumption change reduces VIF immediately. Together these can overwhelm any positive contribution from new business. In the Indian context, medical inflation running at 12–15% p.a. is a perennial EV risk.
